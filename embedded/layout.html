16 · Layout Fundamentals
Stackup · Ground Planes · Return Current · Decoupling · Via Stitching
PCB layout is where electrical engineering meets geometry. The schematic defines what is connected; the layout defines how those connections are physically realised — and the physical realisation adds parasitics that the schematic ignores: trace inductance, via capacitance, return current loops, and coupling between adjacent conductors. Getting layout right means understanding these parasitics and routing so they work for you, not against you.
PCB Basics
A PCB (printed circuit board) is a laminated sandwich of conductive copper layers separated by dielectric substrate. The most common substrate is FR4 — a woven fibreglass cloth impregnated with epoxy resin. FR4 has a relative permittivity (dielectric constant) of roughly εᵣ ≈ 4.3 at 1 GHz, which matters for signal velocity and impedance calculations.
Copper weight is specified in oz/ft² — standard boards use 1 oz copper (35 µm thick). Traces are formed by etching away unwanted copper from a clad layer. Vias are drilled holes with plated copper walls that connect traces on different layers.
Via types by drill technique:
  • Through-hole via — drilled through the entire board; cheapest to manufacture, connects any two layers.
  • Blind via — drilled from one surface but doesn't exit the other side; connects a surface layer to an inner layer.
  • Buried via — connects two inner layers only, invisible from either surface; most expensive, used in high-density designs.
  • Microvia — laser-drilled, very small diameter (typically <0.15 mm); used in HDI (high-density interconnect) boards for routing under BGAs.
Layer Stackup
The choice of how many layers to use and what to put on each one is called the stackup. It is one of the most consequential design decisions for signal integrity, EMC, and cost.
4-layer stackup dedicates the inner layers entirely to power and ground, leaving signal layers sandwiched between reference planes for controlled impedance and return current management.
The classic 4-layer stackup for a mixed digital/analog design is SIG / GND / PWR / SIG (top to bottom). The two signal layers are each adjacent to a solid reference plane — critical for impedance control and return current management. The GND and PWR planes are tightly coupled to each other, forming a low-inductance parallel-plate capacitor that provides high-frequency bypassing across the entire board area.
For cost-sensitive 2-layer boards: run GND as a solid pour on one side and signal on the other. Accept higher loop inductance. Route sensitive signals (crystal, ADC inputs, RF) away from noisy digital signals. 2-layer is appropriate for simple, low-frequency boards; use 4-layer for anything above ~50 MHz or with a sensitive analog front-end.
Ground Planes & Return Current
The single most important concept in PCB layout is return current path. Every signal current needs a return path back to its source. At DC and low frequencies, return current takes the path of least resistance — which may meander anywhere through the ground plane. At high frequencies, return current takes the path of least inductance, which is directly beneath the signal trace on the adjacent reference plane.
A slot or split in the GND plane forces return current to take a long detour, creating a large current loop — the primary source of conducted and radiated EMI in digital PCBs.
The practical rules that follow from this:
  • Never cut slots through the ground plane under a signal trace. Board outlines, connector cutouts, mounting holes, and power plane splits must not cross under any trace that matters. If a signal must cross a plane split, add a bypass capacitor bridging the split at the crossing point.
  • Keep power plane splits away from sensitive signals. On the GND plane, ideally there are no splits at all. On a PWR plane with multiple rails, route high-speed signals around split boundaries, not across them.
  • Via placement matters. When a signal transitions layers via a via, the return current must also transition — it does so through a nearby GND via (see Via Stitching below). Place GND vias adjacent to signal vias for any layer change on a fast signal.
Decoupling Capacitors
Digital logic draws current in sharp bursts as gates switch. The supply rail cannot respond instantly — the PCB trace and via to the power supply have inductance, causing the local VDD voltage to droop during switching. Decoupling capacitors (bypass caps) are local charge reservoirs placed at each IC's power pins; they supply the burst current instantly from nearby storage while the supply rail recovers.
There are two categories:
  • Bulk capacitors (10–100 µF): electrolytic or tantalum; placed near the power entry point (voltage regulator output, connector pin). They handle slower, larger transients — like an MCU waking from sleep and drawing 20 mA suddenly. Their self-resonant frequency is below 1 MHz, so they are not effective at high frequencies.
  • Local bypass capacitors (100 nF ceramic X5R/X7R): placed as close as possible to each VDD pin of each IC — ideally within 1–2 mm. Their low ESL makes them effective at 10–300 MHz. Every VDD pin on every IC gets its own 100 nF capacitor. Add a 10 nF in parallel for higher-frequency ICs (ADCs, RF, oscillators).
One bulk cap at the regulator output handles large slow transients; individual 100 nF ceramics sit as close as possible to each IC's VDD pin for high-frequency bypassing.
Placement and routing of bypass caps matters as much as their value:
  • Caps must be between the via and the IC pin, not the other way around. The via to the power plane should be as close to the capacitor as possible. An IC pin → trace → via → cap configuration puts trace inductance between the cap and the pin and defeats the purpose.
  • Place the cap on the same layer as the IC pin where possible, so no via is needed in the bypass path at all.
  • Minimise the loop area of the bypass path: VDD via → capacitor → GND via must be as small as possible. A cap placed 5 mm away from the IC pin, with long traces on both sides, adds significant inductance and is nearly useless at 100 MHz.
  • Value selection: 100 nF X7R 0402 is the standard local bypass capacitor. X5R and X7R dielectrics maintain capacitance over temperature and DC bias; avoid Y5V/Z5U which lose 80% of capacitance at rated voltage.
Via Stitching
Via stitching is the practice of placing a grid or row of GND vias at regular intervals to electrically connect ground pours or planes on different layers. It serves two related purposes:
  • Plane continuity across layers. Even with a solid GND plane on L2, the top-layer copper pour (GND fill) is a separate conductor until tied down with vias. Without stitching vias, the surface GND pour can float at high frequencies, picking up noise and radiating. Stitch vias every ~5–8 mm across the pour.
  • Return current via at layer transitions. When a signal via transitions from L1 to L3, the return current must also change layers. It does so through the nearest GND via. Place a GND via within 0.5–1 mm of every signal via on a fast net.
  • EMI shielding borders. A row of closely spaced stitching vias along the edge of a sensitive region (e.g., around an RF antenna area or a high-speed clock region) creates a low-impedance fence that reduces common-mode emissions crossing the boundary.
Via stitching spacing. A row of stitching vias acts as a fence with a cut-off frequency below which it is effective. Spacing between vias should be less than λ/20 at the highest frequency of concern. For a 100 MHz clock with harmonics to 500 MHz, λ/20 in FR4 (εᵣ ≈ 4.3) is about 14 mm — stitch at ≤10 mm for margin.
Mechanical Design Rules
PCB fabrication and assembly impose minimum dimensions that must be respected in the layout. The key rules:
Clearance (copper-to-copper spacing) is the minimum distance between two conductors on the same layer. For standard PCB fabrication, the minimum trace width and clearance is 0.1 mm (4 mil), though 0.15 mm (6 mil) is more reliable from budget fabs. Clearance violations cause shorts.
Creepage (surface distance) applies to high-voltage designs (mains-connected circuits, isolated supplies). It is the minimum distance along the board surface between conductors at different potentials. Creepage requirements come from safety standards (IEC 60950, IEC 62368) and depend on working voltage, pollution degree, and insulation class. For a 230 V mains circuit, creepage on a basic insulation requirement may be 2–6 mm. Board slots are cut between primary and secondary-side copper to extend the creepage path.
Board edge keepout is a region near the board perimeter where no copper is placed. The minimum is typically 0.3–0.5 mm from the physical edge. The V-score (scoring for snap-apart panels) and routing operations can crack or delaminate copper that is too close to the edge. For through-hole connectors and mounting hardware, check mechanical interference between the component body and the board edge.
Drill-to-copper clearance: Via and through-hole drill positions have tolerances (±0.1 mm is typical). Maintain a minimum annular ring of 0.15 mm around each via to ensure the pad is not drilled through. In your CAD tool this is usually enforced by the DRC (design rule check) automatically.
DRC before Gerber export. Every PCB CAD tool (KiCad, Altium, Eagle) has a design rule check that catches clearance violations, missing connections (airwires), unconnected net branches, and annular ring violations. Run DRC and resolve all errors before generating Gerber files for fabrication. Fab houses also run their own DRC and will reject boards with clearance violations.
