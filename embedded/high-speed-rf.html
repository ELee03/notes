19 · High-Speed & RF
Transmission Lines · Controlled Impedance · Differential Pairs · Antennas · Matching Networks
At low frequencies, a PCB trace is just a conductor. At high frequencies, it is a transmission line — and the distinction matters enormously. A signal transition that would be clean on a 100 kHz bus becomes distorted by reflections, radiation, and signal integrity violations at 100 MHz and above. Understanding transmission line behaviour, controlled impedance, and RF antenna coupling transforms what seems like "black magic" into predictable physics.
Transmission Lines
The rule of thumb for when a trace behaves as a transmission line: when the trace length exceeds λ/10 at the signal's highest significant frequency, transmission-line effects are important.
$$v = \frac{c}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_{r,\text{eff}}}}, \qquad \varepsilon_{r,\text{eff}} \approx \frac{\varepsilon_r + 1}{2} \approx \frac{4.3 + 1}{2} \approx 2.65$$ $$v \approx \frac{3 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s}}{\sqrt{2.65}} \approx 1.84 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s} \quad (\approx 0.6\,c)$$
Wavelength at frequency $f$: $\;\lambda = v/f$. The $\lambda/10$ threshold — trace length above which transmission-line effects must be considered:
Frequency λ/10 in FR4 Implication
100 MHz184 mmLong traces only — most designs fine
500 MHz37 mmMany longer traces qualify
2.4 GHz8 mmAlmost every trace — Bluetooth/Wi-Fi
USB Full-Speed (12 Mbps, harmonics to ~60 MHz): λ/10 ≈ 300 mm — FS traces don't need controlled impedance. USB High-Speed (480 Mbps, harmonics to ~2.4 GHz): λ/10 ≈ 8 mm — controlled impedance and length matching required.
The characteristic impedance Z₀ of a transmission line depends on the geometry — trace width, dielectric thickness, and dielectric constant. When the source impedance matches Z₀ matches the load impedance, energy transfers completely. When there is a mismatch, the reflection coefficient is:
$$\Gamma = \frac{Z_L - Z_0}{Z_L + Z_0}$$
Examples with $Z_0 = 50\,\Omega$: an open circuit ($Z_L \to \infty$) gives $\Gamma = +1$ — full reflection, same polarity. A short circuit ($Z_L = 0$) gives $\Gamma = -1$ — full reflection, inverted polarity. A matched load ($Z_L = Z_0$) gives $\Gamma = 0$ — zero reflection. A CMOS input (capacitive, $Z_L \approx 1/j\omega C \gg Z_0$) gives $\Gamma \approx +1$. A via stub adds roughly 0.5–1 nH of series inductance; at 2.4 GHz that is $j\omega L \approx 7\text{–}15\,\Omega$ — enough to cause a measurable reflection.
Microstrip vs Stripline
Microstrip (outer layer, partially in air) is the default for most PCB signal traces. Stripline (inner layer, between two reference planes) provides better isolation from adjacent noise and reduced radiation.
For the EFR32 and most embedded MCUs, the RF trace from the IC to the antenna connector or antenna is a 50 Ω microstrip. On a standard 4-layer board with 100–200 µm dielectric between L1 (signal) and L2 (GND): a 50 Ω microstrip trace is approximately 0.18–0.30 mm wide, depending on exact stackup. Always use the PCB manufacturer's impedance calculator or a tool like Saturn PCB Design, as FR4 εr varies ±10% across manufacturers and even within a batch.
Differential Pairs
Differential signalling transmits data as the voltage difference between two traces (V+ − V−) rather than a single trace referenced to GND. The advantage: any noise that couples into both traces equally (common-mode noise) is cancelled at the receiver. This makes differential interfaces (USB, Ethernet, LVDS, CAN) substantially more immune to noise and able to tolerate ground potential differences between communicating nodes.
Differential pair layout rules:
  • Route as a matched pair. The two traces must be the same width, the same copper weight, and as close to the same physical length as possible. Any length difference creates a skew between the two signals, converting common-mode noise into differential noise and degrading timing.
  • Keep traces close together. Tight coupling between the + and − traces means they pick up nearly identical noise, which cancels at the receiver. Typical gap: 0.1–0.2 mm (matching the trace width). Do not separate the pair to route around an obstacle — route the obstacle around the pair.
  • Length-match to within spec. USB 2.0 high-speed: maximum 100 ps skew ≈ 18 mm at 1.84e8 m/s in FR4. In practice, keep within 5 mm for most designs. Use meandering on the shorter trace to equalise length rather than lengthening both.
  • Avoid vias in the differential pair. Each via adds ~0.5–1 nH of inductance and a capacitive discontinuity. If a layer change is unavoidable, add a via to both traces simultaneously (never one alone), and place GND vias on either side.
  • Differential impedance Z_diff = 2 × Z_single × (1 − coupling). For tightly coupled traces, Z_diff is somewhat lower than 2 × Z_single. USB requires 90 Ω differential (±15%); Ethernet 100 Ω differential. PCB stack-up calculators handle this — specify the differential impedance target, not just single-ended.
USB Layout Rules
USB is the most common high-speed differential interface on embedded boards. The layout rules differ by USB speed:
USB Full-Speed (12 Mbps). Frequencies are below 100 MHz. Controlled impedance is recommended but not strictly required for short traces (<50 mm). Keep D+ and D− as a matched pair, away from clocks and switching supplies. Series resistors (27–33 Ω) near the MCU are standard for FS — they damp reflections.
USB High-Speed (480 Mbps). This requires full controlled impedance (90 Ω differential), careful length matching (within 5 mm), no vias in the data pair if possible, and routing entirely within the top signal layer with GND plane on L2. The USB spec allows a maximum of 18 mm length skew between D+ and D−; in practice design for ≤5 mm. 
/* USB HS routing checklist:
 * ✓ D+ and D− routed as differential pair, 90 Ω differential
 * ✓ Trace width and gap sized for 90 Ω diff (verify with calculator)
 * ✓ Length mismatch < 5 mm (meander the shorter trace)
 * ✓ No vias in differential pair
 * ✓ Solid GND plane directly under the pair (no slots)
 * ✓ Series 22 Ω resistors on D+/D− close to the MCU pad (not the connector)
 * ✓ ESD protection IC (TVS diode array) as close to the connector as possible
 * ✓ Place ESD protection IC before the series resistors in signal path:
 *   Connector → ESD TVS → 22 Ω series → MCU
 * ✗ Do not route USB pair parallel to clock or switching regulator traces
 * ✗ Do not split GND plane under the USB pair
 */
PCB Antennas
For the EFR32 family (Bluetooth, Zigbee, Thread, Wi-Fi), the RF output drives either a U.FL/SMA connector for an external antenna, or a PCB antenna fabricated in copper on the board itself. PCB antennas eliminate the cost and reliability issues of connectors, at the expense of layout constraints.
Chip antenna. A pre-tuned ceramic resonator in a small package (0402 or 0603). Low-cost, compact, and consistent. Placement requirements: the chip antenna must be at the board edge with no copper (ground plane, power pour, or other traces) within a specified clearance zone — typically 3–7 mm on each side and below the antenna, as specified in the antenna datasheet. Silicon Labs reference designs specify chip antenna placement precisely for the EFR32.
Inverted-F antenna (IFA). The most common PCB trace antenna for 2.4 GHz. It consists of a horizontal radiating element (approximately λ/4 long), a shorting strip connecting the element to the ground plane, and a feed point where the 50 Ω RF trace connects. The name comes from its cross-section resembling an inverted letter F.
Inverted-F antenna: the radiating element (~λ/4 long), a shorting strip to GND, and a 50 Ω feed tap. The entire structure sits in a copper-free keepout zone at the board edge.
At 2.4 GHz, λ/4 in free space is 31 mm. On a PCB, the element is slightly shorter because it rides over the board substrate, which increases the effective dielectric constant and shortens the resonant length. Typically the element length needs tuning by adjusting length and the position of the shorting strip. Silicon Labs provides reference designs and Simplicity Studio antenna models that are pre-tuned for the EFR32 footprint.
RF Keepouts
The RF keepout is the single most violated antenna layout rule by first-time designers. An antenna that radiates well in free space radiates poorly when surrounded by copper — the nearby copper changes the antenna's resonant frequency, reduces its radiation efficiency, and alters its radiation pattern.
  • Keepout zone dimensions. Follow the antenna manufacturer's application note or the EFR32 reference design exactly. For a typical 2.4 GHz chip antenna: no copper within 4–7 mm on the sides, 3–5 mm below, and the area directly in front of (or below) the radiating element must be completely copper-free on all layers.
  • Keepout applies to ALL copper on ALL layers. The ground plane must be pulled back. The power plane must be pulled back. Even GND via stitching must not enter the keepout zone. A GND via 2 mm from a chip antenna can detune it by tens of MHz.
  • The antenna must be at the board edge. Placing the antenna in the center of the board forces keepout in all directions, wasting board area and limiting routing. At the board edge, copper is only present on three sides instead of four.
  • Enclosures affect antenna tuning. A plastic enclosure (εr ≈ 2–4) placed over the antenna shifts its resonant frequency downward. If the product will live in an enclosure, retune the antenna with the enclosure present. Metal enclosures require a slot or window for the antenna.
Matching Networks
The EFR32's RF output pin has 50 Ω source impedance. Most antennas present an impedance close to 50 Ω when correctly sized and tuned — but never exactly 50 Ω due to manufacturing variation, nearby components, and enclosure effects. A matching network is a network of inductors and capacitors placed between the RF output and the antenna to transform the antenna's actual impedance to 50 Ω at the operating frequency, maximising power transfer and minimising reflections.
The standard topology is a π network (shunt-series-shunt) or L network (series-shunt). The component values are calculated from the load impedance using a Smith chart or an RF calculator (Murata SimSurfing, Keysight ADS, Qorvo Match.ly). 
/* EFR32 RF output to PCB antenna — typical matching network:
 *
 *   RF_PIN ─── L1 ─── ANT
 *                |
 *               C1
 *                |
 *               GND
 *
 * Component placement rules:
 * 1. L1 and C1 go between the RF pin and the antenna, as close to the
 *    RF pin as possible (ideally <3 mm from the pad).
 * 2. The GND via for C1 must be as short as possible — a long GND stub
 *    adds inductance that shifts the match point.
 * 3. Route the 50 Ω trace straight from the RF pin to the match network,
 *    then from the match network to the antenna — no bends, no vias.
 * 4. Keep the matching network away from the antenna keepout zone —
 *    it should be on the GND plane side of the RF trace, not in the
 *    keepout zone.
 * 5. Leave room for a 0 Ω shunt and series options in the footprint for
 *    tuning during bring-up (π topology provides this flexibility).
 *
 * The EFR32 datasheet and AN0715 (EFR32 RF Hardware Design) provide
 * recommended component values for specific antenna configurations.
 */
Measuring the matching network's effect requires a Vector Network Analyser (VNA) to measure S11 (return loss) at the RF pin. A well-matched antenna at 2.4 GHz shows S11 < −10 dB (less than 10% of power reflected back). If this is not available, a spectrum analyser with a directional coupler can give a rough indication. For most EFR32 designs following a reference design layout closely, the default matching component values work without adjustment.
Start from a validated reference design. For the EFR32 family, Silicon Labs provides reference schematics and layouts in Simplicity Studio and on the Silicon Labs website. These are tuned, RF-characterised designs. Unless there is a compelling reason to deviate, replicate the reference layout for the RF section exactly — especially the keepout dimensions, matching network placement, and RF trace routing. RF design has many subtle variables; reducing unknowns with a proven starting point is always the right call.
