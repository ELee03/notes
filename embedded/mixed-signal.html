17 · Mixed-Signal Layout
Analog–Digital Partitioning · ADC Layout · Reference Bypass · Guard Rings
Mixed-signal PCBs contain both digital circuits (MCU, memories, digital logic) and analog circuits (ADC inputs, amplifiers, sensors, voltage references). The challenge is that digital circuits are inherently noisy — they switch at high frequencies, draw pulsed current, and couple energy into nearby conductors — while analog circuits are inherently sensitive. A sensor measuring a 1 mV ECG signal on a board with a 3.3 V digital supply switching at 50 MHz is trying to resolve signals a million times smaller than the interference. Mixed-signal layout is the discipline of keeping these two worlds from corrupting each other.
The Noise Problem
Digital noise enters the analog signal path through three main mechanisms:
  • Ground bounce. Every digital switching event drives return current through the ground plane's finite impedance (recall: at high frequency, impedance is inductive, not just resistive). This creates tiny voltage differences across the ground plane — ground bounce. If the analog circuit's reference point (GND pin, reference input) sits at a different potential than the digital circuit's GND, even by 100 µV, it directly corrupts any analog measurement at that level.
  • Supply coupling. If the analog VDD and digital VDD share a power rail, digital switching noise appears on the analog supply and couples into the amplifier's output through its power supply rejection ratio (PSRR). Most op-amps have 60–80 dB PSRR at DC, falling to 20 dB or less at 10 MHz — so high-frequency supply noise gets through almost unattenuated.
  • Trace coupling. A digital trace running near an analog trace forms a capacitor. A clock line toggling at 3.3 V into 0.5 pF of parasitic capacitance injects dV/dt × C = 1e9 × 0.5e-12 = 0.5 mA pulses into the analog trace. At a 10 kΩ node impedance that's 5 V — an absurd coupling on a sensitive analog line.
Floorplanning
The most effective noise reduction technique is separation at the component placement level, before routing begins. This is called floorplanning: dividing the board into distinct zones and placing components entirely within their zone.
Analog components cluster on one side; digital components on the other. Any signal crossing the partition boundary passes through a filter. Never route digital traces through the analog zone.
Floorplanning rules:
  • Place all analog components (sensors, amplifiers, VREF, ADC input passives) on one side of the board. Place all digital components on the other.
  • The MCU, which contains both digital logic and an ADC, sits at the boundary — as close to the analog section as its package allows, with its analog pins (VDD_ANA, VREF, ADC inputs) facing the analog zone and its digital pins (GPIO, UART, SPI) facing the digital zone.
  • Any signal that must cross the analog/digital boundary should be filtered. An active signal crosses as a digitised output from the ADC (already digital at that point), not as an analog voltage.
  • Never route a digital clock, PWM output, or switching supply trace through the analog zone, even if it would be shorter.
Ground Plane Strategy
The traditional advice — "split the analog ground plane from the digital ground plane" — is now understood to be incorrect in most designs. The classic split-plane approach creates exactly the problem it tries to solve: any signal that crosses the split forces return current to take a long detour, creating a large current loop and substantial radiated EMI.
The correct approach, validated by Henry Ott and Analog Devices, is a single, continuous ground plane beneath the entire board. The key insight is that if you have done the floorplanning correctly, the digital return currents never need to flow through the analog region — they return directly beneath their source traces on the digital side of the plane. The ground plane does not need to be split to prevent this.
The one case where a split makes sense is when there is significant galvanic isolation between two grounds (mains-connected medical equipment, motor drives with high common-mode voltages). In that case, isolate the plane and connect at exactly one point — a star point — so there is no ground loop.
When connecting AGND and DGND on the same PCB: If the MCU has separate AGND and DGND pins (some ADC-equipped MCUs do), join them at a single point as close to the MCU as possible — directly beneath the IC, via a 0 Ω resistor or ferrite bead in the ground connection. Do not rely on the ground plane to connect them with multiple paths, which creates ground loops.
ADC & Reference Layout
The ADC is the most layout-sensitive component in a mixed-signal board. Every millivolt of noise on its supply, reference, or input appears directly in the conversion result. Key rules:
AVDD and DVDD bypass. Most ADC-equipped MCUs (including the EFR32) have separate AVDD (analog supply) and DVDD (digital supply) pins. Each must have its own decoupling capacitor, placed as close to the pin as possible. Do not merge the two supply bypass paths. AVDD should also have a bulk capacitor (1–10 µF) at the regulator output to the analog section.
VREF bypass. The ADC's reference voltage is its measurement standard. Any noise on VREF appears as direct measurement error. Bypass VREF with a large ceramic (1–10 µF) and a small ceramic (10–100 nF), both placed as close to the VREF pin as possible. The large capacitor filters low-frequency power supply noise; the small one handles high-frequency transients. The VREF trace should be short and routed away from any digital switching traces.
ADC input trace. The analog input to the ADC should be:
  • As short as possible from the antialiasing filter to the ADC pin.
  • Routed entirely within the analog zone — never in the digital zone.
  • Shielded from digital traces by ground traces on each side (a guard trace at GND potential, not a guard ring — that's different).
  • Never run parallel to a digital signal, especially a clock.
  • The RC antialiasing filter (typically R = 1–10 kΩ, C = 1–10 nF, chosen to give ≥10× the ADC sample rate) should be at the entry point of the analog zone, before the trace runs to the ADC.
Instrumentation amplifier layout (biosensor front-ends). Biosensors (ECG, EEG, EMG) produce signals of 10 µV to 10 mV from electrodes with source impedances of 1–100 kΩ. The instrumentation amplifier (InAmp) that amplifies these signals has extremely high input impedance (typically >10⁹ Ω differential, >10¹⁰ Ω common-mode). Layout rules for InAmps:
  • Keep the input traces short. The input pins of an InAmp are the most sensitive nodes on the board. The gain-setting resistor R_G must be placed as close as possible to the gain pins (within 2–3 mm for high gain factors).
  • Differential pair routing. Route IN+ and IN− as a matching pair, side-by-side, the same length. Mismatched length or routing creates differential-to-common-mode conversion that degrades CMRR.
  • Reference pin placement. The REF pin of the InAmp sets the output midpoint voltage. Drive it from a low-impedance source (buffered voltage divider or VREF output). Any noise on REF appears at the output.
Guard Rings
A guard ring is a copper trace that surrounds a high-impedance node and is driven to the same potential as that node. It intercepts surface leakage currents — tiny currents that flow along the PCB surface between conductors due to surface contamination, humidity, or flux residue — before they reach the sensitive node.
The guard ring is driven to the same voltage as the signal, so no voltage difference exists between the ring and the trace — leakage current finds no driving force to reach the trace.
The guard ring must be driven — connected to a low-impedance source at the same potential as the guarded node, not to GND. For a unity-gain buffer output: the output drives the ring, and the high-impedance input (from the sensor) is inside the ring. The ring intercepts leakage that would otherwise flow into the input. On both sides of the board, add guard rings on both the top and bottom copper layers and stitch them together with vias.
When guard rings are necessary. Guard rings are needed when the source impedance exceeds ~10 MΩ, or when measuring currents below ~1 nA. Typical applications: transimpedance amplifiers for photodiodes, pH electrode measurements, charge amplifiers for piezoelectric sensors, patch-clamp amplifiers. For standard 10–100 kΩ biosensor sources (ECG electrodes), guard rings on the PCB are not usually necessary — cable shielding and differential measurement are more effective.
Coupling Mechanisms & Mitigation
Understanding precisely how digital noise couples into analog circuitry lets you address each mechanism at its source:
Capacitive coupling (electric field). Two traces separated by distance d over a dielectric form a capacitor C ≈ ε₀ε_r × A/d. A 10 MHz clock trace running 5 mm parallel to an analog trace at 0.5 mm separation on FR4 has roughly 0.1–0.5 pF of coupling. At 10 MHz with a 3.3 V swing, injected noise current = V × 2πfC ≈ 20–100 µA into the analog node. Mitigation: maximize separation; route on different layers with a GND reference plane in between; never run digital and analog traces parallel on adjacent layers.
Inductive coupling (magnetic field). A current loop radiates a magnetic field that induces voltage in any nearby loop. Loop area is the dominant factor: a 10 mm² switching current loop from a 100 mA, 10 ns edge generates substantial field within ~5 cm. Mitigation: minimize switching loop area (keep bypass caps close to switcher); keep analog signal loops small; orient analog trace loops perpendicular to digital current loops.
Common-impedance coupling. The single most impactful coupling mechanism at the PCB level. Digital return currents flow through the shared ground plane impedance, creating ΔV = I × Z_ground voltage differences that appear at analog reference points. Mitigation: good floorplanning ensures digital return currents flow on the digital side of the plane, away from analog references; single-point ground connection between AGND and DGND prevents circulating ground loop currents.
Supply rail coupling. Even a well-regulated supply has finite output impedance. High-frequency current steps from digital switching pull down the rail voltage momentarily. Mitigation: ferrite bead + capacitor LC filter between the digital supply and the analog supply branch (LDO or RC filter feeding AVDD separately from the main 3.3 V rail). The ferrite bead's high impedance at switching frequencies blocks high-frequency noise from crossing from digital to analog supply.
