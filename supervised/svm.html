5 · Support Vector Machines
Maximum Margin · Lagrangian Duality · Kernel Trick · Soft-Margin · SVR
The Support Vector Machine (Boser, Guyon, Vapnik 1992; Cortes & Vapnik 1995) is a classification algorithm built on one geometric principle: among all hyperplanes that correctly separate two classes, choose the one with the largest margin. This single idea leads to a convex quadratic programme whose dual reveals a remarkable structure — the decision boundary depends only on a small subset of training points (the "support vectors") — and to the kernel trick, which allows SVMs to implicitly work in infinite-dimensional feature spaces at no extra computational cost. SVMs were the dominant classification method in the late 1990s and 2000s, and understanding them is essential for understanding kernels, duality, and the structural risk minimisation framework that underlies much of classical machine learning theory.
Why maximum margin?
Consider a binary classification problem with labels $y \in \{-1, +1\}$ and linearly separable data. Many hyperplanes correctly separate the two classes. Which should we pick?
The margin of a hyperplane $\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b = 0$ with respect to a training set is the distance from the hyperplane to the nearest training point. Intuitively, a large margin means the classifier is "confident" — a new test point must be far from the boundary to be misclassified. The SVM principle is: maximise the margin. This is not just intuitive — it is theoretically motivated. The Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) theory shows that for a margin-$\gamma$ classifier, the generalisation error bound depends on $1/\gamma^2$: larger margin implies a smaller effective hypothesis class and better generalisation.
Hard-margin SVM (primal problem)
A hyperplane in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is $\{x : \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b = 0\}$ where $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the normal vector and $b$ is the bias. The signed distance from a point $\mathbf{x}_i$ to this hyperplane is $(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b) / \|\mathbf{w}\|$.
Hard-margin SVM — primal derivation

For a correctly classified point: $y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b) > 0$. Without loss of generality, scale $\mathbf{w}$ so that the nearest points satisfy: $$y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b) \geq 1 \quad \forall i$$ (this is the canonical scaling — points are at functional distance $\geq 1$).

The geometric margin to the boundary from the nearest points is $1/\|\mathbf{w}\|$ on each side, so the total margin is $2/\|\mathbf{w}\|$.

Maximising $2/\|\mathbf{w}\|$ is equivalent to minimising $\|\mathbf{w}\|^2/2$ (the $1/2$ is for algebraic convenience). The hard-margin SVM primal is: $$\min_{\mathbf{w}, b} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b) \geq 1 \quad \forall i$$ This is a convex quadratic programme with $n + 1$ variables ($\mathbf{w}$ and $b$) and $N$ linear inequality constraints (one per training point). It is solvable in polynomial time.

Lagrangian dual
Lagrangian dual of the hard-margin SVM

Introduce Lagrange multipliers $\alpha_i \geq 0$ (one per constraint): $$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}, b, \boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 - \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i \big[y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b) - 1\big]$$

Stationarity conditions (set derivatives to zero): $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}} = 0 \implies \mathbf{w} = \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i y_i \mathbf{x}_i$$ $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial b} = 0 \implies \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i y_i = 0$$

Substituting $\mathbf{w} = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i \mathbf{x}_i$ back into $\mathcal{L}$ and simplifying: $$\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j$$ $$\sum_i \alpha_i y_i (\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b) = \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j + b\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j$$ (using $\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$).

The dual objective becomes: $$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^N\sum_{j=1}^N \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j (\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j)$$ $$\text{subject to} \quad \alpha_i \geq 0, \quad \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i y_i = 0$$

Key observation: the dual depends on the data only through inner products $\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j$ — not the data points themselves. This is the gateway to the kernel trick.

Once $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^*$ is found: $$\mathbf{w}^* = \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i^* y_i \mathbf{x}_i$$ $$\text{Prediction: } \hat{y} = \text{sign}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i^* y_i (\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}) + b^*\right)$$ Again, prediction uses only inner products $\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}$.

KKT conditions & support vectors
KKT conditions and the support vector structure

For the optimum $(\mathbf{w}^*, b^*, \boldsymbol{\alpha}^*)$, the KKT conditions require:

  1. Primal feasibility: $y_i(\mathbf{w}^* \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b^*) \geq 1$
  2. Dual feasibility: $\alpha_i^* \geq 0$
  3. Complementary slackness: $\alpha_i^* \big[y_i(\mathbf{w}^* \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b^*) - 1\big] = 0$

Complementary slackness says: either $\alpha_i^* = 0$ or the constraint is active ($y_i(\mathbf{w}^* \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b^*) = 1$).

Support vectors: the points with $\alpha_i^* > 0$ are exactly those that lie on the margin boundary ($y_i(\mathbf{w}^* \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b^*) = 1$). All other points have $\alpha_i^* = 0$ and contribute nothing to $\mathbf{w}^*$ or the prediction. The entire model is determined by the small subset of support vectors.

Recover $b^*$ from any support vector $i$ (with $\alpha_i^* > 0$): $$b^* = y_i - \mathbf{w}^* \cdot \mathbf{x}_i = y_i - \sum_{j} \alpha_j^* y_j (\mathbf{x}_j \cdot \mathbf{x}_i)$$ In practice, average over all support vectors for numerical stability.

The support vector structure has a profound consequence: if you add or remove non-support-vector training points (those far from the boundary with $\alpha_i = 0$), the decision boundary does not change at all. The SVM is sparse in the training data — only the points closest to the boundary matter.
The kernel trick
The dual formulation depends on the data only through inner products. If we map data to a higher-dimensional feature space via $\phi: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathcal{F}$, all inner products $\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j$ become $\phi(\mathbf{x}_i) \cdot \phi(\mathbf{x}_j)$. The kernel trick: if we can compute $K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \phi(\mathbf{x}_i) \cdot \phi(\mathbf{x}_j)$ directly without computing $\phi$, we can work in feature space implicitly at the same cost as working in input space.
Mercer's theorem — when does a kernel correspond to an inner product?

A function $K: \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a valid Mercer kernel (i.e., corresponds to an inner product in some feature space $\mathcal{F}$) if and only if it is symmetric positive semi-definite: for any finite set of points $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_N$, the Gram matrix $K_{ij} = K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$ is positive semi-definite: $$\mathbf{c}^\top K \mathbf{c} \geq 0 \quad \forall \mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^N$$

When this holds, there exists a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ and a map $\phi: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{H}$ such that $K(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}) = \langle \phi(\mathbf{x}), \phi(\mathbf{z}) \rangle_\mathcal{H}$. The space $\mathcal{H}$ may be infinite-dimensional — but we never need to compute in it explicitly.

With a kernel, the dual SVM becomes: $$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \sum_i \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$$ and prediction: $$\hat{y} = \text{sign}\!\left(\sum_i \alpha_i^* y_i K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}) + b^*\right)$$ The only change from the linear SVM is replacing $\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j$ with $K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$. We never explicitly compute $\phi$ — just the kernel function.
Common kernels
Kernel Formula Feature space Hyperparameters
Linear $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) = \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{z}$ $\mathbb{R}^n$ (no expansion) None
Polynomial $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) = (\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{z} + c)^d$ All monomials up to degree $d$ $d$ (degree), $c \geq 0$
RBF / Gaussian $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{z}\|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$ $\infty$-dimensional (Gaussian RKHS) $\sigma$ (width)
Sigmoid $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) = \tanh(\kappa\,\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{z} + \theta)$ Not always a valid Mercer kernel $\kappa, \theta$
RBF kernel. The RBF kernel is the most widely used. It is equivalent to an inner product in an infinite-dimensional space (expanded as a Taylor series of $\exp(-\|\mathbf{x}\|^2/2\sigma^2)\exp(\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{z}/\sigma^2)$). With small $\sigma$, the kernel drops off steeply — only very similar points have high kernel value, giving very local decision boundaries. With large $\sigma$, the kernel is nearly constant — approaching a linear SVM. The $\sigma$ (or equivalently $\gamma = 1/2\sigma^2$) is chosen by cross-validation.
Soft-margin SVM
The hard-margin SVM fails when data is not linearly separable (in the current feature space). The soft-margin SVM (C-SVM, Cortes & Vapnik 1995) introduces slack variables $\xi_i \geq 0$ allowing some points to violate the margin:
Soft-margin SVM primal and dual

Primal (soft margin): $$\min_{\mathbf{w}, b, \boldsymbol{\xi}} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_{i=1}^N \xi_i$$ $$\text{subject to} \quad y_i(\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}_i + b) \geq 1 - \xi_i, \quad \xi_i \geq 0 \quad \forall i$$

$\xi_i = 0$: point is correctly classified with margin $\geq 1$. $0 < \xi_i \leq 1$: point is inside the margin but correctly classified. $\xi_i > 1$: point is misclassified. $C > 0$ is a regularisation parameter: large $C$ = small slack allowed = hard margin (low bias, high variance); small $C$ = large slack allowed = soft margin (high bias, low variance).

Dual (soft margin): $$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \sum_i \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$$ $$\text{subject to} \quad 0 \leq \alpha_i \leq C, \quad \sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$$

The only change from the hard-margin dual: $\alpha_i \leq C$ (bounded above by $C$, not just $\geq 0$). Support vectors: $\alpha_i^* > 0$. Points on the margin: $0 < \alpha_i^* < C$. Points inside or beyond the margin: $\alpha_i^* = C$.

The hinge loss equivalence: the soft-margin primal objective can be rewritten as $$\frac{1}{C}\sum_i \max(0, 1 - y_i(\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}_i + b)) + \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$$ showing that soft-margin SVM minimises hinge loss + L2 regularisation.

Support vector regression
SVMs extend to regression via the $\varepsilon$-insensitive loss: $$L_\varepsilon(y, \hat{y}) = \max(0, |y - \hat{y}| - \varepsilon)$$ Errors within a tube of radius $\varepsilon$ around the prediction incur zero loss; errors outside the tube are penalised linearly. The SVR primal: $$\min_{\mathbf{w},b,\boldsymbol{\xi},\hat{\boldsymbol{\xi}}} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_i(\xi_i + \hat{\xi}_i)$$ subject to $y_i - \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}_i - b \leq \varepsilon + \xi_i$ (upper tube wall), $\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}_i + b - y_i \leq \varepsilon + \hat{\xi}_i$ (lower tube wall), $\xi_i, \hat{\xi}_i \geq 0$. The dual has the same structure as classification SVM with $K$ replacing inner products. The kernel trick applies identically.
Worked examples
Example 1 — Hard-margin SVM by hand (2D)

Two positive points: $\mathbf{x}_1 = [1, 1]^\top$, $\mathbf{x}_2 = [2, 2]^\top$ ($y = +1$). Two negative points: $\mathbf{x}_3 = [-1, -1]^\top$, $\mathbf{x}_4 = [-2, -2]^\top$ ($y = -1$). Find the maximum-margin separating hyperplane.

By symmetry, the optimal hyperplane passes through the origin ($b = 0$) and is perpendicular to the data's direction. The nearest positive point to any hyperplane through the origin is $\mathbf{x}_1 = [1,1]^\top$ (at distance $\sqrt{2}$ from origin); the nearest negative point is $\mathbf{x}_3 = [-1,-1]^\top$.

For the canonical scaling, $y_i(\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}_i + b) = 1$ at the support vectors. By symmetry, $\mathbf{w} = [w, w]^\top$ and $b = 0$. $\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}_1 = w + w = 2w = 1 \implies w = 1/2$.

So $\mathbf{w}^* = [1/2, 1/2]^\top$, $b^* = 0$. Margin: $2/\|\mathbf{w}^*\| = 2/\sqrt{1/4 + 1/4} = 2/(\sqrt{2}/2) = 2\sqrt{2}$. Decision: $\hat{y} = \text{sign}(x_1/2 + x_2/2) = \text{sign}(x_1 + x_2)$.

Support vectors: $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_3$ (nearest to boundary). $\mathbf{x}_2$ and $\mathbf{x}_4$ have $\alpha_i^* = 0$ and could be removed without changing the boundary.

Example 2 — Kernel SVM: XOR is linearly separable in feature space

The XOR problem: $(+1,+1) \to -1$; $(+1,-1) \to +1$; $(-1,+1) \to +1$; $(-1,-1) \to -1$. This is not linearly separable in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Show that with the polynomial kernel $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) = (\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{z})^2$, the data becomes linearly separable.

The polynomial kernel $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) = (\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{z})^2$ corresponds to the feature map $\phi([x_1,x_2]) = [x_1^2, x_2^2, \sqrt{2}x_1 x_2]^\top$.

Compute $\phi$ for each point:

  • $(+1,+1)$: $\phi = [1, 1, \sqrt{2}]^\top$, label $-1$
  • $(+1,-1)$: $\phi = [1, 1, -\sqrt{2}]^\top$, label $+1$
  • $(-1,+1)$: $\phi = [1, 1, -\sqrt{2}]^\top$, label $+1$
  • $(-1,-1)$: $\phi = [1, 1, \sqrt{2}]^\top$, label $-1$

In feature space, the decision is now determined purely by the third coordinate $\sqrt{2}x_1 x_2$: positive points have $x_1 x_2 < 0$ (i.e., $\sqrt{2}x_1x_2 < 0$); negative points have $x_1 x_2 > 0$. The hyperplane $w_3 \cdot \phi_3 = 0$ (i.e., $x_1 x_2 = 0$) separates them perfectly. This corresponds to the nonlinear boundary $x_1 x_2 = 0$ in the original space — the XOR boundary, which is two diagonals. The kernel "lifted" the data to make it linearly separable.

Example 3 — Effect of C on the soft-margin boundary

You have a 1D dataset: positive points at $x = \{1, 2\}$, negative points at $x = \{-1, -2, 100\}$ (note: the point at $x = 100$ is a mislabelled outlier). Qualitatively describe how the decision boundary changes as $C \to \infty$ vs. $C \to 0$.

$C \to \infty$ (hard margin limit): The SVM tries to correctly classify all points including the outlier at $x = 100$. But no linear boundary can correctly classify $\{1, 2\}$ as positive and $\{-1, -2, 100\}$ as negative (since 100 > 2). The hard-margin SVM is infeasible — no separating hyperplane exists. If we relax slightly, a very large $C$ will push the boundary far right, near $x = 50$ say, and misclassify positive points 1 and 2. The boundary is dominated by the outlier.

$C \to 0$ (soft margin limit): Constraint violations are nearly free — the SVM regularises heavily and pays almost no attention to any individual point. The boundary approaches $x = 0$ (the symmetric midpoint between the bulk of positive and negative points), ignoring the outlier at 100 entirely. Essentially the SVM becomes a margin maximiser over the entire training set treated as infinitely flexible.

Moderate $C$: The boundary is at approximately $x = 0$ (between the two clusters), the outlier is allowed to be a slack violation (with $\xi_{100} = $ large), and the margin is determined by $x = 1$ and $x = -1$. The soft-margin SVM correctly handles the outlier by allowing one violation rather than distorting the entire boundary.

Food for thought
  • The dual SVM objective depends on the data only through inner products $K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$. Suppose you train an SVM and then want to make predictions on a new point $\mathbf{x}$. Does $\mathbf{x}$ need to be in $\mathbb{R}^n$? What does the kernel really require?
    The prediction is $\hat{y} = \text{sign}(\sum_i \alpha_i^* y_i K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}) + b^*)$. This requires computing $K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x})$ for each support vector $\mathbf{x}_i$. The kernel function only requires that $\mathbf{x}$ lies in the same domain $\mathcal{X}$ as the training data — it does not require an explicit vector representation, as long as $K(\cdot, \cdot)$ is defined. This is what makes kernels so general: $\mathcal{X}$ can be strings, graphs, trees, or any structured object, as long as we define a valid Mercer kernel on it. For example, the string kernel counts common substrings between two sequences — you never need a vector representation. The SVM (and any kernel method) works natively with any type of data for which a kernel is defined. This is the "kernel trick" in its most general form.
  • The SVM dual involves $O(N^2)$ terms (all pairwise kernel evaluations) and solving a QP with $N$ variables. For $N = 10^6$ data points, this is clearly impractical. How does the SMO algorithm (Sequential Minimal Optimisation) work around this, and what does it exploit?
    The naive QP solver for the SVM dual requires storing the full $N \times N$ kernel matrix (quadratic in $N$ memory) and takes $O(N^3)$ time. For $N = 10^6$, this is completely intractable. SMO (Platt 1998) exploits the structure of the dual: at the optimum, each pair $(\alpha_i, \alpha_j)$ satisfies a two-variable QP with a simple analytic solution (because the constraint $\sum \alpha_i y_i = 0$ means that when you fix all but two $\alpha$s, the remaining two satisfy a 1D constrained problem). SMO iteratively selects the two "most violating" variables (largest KKT violation), solves their two-variable sub-problem analytically, and repeats until convergence. This means: (1) no large matrix to store — only the kernel values for the selected pair need to be evaluated, (2) each step is $O(N)$ (computing kernel values for one pair against all $N$ points), (3) the algorithm converges in practice in $O(N)$ to $O(N^2)$ steps total. For very large $N$, even SMO is slow — modern practice often uses stochastic gradient descent on the primal (hinge loss + L2 regularisation) via LibLinear, or uses approximate kernel methods (Nyström approximation) rather than exact kernels.
  • The soft-margin SVM objective with a linear kernel is equivalent to minimising hinge loss plus L2 regularisation. Logistic regression minimises cross-entropy plus L2 regularisation. Both are binary classifiers with L2 regularisation — what is the fundamental difference between them, and why do they sometimes give different results?
    The loss functions differ fundamentally. Hinge loss $\max(0, 1 - y\hat{f})$ is zero for points correctly classified with margin $\geq 1$ — it ignores "easy" points completely. Cross-entropy loss $\log(1 + e^{-y\hat{f}})$ is always positive but exponentially small for well-classified points — it never completely ignores any point. Consequence 1 — sparsity: SVM solutions are sparse in training data (only support vectors matter); logistic regression uses all training points. Consequence 2 — calibration: logistic regression outputs calibrated probabilities ($\sigma(\hat{f})$ estimates $p(y=1|\mathbf{x})$). The SVM does not — its score $\hat{f}$ is not a calibrated probability (Platt scaling is needed to calibrate SVM outputs). Consequence 3 — tail behaviour: for points far from the boundary (large margin), hinge loss is exactly 0; logistic loss is $\approx e^{-y\hat{f}} \to 0$ exponentially. Both ignore such points, but hinge does so exactly. This makes SVM sensitive to outliers in the support vector zone (those with $\alpha_i = C$) but robust to outliers far from the boundary. In practice on balanced datasets with good features, the two give similar results. Logistic regression is preferred when probabilities are needed; SVMs (with kernels) are preferred when the feature space needs to be nonlinearly expanded.
Common pitfalls
  • Not normalising features. The SVM margin is a geometric distance — it depends on the scale of features. A feature with values in $[0, 1000]$ will dominate a feature in $[0, 1]$. Always standardise features (zero mean, unit variance) before training an SVM.
  • Using the wrong kernel for the problem. The RBF kernel works well in general but is not always optimal. For text (bag-of-words features), the linear kernel often outperforms RBF because the feature space is already high-dimensional and sparse. For image patches, RBF or polynomial may be better. Always cross-validate the kernel choice and its hyperparameters.
  • Confusing $C$ and $\gamma$ in RBF-SVM. Scikit-learn's SVC has two key hyperparameters: $C$ (soft-margin tradeoff) and $\gamma = 1/2\sigma^2$ (RBF width). Large $\gamma$ = narrow kernel = very local decisions = low bias, high variance. Small $\gamma$ = wide kernel = smooth decisions = high bias, low variance. These interact: large $C$ + large $\gamma$ is always at risk of overfitting; small $C$ + small $\gamma$ always underfits. Grid search both jointly.
  • Using SVMs for large datasets without approximations. Kernel SVM training is $O(N^2)$ to $O(N^3)$ in the number of training points. For $N > 10^5$, exact kernel SVMs are too slow. Use LinearSVC (primal SGD on hinge loss, $O(N)$ per epoch) for linear problems, or Nyström kernel approximation (randomly approximate the kernel with a low-rank matrix) for nonlinear problems.
Connects to
Generalisation & Regularisation (SL4) — the soft-margin SVM is a regularised classifier: $C$ controls the L2 regularisation strength. The margin-based generalisation bound (VC theory) is an alternative to the bias-variance framework for bounding generalisation error.
Probabilistic Perspectives (SL11) — the SVM does not naturally produce probabilities. Platt scaling fits a sigmoid $\sigma(As_i + B)$ to the SVM scores $s_i$ on a held-out validation set to obtain calibrated probabilities. This is a post-hoc calibration step, not inherent to the SVM.
Ensemble Methods (SL7) — kernel SVMs and ensembles (Random Forests, Gradient Boosting) are the two dominant classical ML approaches. Ensembles generally require less hyperparameter tuning and scale better; SVMs are theoretically cleaner and work well in high-dimensional sparse spaces (text).
