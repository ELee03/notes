4 · Generalisation & Regularization
Bias-Variance Decomposition · Cross-Validation · Learning Curves · Model Selection · Regularization
Fitting the training data is easy — given enough parameters, any algorithm can do it perfectly. The hard problem is fitting the underlying pattern without memorizing the noise. This lesson makes that tension precise: the bias-variance decomposition quantifies exactly what goes wrong when a model is too simple or too complex, and cross-validation gives a practical procedure for finding the sweet spot.
The bias-variance decomposition
Suppose the true relationship is $y = f(\mathbf{x}) + \varepsilon$, where $f : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the true function and $\varepsilon$ is zero-mean noise with variance $\sigma^2$ (irreducible error we cannot remove regardless of our model). We train a model $\hat{h}$ on a training set $\mathcal{D}$ drawn randomly from $P$.
Because $\mathcal{D}$ is random, so is $\hat{h}$ — a different training set gives a different fitted model. The expected test error at a fixed test point $\mathbf{x}$ averages over the randomness in $\mathcal{D}$: $$\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}\bigl[(y - \hat{h}(\mathbf{x}))^2\bigr]$$ This decomposes into three terms.
Derivation — Bias-variance decomposition

Let $\bar{h}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}[\hat{h}(\mathbf{x})]$ be the expected prediction of our model class (averaged over all possible training sets). Write: $$y - \hat{h}(\mathbf{x}) = \underbrace{(y - f(\mathbf{x}))}_{\text{noise}} + \underbrace{(f(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{h}(\mathbf{x}))}_{\text{bias}} + \underbrace{(\bar{h}(\mathbf{x}) - \hat{h}(\mathbf{x}))}_{\text{fluctuation}}$$

Square and take expectation. The cross terms vanish:

  • Noise × bias: $(y - f)$ is independent of $\mathcal{D}$, and $E[y - f] = 0$.
  • Noise × fluctuation: $E_\mathcal{D}[\bar{h} - \hat{h}] = 0$ by definition of $\bar{h}$.
  • Bias × fluctuation: bias is constant in $\mathcal{D}$; $E_\mathcal{D}[\bar{h} - \hat{h}] = 0$.

What remains: $$\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}\bigl[(y-\hat{h})^2\bigr] = \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible noise}} + \underbrace{(f(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{h}(\mathbf{x}))^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}\bigl[(\bar{h}(\mathbf{x}) - \hat{h}(\mathbf{x}))^2\bigr]}_{\text{Variance}}$$

The three terms:
  • Irreducible noise $\sigma^2$. The variance of the noise in $y$ itself. No model can do better than this — even a perfect $\hat{h} = f$ still incurs error $\sigma^2$ due to noise in the labels.
  • Bias$^2$. $(f(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{h}(\mathbf{x}))^2$ — the squared difference between the true function and the average prediction of our model class. This measures systematic error: how far off is our model family in principle, even with infinite data? A linear model fitting a quadratic function has irreducible bias. More complex models (neural networks, high-degree polynomials) can reduce bias toward zero.
  • Variance. $\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}[(\bar{h} - \hat{h})^2]$ — how much the model's predictions fluctuate across different training sets. High variance means the model is sensitive to the particular data it was trained on. A model that memorizes training data perfectly (zero training error) is extremely sensitive to which samples appear — its predictions vary wildly with training set. Simpler models have lower variance.
The total expected test error = Bias² + Variance + $\sigma^2$. The irreducible noise is fixed. Reducing bias typically increases variance (more complex model), and vice versa. This is the bias-variance tradeoff.
Bias-Variance Tradeoff — Training vs. Test Error
The tradeoff in practice
Consider fitting a polynomial of degree $D$ to noisy data from a smooth function:
  • $D = 1$ (linear): If the true function is quadratic, no linear model can capture it. Bias is large. But the linear fit is stable across training sets (low variance). High bias, low variance.
  • $D = n-1$ (interpolating): Passes through every training point — zero bias on training data. But it oscillates wildly and a different training set gives a completely different polynomial. Low bias (on training data), catastrophically high variance.
  • $D^*$ (optimal): Some intermediate degree minimizes the sum Bias² + Variance. This is the sweet spot.
The optimal complexity increases with the amount of data $n$: with more data, we can afford a more complex model (variance decreases because more data pins down the fit). With very little data, a simple model is safer even if it has higher bias.
Double descent: the modern view
Classical bias-variance theory predicts a single U-shaped test error curve as model complexity increases: error decreases (bias falls), reaches a minimum, then increases (variance rises). This was considered settled theory.
Modern deep learning has revealed something unexpected: for highly overparameterized models — where the number of parameters far exceeds the number of training examples — the test error decreases again after the classical "overfitting regime." This phenomenon is called double descent.
The picture is: as complexity increases, test error first decreases (bias falls), then spikes sharply near the interpolation threshold (where parameters ≈ training examples, and the model just barely interpolates), then decreases again as the model becomes massively overparameterized. Large neural networks sit far to the right — billions of parameters, far more than training examples — and still generalize well.
Why? Gradient descent with weight decay (L2 regularization) implicitly selects the minimum-norm interpolating solution from the infinitely many that fit the training data exactly. This minimum-norm solution turns out to have low effective complexity even in the overparameterized regime. The classical bias-variance tradeoff is not wrong — it applies within the under-parameterized regime. Double descent extends the picture to the over-parameterized regime that modern deep learning inhabits.
Training, validation, and test sets
To evaluate generalization reliably, data must be split into three disjoint sets:
  • Training set. Used to fit the model parameters ($\mathbf{w}$) by minimizing empirical risk. The model sees this data during learning.
  • Validation set. Used to select hyperparameters (regularization strength $\lambda$, polynomial degree $D$, neural network architecture). The model does not directly train on this data, but hyperparameter selection based on validation performance causes indirect information leakage — the model was chosen to do well here.
  • Test set. Used exactly once at the end to report final generalization performance. Must never be used during model development. Performance on the test set is the honest estimate of true risk.
The test set contamination failure mode. If you adjust your model based on test set performance — even once — you have turned the test set into a validation set. Its performance estimate is now optimistically biased. The only remedy is a fresh held-out test set. In practice, ML competitions often report inflated performance because participants iterate against the (nominally fixed) test set via repeated submissions.
A common split: 70% training, 15% validation, 15% test. For small datasets, a larger validation/test fraction is needed; for very large datasets ($n > 10^6$), even 1–2% for validation/test provides millions of examples.
Cross-validation
With small datasets, reserving 30% for validation/test wastes too much training data. $k$-fold cross-validation provides a more data-efficient estimate of generalization error.
Procedure: randomly partition the training data into $k$ equal folds. For each fold $i = 1, \ldots, k$:
  1. Train on all folds except fold $i$.
  2. Evaluate on fold $i$ — the held-out validation error for this round.
Average the validation errors across all $k$ folds. This is the cross-validated estimate of generalization error. Then retrain on all the training data with the selected hyperparameters, and evaluate once on the held-out test set.
Common choices: $k = 5$ or $k = 10$. A special case, leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV), uses $k = n$ — each example is held out once. LOOCV gives an almost unbiased estimate of test error but is computationally expensive ($n$ model fits). For linear regression, LOOCV has a closed form (the PRESS statistic) that avoids refitting.
Stratified $k$-fold preserves class proportions within each fold — important for imbalanced classification where random splits might accidentally put all examples of a rare class into one fold.
Learning curves: diagnosing bias vs. variance
A learning curve plots training error and validation error as a function of training set size $n$. It is a powerful diagnostic for distinguishing the two failure modes.
High-bias model (underfitting):
  • Training error starts high and stays high — the model cannot fit even the training data well.
  • Validation error is close to training error — both plateau at a level above acceptable.
  • Adding more training data does not help significantly — the bottleneck is model capacity, not data quantity. Fix: increase model complexity.
High-variance model (overfitting):
  • Training error is low.
  • Validation error is much higher than training error — large gap.
  • As $n$ increases, the gap narrows because more data constrains the model. Fix: get more data, add regularization, reduce model complexity.
Reading a learning curve before investing in more data collection is standard practice: if the curves show high bias, more data is wasted; the model must be made more expressive first.
Regularization revisited
Regularization was introduced for linear regression in Lesson 2. Here we see it from the bias-variance perspective: adding a penalty on model complexity directly increases bias (the model can no longer fit arbitrary functions) while decreasing variance (the model is more stable across training sets). The goal is to find the penalty strength $\lambda$ where the reduction in variance outweighs the increase in bias, reducing total test error.
The pattern generalizes far beyond linear regression:
  • Weight decay — L2 regularization applied to neural networks. Every weight $w$ in the network gets a penalty $\frac{\lambda}{2} w^2$ added to the loss, shrinking weights toward zero. Standard practice in training deep networks.
  • Dropout (Lesson 10) — randomly zero out neurons during training, forcing the network not to rely on any single feature. Acts as implicit regularization by reducing co-adaptation.
  • Early stopping — stop gradient descent before it fully minimizes the training loss. Training longer increases variance (the model overfits more). Monitoring validation error and stopping when it starts rising is a form of regularization with the effective number of training steps as the regularization parameter.
  • Data augmentation — artificially expand the training set by applying label-preserving transformations (rotate, flip, crop images; add noise; paraphrase text). Reduces variance by making the model see more diverse examples.
All regularization techniques trade some bias for a larger reduction in variance. The right balance depends on the ratio of model capacity to dataset size — a relationship that must be diagnosed empirically, usually via validation error.
Worked examples
Example 1 — Computing bias and variance

The true function is $f(x) = x^2$. We fit a linear model $h(x; w_0, w_1) = w_0 + w_1 x$ using least squares. Training sets of size $n = 2$ are drawn i.i.d. with $x \sim \text{Uniform}[-1, 1]$ and no noise ($\sigma^2 = 0$).

What is the bias of this model class at $x = 0.5$?

The bias measures how far the average prediction (over all random training sets) is from the truth.

For any two-point training set $\{(x_1, x_1^2), (x_2, x_2^2)\}$, the least-squares linear fit passes through both points. The average prediction at a new point $x$ is the expected value of the linear interpolant over random pairs $(x_1, x_2)$. By symmetry of $\text{Uniform}[-1,1]$, the expected linear fit is $\bar{h}(x) = \mathbb{E}[w_0] + \mathbb{E}[w_1]x$.

By symmetry: $\mathbb{E}[w_1] = \mathbb{E}[(x_1^2 - x_2^2)/(x_1 - x_2)] = \mathbb{E}[x_1 + x_2] = 0$. And $\mathbb{E}[w_0] = \mathbb{E}[x_1^2] - \mathbb{E}[w_1 x_1] = E[x^2] = 1/3$.

So $\bar{h}(x) = 1/3$ for all $x$ — a horizontal line at height $1/3$.

At $x = 0.5$: $f(0.5) = 0.25$. Bias $= \bar{h}(0.5) - f(0.5) = 1/3 - 1/4 = 1/12 \approx 0.083$. Bias² $= (1/12)^2 \approx 0.0069$.

The linear model cannot capture the curvature of $x^2$; it can only match the average height. No amount of training data removes this bias — it is a fundamental limitation of the hypothesis class.

Example 2 — Choosing $\lambda$ via cross-validation

You have a regression dataset with $n = 100$ examples and $d = 50$ features. You want to use Ridge regression and must choose $\lambda$ from the set $\{0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10\}$. Describe the 5-fold cross-validation procedure and interpret the results if the best $\lambda = 1$.

Procedure:

  1. Split the 100 examples into 5 folds of 20 each.
  2. For each candidate $\lambda$ and each fold $i$:
    • Train Ridge regression on the 80 examples not in fold $i$.
    • Compute validation MSE on fold $i$'s 20 examples.
  3. For each $\lambda$, average the 5 validation MSEs.
  4. Select the $\lambda$ with the lowest average validation MSE.
  5. Retrain on all 100 examples with the chosen $\lambda$.
  6. Report final performance on the held-out test set (separate from these 100).

Interpreting $\lambda^* = 1$: $\lambda = 0.001$ likely overfits — with 50 features and 80 training examples per fold, unregularized regression is nearly underdetermined. $\lambda = 10$ likely underfits — penalizing weights too heavily forces the model toward zero everywhere. $\lambda = 1$ achieves the best bias-variance balance: enough regularization to prevent overfitting in the 50-feature space, not so much that useful signal is washed out.

Example 3 — Reading a learning curve

You observe the following as training set size $n$ grows from 100 to 10,000:

  • Training MSE: starts low (~0.1), stays low (~0.1).
  • Validation MSE: starts high (~2.0), decreases to ~1.5 at $n = 10{,}000$.

What does this curve tell you, and what should you do?

The large gap between training (~0.1) and validation (~1.5) at large $n$ is the signature of high variance. The model fits the training data very well but does not generalize — it is memorizing training details. The fact that the gap is still large at $n = 10{,}000$ suggests the model may be significantly overparameterized relative to the problem's complexity.

The validation error is still decreasing slowly with $n$, suggesting more data would help. But given the persistence of the gap, the more impactful interventions are:

  1. Add regularization (increase $\lambda$ for Ridge/Lasso, add dropout for neural networks) — directly reduces variance.
  2. Reduce model complexity — fewer parameters means less capacity to memorize noise.
  3. Data augmentation — if the domain permits it, augmenting training data diversifies the examples and reduces effective variance.

If instead training MSE were 1.0 and validation MSE 1.1 (small gap, both high), that would be high bias — the fix would be to increase model complexity or add features, not add regularization.

Food for thought
  • The bias-variance decomposition is stated for squared loss. Does the same decomposition hold for classification with 0-1 loss? Why or why not?
    Not cleanly. The decomposition works neatly for squared loss because variance is additive in that setting. For 0-1 loss, an analogous decomposition exists (Domingos, 2000) but takes a different form — variance can actually decrease error in some cases when it tips a wrong average prediction to the correct class. The 0-1 loss decomposition does not separate as cleanly into independent additive terms. In practice, machine learning practitioners apply the bias-variance intuition to classification anyway (high bias → underfitting, high variance → overfitting) even though the formal squared-error derivation does not carry over exactly.
  • We select hyperparameters using the validation set, then report test set performance. If you had to choose only one hyperparameter selection method when your dataset has only 200 examples, would you choose a fixed validation split (say 160 train / 40 validation) or 5-fold cross-validation? What are the tradeoffs?
    Cross-validation. With only 200 examples, a fixed 160/40 split means the validation estimate of generalization error is based on just 40 examples — noisy and dependent on exactly which 40 are held out. Cross-validation trains on 160 examples in each fold and averages over 5 validation estimates, giving a much lower-variance estimate of the generalization error. The cost is 5× the computation. The tradeoff: fixed split is computationally cheaper and simpler; cross-validation gives a more reliable hyperparameter selection. With 200 examples, computational cost is trivial, so cross-validation wins clearly. With 10 million examples, the 40-vs-CV tradeoff flips — a random subset of 1 million gives a very low-variance estimate, and the 5× computation cost is substantial.
  • Modern large language models have billions of parameters trained on trillions of tokens — far into the overparameterized regime. Classical theory says this should catastrophically overfit. It doesn't. What aspects of the training process might explain this empirically?
    Several factors: (1) Implicit regularization of gradient descent: SGD with weight decay selects solutions with small norm, which have low effective complexity despite many parameters. (2) The interpolation threshold is far below the training set size: with trillions of training tokens, even a billion-parameter model is underparameterized in the classical sense — the interpolation threshold (parameters ≈ training examples) is far to the right. (3) Data diversity: the training distribution covers so many modes that there is little room for memorization-style overfitting. (4) Early stopping: training stops long before parameters have converged to the minimum of training loss. The double descent phenomenon suggests that in the massively overparameterized regime, test error can be low anyway — the minimum-norm interpolant generalizes well when the true function is smooth relative to the function class.
Common pitfalls
  • Using the test set more than once. Every time you check test performance and use it to make a decision (tweak the model, try a different architecture, add features), you have used the test set as a validation set. Reserve the test set for a single final evaluation. Use a separate validation set or cross-validation for all development decisions.
  • Confusing high training accuracy with a well-fit model. Training accuracy measures fit to the data the model has seen. Near-perfect training accuracy with poor validation accuracy is the textbook overfitting signature. Always monitor both.
  • Applying cross-validation to time-series data with random splits. For time-ordered data (stock prices, sensor readings, user logs), randomly shuffling before splitting causes data leakage — future information appears in the training fold, giving an unrealistically optimistic estimate. Use time-series cross-validation (train on past, validate on future) instead.
  • Assuming regularization always helps. Regularization introduces bias. If the model is already underfitting (high bias), adding regularization makes it worse. Regularization is the cure for overfitting — not a universal improvement. Diagnose first with learning curves, then regularize if variance is the problem.
  • Selecting hyperparameters on the training set. If you tune $\lambda$ to minimize training error, you will always select $\lambda = 0$ (no regularization) — training error monotonically decreases as regularization weakens. Hyperparameters must be selected on held-out data.
Connects to
Probability Theory — the bias-variance decomposition is a statement about the distribution of $\hat{h}$ over random training sets. Deeper generalization theory (PAC learning, VC dimension, Rademacher complexity) gives high-probability bounds on the generalization gap, not just expected values. These bounds are sharp enough to be actionable for classical models; for neural networks, the classical bounds are vacuous but empirical practice fills the gap.
Bayesian Inference (Lesson 11) — the Bayesian perspective on generalization is complementary: instead of controlling complexity via explicit penalties, Bayesian methods place priors over models and average predictions over the posterior. Marginalizing over model parameters automatically applies Occam's razor — simpler models with fewer free parameters receive higher marginal likelihood. Ridge regression is MAP inference under a Gaussian prior.
Deep Learning (Lessons 8–10) — the same concepts recur throughout deep learning: early stopping as implicit regularization, dropout as stochastic regularization, batch normalization as stabilization. The double-descent phenomenon and the mystery of why massively overparameterized neural networks generalize are among the most active open questions in ML theory.
