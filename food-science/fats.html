Fats, Oils & Emulsions

Lipid Chemistry · Smoke Points · Emulsification · Mayonnaise · Butter

Fat does more in the kitchen than conduct heat. It carries most of the flavour molecules in food (they are fat-soluble, not water-soluble), it controls texture and mouthfeel, and it makes emulsification possible — the chemistry that turns oil and water into mayonnaise, hollandaise, vinaigrette, and cream sauce. Understanding fat means understanding why food tastes the way it does, and why sauces behave the way they behave.

What fats actually are

Most dietary fats are triglycerides (also called triacylglycerols): a glycerol molecule with three fatty acid chains attached, one to each of glycerol's three hydroxyl groups, via ester bonds (hence: triacyl-glycerol).

Glycerol (3 –OH groups) + 3 fatty acid chains → triglyceride + 3 water molecules (esterification)

The fatty acid chains are the interesting part. They are long hydrocarbon chains with a carboxyl group (–COOH) at one end. Key properties determined by chain structure:

  • Chain length: most dietary fatty acids are 12–22 carbons. Short-chain fatty acids (4–6 carbons) are volatile — they contribute to the aroma of butter, cheese, and rancid fat. Long-chain fats are odourless at room temperature.
  • Degree of saturation: whether the chain contains carbon–carbon double bonds. This is the most important property for cooking behaviour.
Saturation and physical state

A saturated fatty acid has no double bonds — every carbon is bonded to the maximum number of hydrogens. The chain is straight, flexible, and can pack tightly alongside neighbouring chains like stacked pencils. Dense packing = solid at room temperature. Examples: palmitic acid (16C), stearic acid (18C). Fats rich in saturated FA: butter, lard, coconut oil, palm oil — all solid or semi-solid at room temperature.

An unsaturated fatty acid has one or more double bonds. The double bond introduces a rigid kink in the chain — the two carbon segments on either side of the double bond cannot rotate freely. In natural (cis) unsaturated fats, this kink prevents tight chain packing. Loosely packed chains = liquid at room temperature (an oil). Examples: oleic acid (18C, 1 double bond — mono-unsaturated; dominant in olive oil), linoleic acid (18C, 2 double bonds — poly-unsaturated; dominant in sunflower oil), DHA (22C, 6 double bonds — fish oil).

Trans fats — the kinked-chain exception. Natural unsaturated fats have cis double bonds (hydrogens on the same side → kink). Industrial partial hydrogenation (used to solidify vegetable oils) produces some trans double bonds (hydrogens on opposite sides → nearly straight chain, like saturated). Trans fats pack almost as tightly as saturated fats but behave differently metabolically — they interfere with cell membrane function and raise LDL cholesterol. Most countries have now restricted or banned partially hydrogenated oils. Naturally occurring trans fats (from ruminant animals: conjugated linoleic acid in butter and beef) exist at low levels and appear to be metabolically neutral or beneficial — a reminder that the geometry of the molecule, not just whether it's "trans," determines biological activity.

Why does coconut oil solidify in the fridge but olive oil does not? Coconut oil is ~90% saturated fatty acids (mostly lauric acid, 12C, fully saturated). Its high melting point (~24°C) means it is solid below room temperature on a cool day. Olive oil is ~73% monounsaturated (oleic acid); its melting point is ~–6°C. The kinks from the double bonds prevent close packing even at refrigerator temperatures.

Multiple double bonds make fats more reactive: each double bond is a site vulnerable to oxidation. Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) oxidise faster than monounsaturated, which oxidise faster than saturated. This determines shelf life and which fats are appropriate for high-heat cooking.

Smoke points — what they mean and what they don't

The smoke point is the temperature at which a fat visibly smokes. It signals the beginning of decomposition: triglycerides hydrolyse under sustained heat into glycerol and free fatty acids, and glycerol further breaks down into acrolein (CH₂=CH–CHO) — a highly irritating compound responsible for the acrid smell of burning fat and potentially harmful in quantity.

What lowers smoke point:

  • Free fatty acid (FFA) content: fats with more FFA smoke sooner. Unrefined ("extra virgin") oils have more impurities and FFA than refined oils. This is why extra-virgin olive oil has a lower smoke point than refined olive oil, not because of its oleic acid content.
  • Repeated heating: each frying cycle generates more FFA. Used frying oil has a lower smoke point than fresh oil.
  • Milk solids: whole butter contains ~3% milk solids (proteins and lactose) that scorch well below the smoke point of the fat itself. Clarifying butter (removing solids) raises its smoke point from ~150°C to ~250°C.

The smoke point is not the only factor for high-heat cooking. An oil with a high smoke point but many polyunsaturated bonds (e.g., regular sunflower or canola at 230°C) can still oxidise and produce harmful compounds at prolonged frying temperatures before visibly smoking. For repeated high-heat use, high smoke point plus oxidative stability is the criterion: refined avocado oil, beef tallow, ghee, or refined coconut oil are good choices. Extra-virgin olive oil, though it has a modest smoke point, contains antioxidants (polyphenols) that partially protect it from oxidation — making it more stable under medium heat than its smoke point alone suggests.

Fat as flavour carrier

Most of the volatile flavour molecules in food are fat-soluble (lipophilic), not water-soluble. This is why: cooking in fat extracts and concentrates flavour compounds from ingredients; fat-based sauces carry flavour to your palate more effectively than water-based ones; and removing fat from food (low-fat products) almost always damages perceived flavour intensity and complexity.

Specific ways fat modulates flavour:

  • Lipophilic volatile extraction: terpenes (herb and citrus aromatics), most Maillard volatiles, and many Strecker aldehydes partition strongly into fat. Infusing oil with herbs, garlic, or spices concentrates these compounds in the fat phase. This is the mechanism behind flavoured oils, ghee infusions, and fat-based compound butters.
  • Retronasal delivery: fat coats the mouth and throat, slowly releasing fat-soluble volatiles during and after chewing — extending the duration of flavour perception. This is a large part of what "mouthfeel" means.
  • Taste modulation: fat slows the release of water-soluble taste compounds (sugars, acids, glutamate) from food matrix to saliva, affecting the time course of taste perception. Very fatty food has a "longer" flavour experience.
  • Tri- and di-acylglycerol perception: emerging evidence suggests fat is itself a basic taste — distinct from the mouthfeel contribution — possibly via CD36 receptors in the tongue. Often called "oleogustus" (fatty taste).

The butter vs. margarine flavour difference is almost entirely due to this principle. Butter's flavour comes from short-chain fatty acids (butyric acid: rancid-buttery; caprylic acid: goat-like; trace diacetyl from fermentation), all highly fat-soluble and delivered via the fat matrix. Margarine's flavour complexity is comparatively flat — the hydrogenated plant oils lack these compounds, and artificial flavouring additives cannot fully replicate the matrix delivery mechanism.

Rancidity — fat going wrong

Rancidity is the deterioration of fat through oxidation or hydrolysis, producing compounds that smell and taste unpleasant. Two distinct mechanisms:

Oxidative rancidity (the main culprit in most oils)

Oxygen attacks the carbon–carbon double bonds in unsaturated fatty acids, producing hydroperoxides. These are unstable and break down into a cascade of secondary products: aldehydes (hexanal, nonanal — grassy, cardboard, fishy notes), ketones (2-heptanone, 2-nonanone — blue cheese, musty notes), and short-chain carboxylic acids (butyric acid in rancid butter). This is why polyunsaturated fats (more double bond sites) go rancid faster than saturated fats.

Accelerants: light (photosensitises oxygen), heat, metal ions (copper, iron catalyse free radical chain reactions), oxygen exposure. Inhibitors: antioxidants (vitamin E / tocopherols in plant oils; rosemary extract in commercial products), darkness, cool temperature, minimal oxygen (sealed containers).

Hydrolytic rancidity

Water + lipase enzymes cleave the ester bonds of triglycerides, releasing free fatty acids. In butter and cheese, the short-chain fatty acids liberated (butyric, caprylic, capric acids) produce the characteristic sharp, rancid-butter smell. At low levels, this same reaction produces the complex flavour of aged cheese — enzyme-ripened cheeses (Parmesan, Gruyère) deliberately cultivate lipase activity during aging. The difference between "complex" and "rancid" is a matter of degree and context.

Emulsification — making oil and water coexist

Oil and water don't mix. This is not a mystery — it is thermodynamics. Water molecules form hydrogen bonds with each other (strong, directional). Oil molecules interact only via weak van der Waals forces. When oil droplets are dispersed in water, the water molecules at the oil–water interface cannot form hydrogen bonds with the oil surface — they are energetically "frustrated." The system minimises this interface area by coalescing oil droplets together, separating the phases. This is why a vinaigrette left standing separates: thermodynamics.

An emulsion is a kinetically stable mixture of two immiscible liquids — one dispersed as tiny droplets within the other:

  • Oil-in-water (O/W): tiny oil droplets dispersed in a continuous water phase. Examples: milk, cream, mayonnaise, vinaigrette, hollandaise, béarnaise.
  • Water-in-oil (W/O): tiny water droplets dispersed in a continuous fat phase. Examples: butter, margarine. Less common in sauces.

Kinetically stable means the emulsion does not spontaneously separate — but given enough time or energy (heat, vibration, freezing), it will. Thermodynamic stability (never separating) is essentially impossible for a true emulsion. Good emulsification means creating very small droplets (high surface area, slow coalescence) and stabilising the interface with emulsifiers.

Emulsifiers — amphiphiles at the interface

An emulsifier is an amphiphilic molecule: it has a hydrophilic ("water-loving") head and a hydrophobic ("water-fearing") tail. It positions itself at the oil–water interface — tail pointing into the oil droplet, head pointing into the water — reducing the interfacial tension and forming a physical barrier that prevents droplets from coalescing.

Key emulsifiers in food:

Lecithin (phosphatidylcholine) — the workhorse of egg yolk

A phospholipid: glycerol backbone with two fatty acid chains (hydrophobic tails) and a phosphate-choline head group (hydrophilic). Present at ~1% in egg yolk and ~0.3% in whole eggs. Egg yolk is extraordinarily effective at emulsification partly because of lecithin and partly because yolk's lipoprotein complexes (LDL, HDL, livetins) act as additional emulsifiers. One egg yolk can stably emulsify up to 250 ml of oil — far beyond what pure lecithin alone could achieve.

Proteins — flexible film formers

Proteins can adsorb to oil–water interfaces and form a viscoelastic film around droplets. When proteins partially unfold at the interface (exposing hydrophobic regions into the oil), they provide steric stabilisation. Key examples:

  • Casein micelles in milk — stabilise the fat globules in cream
  • Whey proteins — denatured by heat, form stronger interfacial films (contribution to cooked cream sauce stability)
  • Gelatin — in aspic and cold sauces; when cooled, forms a gel matrix that physically traps fat droplets
  • Mustard — contains mucilage (polysaccharides from mucilage cells in mustard seed) plus some protein; remarkably effective emulsifier for vinaigrettes

Mono- and diglycerides

Partial glycerides (glycerol with one or two fatty acids instead of three) are the most widely used commercial food emulsifiers (E471). Present naturally at trace levels in triglyceride fats; commercially produced by partial hydrolysis of oils. Used in margarine, ice cream (inhibits fat globule coalescence during freezing, preventing icy texture), bread (softening agent — interacts with amylose to prevent retrogradation).

Mayonnaise — an engineering marvel at room temperature

Mayonnaise is an oil-in-water emulsion with approximately 70–80% oil — an extraordinarily high oil fraction. At this ratio, the oil droplets are so densely packed that they are geometrically impossible to be perfectly spherical; they are deformed polyhedra pressed against each other. This close-packed structure is why mayonnaise has a semi-solid, paste-like consistency rather than flowing like a liquid despite being ~75% oil and ~25% water-plus-egg.

Making mayonnaise — mechanics and chemistry:

  1. Start with egg yolk (lecithin + lipoprotein emulsifiers) in an acidic water phase (lemon juice or vinegar; acid denatures proteins slightly, improving their interfacial activity, and lowers pH for flavour and microbial safety).
  2. Add oil drop by drop with vigorous agitation. Each addition creates new oil droplets that must be immediately coated with emulsifier from the continuous phase. If oil is added faster than emulsifier molecules can migrate to the new interface, droplets coalesce before being stabilised — the mayo breaks.
  3. As oil fraction increases, droplets pack together. Viscosity rises rapidly. The fully made mayo is a pale, opaque, viscous paste — the opacity comes from light scattering off millions of microscopic oil droplets.
Why does mayonnaise break — and can it be fixed?
Breaking = droplet coalescence → visible oil separation. Causes: (1) oil added too fast, (2) temperature too cold (lecithin becomes less mobile), (3) too little emulsifier relative to oil volume, (4) overworking a fully made mayo which can destroy the droplet structure. Rescue: begin again with a fresh yolk in a clean bowl. Add the broken mayo very slowly — the new yolk supplies fresh emulsifier to re-coat the existing droplets. Works because the "broken" mayo still contains emulsifier-coated oil; it just needs re-dispersing.

Cooked emulsions (hollandaise, béarnaise) follow the same principle but use heat-denatured egg proteins as the primary emulsifier alongside lecithin. This makes them more temperature-sensitive: proteins over-coagulate above ~70°C, collapsing the interfacial film. A hollandaise held too hot or reheated directly will break for the same reason a custard scrambles.

Butter — the phase-inverted emulsion

Butter starts as cream, which is an oil-in-water emulsion (fat globules dispersed in water). Churning converts it to a water-in-oil emulsion — a phase inversion. Here is how:

  1. Cream's fat globules are surrounded by a phospholipid membrane (the milk fat globule membrane, MFGM). Vigorous agitation during churning damages this membrane, exposing the liquid triglycerides inside.
  2. Damaged globules aggregate into clumps (the solid grains you see when cream is over-whipped). With continued agitation, these clumps coalesce into a continuous fat phase.
  3. At the inversion point, the water droplets are now trapped within a fat matrix — the butter "breaks" in the churning sense, releasing buttermilk (the remaining water phase) as a liquid. The solid material is butter.

Finished butter: ~80% fat, ~17% water (in tiny dispersed droplets), ~3% milk solids (proteins, lactose, minerals — the source of butter's flavour). The water droplets are so small that butter feels dry and firm, not wet.

Whipped cream is a gas-in-liquid foam, not an emulsion, despite involving cream. The fat globules (partially damaged by whipping) cluster around air bubbles, stabilising them. Over-whipping damages globules further and initiates the phase inversion: you get butter, not better whipped cream.

Worked examples
Example 1 — Choosing fat for high-heat searing
You want to sear duck breasts at high heat (~230°C pan temperature). Evaluate three fat choices: (A) extra-virgin olive oil, (B) refined avocado oil, (C) whole butter. Which would you use and why? Address smoke point, oxidative stability, and flavour contribution.

(A) Extra-virgin olive oil:
Smoke point ~190°C (unrefined). At 230°C, it will smoke before the pan is hot enough for effective searing. More importantly, EVOO's polyphenols and chlorophyll can generate acrid off-flavours at sustained high temperatures. Oleic acid (monounsaturated, 73%) provides reasonable oxidative stability for a brief sear, but the impurity content limits high-heat use. Not recommended for 230°C sustained searing.

(B) Refined avocado oil:
Smoke point ~270°C. Very high oleic acid content (~70%, similar to olive oil) provides excellent oxidative stability. Neutral flavour. Good thermal stability. This is the straightforward correct answer for pure high-heat searing with minimal fat flavour contribution to the final dish. Recommended if fat flavour is not desired.

(C) Whole butter:
Smoke point ~150°C — far too low. Milk solids scorch well before pan temperature reaches searing range. The duck skin will taste bitter and the pan will fill with smoke before a crust forms. However: a classic technique is to sear duck in its own rendered fat (duck fat, ~210°C smoke point; ~56% monounsaturated, oxidatively stable), then finish with basting butter at lower heat for flavour. This separates the high-heat phase from the flavour phase.

Best answer for a professional context: Start with refined avocado oil or duck's own rendered fat for the high-heat sear; add whole butter or compound butter only in the last 30–60 seconds for basting, at a lower temperature. Never use unrefined EVOO or whole butter as your primary high-heat medium.

Example 2 — Making a broken vinaigrette stable
A vinaigrette is made from 3 parts oil + 1 part vinegar, shaken. It separates within minutes. Identify why it separates and describe two chemical strategies to increase stability. For each, identify the emulsifier involved and explain its mechanism.

Why it separates:
The shaken vinaigrette creates droplets via mechanical energy, but there is no emulsifier to stabilise the oil–water interface. Surface tension drives droplets to coalesce. Droplets merge, the oil phase rises (lower density), and the phases separate. Without emulsifier, this separation happens in seconds to minutes.

Strategy 1 — Add mustard:
Whole-grain or Dijon mustard contains mucilage (a mixture of polysaccharides) from the mustard seed, plus some protein. Mucilage molecules are amphiphilic — they adsorb to the oil–water interface and form a physical steric barrier around oil droplets, preventing coalescence. Even ~1 teaspoon per tablespoon of vinegar provides enough emulsifier to keep a vinaigrette stable for several minutes to hours. This is why French vinaigrette almost always includes mustard.

Strategy 2 — Add egg yolk:
Egg yolk lecithin (phosphatidylcholine) is an efficient small-molecule emulsifier. Its phosphocholine head group is hydrophilic; its two fatty acid tails are hydrophobic. It inserts tail-first into oil droplets, head facing water, forming a monolayer that reduces interfacial tension. Combined with yolk's lipoprotein complexes, this makes egg-yolk vinaigrettes extremely stable (they approach mayonnaise in stability at yolk-rich ratios). The resulting emulsion is also opaque rather than clear — light scattering off small droplets — which is the visual sign of good emulsification.

Practical note: mustard + small amount of honey (adds some polysaccharide gum) + brisk whisking produces a vinaigrette stable enough for a restaurant service. Blending (high shear) creates smaller droplets and even longer stability.

Example 3 — Why does brown butter (beurre noisette) taste nutty?
Butter heated in a saucepan goes through stages: foaming, clearing, then develops a golden-brown colour and nutty aroma (beurre noisette). Identify the chemical events at each stage. Why does the flavour become noticeably more complex than plain melted butter?

Stage 1 — Foaming:
The ~17% water in butter vaporises. The milk proteins foam at the surface (protein films stabilise steam bubbles). The butter looks opaque and frothy.

Stage 2 — Clearing:
Water has mostly evaporated. Foam subsides. The butter is now golden-yellow, clear fat with milk solids settled at the bottom. Temperature can now rise above 100°C.

Stage 3 — Browning (beurre noisette):
Milk solids are the critical component. Butter contains ~1.5% milk proteins (including casein fragments and whey proteins) and ~0.5% lactose — both Maillard reactants. As temperature rises above ~140°C, the Maillard reaction begins between these proteins and lactose. The same cascade described in Lesson 1 proceeds: Strecker aldehydes (from milk amino acids, particularly leucine → 3-methylbutanal, the defining "brown butter" note, which smells of toffee and hazelnut), pyrazines (nutty), and melanoidins (brown colour).

Why is beurre noisette so much more flavourful than plain butter?
At ambient temperature, butter's volatile flavour compounds (diacetyl, butyric acid, delta-decalactone) are present but relatively modest. Beurre noisette adds an entirely new layer: dozens of Maillard products from the milk solids that were not present before heating. The fat acts as a solvent and carrier for these new volatiles — and because the fat phase remains, it delivers them to your palate via the lipophilic route. The combination of native butter volatiles plus new Maillard products in a fat matrix produces one of the most complex flavour profiles achievable from a single ingredient with simple technique.

Food for thought
  • Ice cream contains mono- and diglycerides as emulsifiers. Counterintuitively, these emulsifiers are added not to make the emulsion more stable, but to allow it to partially destabilise in a controlled way during freezing. What does partial destabilisation of the fat phase achieve in frozen ice cream?
    In ice cream, a small degree of fat globule coalescence (partial coalescence — globules touching but not fully merged) during the freezing–aeration step is desirable. Partially coalesced fat globules cluster around air bubbles that are incorporated during churning, forming a fat crystal network that stabilises air cells and gives ice cream its characteristic body and resistance to melting. If the emulsion were perfectly stable (no coalescence at all), fat globules would remain discrete spheres and could not form this network around air. Mono- and diglycerides, by partially displacing the more stable protein film from the fat globule surface, allow just enough partial coalescence during freezing to form the network — but not full coalescence (which would produce butter). This is a case where the "right" amount of emulsifier instability produces a better product. Over-emulsified ice cream has weak structure and melts rapidly; under-emulsified ice cream is grainy (large ice crystals, no fat network). The balance is tightly engineered in commercial formulations.
  • Olive oil has a smoke point of ~190°C (unrefined) to ~240°C (refined), yet Mediterranean cuisine has been frying with it for millennia. Modern food scientists note that EVOO, despite its lower smoke point, shows reasonable thermal stability in practice. What two properties of EVOO explain this apparent contradiction?
    First, high oleic acid content (~73%): oleic acid is monounsaturated, with only one double bond — far more resistant to oxidative chain reactions than linoleic (2 bonds) or alpha-linolenic (3 bonds). The rate of lipid oxidation is roughly proportional to the number of double bonds, so olive oil oxidises much more slowly than sunflower or soybean oil even at equivalent temperatures. Second, antioxidant content: EVOO contains substantial phenolic antioxidants (hydroxytyrosol, oleuropein aglycone, oleocanthal) that scavenge free radicals, interrupting the oxidative chain reaction. These antioxidants are consumed during frying — repeated use of EVOO does deplete them — but in a single-use frying context, they provide real protection. Refined olive oil lacks these phenolics (they are removed in refining) and has a higher smoke point but is not necessarily more stable under realistic cooking conditions. The smoke point test alone is insufficient to rank practical frying stability.
  • Full-fat cheese melts smoothly; reduced-fat cheese often becomes stringy and rubbery rather than melting. What fat chemistry explains this difference, and what does it imply about the structural role of fat in melted cheese?
    In full-fat cheese, the fat phase (dispersed as fat globules between casein protein strands) acts as a lubricant when heated — it melts and flows around the protein network, reducing viscosity and allowing strands to slide past each other. The protein-fat matrix plasticises. In reduced-fat cheese, the protein-to-fat ratio is higher: when heated, the denatured casein proteins form denser, more entangled networks with less fat to lubricate strand movement. The result is a stringy, elastic, rubbery melt rather than fluid flow. The fat is not just a flavour carrier in cheese — it is a structural plasticiser. Without it, the protein network dominates and the material behaves like a thick protein gel under shear, not like a flowing liquid. This is why reduced-fat mozzarella browns acceptably on a pizza but refuses to achieve the puddles of melted stretch of full-fat mozzarella. Emulsifying salts (sodium citrate, sodium phosphate) in processed cheese achieve similar plasticisation by binding calcium (which cross-links casein strands) — breaking up the protein network to produce smooth melt independent of fat content.
Common pitfalls
  • Using smoke point as the only criterion for fat selection. Oxidative stability, flavour contribution, and intended temperature range all matter. A fat with a high smoke point but many polyunsaturated bonds (regular sunflower oil) may produce more oxidation products than EVOO at equivalent frying temperatures.
  • Adding oil too fast when making an emulsion. The rate of oil addition must not exceed the rate of emulsifier migration to the new interface. For mayonnaise, this means drop-by-drop initially. A modern blender or food processor speeds this by creating much smaller droplets instantly — it's not cheating, it's better droplet formation.
  • Thinking "broken" emulsions are ruined. A broken mayo or hollandaise can almost always be rescued by restarting with fresh emulsifier (new egg yolk) and slowly incorporating the broken sauce.
  • Confusing olive oil grades. Extra-virgin = cold-pressed, unrefined, full phenolic content, lower smoke point. Refined/light olive oil = processed, neutral flavour, higher smoke point, minimal antioxidants. They are not interchangeable; use each where its properties are wanted.
  • Storing polyunsaturated oils incorrectly. Flaxseed, walnut, and hemp oils go rancid within weeks at room temperature in light. They must be refrigerated, in dark containers. The aldehydes from oxidised PUFA not only taste bad but have potential toxicity concerns in quantity.
