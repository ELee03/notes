Chocolate & Confectionery

Cocoa Fermentation · Maillard Roasting · Lipid Polymorphism · Sugar Chemistry

Chocolate is a more complex product than anything else in this cluster. To make it from a raw cacao pod requires five distinct and chemically sophisticated transformations — fermentation, drying, roasting, conching, and tempering — each of which shapes the final character in ways that are irreversible. Skip or mismanage any one of them and nothing in the remaining steps can compensate. The chemistry is also uniquely fascinating: tempering is essentially applied lipid crystallography, and the difference between a chocolate bar that snaps cleanly and one that is waxy and streaked is the difference between one crystal polymorph and another.

From Pod to Nib: Fermentation

The cacao pod of Theobroma cacao contains 30–40 seeds surrounded by white, sweetly acidic pulp. Neither the bean nor the pulp has much chocolate character at this stage — the flavour precursors are locked away as biochemically inert precursors. Fermentation is the key that releases them.

The fermentation cascade

Freshly harvested pods are cracked open and the pulp-covered beans are heaped in wooden boxes or under banana leaves, then left for 5–7 days. Two sequential microbial phases drive the chemistry:

Phase 1 — Anaerobic yeast fermentation (days 1–2): The pulp is rich in sugars (~15%) and citric acid. In the anaerobic interior of the pile, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Kloeckera apiculata, and related yeasts ferment the pulp sugars to ethanol and CO₂. Temperature rises to ~35–40°C. The pile is "turned" periodically to introduce oxygen.

Phase 2 — Acetic acid bacteria (days 3–7): As pulp drains and oxygen enters, Acetobacter and Gluconobacter take over, oxidising ethanol to acetic acid. Temperature rises further to ~45–50°C. The acetic acid and rising temperature are the keys to what happens inside the bean:

  • Bean embryo death — the acetic acid and heat kill the seed embryo, releasing enzymes from their cellular compartments.
  • Proteolysis — storage proteins are hydrolysed by endogenous proteases to free amino acids — the primary substrates for Maillard and Strecker reactions during subsequent roasting.
  • Starch/sugar hydrolysis — amylases produce reducing sugars (the second Maillard substrate).
  • Polyphenol oxidation — catechins (the bean contains exceptionally high levels of catechin and epicatechin) are partially oxidised by polyphenol oxidase — reducing the extreme astringency of raw cacao. Unfermented cacao beans are intensely bitter and astringent.

After fermentation, the beans are dried — typically sun-dried for 1–2 weeks. Most of the acetic acid (volatile, bp 118°C) evaporates during drying. If drying is too rapid (kiln-dried under-dried beans), acetic acid is trapped inside and creates off-flavours described as "hammy" or "vinegary" in the final chocolate.

Roasting

Dried cacao beans are roasted at 120–150°C for 15–40 minutes — one of the most complex Maillard systems in all of food science. The free amino acids and reducing sugars from fermentation are now available in intimate contact, ready to react.

The aroma chemistry of roasted cacao is dominated by:

  • Pyrazines (alkylpyrazines: 2-methylpyrazine, trimethylpyrazine, tetramethylpyrazine) — the characteristic "roasted cocoa" note. These are Maillard products from amino acid + sugar; they increase with higher temperature and longer roast time.
  • Strecker aldehydes — 3-methylbutanal (malty, from leucine), 2-methylbutanal (from isoleucine), phenylacetaldehyde (honey/floral, from phenylalanine). These contribute depth and complexity.
  • Furans and pyrroles — furanmethanol, furfural, methylpyrrole. Earthy, caramel-like notes.
  • Thiols — methanethiol, furfurylthiol (the same compound as in coffee). Roasty, sulfurous background notes at trace concentrations.

Temperature choice is a flavour decision:

  • Low roast (120–130°C): More fruity and floral Maillard intermediates; lower pyrazine; preserves more origin character.
  • High roast (140–150°C): Dominant pyrazines; more intense "chocolate/cocoa" character; origin differences are masked.

After roasting, the shell (husk) is removed by winnowing (cracking + air separation), leaving the cacao nibs — the pure roasted cacao interior that will be ground into chocolate.

Grinding and Conching

Cacao nibs are roughly 55% fat (cocoa butter). Grinding — between stone rollers or in a melangeur — ruptures the cell walls and releases the fat, creating a thick liquid called cocoa liquor (or cacao mass). At this stage, particle size is still too large and flavour is still too harsh.

Conching — the essential refinement step

Conching was invented by Rodolphe Lindt in 1879, and it transformed chocolate from a gritty, harsh, acidic paste into the smooth product we recognise today. The machine (originally shell-shaped, hence "conche") mechanically works the chocolate mass at 50–80°C for 12–72 hours. Three distinct effects occur simultaneously:

  1. Volatile acid removal. Acetic acid and other short-chain fatty acids that survived fermentation and drying are volatile at conching temperatures. The mechanical agitation assists evaporation — these harsh, acidic notes literally leave the chocolate mass during conching. This is why under-conched chocolate tastes harsh and vinegary compared to well-conched chocolate.
  2. Particle coating and texture development. Continuous agitation causes cocoa butter to coat every solid particle (sugar crystals, cocoa cell fragments). As particle coating improves, apparent viscosity drops dramatically — the mass flows more smoothly. This is enhanced by the addition of lecithin late in conching (see below).
  3. Flavour development and rounding. At 60–80°C for many hours, slow Maillard reactions continue; harsh, angular volatile compounds interact and round into more complex, integrated flavour. Over-conching (beyond ~48 hours for most chocolates) begins to lose delicate fruity volatiles, producing a flat character.

Lecithin — the emulsifier

Chocolate is a dispersion of hydrophilic particles (sugar, cocoa powder) in a fat phase (cocoa butter). Lecithin (phosphatidylcholine, typically from soy or sunflower) is added at 0.3–0.5% of total weight. Its amphiphilic structure allows it to coat hydrophilic sugar particle surfaces, reducing their tendency to aggregate in the fat phase. The effect on viscosity is dramatic: adding 0.3% lecithin can halve the flow viscosity of chocolate, allowing the same texture with less cocoa butter. Above ~0.8–1.0% lecithin, viscosity paradoxically increases because excess lecithin forms multilayer coatings that interfere with each other.

Cocoa Butter Polymorphism and Tempering

This is the most chemically distinctive aspect of chocolate science. Cocoa butter is a mixture of triglycerides — principally POS (palmitoyl-oleoyl-stearoyl), SOS (stearoyl-oleoyl-stearoyl), and POP (palmitoyl-oleoyl-palmitoyl). These triglycerides can pack into crystals in six distinct arrangements, called polymorphic forms, each with a different melting point and physical properties:

Form Melting point Stability Properties
Form I (α) ~17°C Very unstable Soft, crumbly; converts rapidly
Form II ~23°C Unstable Converts to III in hours
Form III ~25°C Unstable Converts to IV in days
Form IV ~27–28°C Moderately unstable Hard but dull; converts to V in weeks
Form V (β) ~33–34°C Metastable (TARGET) Glossy, snaps, melts at body temp, contracts from moulds
Form VI (β') ~36°C Most stable Waxy, slow to form (years); responsible for fat bloom in aged chocolate

Why Form V is the target: its melting point of ~33–34°C means it melts just below body temperature (37°C) and just above room temperature (~22°C). This is why good chocolate is solid at room temperature but melts on the tongue. Form V also produces the densest crystal packing — giving the characteristic glossy surface and satisfying snap. It contracts slightly on solidification (~3%), which makes it release cleanly from moulds.

If chocolate cools uncontrolled — poured over ice cream, left in a warm pocket, or simply cooled without tempering — it forms mixed Forms III/IV and slowly converts to VI. The result is dull, waxy chocolate that melts too slowly on the tongue.

The tempering process

Tempering is the controlled crystallisation process that ensures the chocolate solidifies in Form V and only Form V:

  1. Fully melt all crystal forms — heat to 50–55°C. At this temperature, all six polymorphs melt; the chocolate is completely amorphous. If you don't fully melt any existing crystals, they act as seeds for the wrong form.
  2. Cool to 27–28°C — crystal nucleation begins. Both Form IV and Form V nuclei form at this temperature. The mass thickens noticeably. This can be done by pouring onto a marble slab (tabliering) or by controlled temperature in a tempering machine.
  3. Reheat to 31–32°C — this "working temperature" is critical. At 31–32°C, Form IV crystals (melt point ~27–28°C) melt. Form V crystals (melt point ~33–34°C) survive. The result: a chocolate mass seeded exclusively with Form V nuclei. Every new crystal that forms from these seeds is Form V.
  4. Use or cast while maintaining 31–32°C — at this temperature, the chocolate remains fluid enough to pour, dip, or mould, while the Form V seeds direct solidification in the desired direction.

The working temperature window (31–32°C) lies above all unstable forms but below Form V's melting point — ensuring only Form V seeds remain active.

Fat bloom

Fat bloom — grey-white streaks or patches on chocolate — is a Form V → Form VI transition. Form VI is thermodynamically more stable and forms slowly over months to years. The conversion involves cocoa butter molecules migrating through the crystal matrix, segregating into Form VI crystals that grow to visible size at the surface. The resulting surface is matte and waxy; the texture is crumbly rather than snappy.

Fat bloom is accelerated by temperature cycling (warm room → cool fridge repeatedly), incorporation of incompatible fats (vegetable oils mixed with cocoa butter), or insufficient Form V seed crystal density during tempering. It is not a food safety issue, but it is an aesthetics and texture defect. (Not to be confused with sugar bloom — a separate phenomenon where surface moisture dissolves and recrystallises surface sugar, also producing a white matte surface, caused by condensation from temperature changes or storage at high humidity.)

Sugar Chemistry and Confectionery

Sugar confectionery is essentially applied sugar crystallisation chemistry. Different confectionery textures are created by controlling three variables: concentration, cooling rate, and the presence of interfering agents that modulate crystal growth.

Sugar syrup stages

As a sucrose/water solution is heated, water evaporates and concentration rises. At each concentration threshold, the cooled syrup has a different texture because the degree of supersaturation and crystal formation potential changes:

Stage Temperature Sucrose % Cold water test Uses
Thread 106–112°C ~80% Thin thread, breaks Syrups, spun sugar
Soft ball 112–116°C ~85% Soft, flattens Fudge, fondant, Italian meringue base
Firm ball 118–120°C ~87% Holds shape, slightly firm Soft caramels, nougat filling
Hard ball 121–130°C ~90% Holds shape, bouncy Italian meringue, marshmallow, nougat
Soft crack 132–143°C ~95% Hard threads, bends Taffy, butterscotch
Hard crack 149–154°C ~99% Hard, brittle, snaps Hard candies, lollipops, brittles
Caramel 160–185°C Decomposing Brown, bitter at high end Caramel sauce, toffee, croquembouche

Crystal control: why fudge is smooth and toffee is not

Both fudge and toffee are cooked at similar temperatures (~115–120°C), but they have completely different textures. The difference is crystal size:

  • Fudge — deliberately seeded with many tiny crystals. After cooking, the syrup is cooled to ~40°C (supersaturated, not yet crystallising spontaneously) and then beaten vigorously. Beating provides thousands of nucleation sites simultaneously; the result is millions of tiny crystals (<10 μm) that feel smooth to the tongue. Fat (butter) and inverted sugar from the cooking further interfere with crystal growth, keeping them small.
  • Toffee/butterscotch — higher butter and fat content coats sugar particles and physically prevents nucleation of ordered crystals. The result is a partially amorphous, glassy texture that fractures rather than crumbles.
  • Rock candy / fondant gone wrong — if a hot sugar syrup is disturbed prematurely (by a speck of sugar, a wooden spoon edge, or rough handling), large crystals nucleate from a few sites → large crystals → grainy texture. This is crystallisation failure.

Caramelisation

Above ~160°C, sucrose undergoes a series of decomposition reactions (not Maillard — caramelisation requires no amino acids). Sucrose first inverts (hydrolysis to glucose + fructose at elevated temperature), then the monosaccharides undergo enolisation, dehydration, and polymerisation to produce hundreds of compounds:

  • Furans (5-HMF, furfural) — caramel, sweet
  • Pyrones and maltol — caramel, cotton candy-like
  • Diacetyl — butterscotch
  • Volatile acids — lactic, acetic, pyruvic
  • High-MW melanoidins — dark brown colour, increasingly bitter

Maillard vs. caramelisation in toffee/dulce de leche: When milk (proteins + lactose) is cooked with sugar, both Maillard and caramelisation run simultaneously. Lactose (a reducing disaccharide) is more reactive in the Maillard reaction than sucrose (a non-reducing disaccharide), making milk-based confections brown faster and at lower temperatures than pure sucrose. The casein and whey proteins in milk provide abundant Strecker substrates for additional complexity not present in pure sucrose caramel.

Milk Chocolate, Dark Chocolate, and White Chocolate

All three start with cocoa butter. The differences lie in what else is present:

  • Dark chocolate (>60% cocoa mass): cocoa liquor + cocoa butter + sugar. High polyphenol content → bitter, astringent; intense cocoa aroma; lower sugar. Polyphenols in dark chocolate include the same epicatechin/catechin-based tannins as in red wine and tea.
  • Milk chocolate (~30–40% cocoa mass): cocoa liquor + cocoa butter + sugar + milk solids (~12%). Milk proteins (casein, whey) partially complex with cocoa polyphenols, reducing astringency. Lactose adds sweetness and contributes to Maillard reactions, producing caramel-milky notes. Fat from milk fat modifies the cocoa butter crystallisation (milk fat is a less ordered crystalliser than cocoa butter — why milk chocolate tempering is slightly different).
  • White chocolate: cocoa butter + sugar + milk solids; no cocoa solids. Legally must contain ≥20% cocoa butter, ≥14% milk solids. The "chocolate" character is entirely from cocoa butter aroma and milk compounds. Low-quality white chocolate substitutes vegetable fat for cocoa butter and has a waxy, flavourless character because cocoa butter has its own characteristic aroma (from trace volatile compounds).
Worked Examples
Example 1 — Fat bloom diagnosis
A box of moulded chocolates is stored in a 22°C kitchen. Twice a week for three weeks, the homeowner puts them in the refrigerator overnight "to keep them fresh." The chocolates develop grey-white streaks. Separately, chocolate- dipped strawberries stored at high humidity develop a white powder on the surface. Identify both defects and explain the mechanism of each.

Moulded chocolates — fat bloom: The temperature cycling (22°C → 4°C → 22°C repeated) is the primary driver. At 22°C, Form V crystals are close to their lower stability limit; warming slightly destabilises the crystal lattice. Repeated thermal stress provides the energy for Form V crystals to begin converting to the more thermodynamically stable Form VI. This conversion involves cocoa butter molecules migrating to the surface, where they grow into Form VI crystals visible as grey-white streaks or patches. The surface texture changes from glossy to matte. The chocolate is safe to eat but aesthetically degraded and texturally compromised (crumblier, waxier melt). Prevention: stable room temperature storage between 15–18°C; avoid refrigeration unless necessary, and only do so in an airtight container to prevent the humidity cycling below.

Dipped strawberries — sugar bloom: The white powder is not fat — it is recrystallised sucrose. Strawberries introduce moisture into the chocolate coating. In the presence of moisture, surface sucrose dissolves partially and then recrystallises as large crystals as the moisture evaporates. The result is a powdery, gritty white surface — sugar bloom. High ambient humidity accelerates it because atmospheric moisture also contributes. Prevention: consume dipped fruit promptly (within hours); do not store in a sealed humid environment; avoid drastic temperature swings that cause condensation.

Example 2 — Tempering failure modes
A home chocolatier attempts to temper dark chocolate by melting it to 55°C (correct), then cooling it on a marble slab to 27°C and pouring it into moulds. The bars set quickly but are soft, matte, and have an uneven waxy texture. A second attempt: they melt to 55°C, cool to 27°C, then accidentally heat to 38°C before moulding. This batch also fails. Explain both failures using crystal polymorph chemistry.

Attempt 1 — cooled to 27°C then moulded without reheat: At 27°C, both Form IV nuclei (melt point ~27–28°C) and Form V nuclei (melt point ~33–34°C) have formed in the mass. Moulding at this temperature means both Form IV and Form V crystals seed the solidification. The chocolate sets quickly because Form IV is also stable at room temperature, but it is harder and duller. Over days to weeks, Form IV converts to Form V, causing shrinkage cracks, surface changes, and possibly early fat bloom. The bars never had the characteristic snap and gloss because Form V never fully dominated. The missing step: reheat to 31–32°C (melting Form IV, preserving Form V) before moulding.

Attempt 2 — overheated to 38°C after cooling: At 38°C, Form V melts (its melt point is ~33–34°C). The chocolatier inadvertently destroyed all the Form V seeds they had carefully grown in step 2. The chocolate at 38°C is now seeded with nothing useful — it is essentially untampered melt again. When it cools, it will form random mixed polymorphs, predominately Forms III and IV, with the characteristic matte, soft, inferior outcome. Lesson: the reheat step must bring temperature to 31–32°C and hold it there. Even brief excursions to 35°C+ destroy the seed crystals and require starting over.

Example 3 — Fudge crystallisation control
Two batches of fudge are made from identical recipes. Batch A is stirred immediately after reaching 115°C and while still very hot. Batch B is cooled to 40°C without stirring, then beaten vigorously. Predict the texture of each and explain the crystal chemistry.

Batch A — stirred immediately at high temperature: The syrup is at ~115°C and supersaturation is not extreme. Stirring at this temperature provides nucleation sites (from the spoon surface and from any undissolved sugar), but the high temperature means crystals grow rapidly — large, few nuclei, fewer crystals. Large sucrose crystals (>40 μm) are detectable by the tongue as sandy or grainy. Batch A will have a coarse, gritty texture — classic "grainy fudge."

Batch B — cooled to 40°C, then beaten: Cooling to 40°C without stirring allows the syrup to become highly supersaturated (far more dissolved sugar than equilibrium allows) without crystallising. At 40°C, beating introduces thousands of nucleation sites simultaneously — from air bubbles, from the beater surfaces, from any seed crystal on the bowl walls. Thousands of nuclei form at once, and the remaining dissolved sugar distributes across all of them equally. Each crystal gets very little sugar: the result is millions of microscopic crystals (<10 μm), below the tongue's detection threshold. Batch B will be smooth, creamy, fine-grained fudge. This is the "super-seeding" principle — the same concept as seeding ice cream at low temperature for fine ice crystal formation.

Food for Thought
  • Single-origin craft chocolate makers often advertise unfermented or "naturally processed" cacao as superior to conventionally fermented cacao. Based on what you know about fermentation's role in flavour development, evaluate this claim critically.
    The claim is almost certainly misleading. Fermentation is not optional post-processing — it is the biochemical step that converts inert storage compounds into the flavour precursors that roasting then transforms. Unfermented or poorly fermented cacao lacks two things: (1) free amino acids from proteolysis (because proteases never activated) and (2) reducing sugars from carbohydrate breakdown. Without these, the Maillard cascade during roasting produces fewer characteristic cocoa aroma compounds — the pyrazines and Strecker aldehydes that define chocolate. Unfermented cacao also retains full polyphenol astringency (no partial oxidation during fermentation) and often residual acetic acid from the pulp (if any fermentation occurred at all). The result: harsh, astringent, under-aromatic. What "natural process" enthusiasts may be responding to is the *quality* of fermentation — a controlled, well-executed fermentation with appropriate yeast/bacterial succession produces better precursors than a badly managed or interrupted fermentation. The distinction is between good fermentation and no fermentation, not between fermentation and something better.
  • Caramel sauce made with only sucrose has a clean, sweet-butter character. Dulce de leche (made by long-simmering sweetened condensed milk) tastes completely different — more complex, darker, nuttier. Both involve prolonged heating of sugar. What specifically makes them taste so different?
    Pure sucrose caramel involves caramelisation only — sucrose inversion, then monosaccharide decomposition to furans, pyrones, and brown polymers. The available reactants are sugar molecules and their thermal decomposition products. Dulce de leche adds two things: (1) lactose, a reducing disaccharide that participates in the Maillard reaction far more readily than sucrose (which must be inverted first); and (2) milk proteins (casein and whey), which provide abundant amino acids for both Maillard browning and Strecker degradation. The Maillard products from lactose + casein amino acids include the same pyrazines, furans, and Strecker aldehydes found in roasted coffee, bread crust, and cooked meat — a level of complexity impossible from pure sugar alone. The milk proteins also contribute to the thick, viscous texture as they partially denature and interact with the concentrated sugar syrup. The result is dozens of additional compounds not present in pure caramel, producing the characteristic nutty, complex, butterscotch depth that is impossible to replicate by adding caramel to milk after cooking.
  • A pastry chef claims that adding corn syrup to a candy recipe "prevents crystallisation." From what you know about crystal nucleation and sugar chemistry, explain the mechanism by which corn syrup achieves this effect.
    Corn syrup is a mixture of glucose, maltose, and glucose oligomers (from enzymatic hydrolysis of corn starch). It interferes with sucrose crystallisation through three mechanisms: (1) The glucose and maltose molecules are structurally different from sucrose — they cannot slot into a sucrose crystal lattice, so they act as "impurities" that block crystal growth sites. Even small amounts dramatically reduce the maximum crystal size achievable. (2) The high molecular weight oligomers in corn syrup increase viscosity of the syrup, slowing diffusion of sucrose molecules to crystal surfaces — reducing growth rate. (3) At high concentrations, corn syrup molecules compete with sucrose for nucleation sites. The net result is that corn syrup raises the effective supersaturation threshold — the syrup must be cooled further before crystallisation begins, and when it does, smaller, more numerous crystals form. This is the same "interfering agent" principle as using cream of tartar (promotes inversion of sucrose to glucose + fructose, with similar interference effect) or simply using invert sugar.
Common Pitfalls
  1. Confusing fat bloom and sugar bloom. Both look white/grey on the surface, but the causes and contexts are different. Fat bloom: temperature cycling or mis-tempering, Form V → Form VI crystal conversion, greasy texture. Sugar bloom: moisture exposure, dissolved and recrystallised sucrose, powdery/ gritty texture. The distinction matters because the prevention strategies are different.
  2. Overheating during tempering. The reheat step must reach 31–32°C, not 33–35°C. Exceeding Form V's melting point (~33–34°C) melts the seeds you just grew. A candy thermometer or infrared gun is required — touch-testing is not precise enough.
  3. Skipping or inadequate conching. Home chocolate makers often "melt and mould" commercial couverture, which is already fully conched. But attempting to make chocolate from nibs without a conching step produces acidic, harsh chocolate regardless of how well it was fermented and roasted. Conching cannot be substituted by longer roasting or better ingredients.
  4. Stirring fudge at the wrong temperature. The entire texture of fudge depends on when crystallisation is induced. Stirring at high temperature creates large crystals; stirring too late (below 35°C, once spontaneous crystallisation has started) forces you to work with partial crystal masses that produce uneven, streaky fudge. The window (38–45°C) is specific and requires a thermometer to hit reliably.
  5. Treating "percentage" on chocolate as a quality indicator. The percentage on dark chocolate refers to total cocoa solids (cocoa mass + cocoa butter). An 85% chocolate from poor-quality, under-fermented beans is inferior to a 70% chocolate from excellent beans. Percentage determines bitterness and intensity, not quality. Origin, fermentation, and roast are the quality determinants; percentage is a texture and flavour intensity dial.
