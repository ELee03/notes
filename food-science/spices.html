Spices & Aromatics

Terpene Chemistry · Phenylpropanoids · Extraction & Bloom · Capsaicinoids

A spice is a plant-derived flavouring — typically seeds, bark, roots, or dried fruits — that contributes aroma, heat, colour, or all three to food. The chemistry behind them is well understood, and that understanding explains several things that experienced cooks know by feel but rarely articulate: why freshly ground spice is so much more powerful than the jar that's been open for a year; why blooming spices in hot oil extracts more character than simmering them in water; why the heat from a chilli pepper lingers for twenty minutes while the heat from black pepper fades in seconds; and why some spices can tolerate long braises while others must be added at the last moment.

This lesson covers the chemical families that make up spice aroma (terpenes and phenylpropanoids), the extraction and decay of volatile compounds during cooking, the distinct chemistry of pungency (capsaicin, piperine), and detailed profiles of the most chemically interesting spices. Allium sulphur chemistry (garlic, onion) and brassica glucosinolates (mustard, wasabi) are covered in Lesson 9: Vegetables & Fruits.

Chemical Families in Spice Aroma

Almost all spice aroma comes from two chemical families: terpenes (and their oxygenated derivatives, terpenoids) and phenylpropanoids. Understanding their structures explains their behaviour during cooking.

Terpenes and Terpenoids

Terpenes are built from five-carbon isoprene units (C₅H₈) assembled in a head-to-tail pattern. The number of isoprene units determines the class:

  • Monoterpenes (C₁₀, 2 isoprene units): highly volatile, low boiling points (150–180°C), the dominant class in most spice essential oils. Examples: limonene (citrus, cumin), α-pinene (rosemary, bay), linalool (coriander, lavender, cardamom), myrcene (hops, bay leaf), thymol (thyme), carvacrol (oregano), menthol (mint), 1,8-cineole/eucalyptol (cardamom, bay).
  • Sesquiterpenes (C₁₅, 3 isoprene units): heavier, less volatile, more heat-stable. Examples: β-caryophyllene (clove, black pepper, cannabis — the compound behind CB2 receptor activity in cannabis), zingiberene (ginger), bisabolene.
  • Diterpenes and above (C₂₀+): essentially non-volatile at cooking temperatures — contribute colour and bitterness rather than aroma.

Terpenoids are terpenes with additional functional groups (alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, oxides). Geraniol (rose, geranium), citral (lemon grass, citrus zest), menthone (mint), and carvone (caraway, dill, spearmint) are all oxygenated monoterpenes with distinct and identifiable aromas.

Phenylpropanoids

Phenylpropanoids are derived from the amino acid phenylalanine via the phenylpropanoid pathway — the same pathway that produces lignin and tannins. They have a benzene ring attached to a three-carbon chain, and many have distinctive intense aromas at low concentrations:

  • Cinnamaldehyde: cinnamon bark's dominant aroma compound (60–80% of essential oil); an α,β-unsaturated aldehyde.
  • Eugenol: clove's dominant compound (70–90% of essential oil); also present in cinnamon, bay leaf, nutmeg, and basil; warm, spicy-sweet.
  • Anethole: anise, fennel, star anise; the compound behind the characteristic "licorice" character.
  • Estragole (methyl chavicol): tarragon, sweet basil; structurally related to anethole but with a distinctly different, more herbal character.
  • Vanillin: vanilla; a methoxylated benzaldehyde.
  • Safrole: was historically important in sassafras; now recognised as a carcinogenic precursor and largely removed from food use.

Phenylpropanoids are generally less volatile than monoterpenes (higher boiling points, ~250–300°C) and correspondingly more heat-stable. A clove stud can sit in a slow braise for hours and still flavour the dish; a handful of fresh basil added to the same braise would contribute almost nothing because its linalool and estragole evaporate immediately.

Extraction: Fat vs. Water

"Blooming" spices — briefly frying whole or ground spices in hot oil or ghee before adding other ingredients — is a technique used across Indian, Middle Eastern, and Southeast Asian cooking. The chemistry behind it is real and quantifiable.

Terpenes and most phenylpropanoids are lipophilic — they dissolve much more readily in fat than in water. The partition coefficient (log P, a measure of lipophilicity) for key spice compounds typically ranges from 2 to 5, meaning the compound is 100–100,000 times more concentrated in fat than in an equal volume of water at equilibrium.

Practical consequences:

  • Blooming extracts far more of the volatile aromatics from the spice cell matrix than simmering in water does. The hot fat also heats the spice rapidly, driving volatilisation while immediately dissolving what's released.
  • Spice flavours added to an oil-based dish are perceived differently from the same spice added to a water-based dish: fat carries the lipophilic aromatics to the palate more efficiently, providing longer retronasal persistence.
  • Temperature matters for bloom: oil at 170–190°C extracts efficiently and triggers some desirable thermal transformations (Maillard-adjacent reactions between spice compounds and the oil). Below ~120°C, extraction is slower and thermal transformations are minimal. Above ~220°C, many monoterpenes simply evaporate or char.

The exception is water-soluble flavour compounds. Saffron's pigment (crocin) and some bitter compounds are best extracted in warm liquid (water or dairy). Paprika's capsanthin and capsorubin (carotenoid pigments) are fat-soluble and must be bloomed in oil to release their colour and flavour effectively.

The fat-vs-water rule for specific spices:
Bloom in fat: cumin, coriander, mustard seeds, fenugreek, cardamom, star anise, paprika, chilli — all fat-soluble terpene/phenylpropanoid dominated.
Extract in liquid: saffron (warm water or milk), vanilla (alcohol + water extract best), bay leaf (water fine — cineole is partially water-miscible).
Both work: black pepper, cinnamon (add to fat for depth, to liquid for colour/flavour).
Volatile Stability During Cooking

Not all aroma compounds survive cooking equally. The key variable is the compound's vapour pressure at cooking temperature — and whether it undergoes thermal degradation or transformation into something else.

A rough thermal stability ranking for common spice volatiles:

Compound Found in BP (approx) Heat stability When to add
Myrcene Bay leaf, hops, ginger 167°C Very low Last minute / raw
Limonene Citrus, cumin, coriander 176°C Low Brief cooking only
Linalool Coriander, lavender, cardamom 198°C Moderate Early–mid cooking
Thymol / Carvacrol Thyme, oregano 232°C Good Early cooking
Cinnamaldehyde Cinnamon 248°C Good Early or mid cooking
β-Caryophyllene Clove, black pepper 260°C High Any point
Eugenol Clove, cinnamon, bay 254°C High Any point
Capsaicin Chilli peppers 210°C Very high Any point (heat doesn't reduce it)

The key insight from this table: there is no single rule for when to add spices. Fragile monoterpenes (myrcene, limonene) must be added at the end, or they simply evaporate. Robust phenylpropanoids (eugenol, cinnamaldehyde) and sesquiterpenes (β-caryophyllene) tolerate prolonged cooking. Mixing a spice blend that is added at the start may lose its most volatile components entirely while the stable ones intensify — this shifts the flavour profile away from what was intended.

Why freshly ground spice is stronger

A whole spice has a protective outer coat — husk, seed coat, or bark — that physically limits the surface area through which essential oils can escape or oxidise. Grinding shatters this and creates an enormous surface area increase. A 1 cm spice cube ground to 1 mm particles increases surface area by roughly 1000-fold.

Three processes then race to destroy the aroma:

  1. Volatilisation — monoterpenes evaporate at room temperature once exposed. The smell of freshly ground pepper or coriander in the air is the spice literally losing its flavour.
  2. Oxidation — O₂ attacks the double bonds in terpenes. Limonene oxidises to peroxides that smell like turpentine and are mild irritants. Other terpene peroxides are allergens. A jar of old pre-ground cumin smells "off" because oxidised terpene products dominate over the fresh limonene.
  3. Moisture absorption — ground spice is hygroscopic; absorbed water dilutes essential oils and accelerates hydrolysis of some ester compounds.

The practical recommendation: buy whole spices, toast briefly in a dry pan before grinding (heat ruptures more oil cells for fuller extraction), grind immediately before use. For spices used infrequently, buy small quantities and replace often rather than grinding large batches.

Pungency: Capsaicin and Piperine

Capsaicin and piperine are not aromas. They do not activate smell receptors or taste receptors. They activate pain and temperature receptors in the oral mucosa (and elsewhere), creating the sensation of heat.

Capsaicin and the TRPV1 receptor

Capsaicin (and its close analogue dihydrocapsaicin, which is equally potent) is a vanillyl amide — it has a vanillin-derived ring structure and a long lipophilic tail. It binds to and activates TRPV1 (Transient Receptor Potential Vanilloid 1), a non-selective cation channel that normally responds to temperatures above 43°C, acidic conditions, and some lipids. When capsaicin binds TRPV1, the channel opens and produces exactly the same signal as actual high heat — hence the burning sensation.

This is fundamentally different from taste (which involves G-protein-coupled receptors or ion channels in taste receptor cells) and from aroma (which involves olfactory receptors). Capsaicin bypasses flavour entirely and talks directly to the nervous system's pain-and-heat signalling pathway.

Why dairy relieves chilli heat but water does not:
Capsaicin is lipophilic (log P ≈ 3.6) — it is essentially insoluble in water but dissolves readily in fat. Water cannot wash it off mucosal surfaces. Dairy works through two mechanisms: (1) the fat phase dissolves and removes capsaicin; (2) casein micelles (the main milk proteins) act as detergent-like structures that encapsulate capsaicin molecules and carry them away. Alcohol (ethanol) also dissolves capsaicin, which is why a high-alcohol drink provides relief. Bread or starchy foods provide mild relief by mechanical absorption at the mucosal surface.

Where capsaicin is in a pepper: Almost entirely in the placenta — the white spongy pith to which the seeds are attached. The seeds themselves contain little capsaicin; they are hot by physical proximity to the placenta. Removing the placenta (and seeds attached to it) substantially reduces heat level.

Heat stability: Capsaicin does not break down at normal cooking temperatures. A dish does not get milder by cooking longer. The only ways to reduce heat are dilution (adding more non-spicy ingredients) or fatty extraction (absorbing capsaicin into a fat phase that is then discarded — rinsing a cut pepper surface with oil, then wiping dry, removes significant capsaicin).

The Scoville Scale

The Scoville scale was originally a bioassay: a capsaicin extract is diluted in sugar water until a panel can no longer detect heat; the Scoville Heat Unit (SHU) is the dilution factor. Modern measurement uses HPLC to quantify capsaicinoid content directly, then converts: 1 ppm (by weight) capsaicinoid ≈ 15 SHU.

Chilli Pepper Heat Scale (log SHU)

Scoville heat units (SHU) plotted on a log₁₀ scale. Note that the scale spans eight orders of magnitude from bell pepper to pure capsaicin. All units are approximate means — individual peppers from the same plant can vary 2–3× depending on growing conditions and stress.

Piperine and black pepper

Piperine — an amide of piperic acid and piperidine — is responsible for black pepper's heat. It activates TRPV1 but also TRPA1 (Transient Receptor Potential Ankyrin 1 — also the receptor for wasabi and mustard). TRPA1 activation adds the sharp, "catching-in-the-throat" quality that distinguishes pepper heat from chilli heat.

Piperine heat dissipates faster than capsaicin — partly because piperine's binding to TRPV1/TRPA1 is more transient, and partly because it is less lipophilic (log P ≈ 2.6) and more easily cleared from mucosal surfaces.

Beyond pungency, piperine interacts with taste. It enhances perceived saltiness and umami — a practical reason why a dish that "needs more seasoning" sometimes needs pepper rather than salt. It is also a potent inhibitor of CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein (metabolic enzymes in the gut and liver), which dramatically increases the bioavailability of co-ingested compounds. This is the scientific basis for combining turmeric with black pepper: curcumin (turmeric's active compound) has extremely poor oral bioavailability, but piperine can increase it by 2000%.

Black pepper's aroma comes from monoterpenes: α-pinene and β-pinene (pine), limonene (citrus), myrcene (woody). These are volatile and fragile; freshly ground pepper contributes aroma that pre-ground pepper cannot. The piperine itself is relatively heat-stable, so black pepper's heat survives cooking, but its aroma does not unless added at the end.

Individual Spice Profiles

Cinnamon

Two quite different plants are sold as cinnamon. True cinnamon (Cinnamomum verum, Ceylon cinnamon) has thin, layered bark and a delicate, complex aroma dominated by cinnamaldehyde at lower levels (~50–60%), supported by eugenol, linalool, and β-caryophyllene. Cassia (C. cassia, C. aromaticum — most "cinnamon" sold in North America) is thicker, harder, and dominated by cinnamaldehyde at higher levels (~75–90%) with far less supporting cast. Cassia tastes stronger and more one-dimensional; Ceylon is more nuanced at the same weight.

Cinnamaldehyde is an α,β-unsaturated aldehyde — it is reactive and can form Schiff bases with amines, which may contribute to its antimicrobial properties. It is relatively heat-stable but does slowly oxidise to cinnamic acid (less aromatic) on prolonged storage. The whole sticks in a slow braise release compound continuously over hours.

Clove

Clove (Syzygium aromaticum) contains the highest eugenol concentration of any common spice — 70–90% of the essential oil. Eugenol is warm, spicy, and clove-characteristic; at lower concentrations it appears in cinnamon, bay leaf, and basil. β-Caryophyllene adds a woody, peppery note. Acetyleugenol is a minor component that modifies character slightly between bud and leaf clove.

Eugenol is a mild local anaesthetic — historically used in dentistry (zinc oxide eugenol cement, ZOE) and still sold as a home toothache remedy. The same compound creates the numbing sensation that can develop when too much clove is used in cooking.

Vanilla

Real vanilla flavour is the product of a slow enzymatic and chemical curing process applied to the pod of Vanilla planifolia. The fresh pod has little aroma: vanillin exists as a glycoside (glucovanillin) which is odourless. Curing (alternating sweating and drying over several weeks to months) activates endogenous β-glucosidase enzymes, cleaving the glucose to release free vanillin. Oxidative reactions simultaneously produce hundreds of supporting compounds: 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde, guaiacol, p-anisaldehyde, furaneol, and numerous others.

Synthetic vanillin — made from guaiacol (from petrochemicals) or from lignosulfonates (a paper-making by-product) — reproduces the dominant compound but not the supporting cast. This is why a dish made with "vanilla flavour" is detectable as inferior to one made with real vanilla extract: the minor compounds create the rounded complexity that vanillin alone cannot. Vanilla extract (ethanol + water extraction of cured beans) contains both vanillin and the supporting compounds and is the best practical format for cooking.

Saffron

Saffron (Crocus sativus) is the dried stigmas of the saffron crocus; it is expensive because each flower has three stigmas that must be harvested by hand. Three distinct chemical systems contribute to its character:

  • Crocin (and crocetin): water-soluble carotenoid glycosides responsible for the vivid yellow-orange colour. Unlike most carotenoids, crocin is water-soluble (the glucose groups make it hydrophilic). Heat-stable once extracted.
  • Picrocrocin: a bitter glycoside derived from the same carotenoid degradation pathway. Contributes the characteristic saffron bitterness.
  • Safranal: a monoterpenoid aldehyde that forms from picrocrocin during the drying/curing process (the glycosidic bond cleaves, releasing the volatile aglycone). Responsible for saffron's distinctive honey-hay aroma.

Extraction technique matters. Crocin and picrocrocin are water-soluble; warm water or dairy are optimal solvents. Safranal is volatile and partially lipophilic. Brief blooming in a little warm liquid (not boiling — above ~85°C safranal evaporates quickly) releases maximum aroma and colour.

Ginger and Turmeric

Ginger's pungent compounds transform radically with processing:

  • Gingerols (6-, 8-, 10-gingerol): the dominant pungent compounds in fresh ginger. Warm, slightly less aggressive than capsaicin. Activate TRPV1.
  • Shogaols: formed by dehydration of gingerols during drying or prolonged heating. About twice as pungent as the corresponding gingerol. Dominant in dried ground ginger.
  • Zingerone: forms from gingerol by retro-aldol condensation during cooking — milder, sweeter, "cooked ginger" note. The reason cooked ginger is gentler than raw ginger.

Fresh ginger ≠ dried ginger in flavour. They are not interchangeable at any conversion ratio: shogaol-dominated dried ginger is hotter and lacks fresh ginger's terpene top notes (zingiberene, β-bisabolene). A recipe specifying "fresh ginger" developed its character around the gingerol-terpene profile; substituting dried will make a different, hotter dish.

Turmeric's colour compound is curcumin (a polyphenol, not a terpene), which has almost no aroma but contributes vivid yellow. Curcumin is famously poorly bioavailable — but piperine co-ingestion increases absorption dramatically. Turmeric's aroma comes from sesquiterpene ketones (ar-turmerone, α- and β-turmerone) which are responsible for its earthy, slightly bitter notes.

Star Anise, Fennel, and Anise

Three unrelated plant families converge on almost identical aroma through convergent evolution to produce anethole: star anise (Illicium verum, a magnoliid), fennel (Foeniculum vulgare, an umbellifer), and anise seed (Pimpinella anisum, also an umbellifer). All contain 70–90% trans-anethole as the dominant volatile, giving the characteristic licorice character.

Anethole is a phenylpropanoid and correspondingly heat-stable; these spices survive long simmering. Star anise is by far the cheapest source and is used in Vietnamese pho, Chinese five-spice, and many braised preparations for extended cooking. Fennel fronds are more delicate and should be added fresh; fennel seeds tolerate cooking.

Tarragon and sweet basil contain estragole (methyl chavicol) as a major compound — structurally very similar to anethole (just a methyl group difference) but with a distinctly fresher, more herbal character. These are volatile and heat-labile: both herbs must be added at the end or their characteristic aroma is lost.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Toast-and-grind vs. pre-ground: how much difference does it make?
You are making a curry paste. You have two options: (A) use pre-ground cumin and coriander from jars that have been open for six months; (B) buy whole seeds, toast them in a dry pan, grind fresh. A colleague argues the difference is minimal — "cumin is cumin." Explain the chemistry of why option B produces a dramatically different result.

Pre-ground, six months open (A): Cumin's dominant fresh aromatics are limonene (citrus), α-pinene, p-cymene, and cumin aldehyde. Limonene begins oxidising to terpene hydroperoxides and terpineol within weeks of grinding. After six months in an open jar, most of the limonene is gone or transformed. What remains are oxidised products — musty, turpentine-like, slightly harsh notes. The characteristic fresh, lemony-spicy top notes of cumin simply no longer exist.

Toasted and freshly ground (B): The whole seed's coat protects essential oils from O₂ and light. Toasting briefly in a dry pan (120–160°C, ~2 minutes) does two things: (1) ruptures more oil cells in the seed, giving better extraction when ground; (2) triggers Maillard reactions on the seed surface, adding roasted depth compounds. Grinding immediately before use preserves the freshly exposed volatiles. The result: full limonene top notes, roasted depth, and no oxidised off-character.

Quantitative context: Freshly ground cumin can contain 2–4% limonene by weight; pre-ground cumin stored 6 months may have <0.1%. Given that limonene's detection threshold is ~10 ppb in water, this difference is easily perceptible. The colleague's "cumin is cumin" claim is wrong — the molecular composition has fundamentally changed.

Example 2 — Deconstructing a slow-braise spice blend
A Moroccan lamb tagine recipe calls for: cinnamon stick, cloves, ground cumin (added at start), fresh coriander (added at finish), and ground chilli. All are added to the pot together at the beginning except the coriander. Explain from a volatility standpoint why this recipe is structured this way, and whether any of the start-of-cook spices are correctly placed.

The structure reflects an intuitive understanding of volatile stability, and it is largely correct for all but one spice.

Cinnamon stick (correct): Cinnamaldehyde (BP ~248°C) and eugenol (BP ~254°C) are stable at braising temperatures (~100°C). The whole stick releases continuously over the cook, maintaining character throughout. This is the ideal form for long braises.

Cloves (correct): Eugenol is highly heat-stable. Cloves in a long braise contribute continuously. The risk is over-addition — eugenol's intensity means even one extra clove can dominate everything else.

Ground cumin (marginal): Cumin's aroma includes some moderately stable compounds (cuminaldehyde, p-cymene) that survive extended cooking. However, its most volatile limonene and α-pinene components will evaporate in the first 15 minutes. Adding some cumin at start contributes stable depth; ideally a small second addition of fresh-ground cumin 15–20 minutes before serving would restore the fresh top notes.

Chilli (correct): Capsaicin is essentially heat-indestructible at braising temperatures. Adding at the start ensures even distribution through the fat phase; it contributes the same heat whether added at start or finish.

Fresh coriander at finish (correct and critical): Coriander's characteristic compounds — linalool, decanal, dodecenal — have BPs of ~198–200°C and are highly volatile. Added at the start, they would evaporate entirely within minutes. Added as a finish, they provide the fresh herbal top note that lifts the whole dish. This is the most important placement decision in the recipe.

Example 3 — Saffron: colour vs. flavour extraction
A recipe says to bloom saffron in hot water and add the liquid to the dish. A second approach says to add the saffron threads directly to the simmering dish. A third approach suggests blooming in warm cream (for a risotto). Which approach best extracts (a) colour, (b) aroma, and (c) full flavour complexity? Justify using saffron's chemistry.

Saffron has three distinct compounds to extract: crocin (colour), picrocrocin (bitterness), and safranal (aroma). Their solubilities and stabilities differ.

Crocin (colour) — best extraction: Water. Crocin is water-soluble (glycosylated carotenoid). Warm water (50–65°C) dissolves it efficiently. All three methods will extract colour; direct addition to simmering water/stock works, but pre-blooming in hot water ensures the threads are fully hydrated and every molecule is dissolved before it reaches the dish.

Safranal (aroma) — most vulnerable: Safranal is volatile (BP ~205°C) and partially lipophilic. In hot water above ~80°C, it evaporates rapidly from the cooking liquid into the air rather than staying in the dish. Direct addition to a simmering risotto means much of the aroma is lost before the dish is eaten. Optimal approach: bloom in warm (not hot) liquid at 60–70°C, then add to the dish just before service.

Warm cream bloom (best for risotto): The fat in cream dissolves and retains safranal — lipophilic compounds are less volatile from a fat phase than from water. The cream's protein (casein) can also bind some aromatic compounds. Blooming saffron in warm cream at ~60°C, then adding the cream at the end of risotto cooking, maximises aroma retention while extracting colour from the aqueous phase simultaneously. This is the most complete extraction approach.

Food for Thought
  • Drinking water does not relieve chilli heat. Beer (a water + alcohol + carbonation solution) relieves it somewhat. Full-fat milk relieves it well. A shot of vodka provides significant relief. Rank these from least to most effective and explain why, drawing on both the chemistry of capsaicin and the mechanisms of each beverage.
    In order of effectiveness: water (no help) < beer (minimal) < vodka (good) < full-fat milk (best). Water cannot dissolve lipophilic capsaicin and simply spreads it around the oral cavity. Beer is mostly water but contains ~5% ethanol — the ethanol dissolves some capsaicin, providing mild relief, but the low concentration and carbonation (which activates TRPA1, briefly worsening sensation) make it limited. Vodka at 40% ethanol dissolves capsaicin far more effectively, and the higher alcohol concentration is directly proportional to solubilisation. Full-fat milk is most effective: the fat phase physically extracts capsaicin from mucosal surfaces (fat is a better solvent than 40% ethanol for capsaicin's log P 3.6), and the casein micelles act as surfactant- like structures to encapsulate dissolved capsaicin and prevent rebinding. The combination of fat + casein outperforms pure ethanol solubilisation.
  • A recipe is described as "the same as the original but with dried herbs substituted for fresh to simplify shopping." The dish tastes flat and lacks the fresh herbal character of the original. Is this just a matter of quality, or is there a chemical reason dried herbs cannot replicate fresh herbs in this particular use case?
    There is a fundamental chemical reason, not just a quality difference. Fresh herbs contain intact cell structure with essential oils in oil glands or trichomes. The specific monoterpene profile (e.g. linalool in fresh basil, myrcene in bay leaf) is present in full. Drying inevitably loses a large fraction of the most volatile compounds — myrcene, limonene, some linalool — either through evaporation or oxidation. What remains in dried herb is enriched in the more heat-stable sesquiterpenes and phenylpropanoids (thymol, carvacrol in dried thyme and oregano), which is why dried thyme and oregano can substitute more gracefully than dried basil or fresh coriander. Additionally, the LOX pathway is active in fresh herbs (like in fresh vegetables) and produces C6 "green" aldehydes on cell damage — crushing fresh herbs releases these grassy, vivid notes that have no equivalent in dried. If the recipe was designed around those fresh C6 volatiles (basil, fresh coriander, tarragon), no amount of dried herb will replicate them.
  • You are designing a spice rub for a steak that will be seared at very high heat (250°C surface) for 3 minutes per side, then rested. The rub contains smoked paprika, ground black pepper, dried thyme, and garlic powder. Which compounds from the rub will survive to the plate, which will not, and how would you modify the rub composition or application method to improve flavour delivery?
    At 250°C surface temperature, the outer layer of the rub will exceed 150°C for extended periods. Analysis by compound: Smoked paprika's carotenoid pigments (capsanthin, capsorubin) are fat-soluble and relatively stable — they will survive and contribute colour and mild earthy flavour; the capsaicinoids will survive fully. Ground black pepper: piperine is heat-stable and survives; the monoterpene aromatics (limonene, myrcene, pinene) will largely evaporate in the first minute of high-heat contact. Dried thyme: thymol and carvacrol (BP ~232°C) will partially survive; the more volatile fraction is lost quickly. Garlic powder: the sulphur compounds formed during drying (polysulfides) are moderately stable and will contribute garlic character throughout. To improve: (1) Hold back some black pepper and a portion of the thyme to add post-sear/ during the rest — they contribute more aroma at lower temperature. (2) For the garlic, freshly minced garlic added in the final 30s of searing or during basting creates more complex allicin-derived compounds than garlic powder. (3) For maximum aroma delivery, apply a fresh herb butter (compound butter) during the rest — fat dissolves and holds aromatics that would otherwise be lost to air.
Common Pitfalls
  1. Equating heat stability with flavour stability. Capsaicin survives hours of cooking unchanged; piperine survives too. But most terpene aromatics that make a spice smell interesting do not. A spice blend added at the start of a 3-hour braise delivers heat and body — but its fragrant top notes are gone. Design spice addition in stages: stable compounds early, volatile ones late.
  2. Treating dried ginger as interchangeable with fresh. Shogaols (dried ginger) are ~2× more pungent than gingerols (fresh), and the terpene aroma profile is completely different. The substitution rate often cited (1 tsp dried = 1 tbsp fresh) is for volume, not flavour impact — and even with correct substitution, the flavour profile is different, not equivalent.
  3. Storing pre-ground spice indefinitely. Monoterpenes oxidise within weeks to months; the resulting terpene peroxides smell harsh and can be irritants. Six-month-old pre-ground cumin or paprika at the back of the spice drawer should simply be discarded. The cost of replacing it is small compared to the flavour loss in a whole dish.
  4. Adding water to dilute chilli heat. This does not work. Capsaicin is insoluble in water and is redistributed rather than removed. The correct remedies are: fat/dairy for immediate relief, more total dish volume (dilution), or starch to absorb it physically.
  5. Assuming Ceylon cinnamon and cassia are the same product. They have different cinnamaldehyde concentrations, different flavour profiles, and different levels of coumarin (a compound with potential hepatotoxic effects at very high doses, present in cassia at levels ~20–50× higher than Ceylon). Recipes developed with Ceylon cinnamon can taste harsh with cassia; recipes using cassia may need less cinnamon if switched to Ceylon.
