Coffee & Tea

Roasting Chemistry · Extraction Kinetics · Polyphenol Oxidation · Brewing Science

Coffee and tea are beverages whose character is determined almost entirely by chemistry — and in both cases the chemistry is more complex, more specific, and more interesting than the experience of drinking them might suggest. Coffee involves one of the most chemically complex Maillard transformations in all of food science: green beans are turned from a biochemically inert seed into a matrix of over a thousand volatile compounds. Tea involves a precisely timed enzymatic oxidation that determines the entire character of the final drink. In both cases, extraction — how much, how fast, at what temperature — governs whether the resulting beverage is brilliant or flat.

Coffee: The Green Bean

A green coffee bean is a seed of Coffea arabica (or C. canephora for robusta). Its composition determines what roasting can produce:

Component % dry weight Role in roasting
Sucrose 6–9% Primary Maillard substrate; caramelises; CO₂ precursor
Chlorogenic acids 5–8% Major Maillard substrate; converts to 4-vinylguaiacol, phenolics; drives roast bitterness in dark roasts
Proteins / free amino acids 10–13% Maillard reaction partners; Strecker degradation → characteristic aldehydes
Trigonelline 0.5–1% Converts to pyridines and nicotinic acid (niacin) at roast temperatures; contributes mellow, buttery notes in light roasts
Lipids 12–18% Carrier for fat-soluble volatiles; migrate to surface in dark roasts; include cafestol and kahweol (diterpene alcohols)
Caffeine 1–2.5% Heat-stable; survives roasting essentially unchanged; contributes bitterness in cup
Roasting Chemistry

Roasting is the most chemically transformative process in food — it converts an essentially odourless bean into a matrix of over 1000 identified volatile compounds. The key reactions proceed in stages as temperature rises:

Drying and early reactions (~100–150°C)

Free water evaporates (~11% of green bean weight). The bean lightens in colour slightly and becomes brittle. Early Maillard intermediates (Amadori compounds) form from sucrose degradation products and amino acids — no aroma yet.

Maillard cascade and first crack (~150–205°C)

The Maillard reaction accelerates sharply above 150°C. Several parallel pathways produce hundreds of aroma compounds simultaneously:

  • Strecker degradation — amino acids react with α-dicarbonyl Maillard intermediates. Each amino acid produces a characteristic Strecker aldehyde: methionine → methional (potato-like); cysteine → methanethiol; leucine → 3-methylbutanal (malty); valine → 2-methylpropanal. These contribute the initial wave of roasted aroma.
  • Pyrazine formation — two Strecker amino-ketones combine and cyclise. Pyrazines are the main carriers of "roasted, nutty" character; they increase with darker roasts.
  • Furans — from sugar degradation. Furfural (from pentoses), 2-acetylfuran, furfuryl alcohol. Caramel-like, sweet.
  • Chlorogenic acid degradation — chlorogenic acids fragment to 4-vinylguaiacol (clove-smoky), guaiacol, phenol, and catechol. These compounds accumulate in darker roasts and contribute body and phenolic depth.
  • Trigonelline pyrolysis — above ~170°C, trigonelline loses its methyl group to form nicotinic acid (niacin, a B vitamin) and a range of pyridines (mellow, slightly musty).

Around 196–205°C, rapid CO₂ production from the bean interior causes the cell walls to fracture with an audible pop — first crack. The bean expands and lightens. Light and medium roasts are pulled here or shortly after.

Second crack and dark roasts (~225–240°C)

Continued heating breaks down cell wall carbohydrates (cellulose pyrolysis), releasing more CO₂ in a second audible cracking sequence. The bean surface turns oily as lipids migrate outward. The key changes:

  • Most chlorogenic acids are now destroyed; the moderate bitterness they contributed is replaced by harsher pyrolysis products.
  • Sucrose is fully consumed; organic acids (malic, citric, quinic) that survived are progressively degraded — dark roast coffee is less acidic than light roast, counterintuitively.
  • 2-Furfurylthiol (FFT) — the single most important coffee aroma compound (also called coffee furanethiol) — forms from the reaction of furfuryl alcohol and H₂S. Its detection threshold is ~0.01 ppb; it contributes the characteristic "fresh coffee" character at concentrations in the parts per trillion range.
  • Beyond second crack: significant charring. Carbon compounds dominate. The bean is losing rather than gaining character.
Light vs. dark roast: what changes?
Light: more chlorogenic acid (higher perceived acidity), higher caffeine per unit weight (beans are less expanded), more nuanced Maillard products, more original bean character (origin, variety, processing method detectable).
Dark: more pyrazines (roasted/burnt notes), less chlorogenic acid, less caffeine per unit weight, surface oils present (affects crema differently in espresso), lower perceived acidity, heavier body.

CO₂ degassing and the bloom

During roasting, CO₂ is produced and trapped in the bean cell structure under pressure. A freshly roasted bean can contain 2–12 mL of CO₂ per gram of coffee. This degasses over days to weeks after roasting. For brewing:

  • Espresso: CO₂ is a major contributor to crema formation. Too fresh (within 2–3 days of roast) gives uneven extraction; too old (4+ weeks) and there's no crema. Most espresso is at its best 7–21 days off roast.
  • Filter/pour-over: the bloom step (pre-wetting grounds with ~2× their weight in water for 30–45 seconds) allows CO₂ to off-gas before extraction starts. CO₂ impedes even water penetration; skipping the bloom produces uneven extraction with channelling.
  • Cold brew: very slow degassing over days; CO₂ contributes mild carbonation.
Coffee Extraction Chemistry

No single brewing method is "better"; each is optimised for a different target. What they all have in common is that flavour compounds are not all equally soluble or equally fast to extract. The sequence matters:

The extraction sequence

As water contacts coffee grounds, compounds dissolve in a predictable sequence based on their solubility and molecular weight:

  1. Acids and fruity esters (fast) — highly soluble organic acids (acetic, citric, malic) and fruity ester compounds extract first. Under-extracted coffee (<18% extraction yield) tastes predominantly sour and sharp because these compounds dominate without the subsequent balancing compounds.
  2. Sugars and Maillard sweetness compounds (medium) — sucrose residues and sweet Maillard intermediates extract in the middle of the extraction. These provide body, sweetness, and the complex aroma of properly extracted coffee.
  3. Bitter compounds (slow) — phenolics from chlorogenic acid degradation, heavier Maillard products, and some caffeine compounds extract last. At >22–24% extraction yield, these dominate and produce the astringent, hollow bitterness of over-extracted coffee.

Extraction variables

Four variables determine extraction yield and therefore flavour:

  • Grind size — the most controllable variable. Finer grind = more surface area = faster extraction of everything. Grind too fine: over-extracted bitterness even at short brew times. Too coarse: under-extracted sourness even at long brew times. Each brewing method has an optimal grind range.
  • Temperature — higher temperature increases solubility and diffusion rates of all compounds, including bitter ones. Most filter brewing targets 90–96°C; temperatures above 96°C extract harsh phenolics faster than desirable compounds. Cold brew at 4–20°C achieves 16–18% extraction over 12–24 hours — the low temperature kinetics shift extraction toward acids and sweetness, producing a less bitter, lower-acid-perceived cup despite actually having similar total acid levels.
  • Brew ratio — g of coffee to mL of water. More coffee per water → higher TDS (concentration) but not necessarily higher extraction yield. Specialty coffee targets 1:15–1:17 for filter.
  • Brew time / contact time — longer contact at the same grind/temperature = more extraction. Espresso (~25–30 seconds) reaches 20–22% extraction at fine grind and 9 bar pressure. French press at 4 minutes reaches similar extraction at much coarser grind.

Espresso physics

Espresso is distinguished from other brewing methods not primarily by roast level but by the 9-bar pressure used to force hot water through a compact puck of finely ground coffee. The pressure achieves several things:

  • Emulsification of coffee oils — CO₂ and lipids are forced into colloidal suspension, forming crema (a CO₂-stabilised oil-in-water emulsion). Crema is not primarily flavour — it is texture and CO₂; the actual aroma of espresso comes primarily from the liquid beneath.
  • Higher TDS (~8–12% dissolved solids vs. ~1.2–1.5% for filter). Espresso is a concentrate, not a different kind of coffee.
  • Faster extraction than immersion or filter — 25–30 seconds vs. minutes. This requires a very fine, very uniform grind; any channelling (water finding a path of least resistance through the puck) produces both over-extracted and under-extracted zones simultaneously.
Tea: The Oxidation Spectrum

All non-herbal teas come from the same plant, Camellia sinensis, in two main varieties: var. sinensis (smaller leaf, grows in China and Japan) and var. assamica (larger leaf, more catechin-rich, grows in Assam and Africa). The staggering diversity of tea types — from pale Japanese gyokuro to dark Yunnan pu-erh — comes almost entirely from differences in how the leaf is processed after picking, specifically the degree of polyphenol oxidation.

Catechins: the starting material

Tea leaves contain 12–25% dry-weight catechins — a family of flavonoid polyphenols. The four major catechins are EGCG (epigallocatechin gallate, the most abundant), EGC, ECG, and EC. Catechins are colourless to pale yellow and contribute astringency by binding to and precipitating salivary proteins. They are also potent antioxidants and are responsible for most of tea's putative health effects.

In the freshly picked leaf, catechins are stored in vacuoles and polyphenol oxidase (PPO) is in the chloroplast — the same separation as in Lesson 9's enzymatic browning story. Cell damage during processing mixes them.

The oxidation cascade

When PPO contacts catechins in the presence of O₂, the oxidation cascade begins:

Catechins + PPO + O₂ → Theaflavins (orange-red, bright, brisk)
Theaflavins + more catechins → Thearubigins (dark brown, malty, full)
Green tea: PPO inactivated before oxidation → catechins preserved
Black tea: full oxidation → most catechins converted

Theaflavins (TF1, TF2a, TF2b, TF3) give Assam and Darjeeling black teas their bright, orange-red liquor colour and their characteristic "briskness" — the clean, astringent quality that makes black tea feel lively. Theaflavins are good antioxidants, more potent per molecule than the original catechins.

Thearubigins — a heterogeneous group of high-molecular-weight polymers — develop from theaflavin oxidation. They give black tea its dark reddish-brown colour and the heavy, malty body. A well-made Assam has both: theaflavins for brightness, thearubigins for depth. Poor processing (over-oxidation, high temperature) produces a flat, astringent black tea dominated by thearubigins with little brightness.

Green tea aroma and L-theanine

Japanese green teas (steamed) taste markedly different from Chinese green teas (pan-fired). The difference is the kill-green method:

  • Steam (Japanese — sencha, gyokuro): Very rapid PPO inactivation. Preserves more chlorophyll (vivid green liquor), more EGCG, and more LOX-pathway C6 volatiles (hexanal, (Z)-3-hexenol) — responsible for the fresh, grassy, marine character of sencha. DMS (dimethyl sulfide) from S-methylmethionine in the leaf contributes the characteristic seaweed note of high-grade Japanese teas.
  • Pan-fire (Chinese — Longjing, Bi Luo Chun): Maillard reactions occur on the hot pan surface, adding roasted, chestnut-like notes. The toasting also converts some LOX volatiles, reducing grassiness. This is why Longjing tastes toasty where sencha tastes grassy.

L-theanine is an amino acid nearly unique to tea (also found in trace amounts in mushrooms). It contributes:

  • A mild umami/broth taste quality — distinct from glutamate but similar in mouthfeel. High-grade gyokuro (grown under shade to increase L-theanine) has a pronounced savoury depth absent from ordinary green tea.
  • Pharmacological interaction with caffeine — L-theanine modulates caffeine's stimulant effect, producing what practitioners describe as "calm focus" rather than the jittery urgency of coffee. L-theanine increases alpha brain wave activity while caffeine increases beta activity; the combination produces a distinctly different alertness state.
  • Formation of Maillard-like compounds during roasting of hojicha (pan-roasted green tea), contributing caramel character.

Brewing temperature and catechin extraction

Tea is highly sensitive to brewing temperature — particularly green teas. The key tradeoff: catechins (and therefore bitterness and astringency) extract faster at higher temperatures; delicate volatile aromatics (linalool, DMS, C6 volatiles) are destroyed by boiling water.

Optimal brewing temperatures:
Gyokuro / high-grade Japanese green: 55–65°C (preserves sweet/umami, minimises bitterness)
Sencha / standard green tea: 70–80°C
Oolong (light): 80–85°C
Oolong (dark/roasted): 90–95°C
Black tea: 95–100°C (robust theaflavins/thearubigins tolerate full boiling point)
Pu-erh: 95–100°C (aged polyphenols also heat-tolerant)

The traditional instruction to never use boiling water for green tea is chemically justified: at 100°C, EGCG and other catechins extract so rapidly that within 2 minutes the cup is dominated by bitter astringency. The same tea brewed at 70°C for 2 minutes is delicate and sweet. The temperature is not about "scalding" the leaves (a myth) — it is about extraction kinetics of bitter vs. pleasant compounds.

Caffeine–catechin complexes and tea cream: Caffeine and EGCG form non-covalent complexes in solution. At low temperatures, these complexes precipitate, forming a milky turbidity in the cup — "tea cream." This is especially visible in cold-brewed black tea and explains why cold black tea looks cloudy. Adding milk before the tea (heated) denatures milk proteins which can encapsulate catechins, slightly altering the astringency profile. The "milk first or milk after" debate is chemically real but the effect size is small.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Diagnosing under-extracted vs. over-extracted coffee
A filter coffee tastes unpleasantly sour with a thin, watery body. You adjust the same parameters next time and the coffee becomes bitter and hollow with an unpleasant dryness at the back of the throat. What went wrong each time, and what single-variable change would move each cup toward the ideal?

Cup 1 — sour, thin, watery: under-extracted. Organic acids (acetic, citric, malic) and a small amount of fruity esters extracted but the balancing sugars and Maillard sweetness compounds did not. Extraction yield was below 18%. Causes: grind too coarse (insufficient surface area), water too cool (low extraction kinetics), or brew time too short.

Single-variable fix: grind finer. This is the most reliable and largest-effect variable. Finer grind increases surface area, which speeds up extraction of all compounds. The acids and sweetness compounds will now both extract sufficiently within the same brew time. Do not increase brew time first — it risks channelling (uneven extraction) in a coarse grind.

Cup 2 — bitter, hollow, dry: over-extracted. Extraction yield exceeded ~23–24%. All the desirable acids and sweetness compounds extracted fully, then continued extraction pulled the slow-dissolving bitter phenolics (from chlorogenic acid degradation), harsh tannins, and heavy Maillard polymers. "Hollow" indicates the sweetness compounds were already fully extracted and diluted by the additional bitter water volume.

Single-variable fix: grind coarser. The coarser grind slows extraction at the same brew time, leaving the bitter fraction incompletely extracted while still achieving full extraction of acids and sweetness compounds. Alternatively: reduce brew time — but this requires a corresponding grind adjustment to maintain extraction yield, making it a two-variable change.

Example 2 — Why sencha brewed at 100°C tastes awful
A visitor brews a high-grade Japanese sencha by pouring freshly boiled water over 5g of leaves and steeping for 2 minutes. The result is harshly bitter and astringent. The same tea brewed at 75°C for 2 minutes by a more experienced person is sweet, grassy, and umami-rich. Explain the chemistry of this difference.

The central issue is the temperature-dependent extraction kinetics of catechins (primarily EGCG) vs. the desirable sweet/umami compounds (L-theanine, free amino acids, sugars).

At 100°C: EGCG and other catechins extract very rapidly. Within 30–45 seconds, catechin concentration in the cup is approaching maximum. By 2 minutes, essentially all extractable EGCG is in the cup. EGCG is the dominant contributor to astringency: it precipitates salivary proline-rich proteins, creating the characteristic drying, puckering sensation. L-theanine and amino acids also extract quickly at this temperature, but their concentration is overwhelmed by the catechin flood.

At 75°C: Catechin extraction is dramatically slower — maybe 40–50% of the 100°C rate at equivalent time. In 2 minutes, a much smaller fraction of the EGCG has extracted. L-theanine and free amino acids, being smaller and more soluble, extract at proportionally higher yields relative to catechins at this temperature. The resulting cup is richer in the sweet/umami fraction and lower in astringency — the intended flavour profile.

The volatile dimension: At 100°C, the LOX-pathway C6 volatiles (hexanal, (Z)-3-hexenol) that give sencha its fresh grassy/marine character immediately evaporate out of the cup. At 75°C, they are retained long enough to be perceived retronasally during drinking. This is an additional reason why high-grade green teas are sensitive to temperature beyond just catechin kinetics.

Example 3 — Light vs. dark roast: caffeine content and acid perception
Two popular coffee myths: (1) dark roast coffee has more caffeine than light roast; (2) dark roast coffee is more acidic. Evaluate both claims using roasting chemistry.

Claim 1 — Dark roast has more caffeine: False by weight, varies by volume.

Caffeine is heat-stable and survives roasting essentially unchanged in absolute quantity per bean. However, roasting causes the bean to expand and lose mass (water + CO₂). A dark roast bean is less dense than a light roast bean from the same origin. When comparing equal weights: dark roast has slightly more caffeine per gram (because water/CO₂ mass was lost without taking caffeine with it). When comparing equal volumes by scoop: dark roast has less caffeine per scoop (fewer, lighter beans fit in a spoon). The difference is small (~10–15%) and often swamped by batch variation. Neither direction of the claim is strongly "more caffeine" in any practical sense.

Claim 2 — Dark roast is more acidic: False. Light roast is more acidic.

Perceived acidity depends primarily on the organic acid content. Green beans contain chlorogenic acids (~6%), citric acid, malic acid, and phosphoric acid. Light roasting degrades chlorogenic acids to some extent but preserves more of the citric, malic, and phosphoric acids. These are the acids primarily responsible for coffee's perceived brightness and acidity. Dark roasting continues to degrade all of these; citric acid is largely destroyed at second-crack temperatures. Dark roast coffee has lower total organic acid content than light roast and is perceived as less bright and acidic. The paradox is that the "harsh" sensation associated with dark roast is from bitter phenolics, not from acid — and some people conflate "harsh" with "acidic."

Food for Thought
  • Cold brew coffee is described as "less acidic" than hot-brewed coffee, but analytical measurements show total acid levels are similar or even slightly higher in cold brew. How can it taste less acidic if the acid content is the same?
    The perception of acidity in coffee is not purely a function of total organic acid concentration. Several factors contribute: (1) Certain organic acids are perceived as harsher at higher temperatures (quinic acid, which forms from chlorogenic acid degradation, is particularly temperature-sensitive in extraction). Cold brew extracts a different acid profile — more malic, less quinic acid — which is perceived as milder even at equivalent total acid levels. (2) The ratio of bitter compounds to acids matters: cold brew extracts proportionally more acids and sweetness compounds relative to bitter phenolics because bitter compounds have lower solubility at low temperatures. The overall flavour balance is sweeter and rounder, which reduces the perceived prominence of the acids even if absolute acid levels are comparable. (3) Temperature of consumption: cold brew is served cold, and cold temperature reduces volatile aroma, which contributes to perception of acidity (volatile acids are part of the acidic "brightness" sensation at warm temperatures).
  • Gyokuro — the highest grade of Japanese green tea — is grown under 90% shade for three to four weeks before harvest. The shading stresses the plant and dramatically increases L-theanine content. Explain why shade would increase L-theanine, using what you know about amino acid metabolism in plants.
    L-theanine is synthesised in tea roots from ethylamine and glutamate, then transported to the leaves. In full sunlight, L-theanine in the leaves is converted to catechins via the phenylpropanoid pathway — the plant uses it as a nitrogen source for polyphenol biosynthesis, which serves as UV protection and pest deterrence. In shade, UV exposure is reduced; the plant has less need for UV-protective catechins, so less L-theanine is converted. Simultaneously, reduced photosynthesis slows the energy-requiring catechin synthesis further. L-theanine therefore accumulates rather than being consumed. The result: gyokuro has ~3–4× the L-theanine of ordinary sencha grown in full sun, producing the pronounced savoury, umami-rich sweetness that defines the tea style. This is also why gyokuro has lower catechin content and lower astringency than regular sencha.
  • A café offers to serve you their "fresh single-origin coffee, roasted this morning." You hesitate. Should you wait, and if so, how long? What specifically goes wrong if you brew it today?
    Yes, you should wait — ideally 5–10 days for espresso, 3–7 days for filter brewing. A bean roasted today contains enormous quantities of CO₂ trapped under pressure in its cell structure. For espresso: the outgassing CO₂ forms excessive, unstable crema and causes channelling in the puck (CO₂ in the grounds creates uneven resistance, and high-pressure water finds paths through rather than through the entire puck uniformly). The result is an uneven extraction — some pathways over-extracted, most under-extracted. For filter brewing: the bloom step will be extremely active, producing foam that impedes even wetting of the coffee bed; extraction will be uneven and typically under-extracted even if the bloom step is performed. Beyond extraction mechanics, some roasting-derived volatile precursors continue transforming in the days after roasting — particularly the compounds that form from Maillard intermediates reacting with each other in the cool, CO₂-rich bean interior. Most coffees reach their flavour peak 7–14 days after roast.
Common Pitfalls
  1. Blaming roast level for bitterness. Bitterness in coffee is primarily an extraction problem — over-extraction at any roast level produces bitter cups. A light roast over-extracted will be more bitter than a dark roast properly extracted. Fix grind and technique before switching roast levels.
  2. Using boiling water for green tea. The result is not "scalded leaves" (a metaphor that explains nothing) — it is rapid extraction of harsh catechins that overwhelm all other compounds. Green tea requires 65–80°C. The technique of pouring boiling water into the cup first, then adding water from the kettle, quickly achieves 75–80°C.
  3. Assuming all espresso is dark roast. Espresso is a brewing method, not a roast level. Medium-light roast coffees can and are used for espresso in specialty settings; the high TDS of espresso extraction makes origin character more detectable, not less. The "espresso = dark roast" association comes from historical commercial practice, not chemistry.
  4. Storing coffee in the freezer and thawing repeatedly. Freezing whole beans is actually effective for long-term storage if done correctly — frozen beans should be portioned before freezing and brought to room temperature in the sealed bag before opening (condensation on cold beans absorbs moisture and accelerates staling). Repeatedly opening and re-closing a freezer bag causes moisture cycling that degrades coffee quickly.
  5. Treating steeped tea and brewed tea as simply stronger or weaker versions of each other. Brew time and temperature interact non-linearly: a 5-minute steep at 70°C extracts a different compound profile than a 2-minute steep at 85°C, even if the final catechin concentration is similar. Shorter time at higher temperature extracts more volatile aromatics before they evaporate; longer time at lower temperature extracts a higher proportion of sweet/amino acid fractions. Neither is universally better — they produce different cups.
