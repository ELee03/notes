Food Science

Applied Chemistry · Biochemistry · Thermodynamics · Sensory Science

Cooking is applied science. Every time you sear a steak, bake bread, or make a sauce, you are running chemical reactions — controlling temperature, pH, water activity, and protein structure with the goal of producing a specific result. Most cooks do this by feel, by rule of thumb, by tradition. Food science does it by understanding the underlying mechanisms.

This cluster covers the full scope of McGee's On Food and Cooking — one of the rare books that is simultaneously readable and scientifically rigorous. That means not just the chemistry of cooking (browning, emulsification, fermentation) but the biology of individual foods: meat, fish, vegetables, fruits, beverages, chocolate, and the full toolkit of hydrocolloids and gelling agents.
