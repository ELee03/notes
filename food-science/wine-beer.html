Wine & Beer

Fermentation · Phenolic Chemistry · Hop Biochemistry · Varietal Aromas

Wine and beer are the most chemically sophisticated fermented beverages in regular human consumption — and the chemistry behind them goes far deeper than "yeast eats sugar and makes alcohol." Wine involves the complete transformation of grape biochemistry through alcoholic fermentation, optional secondary malolactic fermentation, and years of oxidative and reductive ageing reactions. Beer involves controlled starch hydrolysis, precise enzymatic mashing, a Maillard-heavy boil, and the extraction of hop chemistry under tightly controlled conditions. Understanding the mechanisms explains why the same grape variety tastes different from different climates, why butter notes in some Chardonnays come from bacteria, and why the same hops added at different points in the boil produce completely different flavour contributions.

Wine: Grape Composition

Wine's starting material is grape juice — a relatively simple solution of sugars (glucose and fructose, ~15–25% by weight at harvest), organic acids, phenolics, proteins, minerals, and aroma precursors. The vineyard determines the composition; the winery transforms it.

Sugars: Glucose and fructose in roughly equal ratio. Yeast metabolises both, but preferentially consumes glucose first at high concentrations. The Brix scale (° Bx) measures sugar concentration: 1° Bx ≈ 10 g/L sugar ≈ ~0.55% potential alcohol. Grapes at ~24 Brix produce ~13.2% alcohol.

Organic acids: Tartaric acid is unique to grapes — it is not metabolised by yeast or most bacteria, so it persists in the finished wine. Its potassium salt (potassium bitartrate) can crystallise at low temperatures, depositing harmless "wine diamonds" on the cork or bottom of the bottle. Malic acid gives wine an apple-like sharpness and is the substrate for malolactic fermentation. Citric acid contributes fresh notes.

Phenolics: Divided into flavonoids (flavonols, flavanols/tannins, anthocyanins) in skins and seeds, and non-flavonoids (hydroxycinnamates, stilbenes including resveratrol) distributed throughout the berry. Skins contain the colour (anthocyanins in red varieties) and much of the tannin; seeds contain highly astringent, harsh seed tannins. White wine fermentation avoids skin contact; red wine fermentation requires it.

Alcoholic Fermentation in Wine

The core reaction is already covered in Lesson 5: Fermentation: Saccharomyces cerevisiae converts glucose (and fructose) to ethanol and CO₂ under anaerobic conditions. Wine fermentation adds critical specifics:

Yeast and ester production

Beyond alcohol, yeast produces dozens of secondary metabolites that define wine character. Two major classes:

  • Esters — formed by the enzyme alcohol acetyltransferase (ATF1) coupling acetyl-CoA with alcohols. Ethyl acetate (solvent/nail-varnish at high levels), isoamyl acetate (banana), ethyl hexanoate (apple). Ester production is higher at warmer fermentation temperatures and under nitrogen limitation.
  • Higher alcohols (fusel alcohols) — from the Ehrlich pathway: yeast deaminates amino acids from the must, producing iso-amyl alcohol (isoamyl acetate precursor), 1-propanol, 2-methyl-1-butanol. At moderate levels they add complexity; at high levels (fusel oil overload from high-temp fermentation) they are harsh and hot on the palate.

Temperature matters enormously. White and rosé wines fermented cold (10–15°C) retain more delicate volatile esters and fruity terpenes; warm fermentation (25–30°C) produces more body, richer flavour, but risks losing fresh aromatics. Red wine fermentation is typically warmer (25–30°C) to assist colour and tannin extraction from skins.

Thiol precursors and reductive winemaking

Sauvignon Blanc and some other varieties contain aroma precursors — cysteine- and glutathione-conjugated thiols bound in the grape flesh. These are odourless in the grape. During fermentation, yeast lyase enzymes cleave the conjugate, releasing free volatile thiols:

  • 3-mercaptohexanol (3-MH): passion fruit, grapefruit (threshold ~60 ppt)
  • 3-mercaptohexyl acetate (3-MHA): more intense boxwood, passion fruit (threshold ~4 ppt)
  • 4-mercapto-4-methylpentan-2-one (4-MMP): boxwood, blackcurrant bud, tomato leaf (threshold ~3 ppt — one of the most potent wine aroma compounds known)

Thiols are extremely oxygen-sensitive — they oxidise to odourless disulfides on contact with air. "Reductive winemaking" (limiting oxygen exposure through inert-gas blanketing, early SO₂ addition, sealed tanks) preserves thiol character. Oxidative handling destroys it.

Malolactic Fermentation

After alcoholic fermentation completes, winemakers often allow a second fermentation conducted by lactic acid bacteria — primarily Oenococcus oeni. This is not actually a fermentation in the sugar → alcohol sense; it is a decarboxylation:

L-Malic acid → L-Lactic acid + CO₂
(catalysed by malate decarboxylase / malolactic enzyme)

The consequences are significant:

  • Softened acidity: Malic acid (pKa 3.46) is stronger than lactic acid (pKa 3.86). Converting malic to lactic at typical wine volumes reduces titratable acidity by 1–3 g/L and raises pH by 0.1–0.3 units. The wine tastes softer and rounder.
  • Diacetyl production: O. oeni produces diacetyl (buttery aroma) as a by-product of citrate metabolism. At high levels, diacetyl is a defect; at low to moderate levels in a full-bodied white (Chardonnay, Viognier), it contributes a "creamy" richness. Diacetyl concentration depends on SO₂ levels, oxygen exposure, and timing of SO₂ addition after MLF.
  • Textural complexity: MLF also contributes to mouthfeel through production of polysaccharides by O. oeni, which can increase viscosity slightly.

MLF is nearly universal in red wines (it also increases microbial stability) and standard in many full-bodied white wines (California Chardonnay). It is blocked in wines where freshness and high acidity are desirable: Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Pinot Grigio in crisp styles.

Tannins, Colour, and Phenolic Chemistry

Tannins

Tannins are polyphenols capable of precipitating proteins — this is their defining functional property. In wine, they precipitate salivary proline-rich proteins, creating the drying, puckering sensation called astringency. Tannin quality (not just quantity) matters: seed tannins are harsh and unripe-tasting; skin tannins are softer; oak tannins (ellagitannins) are different in structure and integrate into wine character over years of barrel ageing.

During ageing, tannin molecules undergo polymerisation reactions — larger polymers precipitate as sediment (why old wines throw a deposit), while remaining polymers become softer in astringency. This is why a tannic young red wine softens with age. Oak barrel ageing also introduces oxygen (via the porous wood stave) which catalyses these polymerisation reactions.

Anthocyanins and colour

Red wine colour comes from anthocyanins extracted during maceration. As in Lesson 9's red cabbage chemistry, anthocyanins are pH-sensitive: at wine pH (~3.3–3.8), they are primarily in the flavylium cation form (red), but a significant fraction is in the colourless carbinol form even at typical wine pH. This is why pH affects colour intensity: a lower-pH wine looks more deeply coloured.

Over time, monomeric anthocyanins react with tannins (and other compounds) to form anthocyanin-tannin condensates (copigmentation complexes and covalent adducts), then pyranoanthocyanins — more stable, pH-independent forms that resist the colourless carbinol equilibrium. This is why aged red wines retain vibrant colour even as free anthocyanins disappear.

Varietal Aroma Chemistry

Different grape varieties produce different characteristic aromas not because they have different yeast or different winemaking, but because the grapes themselves contain different profiles of aroma compounds and precursors.

Variety Key compound(s) Aroma descriptor Notes
Sauvignon Blanc 3-MH, 3-MHA, 4-MMP Passion fruit, grapefruit, boxwood Released from cysteine-conjugated precursors by yeast
Sauvignon Blanc (unripe) Isobutyl methoxypyrazine Bell pepper, grass, green Degrades with sun exposure; too much = fault
Riesling Geraniol, linalool, TDN Floral; aged = "petrol" TDN from carotenoid degradation during bottle ageing
Muscat / Gewürz. Linalool, geraniol Rose, lychee, orange blossom Monoterpenes at directly detectable concentrations in grape
Syrah / Shiraz Rotundone Black pepper, spice 25% of population anosmic to rotundone
Pinot Noir β-ionone, α-ionone, ethyl esters Red berry, violet, silky Ionones from carotenoid degradation; low tannin

Rotundone deserves special mention. It is a sesquiterpene ketone present in the skin of Syrah grapes (and also in black pepper — explaining the classic descriptor). Its detection threshold in wine is about 16 ppb. Remarkably, approximately 25% of the human population is specifically anosmic to rotundone — they cannot smell it at any concentration — while the other 75% can detect it clearly. This means that roughly a quarter of all Syrah drinkers are physiologically unable to detect one of that variety's most characteristic aromas.

Wine Faults

Several common wine faults connect back to chemistry covered in earlier lessons:

  • Cork taint (TCA) — 2,4,6-trichloroanisole from chlorophenol methylation by moulds in the cork. Threshold ~1.4–5 ppb; suppresses other aromas (see Lesson 6: Flavour Science).
  • Brettanomyces (Brett) — the yeast Brettanomyces bruxellensis converts hydroxycinnamic acids (coumaric, ferulic acids from grape skin) to 4-ethylphenol ("barnyard," "horse sweat") and 4-ethylguaiacol ("smoky," "spicy"). The flavour threshold is ~400 ppb for 4-EP in red wine. At low levels some consider it complexity; above threshold it is a fault.
  • Volatile acidity (VA) — acetic acid produced by Acetobacter in the presence of oxygen. Legal limit ~0.9–1.2 g/L; above ~1.5 g/L it is a prominent vinegar defect. At sub-threshold levels, some acetic acid contributes to complexity.
  • Reduction (sulphur faults) — H₂S (rotten egg) from yeast under nitrogen or pantothenic acid deficiency; methyl mercaptan (vegetal, rubbery) from methionine degradation. Both dissipate with aeration; winemakers use copper finings to precipitate them as insoluble copper sulfides.
  • Mousiness — 2-acetyltetrahydropyridine (ATHP) from Lactobacillus or Brett under high pH conditions. Unique mechanism: ATHP is protonated in the mouth after swallowing, releasing the volatile form retronasally. Tasters perceive it only as an aftertaste, often described as "mouse cage" or "crackers." Not detectable by sniffing the wine because the inactive form doesn't evaporate below wine pH.
Beer: From Grain to Glass

Beer production requires converting insoluble starch in barley into fermentable sugars — a step wine does not need because grapes start as sugar. This is the fundamental challenge of brewing and the reason malting and mashing exist.

Malting

Raw barley grain is inert — its starch is locked in tight granules surrounded by protein matrix and protected by the cell wall. Malting solves this:

  1. Steeping — barley is soaked in water to ~45% moisture, triggering germination. The embryo releases gibberellin hormones.
  2. Germination — gibberellins activate amylase and protease genes in the aleurone layer. α-amylase and β-amylase are synthesised; proteases break down the protein matrix surrounding starch granules ("modification"). Cell walls are broken down by β-glucanases.
  3. Kilning — heat dries the malt to ~4–5% moisture, halting germination and fixing enzyme levels. Temperature determines character: pale malt (80°C, minimal Maillard), crystal malt (stewed wet then kilned at higher temp → caramelised sugars), roasted malt (200+°C, heavily Maillard, coffee/chocolate character).

Mashing

Crushed malt is mixed with hot water in a "mash tun." Temperature controls which enzymes dominate and therefore the beer's body and fermentability:

β-amylase (55–65°C optimum): Cleaves maltose units from non-reducing ends of starch chains → high fermentability → dry, crisp beer
α-amylase (65–72°C optimum): Random internal cleavage → dextrins that yeast cannot ferment → fuller body, more residual sweetness

Lower mash temperature (63–65°C): more β-amylase activity → more fermentable wort → drier beer (good for IPAs, session beers)
Higher mash temperature (68–72°C): more α-amylase contribution → more dextrins → fuller-bodied beer (good for stouts, porters, English bitters)

The mash is then lautered (filtered through the grain bed), producing clear sweet liquid called wort, which is rinsed with hot water (sparging) to extract remaining sugars.

Boiling and hop chemistry

Wort is boiled for 60–90 minutes. Several things happen simultaneously: sterilisation, protein coagulation (hot break), evaporation of volatile off-compounds (particularly DMS — see below), concentration to target gravity, and the critical transformation of hops.

Bitterness — iso-alpha acids: Hops contain alpha acids (humulone, cohumulone, adhumulone) in lupulin glands. These are water-insoluble and non-bitter in their native form. At boiling temperature, they undergo isomerisation to iso-alpha acids — soluble, stable, and intensely bitter (threshold ~5 ppm; beer contains 15–100+ ppm). Isomerisation efficiency is only ~25–35% of total alpha acids added; longer boil time and lower wort gravity improve efficiency.

Timing is everything for hops: iso-alpha acid formation requires sustained high temperature; aroma volatiles evaporate quickly. Adding hops early in the boil (60 minutes) maximises bitterness; adding late (5–0 minutes, "flameout") extracts aroma terpenes with minimal isomerisation. Dry hopping (adding hops after fermentation, at ~10–20°C) extracts terpenes directly into finished beer with no isomerisation and no thermal loss — the basis of American IPA aroma.

Hop Addition Timing — Bitterness vs. Aroma

Relative bitterness (IBU contribution, from iso-alpha acid isomerisation) and aroma contribution (terpene retention) as a function of when hops are added before the end of the boil. Early additions maximise bitterness; late additions maximise aroma. Dry hopping operates outside this chart — at fermentation temperatures, zero isomerisation but near-complete terpene extraction.

DMS and the boil

Dimethyl sulfide (DMS) — a cooked corn/vegetable off-flavour — is a major quality concern in lager brewing. It forms from S-methylmethionine (SMM), a compound present in malt, which converts to DMS thermally. SMM hydrolysis continues even after the boil during the "hot standby" period before chilling. A vigorous boil with an open kettle (uncovered) continuously strips DMS by steam stripping. A covered or very gentle boil allows DMS to accumulate. Rapid chilling after the boil is equally important — slow cooling through the 60–100°C range continues SMM conversion without DMS removal.

Fermentation: ale vs. lager

The two primary yeast species produce fundamentally different flavour profiles:

  • Saccharomyces cerevisiae (ale yeast): Fermenting at 18–22°C, it produces abundant esters (isoamyl acetate — banana, ethyl acetate, ethyl hexanoate — apple/anise) and fusel alcohols. English ale strains produce more diacetyl; Belgian strains produce dramatic ester and phenolic (4-vinylguaiacol, "clove") profiles. Top-fermenting — floats to the top during active fermentation.
  • Saccharomyces pastorianus (lager yeast): A hybrid of S. cerevisiae and S. eubayanus, adapted to cold fermentation at 7–12°C. Produces very low ester and fusel levels — the clean, neutral profile of pilsners and lagers. Bottom-fermenting — settles to tank bottom.

Diacetyl and conditioning: Yeast produces diacetyl during fermentation as a by-product of the valine biosynthesis pathway. Under normal conditions, yeast reabsorbs and reduces it to acetoin (odourless) during conditioning (lagering). If the yeast is removed too early by chilling or filtration, diacetyl remains in the beer — a buttery defect. Quality lager production includes a "diacetyl rest" (briefly raising temperature to 16–18°C at the end of fermentation) to encourage yeast to clean up residual diacetyl.

Hop variety chemistry

The 1990s–2010s craft beer revolution was driven largely by the discovery and development of highly aromatic hop varieties. The key terpene differences:

  • Traditional European hops (Saaz, Hallertau): High in humulene and farnesene (noble, herbal); low myrcene; used in pilsners for subtle spicy/floral character.
  • American hops (Cascade, Centennial, Citra, Mosaic): High in myrcene, linalool, and geraniol; limonene and citrus terpenes. Responsible for the tropical/citrus IPA aroma.
  • New World hops (Nelson Sauvin, Motueka): Exceptionally high in thiol precursors — the same 3-MH/3-MHA precursors as Sauvignon Blanc grapes. Active yeast during dry hopping (biotransformation) cleaves these to free volatile thiols, producing "white wine," passion fruit character.
Worked Examples
Example 1 — MLF in Chardonnay: why the "buttery" note
A California Chardonnay undergoes full malolactic fermentation. A Chablis (French white Burgundy) from the same vintage blocks MLF entirely. Both wines were made with essentially the same grapes and winemaking technique otherwise. Explain the chemical differences between the two wines and predict how each will taste in terms of acidity, texture, and aroma.

California MLF (full): Essentially all malic acid has been converted to lactic acid by Oenococcus oeni. The wine now has approximately 1–3 g/L lower titratable acidity and a pH 0.1–0.3 higher than before MLF. The softer acid profile makes the wine feel rounder and less sharp. During MLF, O. oeni metabolised citric acid (residual in the wine) and produced diacetyl as a by-product. At the typical levels in a carefully managed MLF (~0.5–1.5 mg/L), diacetyl contributes a creamy, buttery note. The winemaker likely also used oak barrels — ellagitannins from oak plus the MLF diacetyl creates the classic California Chardonnay profile: soft, creamy, rich, slightly buttery, lower perceived acidity, vanilla/toasty oak overlay.

Chablis (MLF blocked): Typically blocked by early SO₂ addition, cooling, or sterile filtration before MLF bacteria can establish. All malic acid remains intact. The wine is notably more tart and bright — higher titratable acidity, lower pH, more crisp apple-like freshness (malic acid's character). No diacetyl production. Chablis also typically sees little to no new oak. The result: lean, mineral, high-acid, crisp, no butter note. The same grape, processed differently, produces a wine that seems to be a completely different style.

Example 2 — Hop addition timing and IPA design
A brewer is designing an IPA targeting 65 IBUs (International Bitterness Units) and very high aromatic impact — tropical and citrus fruit notes — with minimal "dank" resin character. Using the chemistry of iso-alpha acid formation and terpene retention, design a hop addition schedule with three additions and explain the role of each.

The key tension is that bitterness requires sustained high temperature for isomerisation (α-acid → iso-α-acid), but that same high temperature rapidly volatilises the terpenes responsible for aroma. Solution: separate the bitterness additions (early in the boil) from the aroma additions (late or post-fermentation).

Addition 1 — 60 minutes (bittering): A bittering-efficient high-alpha acid hop (e.g. Magnum, Warrior). Alpha acid isomerisation at 30–35% efficiency over 60 minutes. This addition provides the IBU target (~55 IBUs from this addition). Very little aroma contribution — volatile terpenes fully evaporated. Choose a neutral bittering variety to avoid adding unwanted flavour.

Addition 2 — 10 minutes (late addition): Citra or Mosaic hops. ~10–20% isomerisation efficiency gives ~10 more IBUs. But crucially: 50% or more of the terpene volatile oil survives a 10-minute boil. This addition contributes a burst of citrus/tropical terpenes (linalool, geraniol, myrcene) and initiates hop oil integration into the wort.

Addition 3 — dry hop after primary fermentation (aroma): Additional Citra/Mosaic at room temperature. Zero iso-alpha acid formation (no boil), zero thermal terpene loss. Extracts full terpene complement directly into the beer. Active yeast biotransformation converts geraniol to floral citronellol and converts bound thiol precursors to free 3-MH — the tropical fruit aroma. This is where the "juicy" IPA character originates. The "dank" resin character the brewer wants to avoid comes from myrcene at high concentrations — use hop varieties with low myrcene ratio relative to linalool/ geraniol for cleaner tropical rather than resiny character.

Example 3 — Diagnosing a wine fault
A Pinot Noir smells intensely of Band-Aid and horse barn, with a plastic/medicinal quality. This is only noticeable on the finish, not on initial nosing. A second glass of a different Syrah from the same producer smells strongly of black pepper and grilled meat, but your dining companion — who is otherwise sensitive to aromas — reports smelling only fruit. Identify both faults/features and explain the mechanism of each.

Pinot Noir — Brettanomyces contamination: The Band-Aid and horse barn descriptors are diagnostic for 4-ethylphenol (4-EP) and 4-ethylguaiacol (4-EG), produced by the yeast Brettanomyces bruxellensis from hydroxycinnamic acid precursors (para-coumaric acid → 4-EP; ferulic acid → 4-EG). The "only noticeable on the finish" characteristic is interesting — it may suggest the wine is near the threshold, where retronasal perception (via swallowing) is more effective than orthonasal (nosing) at picking up the compound. Brett contamination typically comes from barrel cellar hygiene issues; Brettanomyces thrives in used barrels. Management: SO₂ additions, barrel sanitation, sterile filtration.

Syrah — rotundone anosmic in dining companion: The black pepper note in Syrah is specifically due to rotundone (a sesquiterpene ketone), not a fault. However, approximately 25% of the population is specifically anosmic to rotundone — their olfactory receptors do not respond to it at any concentration. Your companion is almost certainly in this group. They experience the same wine minus one of its defining aromas. This is not abnormal — rotundone- specific anosmia is one of the best-documented specific anosmias in wine research. The wine itself is not faulty; the companion's nose is simply tuned to a different range of compounds.

Food for Thought
  • Old World wine regions (Burgundy, Bordeaux, Champagne) are at latitudes where grapes barely ripen. New World regions (Napa, Barossa) are significantly warmer. From what you now know about fermentation and grape biochemistry, predict how grape chemistry differs between cool and warm climates — and what this means for alcohol levels, acidity, and primary fruit character.
    In cool climates, slow, extended ripening allows flavour compound development (including terpene synthesis) while organic acids are retained — malic acid degrades slowly in cool conditions. Grapes may not reach full sugar ripeness; Brix at harvest might be 19–21 → ~10.5–11.5% potential alcohol. The result: lower alcohol, higher acidity (including significant malic acid), more restrained and complex fruit character where fermentation by-products and vineyard character dominate over primary fruit. Thiols and aromatic terpenes developed slowly. In warm climates, rapid sugar accumulation often outpaces flavour development. Grapes can reach 26+ Brix before full flavour maturity → 14.5%+ alcohol. Malic acid degrades rapidly in heat (enzymatic process in the grape). The result: richer, lower-acid wine; riper, jammy fruit character; higher alcohol can mask delicate aromas. Hot climates often require acidification (tartaric acid addition) to restore balance. This explains why Burgundy Pinot Noir tastes like cherry/ earth/forest and California Pinot Noir tastes like strawberry jam with more body — same variety, dramatically different chemistry.
  • A home brewer makes a pale ale and it smells intensely of creamed corn and canned vegetables when cold. After warming to room temperature, the smell mostly disappears. Identify the compound, explain the production mechanism, and describe what the brewer should do differently next time.
    The compound is dimethyl sulfide (DMS), threshold ~30–50 ppb in beer. The creamed corn/cooked vegetable descriptor is highly characteristic. DMS forms primarily from S-methylmethionine (SMM), a compound in malt that degrades thermally to DMS. A vigorous open boil continuously strips DMS by steam; however, during the hot standby and chilling period after the boil (when the wort sits in the kettle, still hot, waiting to be chilled), SMM continues to convert to DMS without the volatilisation that an active boil provides. The faster the wort is chilled through 50–100°C, the less DMS accumulates. DMS is volatile (BP 37°C) — this explains the disappearance at room temperature: warming the beer liberates the DMS from solution and it evaporates. The brewer should: (1) ensure a vigorous, uncovered boil (not a gentle simmer or covered boil); (2) minimise hot standby time after flame-out; (3) chill the wort as rapidly as possible. An immersion chiller or counter-flow chiller that quickly drops the wort from boiling to yeast-pitching temperature significantly reduces DMS precursor conversion.
  • Red wine is often described as improving with age, while most white wine is described as best consumed young. But some white wines (aged white Burgundy, Riesling Spätlese) improve dramatically over 10–20 years. What chemical processes specifically make red wine age well, and what makes certain white wines age-worthy when most whites do not?
    Red wine ageing: tannin polymerisation and precipitation softens astringency over years; anthocyanin-tannin condensates and pyranoanthocyanins develop more stable colour; esters develop from slow acid-alcohol reactions (tertiary "bouquet" — leather, tobacco, forest floor, dried fruit notes); reductive conditions under cork allow reductant-driven thiol chemistry (secondary bottle bouquet). Tannins also act as antioxidants, protecting the wine from premature oxidation. The structure (tannin + acidity) effectively "scaffolds" the wine against collapse while these slow reactions proceed. White wine ageing: most whites lack tannins and phenolics to protect against oxidation — they flatten quickly. But high-acid whites have acidity as their structural backbone. Riesling Spätlese: high tartaric + residual sugar + high acidity creates a buffer system; TDN ("petrol") develops from carotenoid degradation over years; floral terpene precursors (geraniol glycosides) hydrolyse to release more geraniol. Aged white Burgundy: in oak-aged, MLF- complete Chardonnay, oxidative compounds integrate; diacetyl polymerises and fades; reductive aging develops more complex bottle bouquet. In both cases, acidity + some phenolic structure + appropriate closure (low oxygen transmission) are prerequisites — most thin, low-acid whites have none of this.
Common Pitfalls
  1. Confusing fermentation temperature effects in wine vs. beer. In beer, warm ale fermentation produces more esters (which is often desirable in ales). In wine, warm fermentation can also produce esters but often loses the delicate volatile thiols and primary fruit aromatics. The key difference is that beer has no thiol precursor system requiring reductive conditions — wine does. What is beneficial in ale fermentation (warm, slightly oxidative) is detrimental for aromatic white wine.
  2. Assuming higher IBUs = more bitter beer. IBUs measure iso-alpha acid concentration; perceived bitterness also depends on residual sweetness, body, pH, and thiol/terpene content that interact with bitter perception. A 60-IBU West Coast IPA may taste less bitter than a 40-IBU British Bitter because the IPA has hop oils that buffer bitterness perception and the British Bitter has higher residual dextrins and sweetness from a different mash temperature.
  3. Attributing all wine aroma to fermentation. Fermentation contributes esters and fusel alcohols, but many of wine's most distinctive aromas come from varietal precursors in the grape — thiols, terpenes, methoxypyrazines — that are either present in the grape directly or released from bound precursors by yeast enzymes. The yeast makes the alcohol; the grape makes the aroma.
  4. Treating diacetyl in beer and wine as the same compound. It is the same molecule, but the context differs. In wine, small amounts of diacetyl post-MLF are accepted and stylistically deliberate in some whites. In lager beer, any detectable diacetyl is a defect; even at 50 ppb it is noticeable and unwanted in the style. The sensory threshold in lager (~15–40 ppb) is lower than in wine (~100–200 ppb) partly because the rest of the flavour profile doesn't mask it.
  5. Thinking that tannins are only from skins. Grape seeds contain tannins (seed tannins) that are harsher and greener-tasting than skin tannins. Overlong maceration at high temperatures, excessive pump-overs, or vigorous crushing can extract harsh seed tannins. Oak adds ellagitannins, which are structurally different again. Winemakers manage each tannin source separately — "more extraction" is not better, it depends entirely on what is being extracted.
