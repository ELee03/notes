Fermentation

Yeast · Lactic Acid Bacteria · Bread · Cheese · Sourdough · Sauerkraut · Vinegar

Fermentation is the oldest food technology humans have, predating agriculture. Every culture independently discovered that leaving food under the right conditions produced something more complex, more preserved, and more interesting than the starting material. We now understand why: microorganisms metabolise the food, producing acids, alcohols, gases, and hundreds of flavour compounds. The science explains not just how fermentation works, but why sourdough develops complexity over 24 hours while commercial yeast bread is flat in 2, and why aged cheese tastes so different from fresh curd.

What fermentation is

Biochemically, fermentation refers specifically to anaerobic energy metabolism — the breakdown of organic compounds without oxygen, producing ATP (energy) and organic by-products rather than CO₂ and water. In food science, the term is used more broadly to mean any desirable microbial transformation of food, including some aerobic processes (e.g. vinegar production, mould-ripened cheese).

What fermentation does to food:

  • Preservation: lactic acid lowers pH to 3.5–4.5, alcohol reaches bactericidal concentrations, and the metabolic activity of beneficial microorganisms out-competes pathogens and spoilage organisms. This is why fermented foods have far longer shelf life than their raw ingredients.
  • Flavour development: microbial enzymes produce hundreds of secondary metabolites — organic acids, alcohols, esters, aldehydes, and sulfur compounds — that raw food lacks entirely. The flavour difference between fresh milk and aged Parmesan is entirely a function of microbial metabolic activity over months.
  • Texture change: microbial enzymes and acid production modify protein and carbohydrate structure (casein coagulation in cheese, gluten modification in sourdough, pectin softening in fermented vegetables).
  • Nutritional improvement: fermentation can improve digestibility (partial protein hydrolysis), synthesise vitamins (B12 in some fermented dairy), and reduce anti-nutritional factors (phytates in grains that inhibit mineral absorption are partially degraded by the acidic environment of sourdough fermentation).
Key organisms in food fermentation

Three major groups of microorganisms drive most food fermentation:

Yeasts — primarily Saccharomyces cerevisiae and related species. Facultative anaerobes (can work with or without oxygen, preferring anaerobic for fermentation). Ferment glucose and fructose primarily. Produce ethanol and CO₂. Central to: bread, beer, wine, and most alcohol fermentations. Wild yeast species (Kazachstania humilis, Wickerhamomyces anomalus) also contribute in sourdoughs.

Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) — a diverse family including Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc, Streptococcus thermophilus, Pediococcus, and others. Obligate fermenters: they only do anaerobic metabolism. Produce lactic acid (and sometimes acetic acid, CO₂, and ethanol depending on species). Central to: yogurt, cheese, sourdough, sauerkraut, kimchi, salami.

Moulds — primarily Penicillium species (P. camemberti for Brie/Camembert, P. roqueforti for blue cheese), Aspergillus oryzae (koji, used for soy sauce, miso, sake), and Rhizopus (tempeh). Moulds are aerobic — they grow on surfaces exposed to air. They produce powerful proteases and lipases that break down protein and fat during aging, generating the complex flavour of aged cheeses and fermented soy products.

Yeast fermentation — CO₂ and ethanol from sugar

The core reaction of yeast fermentation (anaerobic glycolysis):

Glucose → 2 ethanol + 2 CO₂ + 2 ATP

Each glucose molecule yields two molecules each of ethanol and carbon dioxide. The ATP (energy) is what the yeast is actually after — ethanol and CO₂ are waste products. In food fermentation, these waste products are the valuable outputs: CO₂ for bread leavening, ethanol for wine and beer, both for champagne and sourdough.

Factors controlling yeast activity:

  • Temperature: yeast activity increases with temperature up to ~35–38°C (optimal for S. cerevisiae), then drops sharply as enzymes denature. Cold slows fermentation (4°C fridge = slow but ongoing); above 50°C yeast dies. This is exploited in bread baking: the initial oven temperature range (up to ~60°C) allows a final burst of yeast activity before death — "oven spring."
  • Sugar concentration: at low concentrations, sugar is the limiting substrate. At very high concentrations (>~25–30% sugar), osmotic stress inhibits yeast — this is why some sweet doughs use osmotolerant yeast strains.
  • Salt: inhibits yeast at high concentrations. In bread dough, salt is added after initial mixing partly to avoid direct contact with yeast until distributed; salt concentrations above ~2% meaningfully slow fermentation.
  • pH: yeast prefers slightly acidic conditions (pH 4–6); extremely acidic or alkaline environments (pH <3 or >8) inhibit growth.
  • Alcohol: ethanol is toxic to yeast above ~12–15% — this is why wine and beer fermentation stops naturally around this concentration (and why spirits require distillation to reach higher alcohol percentages).

Lactic acid bacteria — the acid producers

LAB convert sugars (glucose, fructose, lactose, maltose) into organic acids. Two metabolic strategies:

  • Homofermentative LAB (e.g. Lactobacillus bulgaricus, many Lactococcus): convert glucose almost entirely to lactic acid. Produce no CO₂, no alcohol. Fast acidification. Used in yogurt and fresh cheese where quick, clean acidification is wanted.
  • Heterofermentative LAB (e.g. Leuconostoc mesenteroides, L. brevis, L. fermentum): produce lactic acid + acetic acid + CO₂ + small amounts of ethanol. Slower, more complex. Central to sourdough and lacto-fermented vegetables. Acetic acid (vinegar flavour) contributes "tang" and sharpness distinct from lactic acid's milder sourness.

Why acid matters beyond flavour: as LAB lower pH, they create conditions that are hostile to most pathogens (Listeria, Salmonella, E. coli are progressively inhibited below pH 5, killed below pH 4). LAB also produce bacteriocins — peptide antibiotics that inhibit competitor organisms. This is why properly acidified fermented foods are microbiologically safe despite not being heated. The acid is the preservative.

Bread fermentation — gas, structure, and flavour

Commercial bread yeast (S. cerevisiae) added to dough ferments primarily:

  • Simple sugars from the flour (glucose, fructose, sucrose from sucrase activity)
  • Maltose from amylase breakdown of starch (more important for a long fermentation)

The CO₂ produced is trapped in the gluten network (developed by kneading — see Lesson 2), causing dough to rise. The ethanol evaporates entirely during baking. Flavour from commercial yeast bread is primarily from yeast metabolism byproducts — small amounts of organic acids, esters, and aldehydes — modest compared to sourdough.

Cold fermentation (retarding): refrigerating shaped dough overnight slows yeast activity dramatically (~10× slower than at room temp) but allows enzymatic activity (proteases softening gluten, amylases producing more sugar substrate, yeast at low level continuing to produce flavour compounds) to proceed over a longer time. Cold-retarded dough produces noticeably more complex flavour — used by most artisan and high-end bakeries. The slow fermentation also allows better gluten relaxation and dough extensibility.

Oven spring: in the first 10–15 minutes of baking, yeast activity accelerates (from room temperature to ~38°C), CO₂ dissolved in the dough expands, and steam from the dough's water expands the gas cells — the bread "springs" upward by 30–50% before yeast dies at ~60°C. The gluten network and gelatinising starch set this expanded structure permanently.

Sourdough — a mixed culture ecosystem

A sourdough starter is a stable mixed culture of wild yeasts and LAB living in a flour-water matrix, maintained by regular feeding (refreshing with fresh flour and water). The two groups coexist because they occupy different ecological niches: yeasts prefer maltose (producing CO₂ for leavening); LAB prefer fructose and other simple sugars (producing acids that inhibit competing organisms but are tolerated by the acid-adapted yeasts).

Two acids, two flavours:

  • Lactic acid (produced by homofermentative LAB, favoured at higher temperatures and higher hydration): mild, creamy, yogurt-like sourness. Dominant in mild San Francisco sourdoughs and many French pain de campagne.
  • Acetic acid (produced by heterofermentative LAB, favoured at lower temperatures and lower hydration): sharp, vinegary, assertive tang. Dominant in more "punchy" sourdoughs — many San Francisco-style loaves, whole-grain breads that ferment at cooler temperatures.

The ratio of lactic to acetic acid is controlled by:

  • Hydration: stiffer doughs (lower hydration) favour acetic acid (less available water shifts metabolism). Wetter doughs favour lactic acid.
  • Temperature: warmer fermentation (>25°C) favours lactic-producing homofermenters; cooler fermentation (<18°C) or long cold retards favour acetic.
  • Fermentation time: longer = more acid production overall, with acetic acid increasing disproportionately in prolonged fermentations.
Why does sourdough develop so much more flavour than commercial yeast bread?
Three reasons acting together: (1) Longer fermentation allows enzymatic breakdown of starch and protein, producing more sugar substrate for yeast and more amino acids for flavour reactions. (2) LAB produce a complex mixture of organic acids plus dozens of secondary metabolites (ethyl acetate, acetaldehyde, diacetyl, 2,3-butanediol) that commercial yeast barely produces. (3) Proteolytic enzymes from both the LAB and flour itself partially break down gluten — not enough to compromise structure but enough to release amino acids that become flavour compounds in the Maillard reactions during baking. Commercial yeast bread optimises speed; sourdough optimises flavour depth.
Cheese making — coagulation, dehydration, and aging

Cheese is concentrated milk protein and fat, with most of the water removed and then aged under conditions that allow extensive enzymatic flavour development. The sequence:

Step 1 — Coagulation

Milk can be coagulated by two mechanisms, often used together:

  • Acid coagulation: LAB acidify milk, lowering pH toward 4.6 (the isoelectric point of casein). At this pH, casein micelles lose their surface charge and κ-casein "hairy layer" — they aggregate and form a soft, fragile gel. Produces fresh cheeses (ricotta, queso fresco, paneer) and the base for acid-set styles.
  • Enzymatic coagulation (rennet): rennet contains chymosin (or similar proteases) that specifically cleave κ-casein, removing the steric stabilisation of micelles. In the presence of calcium ions, the micelles then aggregate even at normal milk pH (6.6–6.8) — producing a firmer, more elastic curd that can be cut, cooked, and pressed. Most aged cheeses use rennet.

Step 2 — Curd processing

The curd is cut into pieces (larger pieces = more moisture retained = softer cheese; smaller pieces = more whey expelled = harder cheese). Cut curd is then cooked (further whey expulsion, protein contraction) and/or pressed. Salt is added by brining or rubbing — it draws out more moisture, inhibits unwanted organisms, and contributes to flavour and rind formation. The final moisture content determines the cheese style: soft (Brie, ~50% moisture), semi-hard (Gouda, ~40%), hard (Parmesan, <30%).

Step 3 — Aging (affinage)

This is where flavour is built. During aging:

  • Proteolysis: rennet residues + bacterial proteases + plasmin (native milk protease) break casein chains into smaller peptides and free amino acids. Bitter peptides (from non-specific proteolysis) are problematic in young cheese; continued breakdown into free amino acids eliminates bitterness. Free amino acids are then metabolised into flavour compounds: glutamic acid gives umami; tyrosine produces tyrosine crystals (the white "crunchy" bits in aged Parmesan); branched-chain amino acids (leucine, valine) produce fruity and nutty aldehyde flavours via Strecker-like degradation.
  • Lipolysis: lipases release free fatty acids from triglycerides. Short-chain FFA (butyric, caprylic, capric) are responsible for the pungent, sharp aroma of aged cheeses — especially in mould-ripened and washed-rind styles. At low levels this is "complex"; at high levels it is "rancid."
  • Mould contribution: Penicillium camemberti (Brie) grows on the rind, producing proteases that digest inward — this is why Brie is runny near the rind and firm in the centre in an under-ripe wheel. P. roqueforti (Roquefort, Stilton, Gorgonzola) grows in air channels through the cheese body — creating the characteristic blue-green veins and the sharp, piquant flavour from its lipase activity.
Yogurt — rapid acid coagulation

Yogurt is essentially a simplified cheese without the whey removal or aging step. The standard yogurt culture is a symbiotic pair: Streptococcus thermophilus (grows first, produces lactic acid quickly at 42–45°C) and Lactobacillus delbrueckii bulgaricus (grows more slowly, produces more acid and acetaldehyde — the characteristic yogurt aroma compound).

Why milk is heated before fermentation: milk is heated to 85–90°C for 15–30 minutes (or briefly to 95°C) before inoculation. This does three things: (1) kills competing organisms, (2) denatures whey proteins — denatured β-lactoglobulin associates with casein micelles and strengthens the eventual acid gel (more protein network = thicker, creamier yogurt), and (3) reduces dissolved oxygen. UHT milk works reasonably but the already-denatured proteins behave differently — one reason why homemade yogurt from UHT milk can be thinner than from pasteurised milk.

Incubation: inoculate with live culture at ~43–45°C and hold at temperature for 4–8 hours. The LAB multiply and produce lactic acid; pH drops steadily from ~6.6 to ~4.5. At pH 4.6, casein micelles coagulate into a soft gel — the yogurt sets. Longer incubation = more acid = more sour. Very long incubation = over-acidification, whey separation (syneresis), and a thin, overly sour product.

Fermented vegetables — salt, brine, and bacterial succession

Sauerkraut, kimchi, pickled cucumbers, and fermented hot sauce all rely on wild LAB naturally present on vegetable surfaces. No starter culture is needed — the microorganisms are already there. Salt is the only added ingredient, and it does the critical work:

  • Osmosis: salt draws water out of vegetable cells, creating the brine that submerges the vegetables. This anaerobic, salt-rich brine selects for salt-tolerant LAB while inhibiting most spoilage organisms and pathogens.
  • Salt concentration determines speed and community: ~2% salt produces a good fermentation in most vegetables at room temperature. Too little (<1.5%) and undesirable organisms can dominate. Too much (>3%) and fermentation is very slow; above ~5–6%, most LAB are inhibited.

Bacterial succession: sauerkraut fermentation follows a classic ecological succession:

  1. Leuconostoc mesenteroides dominates early (day 1–3): heterofermentative, produces CO₂ (purges oxygen), lactic acid, acetic acid. Creates the initial anaerobic acid environment.
  2. Lactobacillus plantarum takes over (day 3–14): more acid-tolerant, continues lactic acid production. Drives pH down to 3.5–4.
  3. L. brevis (if fermentation extends to weeks): most acid-tolerant LAB, completes fermentation at pH <3.5 where others struggle.

The final pH (~3.2–3.8) makes sauerkraut and kimchi shelf-stable at room temperature without refrigeration — the original purpose of the technique.

Kimchi adds complexity: garlic, ginger, and Korean red pepper introduce additional substrates and their own microbial populations. The flavour compounds from garlic fermentation (allicin breakdown products, S-compounds) integrate with the LAB metabolites for kimchi's distinctive pungency. The sequence of bacterial species is similar to sauerkraut but faster at higher temperatures and influenced by the additional substrates.

Vinegar — two sequential fermentations

Vinegar production requires two sequential microbial steps:

  1. Alcoholic fermentation: yeast converts sugars → ethanol + CO₂ (wine, cider, beer, or any dilute alcohol solution provides the substrate).
  2. Acetic acid fermentation: Acetobacter and related acetic acid bacteria (AAB) oxidise ethanol → acetic acid + water. This step requires oxygen — it is strictly aerobic, unlike the first step. The reaction: C₂H₅OH + O₂ → CH₃COOH + H₂O. AAB form a surface film (the "mother of vinegar" — cellulose matrix produced by the bacteria) at the liquid–air interface where oxygen is available.

The flavour of vinegar depends entirely on the starting material: wine → wine vinegar (with wine-specific flavour esters and phenolics); cider → cider vinegar; rice wine → rice vinegar (mildly sweet); malt → malt vinegar (more complex, bready). All contain acetic acid as the primary acid, but the secondary compounds produce the character.

Balsamic vinegar is a more complex version: concentrated grape must (cooked-down grape juice) undergoes both alcoholic fermentation and acetification, then aged for years to decades in a series of wooden barrels of decreasing size (each wood type contributes different flavour compounds). The long aging produces a syrupy, deeply complex condiment far removed from standard wine vinegar.

Worked examples
Example 1 — Why sourdough bread keeps longer than commercial yeast bread
A sourdough loaf stays mould-free and palatable for 4–7 days at room temperature, while a commercial yeast loaf of similar composition moulds within 2–3 days. Explain the chemical and microbiological mechanisms behind this difference.

Acetic acid — the primary antimicrobial:
Heterofermentative LAB in sourdough produce acetic acid (vinegar) alongside lactic acid. Acetic acid is a far more effective antifungal than lactic acid at equivalent concentrations — it is active in its undissociated form (pKa 4.75), and at the typical sourdough crumb pH of 4.5–5.0, a significant fraction remains undissociated and can diffuse across mould cell membranes. Lactic acid (pKa 3.86) is mostly dissociated at crumb pH and thus less membrane-permeable. Commercial yeast bread typically contains no significant acetic acid and minimal lactic acid — its pH is near neutral (~5.8–6.2), far less hostile to mould growth.

Lower pH overall:
Sourdough crumb pH is typically 4.3–5.2 vs. commercial yeast bread's 5.5–6.2. Most bread moulds (Aspergillus, Penicillium, Rhizopus) have a pH optimum of 5–6 and are inhibited below ~4.5. The combined acid environment of sourdough creates a less hospitable surface for mould colonisation.

Propionate production (in some sourdoughs):
Some LAB strains produce propionic acid — a potent antifungal used commercially as calcium propionate preservative in bread. Sourdoughs with appropriate LAB communities produce this natively.

Water activity effects:
The acid in sourdough reduces water activity slightly (acid molecules displace water in the aqueous phase), marginally reducing the free water available for microbial growth.

Practical note: covering bread in plastic accelerates mould regardless of type (trapped moisture creates a humid surface layer ideal for mould). Sourdough's advantage is chemical; storage conditions still matter.

Example 2 — Why does blue cheese taste so different from fresh curd?
Roquefort and Stilton are made from milk using rennet coagulation, like most cheeses. But their flavour is dramatically different from fresh curd or young cheese. Trace the specific biochemical steps that transform mild curd into pungent blue cheese, identifying which organisms are responsible and which chemical transformations they drive.

Starting material:
Fresh milk or curd: mild, milky, slightly lactic. Proteins (casein) mostly intact. Fat in fat globules. Minor flavour compounds from milk itself.

Step 1 — Initial acidification and pressing:
LAB produce lactic acid; pH drops to ~5.2. Rennet coagulation and pressing expel whey; moisture drops to ~40%. Still mild at this stage — similar to a young Cheddar or Edam.

Step 2 — Penicillium roqueforti inoculation and piercing:
P. roqueforti spores are added to the milk or curd. After pressing, the wheel is pierced with long needles to create air channels throughout the body — P. roqueforti is aerobic and can only grow where oxygen is available. The blue-green veins form precisely along these air channels.

Step 3 — Lipolysis (the source of pungency):
P. roqueforti produces powerful lipases. These cleave triglycerides to release free fatty acids — particularly the short- and medium-chain fatty acids (caprylic acid C8, capric acid C10, butyric acid C4). These FFA are responsible for the sharp, pungent, "blue cheese" aroma. Methyl ketones (2-heptanone, 2-nonanone) are also produced from fatty acid oxidation — these contribute the "blue cheese" note specifically. The mould essentially converts flavourless fat into a complex mixture of potent aroma compounds.

Step 4 — Proteolysis (the source of creaminess and complexity):
P. roqueforti and resident LAB produce proteases and peptidases that break casein into peptides and free amino acids. Bitter peptides are further hydrolysed to individual amino acids (reducing bitterness). Glutamic acid contributes umami. Branched-chain amino acids (leucine, isoleucine, valine) are deaminated and decarboxylated to produce fruity aldehydes. The combined effect: creamy texture (protein network partly solubilised), complex layered flavour (acid + FFA + amino acid metabolites), and umami depth.

Summary: fresh curd has 2–3 dominant flavour notes; Roquefort after 3–4 months of aging has dozens, generated by two distinct enzymatic pathways (proteolysis and lipolysis) running simultaneously under the activity of the mould.

Example 3 — Controlling sourdough sourness
A baker wants a sourdough loaf that is mildly tangy (creamy, yogurt-like) rather than sharp and vinegary. Using the chemistry of LAB metabolism, design a fermentation protocol (hydration level, temperature, time) that will favour lactic acid over acetic acid production.

The two acids and their determinants:
Lactic acid: produced by homofermentative LAB (and as the primary product of heterofermentative LAB under certain conditions). Dominant under warm temperatures (25–30°C) and higher hydration (wetter dough).
Acetic acid: produced by heterofermentative LAB when they use the phosphoketolase pathway under conditions favouring acetyl phosphate accumulation — i.e., cool temperatures and lower hydration (more limited water activity).

Protocol for lactic-dominant (mild) sourdough:

  • Hydration: use a high-hydration dough (70–80% water:flour ratio). Excess water promotes homofermentative metabolism and lactic acid production.
  • Temperature: ferment the bulk dough at 26–30°C. Warm conditions favour homofermentative strains and the lactic acid pathway. Avoid cold retarding the bulk (save any fridge time for the shaped loaf, and keep it short).
  • Time: 3–5 hours bulk fermentation at 28°C produces mild acidity. Do not extend to 12+ hours at room temperature — prolonged fermentation allows heterofermentative organisms to become more active.
  • Starter: a young, recently fed starter (peaked activity) is predominantly lactic-favoured. An older, more acidic starter or one maintained at room temperature has more heterofermentative organisms and will contribute more acetic character.

Protocol for acetic-dominant (sharp) sourdough: The opposite: stiffer dough (~65% hydration), 18–20°C bulk, 12–18+ hours or long cold retard at 4–8°C, older starter. San Francisco-style sourdoughs deliberately use this approach for their characteristic assertive tang.

Food for thought
  • Traditional fermented foods (sourdough, sauerkraut, cheese) developed long before any understanding of microbiology. People observed empirically which conditions produced good results and which produced spoilage. Which specific environmental variables would have been most salient to pre-scientific observation — and which would have been invisible until Pasteur?
    Observable variables: temperature (when the bread rises well, when the cheese ripens correctly, when the sauerkraut stays crisp vs. goes mushy), salt concentration (experienced empirically as the ratio of salt to vegetable weight), time (how long to ferment, when the bread has proofed "enough"), and sensory cues (appearance of mould, colour of rind, texture of curd, smell of the starter). These were transmitted as rules of thumb across generations. Invisible until Pasteur's germ theory (1860s): the existence of microorganisms at all, the fact that fermentation and spoilage were caused by distinct organisms rather than spontaneous generation, why a contaminated vessel produced different results than a clean one, and why milk from healthy vs. sick animals fermented differently (different microbial loads). Pre-scientific fermenters controlled the community structure of their cultures without knowing communities existed — by selecting for favourable conditions that we now understand to select for specific organisms. The knowledge was entirely correct; only the explanation was missing.
  • Sourdough bread has a lower glycaemic index than commercial yeast bread made from the same flour. What fermentation chemistry might explain this, and why would it matter metabolically?
    Several mechanisms contribute. First, the organic acids (primarily lactic and acetic) in sourdough lower bread pH to ~4.5–5.0. Acid slows gastric emptying — food stays in the stomach longer, reducing the rate of glucose delivery to the bloodstream (lower postprandial blood glucose spike). Second, the acidic environment during long fermentation partially modifies starch structure: protracted amylase activity (endogenous flour amylases are active during the long bulk fermentation) creates more starch–lipid complexes (amylose wrapping around the fatty acids from wheat) that are more slowly digestible. Third, phytate degradation: the acidic, prolonged fermentation activates phytases in flour, breaking down phytic acid (which chelates minerals and inhibits starch digestion enzymes). Reduced phytate = better mineral absorption and possibly less interference with amylase. The practical result: sourdough bread produces a meaningfully lower blood glucose response than equivalent commercial yeast bread — a meaningful difference for people managing blood sugar, not an incidental observation.
  • Vinegar is made by aerobic oxidation of ethanol to acetic acid. Yet the pH of vinegar (typically 2.4–3.4) is low enough to kill most bacteria, including the Acetobacter that make it. How does Acetobacter survive in its own product, and what limits the final acidity of vinegar?
    Acetobacter is uniquely acid-tolerant among the bacteria that make it: it survives acetic acid concentrations up to ~10–12% (well above the inhibitory threshold for most microorganisms). Its tolerance mechanisms include efflux pumps that export undissociated acetic acid from the cytoplasm before it can acidify the interior, and adaptations in its cell membrane composition that maintain integrity at low external pH. The limits on final acidity are practical rather than microbial: (1) substrate availability — Acetobacter requires ethanol; once the alcohol substrate is exhausted, acetic acid production stops. A 10% ABV starting wine/cider produces roughly 10% acetic acid (~10 g/100 mL), near the maximum for most commercial vinegar. (2) Product inhibition — above ~12% acetic acid, even Acetobacter is inhibited by its own product. (3) Oxygen limitation — the reaction requires oxygen; deep vessels with limited surface area slow production naturally. Industrial vinegar production uses submerged fermentation with forced aeration to maintain oxygen supply and accelerate the process.
Common pitfalls
  • Killing yeast with hot liquid. Yeast dies above ~50°C. Adding water hotter than ~40°C to yeast (or adding yeast directly to a hot mixture) produces dough that never rises. Water for bread baking should feel warm on the wrist, not hot.
  • Refrigerating yogurt culture at the wrong stage. Incubation must complete (gel set) before refrigeration. Refrigerating mid-fermentation stops LAB activity — the yogurt will never set properly. Once set, refrigeration slows (but does not stop) continued acidification; very long refrigerator storage makes yogurt progressively more sour.
  • Not submerging fermented vegetables in brine. Any vegetable above the brine surface is exposed to oxygen and becomes a site for mould growth rather than LAB fermentation. Use a weight or zip-lock bag filled with brine to keep everything submerged. Surface kahm yeast (a harmless white film) is normal; visible mould (green, black, pink) is a contamination issue.
  • Confusing homofermentative and heterofermentative LAB outcomes. Adding more starter or a warmer temperature to get more sourness may produce a lactic-dominant (milder) fermentation. For a sharper, more assertive sour, lower temperature and longer time produces acetic acid. These are different outcomes from different metabolic pathways, not just more or less of the same thing.
  • Assuming "fermented = safe" unconditionally. Acid fermentation to pH <4.6 with adequate salt is safe. Fermentation at insufficient salt, improper temperature, or incomplete acidification can allow pathogen survival. Home fermentation of vegetables at 2% salt by weight, fully submerged, produces consistently safe results. Never skip the salt.

Proteins in the Kitchen — Cheese making is acid and enzymatic coagulation of casein proteins, followed by enzymatic proteolysis during aging. The denaturation of whey proteins during milk heating determines yogurt texture. All of the protein chemistry from Lesson 2 is active here.
Carbohydrates & Starches — Yeast ferments sugars (glucose, fructose, maltose); amylases produce maltose from starch; phytase in acid dough breaks down antinutritional phytates. Carbohydrates are the substrate for fermentation.
Flavour Science — Fermentation is one of the most prolific sources of flavour compounds: esters, organic acids, aldehydes, sulfur compounds, and ketones. Understanding which organisms produce which compounds is the mechanistic foundation of flavour pairing in fermented food.
