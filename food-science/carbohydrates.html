Carbohydrates & Starches

Sugars · Starch Gelatinisation · Retrogradation · Pectin · Candy Stages

Carbohydrates are the most abundant food molecules on earth — and in the kitchen, they are the most structurally versatile. Starch gelatinisation is what makes a sauce thick and a custard smooth. Retrogradation is why bread goes stale. Sugar's behaviour at different temperatures produces everything from a soft caramel to a glassy hard candy. Pectin, in the right conditions, turns boiled fruit into jam. These are not separate topics — they are different expressions of the same carbohydrate chemistry.

Sugars — the building blocks

All carbohydrates are built from monosaccharides — simple sugar units with the formula (CH₂O)ₙ. The most important in food are:

  • Glucose (dextrose) — the universal biological energy currency. In solution, glucose exists mostly in ring form (pyranose) with a free anomeric hydroxyl that makes it a reducing sugar (available to react with amino groups in the Maillard reaction). Slightly less sweet than sucrose.
  • Fructose (fruit sugar) — found in fruit, honey, and high-fructose corn syrup. Sweeter than glucose or sucrose; reacts faster in Maillard (Lesson 1). High hygroscopicity (fructose absorbs water from the air more strongly than other sugars) — why honey-based baked goods stay moist longer.
  • Galactose — rarely found free; primarily as part of lactose. Less sweet than glucose.

Disaccharides are two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond:

  • Sucrose (glucose + fructose, 1→2 linkage): table sugar. Non-reducing — both anomeric carbons are locked in the glycosidic bond, so sucrose cannot react directly in Maillard without first hydrolysing to its components. Extremely soluble and highly crystallisable.
  • Lactose (glucose + galactose, 1→4 linkage): milk sugar. A reducing sugar. Low sweetness. Limited solubility — crystallises readily, which is why improperly stored milk chocolate develops a grainy texture (lactose crystals = "sugar bloom").
  • Maltose (glucose + glucose, 1→4 α linkage): produced by amylase enzyme breakdown of starch. Reducing sugar. Central to bread fermentation and beer brewing (malted barley's enzymes convert starch to maltose for yeast).

Sweetness is relative and context-dependent. Approximate relative sweetness (sucrose = 100): fructose 170, glucose 70–80, maltose 30–50, lactose 16–20. Cold temperature reduces perceived sweetness — ice cream is formulated noticeably sweeter than it would taste warm.

Invert sugar — sucrose hydrolysed to glucose + fructose by acid or the enzyme invertase. Inverted sugar is sweeter than sucrose (due to the free fructose), more hygroscopic, and critically: it does not crystallise as easily. Why? Because a mixture of glucose and fructose molecules disrupts the crystal lattice that pure sucrose would form. Confectioners add cream of tartar (tartaric acid) or glucose syrup to sugar solutions specifically to inhibit crystallisation — producing fudge or caramel rather than rock candy.

Starch structure — two polymers, one granule

Starch is glucose stored by plants. Unlike cellulose (the structural polysaccharide), starch uses α-linkages between glucose units — a geometry our digestive enzymes (amylases) can break. Starch exists as two structurally distinct polymers:

Amylose (~20–30% of most starches) is a long, essentially unbranched chain of glucose units linked α-1,4. The chain coils into a helix in solution — this helix shape is important: it can trap lipid and flavour molecules inside its hydrophobic core (iodine forms a deep blue inclusion complex in this helix, which is the basis of the classic starch test). Amylose molecules associate strongly with each other on cooling, forming ordered crystalline regions — the primary driver of retrogradation (staling).

Amylopectin (~70–80%) is a massive branched polymer — the same α-1,4 backbone as amylose but with α-1,6 branch points every 24–30 glucose units. Its tree-like structure is enormous (10⁷–10⁹ g/mol) and prevents tight packing, making it harder to retrograde and producing more viscous, sticky gels.

In the plant, both polymers are packed into semicrystalline starch granules — compressed spheres or polygons, 2–100 µm in diameter depending on source. The crystalline regions (ordered amylopectin chains) and amorphous regions alternate in growth rings. Raw starch granules are insoluble in cold water and resist digestion — they require heating to gelatinise.

Gelatinisation — the key cooking event

When starch granules are heated in water, several things happen in sequence:

  1. Hydration: below the gelatinisation temperature (~55–75°C depending on starch source), water slowly penetrates the amorphous regions of the granule, causing limited swelling. Granule structure is still intact. Viscosity is low.
  2. Gelatinisation proper: a threshold temperature is crossed and the crystalline regions lose order — they "melt." Granules swell dramatically (up to 100× their original volume) as water floods in. The granule surface becomes permeable and amylose leaches out into the surrounding water. Viscosity rises sharply. The starch dispersion becomes translucent.
  3. Beyond gelatinisation: with continued heating, granules rupture. Amylose and amylopectin fully disperse into the water, creating a viscous colloidal sol. Starches differ in how readily their granules rupture — waxy maize (nearly all amylopectin) produces a clear, stringy gel; wheat starch produces a cloudy, short-textured gel.
Why does a sauce thicken when you cook it?
When you add flour or cornstarch to liquid and heat, gelatinisation is occurring. The starch granules swell, amylose leaches into solution, and amylose chains entangle — dramatically increasing viscosity. A 2–3% starch concentration gives a pourable sauce; 6–8% gives a pudding or custard consistency. The relationship between starch concentration and thickness is not linear — small increases in starch dramatically increase viscosity once granules start to swell and interact.

Temperature matters for how you add starch: raw starch granules in cold liquid are a suspension (they sink). To avoid lumps: (1) disperse starch in cold water first (slurry), then add to hot liquid; or (2) coat starch in fat (a roux) before adding liquid — the fat separates granules and prevents them from clumping when they hit hot water. Adding raw starch directly to hot liquid causes the outer granules to gelatinise immediately and stick together — producing lumps with uncooked starch inside.

Retrogradation — why bread goes stale

Retrogradation is the process by which gelatinised starch chains reassociate into ordered crystalline structures on cooling and storage. It is essentially a partial reversal of gelatinisation — the chains do not return to their native granule structure, but they do form new hydrogen-bonded crystalline regions that change the texture of the food dramatically.

Amylose retrograde fast (within hours): amylose chains are linear and associate rapidly to form stable microcrystals. This is the major mechanism of bread firming in the first day after baking. The starch gel that gives fresh bread its soft, springy crumb becomes rigid as amylose chains order.

Amylopectin retrograde slowly (over days to weeks): the branched structure slows chain association, but amylopectin retrogradation is the dominant cause of long-term staling because of its sheer abundance. This is why bread that still smells fresh on day 3 has lost its springiness.

Temperature and staling rate: retrogradation is fastest just above 0°C (refrigerator temperature). Room temperature is slower. Freezer temperature stops it almost completely (chains are immobile). This is the paradox of bread storage: bread stored in the refrigerator goes stale faster than bread left at room temperature. The correct storage is either room temperature (for 1–2 days) or frozen (for weeks). Refrigerating bread is the worst of both worlds.

Re-heating reverses retrogradation: heating stale bread above the gelatinisation temperature (~60°C) disrupts the newly formed crystalline regions and restores softness — temporarily. This is why toasting stale bread works, and why a stale baguette heated in a 180°C oven for 10 minutes tastes almost fresh again. Once cooled, staling resumes where it left off.

Emulsifiers slow retrogradation: monoglycerides (found naturally in foods and added commercially) form inclusion complexes with amylose chains — occupying the helix interior and physically blocking chain-chain association. This is why commercial bread with mono/diglycerides listed as ingredients stays soft for a week, while an artisan loaf with no additives firms within a day.

Starch types in the kitchen — not all are equivalent
Starch Gels at Gel appearance Notes
Cornstarch 62–72°C Clear to semi-opaque Breaks down with prolonged heat; doesn't freeze-thaw well
Arrowroot 60–70°C Very clear Ideal for clear sauces; loses thickening power with extended boiling
Potato starch 59–68°C Clear, slightly sticky High viscosity, gelatinises at lower temp; can become gluey
Wheat flour 65–70°C Opaque, cloudy Contains protein (gluten) as well; robust to heat; freezes well in a roux sauce
Waxy maize 63–72°C Very clear, stringy ~100% amylopectin; excellent freeze-thaw stability; used in commercial frozen foods
Tapioca 58–70°C Clear, chewy texture Good in acidic preparations; glossy clear appearance preferred for fruit pies

The practical implication: cornstarch and flour are not interchangeable as thickeners. Flour produces an opaque, matte sauce (good for gravy); cornstarch produces a translucent, glossy sauce (good for Chinese-American stir-fry sauces and fruit pie fillings). Arrowroot is best when clarity and a neutral flavour are critical (fruit sauces, clear glazes). For sauces that will be frozen, a waxy starch or a flour-based roux is more stable than cornstarch (which turns spongy on thawing due to amylose retrogradation).

Sugar syrup stages — thermodynamics of crystallisation

As a sugar syrup is heated, water evaporates and the sugar concentration rises. The boiling point of the solution increases with concentration (boiling point elevation — colligative property). Different concentrations produce radically different textures when the syrup is cooled, because the ratio of sugar to water determines whether, and how fast, the sugar crystallises.

Sugar syrup stages and their temperatures

Temperature ranges for each stage. The boiling point of water (100°C) is the floor — as concentration rises, boiling point rises. Use a thermometer: guessing is unreliable.

The cold-water test (drop syrup into cold water and feel the texture) remains a useful backup when you lack a thermometer — but temperature is more precise. The reason each stage has a different texture: at higher concentrations, there is less water to hydrate and separate sugar crystals, so the cooled product has less mobility. Thread stage syrup has enough water to flow freely; hard crack syrup has so little water that the amorphous solid becomes a brittle glass.

Why does fudge have a different texture than toffee? Both are cooked to similar concentrations (soft- to firm-ball range), but fudge is intentionally allowed to crystallise in a controlled way (fine crystals = smooth texture) while toffee uses cream of tartar or glucose syrup to suppress crystallisation (remaining amorphous = chewy, stretchy). Crystallisation is seeded by mechanical action — working fudge while it cools by stirring or beating introduces seed crystals that grow into fine, uniform crystals throughout. Toffee is not stirred, avoiding seed nucleation.

Pectin and fruit gels — jam chemistry

Pectin is a structural polysaccharide found in plant cell walls (particularly in fruit skin and pith). It is a polymer of galacturonic acid units linked α-1,4. In ripe fruit, pectin is partially hydrolysed by enzymes — the fruit softens. In under-ripe or just-ripe fruit, pectin concentration is high — the cell walls are firm and intact.

High-methoxyl pectin (the natural form in most fruit) requires two conditions to gel:

  • Acid (pH 2.8–3.5): protonates the galacturonic acid carboxyl groups, reducing electrostatic repulsion between chains and allowing them to associate via hydrogen bonds.
  • High sugar concentration (>55% soluble solids): sucrose competes with pectin for water molecules, reducing hydration of the pectin chains and allowing them to associate.

Both must be present simultaneously. This is why jam requires both enough acid (from the fruit or added lemon juice) and enough sugar. Low-sugar jams fail to set properly unless low-methoxyl pectin or added gelling agents (agar, gelatin) are used.

Low-methoxyl pectin (commercially modified; methoxyl groups partially removed by amidation or alkali treatment) gels with calcium ions rather than acid + sugar. Calcium bridges between adjacent carboxyl groups on pectin chains, cross-linking them into a gel. Used in low-sugar jams, dairy desserts, and restructured fruit products.

Why does lemon juice help jam set? Lemon juice adds acid (lowering pH into the pectin gel range) and contains its own pectin (high in citrus pith). A sous-ripe fruit jam that refuses to set often fixes with the addition of 1–2 tablespoons of lemon juice.

The setting point test: jam is done when a drop placed on a chilled plate wrinkles when pushed. At this point, sugar concentration has reached ~68–70% (measured by refractometer as °Brix), pH has concentrated, and pectin concentration is high enough to form a gel network. Below this point, the jam will be runny; above it, it becomes too stiff or crystallises on storage.

Worked examples
Example 1 — Bread going stale in the fridge
A bakery stores its sourdough loaves in the refrigerator to extend shelf life. Customers complain the bread goes stale faster than when left at room temperature. Explain the chemistry behind this complaint and recommend a better storage protocol.

The chemistry:
Staling is caused by starch retrogradation — gelatinised starch chains in the crumb re-associate into crystalline regions, increasing crumb rigidity. Retrogradation rate follows a complex temperature dependence: it is fastest just above 0°C (refrigerator temperature, ~4°C) and slower at room temperature (~20°C) or very cold (frozen, <–18°C). The maximum retrogradation rate near 0°C is a consequence of the balance between chain mobility (higher at higher temps) and the thermodynamic drive to crystallise (stronger at lower temps). At refrigerator temperature, both factors are present simultaneously in their most damaging combination.

Why room temperature is better:
At 20°C, retrogradation rate is roughly 3–5× slower than at 4°C. Bread held at room temperature in a sealed bag or bread box stays acceptably soft for 2–3 days depending on formulation. Moisture loss (drying out) is the competing degradation mechanism at room temperature — hence the sealed bag.

Recommended protocol:
(1) Room temperature in a sealed bag for 1–2 days. (2) If longer storage is needed, slice and freeze individually. Freezing stops retrogradation almost completely — frozen bread thawed and reheated will taste fresh because the heat both thaws and reverses the limited retrogradation that occurred during freezing. Never refrigerate bread; it is the worst storage condition for staling.

Example 2 — Thickening a cream sauce: flour vs. cornstarch
A chef is making a cream sauce that will be prepared in advance, refrigerated, reheated, and served. They are choosing between (A) a flour-based roux thickener and (B) a cornstarch slurry. Which should they choose, and why? Consider: sauce appearance, flavour, and behaviour after refrigeration and reheating.

Flour-based roux (Option A):

  • Appearance: opaque, matte — classic French béchamel look. Acceptable for a cream sauce where opacity is expected.
  • Flavour: flour contributes a subtle starchy/wheaty note unless cooked long enough. A properly cooked roux (heated until faint nutty aroma, no raw flour smell) adds depth. Contains gluten proteins as well as starch.
  • Freeze-thaw/reheat stability: flour sauces are stable because wheat starch has a higher amylose content mixed with gluten — the gluten network prevents complete starch granule rupture and the sauce does not weep on cooling. Reheats cleanly without texture change.

Cornstarch slurry (Option B):

  • Appearance: translucent, glossy — attractive for Chinese-style or fruit-based sauces; slightly odd looking in a classical cream sauce.
  • Flavour: nearly neutral; does not add flavour complexity.
  • Freeze-thaw/reheat stability: cornstarch gels are unstable on cooling. Amylose retrograde rapidly, and the gel can become spongy and weep water (syneresis) when refrigerated and reheated. The sauce may appear "broken" or watery after overnight refrigeration.

Recommendation: Flour-based roux for a make-ahead cream sauce. The stability difference is not marginal — cornstarch cream sauces can be noticeably spongy after refrigeration. If a glossy, neutral-flavour thickener is needed with good reheat stability, waxy maize starch or a modified food starch (used commercially) is the professional solution. For home cooking, flour wins for this application.

Example 3 — Why does a fruit jam fail to set?
A strawberry jam is cooked to temperature (105°C) but does not set after cooling — it remains pourable. The recipe used ripe supermarket strawberries with added sugar. Diagnose the possible causes and explain a corrective approach.

Diagnosing the failure:
High-methoxyl pectin needs both sufficient pectin concentration, pH 2.8–3.5, and >55% sugar. At least one of these is missing.

Cause 1 — Low pectin (most likely with very ripe strawberries):
Ripe and over-ripe strawberries have had their cell wall pectin partially hydrolysed by ripening enzymes (pectinases). Compared to a just-ripe berry, a very ripe berry may have 30–60% less functional pectin. The jam lacks the polymer concentration needed to form a gel network.

Cause 2 — Insufficient acid:
Ripe strawberries are lower in acid than under-ripe ones. If the pH is above ~3.5, the carboxyl groups on pectin chains remain ionised and repel each other — gel formation is impossible regardless of pectin concentration or sugar level. A test: add lemon juice and retest the set (cold plate test). If it sets with lemon juice, acid deficiency was the cause.

Cause 3 — Insufficient cooking time:
If the jam passed through 105°C very quickly, concentration may be lower than expected (thermometer inaccuracy is common). Refractometer measurement of °Brix is more reliable than temperature for confirming readiness.

Corrective approach: Reheat the jam with 2 tbsp lemon juice + 1–2 tsp commercial pectin dissolved in a small amount of sugar. Return to boil, test set again. If using commercial pectin, follow its ratio guidelines — it varies by brand and type (high-methoxyl vs. low-methoxyl pectin have different requirements).

Food for thought
  • A roux is made by cooking flour in fat before adding liquid. The standard explanation is that the fat coats starch granules, preventing lumping. But a roux also browns (develops flavour) as it cooks, and the ratio of flour to fat can vary widely. What is the fat actually doing chemically, and is "coating granules" a sufficient explanation for how a roux works?
    "Coating starch granules to prevent lumping" is partially true but incomplete. Three things are happening simultaneously. First, the fat does physically separate granules, slowing their rate of swelling when hot liquid is added — this delays gelatinisation slightly and prevents the outer-granule clumping that produces lumps. Second, the cooking process at roux temperatures (120–200°C) partially dehydrates the starch granules — reducing their hydration drive — and breaks down some starch chains (dextrinisation). Dextrins (shorter starch fragments) gelatinise differently, producing a less viscous, more stable sauce. A dark roux (used in gumbo) is cooked to the point of extensive dextrinisation — it has lost most of its thickening power but gained rich, complex Maillard flavours from the proteins in flour reacting with the starch-derived reducing sugars. Third, the fat has no permanent role once hot liquid is added — it disperses. The anti-lumping effect is kinetic: the fat slows the rate of water contact with starch granules during the initial mixing, allowing even distribution before mass gelatinisation begins. A very thorough cold-liquid whisking of cornstarch slurry achieves a similar kinetic goal by different means.
  • Cellulose is a polymer of glucose, exactly like starch, but humans cannot digest it. The only difference is the glycosidic linkage: starch uses α-1,4; cellulose uses β-1,4. Why does one small geometric difference — a configuration change at a single carbon — produce such a dramatically different material (soluble, digestible starch vs. tough, indigestible wood)?
    The α vs. β configuration at C1 of glucose changes the bond angle and the chain geometry at every linkage. In α-1,4 starch, the chain adopts a slightly helical coil — the overall structure is compact and curved, and the glucose units are accessible to amylase enzymes which evolved specifically to hydrolyse this geometry. In β-1,4 cellulose, each glucose unit is rotated 180° relative to its neighbour — the chain is perfectly straight, and straight chains can pack tightly side by side via hydrogen bonds between adjacent chains, forming rigid crystalline bundles (microfibrils). This packing creates a material with extraordinary tensile strength (wood, cotton) that is insoluble in water and impervious to most enzymes (no animal produces cellulase; the few that digest cellulose do so via gut microorganisms). The consequence of this single geometric difference: starch is food (energy-available) and cellulose is structure (dietary fibre, fermentable by gut microbiota to short-chain fatty acids but never directly hydrolysed to glucose for systemic absorption). This is one of the more striking examples of how molecular geometry determines macroscopic material properties — the difference between digestion and construction.
  • Fudge and toffee are both sucrose-based candies cooked to similar temperatures, but they have completely different textures. The difference is entirely in how cooling and working are handled. What does this tell you about the role of kinetics (vs. thermodynamics) in determining food texture?
    Thermodynamics determines the equilibrium state: at fudge/toffee concentrations, highly ordered crystalline sucrose is the minimum-energy state. Given infinite time, both fudge and toffee would crystallise to the same thermodynamic end state. But kinetics — the rate at which the system approaches equilibrium — determines what you actually get in the kitchen. Toffee suppresses crystallisation kinetics via glucose syrup (which disrupts the sucrose crystal lattice geometry, making nucleation slow) and rapid cooling (reduces molecular mobility). The result is a metastable amorphous glass. Fudge encourages and controls crystallisation kinetics: seeding with crystal nuclei (the stirring action as it cools) and slow cooling create many small crystals simultaneously, rather than allowing a few large crystals to grow (which would feel grainy). The lesson: in food texture, thermodynamics tells you what's possible but kinetics tells you what you get. Most interesting food textures — whipped cream, tempered chocolate, fudge, ice cream — are kinetically controlled metastable states, not thermodynamic equilibria.
Common pitfalls
  • Refrigerating bread to keep it fresh. Retrogradation rate peaks near 4°C. Refrigerating bread makes it go stale faster than room temperature storage. Freeze if you need to store it longer than 2 days.
  • Adding raw starch directly to hot liquid. Causes lumping as surface granules gelatinise before inner ones are dispersed. Always disperse in cold liquid first (slurry), or coat in fat (roux) before adding hot liquid.
  • Using cornstarch in sauces destined for freezing. Cornstarch sauces turn spongy and watery after freeze-thaw. Use a flour-based thickener or waxy maize starch for make-ahead or frozen sauces.
  • Assuming all starches thicken equally. Cornstarch thickens approximately twice as much as an equivalent weight of all-purpose flour. Using a 1:1 substitution produces either a water-like sauce or a paste. Correct ratio: 1 tbsp cornstarch ≈ 2 tbsp flour.
  • Not adding acid when making jam from low-acid fruit. Very ripe strawberries, overripe figs, and most stone fruits are naturally low in acid at peak ripeness. Without added lemon juice, the pH is too high for high-methoxyl pectin to gel regardless of cooking time and sugar concentration.
Connects to

Heat & Browning — Starch hydrolysis produces reducing sugars (glucose, maltose) that are Maillard reactants. Bread crust browning comes partly from Maillard between flour proteins and starch-derived sugars.
Proteins in the Kitchen — Gluten network formation interacts directly with starch: starch gelatinisation fills the gluten network during baking, contributing to crumb structure. Fat's anti-gluten effect (shortening) and starch's water absorption compete in baked goods.
Fermentation — Yeast and bacteria ferment sugars. Amylase enzymes in flour (and added in commercial baking) break starch to maltose, providing substrate for yeast. Starch's role in bread is half-structural, half-fermentation-substrate.
