Flavour Science

Taste · Olfaction · Aroma Compounds · Umami · Off-Flavours

Most of what we call "taste" isn't. The tongue detects exactly five categories of signal. Everything else — the difference between strawberry and raspberry, the roast in coffee, the funk in aged blue cheese, the brightness of fresh herbs — is smell, experienced via a back-route through the throat that most people never think about. Flavour science is the study of what those molecules are, how we detect them at concentrations that can be counted in atoms per glass of wine, and what happens when you understand the chemistry well enough to control it.

Taste is not flavour

Taste is a precise technical term: the sensation produced by taste receptor cells on the tongue, soft palate, and epiglottis in response to dissolved chemical compounds. There are exactly five confirmed basic tastes — sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami — each with distinct receptor mechanisms and distinct evolutionary signals.

Flavour is the full perceptual experience of eating. It integrates taste with retronasal olfaction (by far the dominant channel), texture, temperature, trigeminal sensations (the "heat" of capsaicin, the "cool" of menthol — detected by TRP channels, not taste receptors), and visual cues. The distinction matters practically: when you say a dish "tastes flat," the problem is almost certainly in the aroma or acid/salt balance — not in the five basic taste modalities specifically.

The clearest demonstration: pinch your nose hard and eat a piece of food. You retain the five tastes (sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami) but lose essentially everything that makes the food distinct — the difference between apple and pear, coffee and tea, beef and pork largely disappears. What remains is a coarse chemical signal. Everything that makes food interesting is in the aroma.

The five basic tastes

Each basic taste has a distinct receptor class and a distinct biological signal it evolved to provide. Understanding the receptor mechanisms explains otherwise puzzling kitchen phenomena — why salt suppresses bitterness, why umami makes other flavours rounder, why capsaicin "burns."

Sweet

Detected by the T1R2+T1R3 heterodimer — a G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) complex on the tongue. The receptor has an extracellular "Venus flytrap" domain that binds glucose, fructose, sucrose, and also amino acids (glycine, alanine) and all artificial sweeteners, despite their structural diversity. Multiple binding sites on the same receptor complex explain why many unrelated molecules taste sweet. Evolutionary signal: energy-dense carbohydrate food.

Sour

Detected by proton (H⁺) influx, primarily through the OTOP1 proton channel recently identified in sour taste cells. Sour taste is the direct detection of acidity. Interestingly, it is the concentration of undissociated acid — not pH alone — that determines sourness: acetic acid (vinegar, pKa 4.75) tastes more sour than hydrochloric acid at the same pH because more of the acid is in the membrane-permeant undissociated form. Evolutionary signal: fermentation, unripe fruit, bacterial spoilage — valid but context-dependent; high acid can signal both "not yet ripe" and "safely fermented."

Salty

Sodium ions (Na⁺) pass directly through ENaC epithelial sodium channels on salt-sensitive taste cells, depolarising them. Other cations (K⁺, Li⁺) produce partial salt-like responses; most anions are tasteless or mildly bitter. Two practically important effects: (1) sub-threshold amounts of salt suppress bitterness perception — a known but poorly-mechanised phenomenon (possibly via Na⁺ modifying bitter receptor function). This is why a pinch of salt in coffee reduces perceived bitterness. (2) Salt enhances the perception of sweetness and umami by reducing the masking effect of other taste compounds. Evolutionary signal: electrolyte balance.

Bitter

Detected by approximately 25 different T2R receptors — each a GPCR with a different ligand profile. No other taste modality has this diversity. The range reflects the diversity of plant secondary metabolites (alkaloids, glucosinolates, polyphenols, terpenes) that plants evolved to deter herbivores and that animals evolved to detect as toxicity warnings. Caffeine (adenosine receptor antagonist, primarily detected by T2R7 and T2R14), quinine, isohumulones in hops, and phenolic tannins all activate different T2R subsets.

Genetic variation in bitterness is larger than for any other taste modality. The TAS2R38 gene determines sensitivity to PROP (6-n-propylthiouracil) and a range of bitter glucosinolates; "supertasters" homozygous for the sensitive allele find Brussels sprouts, kale, and dark chocolate considerably more bitter than non-tasters with the insensitive allele. This is a meaningful source of variation in food preference that has nothing to do with culinary sophistication.

Umami

Detected by the T1R1+T1R3 heterodimer — distinct from the T1R2+T1R3 sweet receptor, sharing only the T1R3 subunit. The principal ligand is L-glutamate (a free amino acid), with strong synergistic amplification by 5'-ribonucleotides (IMP, GMP) — covered in detail in the next section. Umami is often described as "savoriness" or "meatiness" but is more accurately a signal of free amino acids and hence protein content. Unlike sweetness or saltiness, umami has no sharp onset or clear satiation — it rounds and extends other flavours, increasing the duration of the overall taste experience.

Capsaicin is not a taste. The burning sensation from chilli is produced by TRPV1 (transient receptor potential vanilloid 1), a thermosensor that normally responds to temperatures above ~43°C. Capsaicin opens it at room temperature, producing a "hot" signal identical in mechanism to actual heat — hence "burning." TRPV1 is not on taste cells; it is on trigeminal sensory neurons throughout the oral cavity. Menthol activates the cold-sensing TRPM8 channel. Neither is taste; both are part of flavour.
Orthonasal vs. retronasal olfaction

The olfactory epithelium sits at the top of the nasal cavity, about 7 cm above the nostrils. It contains roughly 6–10 million olfactory receptor neurons (ORNs), each expressing one type of olfactory receptor (humans have ~400 functional olfactory receptor genes, dogs ~1,000). Volatile molecules reach this tissue via two routes:

  • Orthonasal — inhaled through the nostrils while sniffing or breathing. Used to assess food before eating: "does this smell safe?" Dominant during wine evaluation by aroma (nosing), coffee assessment, detecting a gas leak.
  • Retronasal — vapours from food in the mouth travel backward through the nasopharynx (the opening at the back of the throat that connects to the nasal passage) and up to the olfactory epithelium. This happens during chewing and especially during swallowing, which creates a piston action that pushes air up from the oral cavity. This is the dominant channel for flavour perception.

The distinction matters: the same molecule can produce different perceptual experiences via the two routes. Retronasal olfaction is interpreted by the brain in the context of eating (taste inputs, texture, temperature) and is fused into a unified flavour percept. The same molecule inhaled orthonasal is just a smell. This is why food scientists distinguish between the "aroma" of a food (orthonasal, before eating) and its "flavour" (retronasal, while eating) — they can differ meaningfully.

Retronasal olfaction accounts for roughly 70–80% of what people describe as "taste." Patients with complete anosmia (inability to smell) still taste sweetness, sourness, saltiness, bitterness, and umami normally, but report food as almost entirely flavourless. The five basic tastes provide structure; retronasal aroma provides specificity.

Each ORN expresses one receptor type, and ~10,000 ORNs expressing the same receptor converge onto a single glomerulus in the olfactory bulb. The brain decodes smells by reading the pattern of activated glomeruli — not a single on/off signal, but a combinatorial code across 400 receptor types. A single aroma molecule typically activates a combination of 3–20 receptor types; complex food aromas activate hundreds simultaneously. This combinatorial coding allows approximately 1 trillion distinguishable odours (Bushdid et al., Science, 2014).

Volatile compound families

Aroma compounds are volatile organic molecules — generally small (MW < 300 Da), with sufficient vapour pressure to reach the olfactory epithelium. They span many chemical families; each family has a characteristic sensory signature and a characteristic origin in food chemistry.

Esters

R–C(=O)–O–R'. Fruity, sweet, solvent-like. Formed by esterification of alcohols with organic acids — either enzymatically (fruit ripening) or by yeast fermentation. Isoamyl acetate (banana, pear) is the dominant ester in banana ripening and in some yeasts (hefeweizens). Ethyl butyrate (pineapple, tropical). Ethyl acetate (solvent/nail-polish at high concentration, fruity/pineapple at low) — a common fermentation by-product. Esters generally have higher detection thresholds than sulfur compounds; they contribute background fruitiness rather than sharp individual notes.

Aldehydes

R–CHO. Character varies strongly with chain length and structure.

  • C6 aldehydes (hexanal, (E)-2-hexenal): fresh grass, green apple, unripe fruit. Produced by the lipoxygenase (LOX) pathway when plant cells are damaged — oxidative cleavage of linoleic acid. This is the "fresh-cut" smell of crushed herbs, freshly cut grass, and unripe tomatoes.
  • Trans-2-nonenal: cardboard, wet cardboard, stale fat. The primary off-odour of oxidized cooking oil, stale beer ("cardboard defect"), and old butter. Very low threshold (~80 ng/L).
  • Strecker aldehydes (produced by the Maillard reaction — see below): 3-methylbutanal (malty, cocoa), methional (boiled potato, soup), phenylacetaldehyde (honey, rose), furfural (caramel, almond). These are the aroma signature of roasted and cooked foods.
  • Benzaldehyde: almond, marzipan, cherry. Released from amygdalin in stone fruit pits and bitter almonds.
Pyrazines

Bicyclic heterocycles containing two nitrogen atoms. Roasty, nutty, earthy, green-pepper. Maillard-derived pyrazines are among the most flavour-active products of cooking: 2-acetylpyrazine (popcorn, tortilla chips — the specific roast character of nixtamalized corn), 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (the key aroma of basmati rice and fresh bread crust). 2-Isobutyl-3-methoxypyrazine — the characteristic bell pepper aroma, formed by Capsicum plants enzymatically (not by cooking) — is one of the most potent aroma compounds known, with a detection threshold around 2 ng/L in water.

Thiols and other sulfur compounds

Generally the most potent class by detection threshold. The sulfhydryl group (–SH) is particularly effective at activating olfactory receptors, resulting in thresholds in the parts-per-trillion range.

  • 3-Mercaptohexanol and 3-mercaptohexyl acetate: grapefruit, passion fruit, tropical. Responsible for the varietal character of Sauvignon Blanc; also found in hops and some stone fruits.
  • 2-Furfurylthiol: roasted coffee. One of the most characteristic coffee aroma compounds; threshold ~10 ng/L. Extremely sensitive: a single-atom difference from the non-thiol analog changes the character completely.
  • Allicin and diallyl disulfide: garlic. Allicin (the initial compound from crushing garlic) is pungent and reactive; it rapidly converts to diallyl disulfide and other polysulfides, giving the more mellow "cooked garlic" character.
  • Methanethiol (MeSH): rotten cabbage, some aged cheeses (Limburger, Camembert rind). Produced by bacterial degradation of methionine.
  • Dimethyl sulfide (DMS): cooked corn, canned tomatoes, some lager beers (a common defect at high concentrations). Produced during malt kilning and from methionine precursors.
Terpenes

Built from isoprene (C5) units. The largest class of plant secondary metabolites; present in the essential oils of almost every herb and spice. Most have higher thresholds than thiols but dominate by sheer concentration in aromatic plant material. Limonene (citrus peel) — the most abundant single compound in many citrus essential oils. Linalool (lavender, coriander, bergamot) — floral and slightly woody. β-Caryophyllene (black pepper, cloves, hops) — earthy, woody, spicy. Geraniol (rose) and citronellol (rose, geranium, citrus) are the main terpene alcohols in floral fragrances.

Lactones

Cyclic esters formed by intramolecular esterification of hydroxy fatty acids. Dairy-like, coconut, peach. δ-Decalactone (peach, cream) — one of the characteristic lactones of peaches and of sweet cream butter; also formed by yeast fermentation of ricinoleic acid (from castor oil). γ-Undecalactone (peach, coconut) — the characteristic "peach candy" note. Lactones increase in concentration as fatty acids in dairy age and cyclize.

Maillard aroma products — a brief recap

The Maillard reaction (Lesson 1) does not just produce brown colour — it generates hundreds of volatile compounds. Three mechanisms are especially important:

  • Strecker degradation: free amino acids react with α-dicarbonyl Maillard intermediates, losing one carbon as an aldehyde. Each amino acid produces a characteristic Strecker aldehyde: leucine → 3-methylbutanal (malty, cocoa); methionine → methional (boiled potato, meat stock); phenylalanine → phenylacetaldehyde (honey, rose); cysteine → H₂S (which then reacts further to produce meat thiols and thiophenes). Strecker aldehydes are key to what distinguishes roasted from raw.
  • Pyrazine formation: two Strecker-derived amino-carbonyl fragments condense and cyclize to form pyrazine rings — the nutty/roasty character of coffee, bread crust, and seared meat.
  • Furanone formation: Furaneol (DMHF — 2,5-dimethyl-4-hydroxy-3(2H)-furanone) is a Maillard product that is also produced enzymatically in ripe strawberries. It is simultaneously the "caramel" note in caramelised sugars and the "strawberry" note in fresh fruit — a convergence of two very different reaction pathways producing the same compound.
Umami synergy

The umami taste receptor (T1R1+T1R3) responds to L-glutamate. But it has a second binding site for 5'-ribonucleotides — specifically inosine monophosphate (IMP) from animal muscle and guanosine monophosphate (GMP) from fungi and yeast. When both ligands are present simultaneously, the perceived umami intensity is dramatically greater than either produces alone.

Glutamate alone (1× baseline)  +  IMP alone (1.1×)  →  Glutamate + IMP together: ~8–30× baseline, depending on ratio and concentration.

The mechanism: the T1R1 subunit has a Venus flytrap domain that opens and closes around glutamate. IMP and GMP bind to a nearby site and stabilise the closed (active) conformation, dramatically increasing the probability that a bound glutamate molecule triggers the receptor. They don't activate the receptor on their own — they act as positive allosteric modulators of glutamate binding.

This has direct and quantitative consequences for cooking:

  • Free vs. bound glutamate. Protein-bound glutamate has zero taste — the amino acid is unavailable to the receptor. Only free L-glutamate (released by hydrolysis) activates T1R1. This is why aged Parmesan (~1.2 g free glutamate/100g), ripe tomatoes, soy sauce, and fish sauce have intense umami, while an equivalent mass of fresh meat has comparatively little: most glutamate is still locked in peptide bonds.
  • The dashi principle. Japanese dashi combines kombu seaweed (very high free glutamate, ~2.5 g/100g dry weight) with katsuobushi (dried bonito, high in IMP from ATP degradation post-slaughter). The combination is not just additive — the IMP amplifies the glutamate signal at the receptor level. The broth is greater than the sum of its parts in a precise biochemical sense.
  • Mixed nucleotide sources. IMP (animal muscle) and GMP (fungi/yeast) both act at the same receptor site. Combining glutamate-rich with IMP-rich and GMP-rich sources (e.g. beef + dried shiitake in a braise) can push synergy beyond what either nucleotide alone provides.
  • MSG is the isolated compound. MSG (monosodium glutamate) is simply free L-glutamate with sodium. It activates the T1R1+T1R3 receptor identically to free glutamate from kombu or Parmesan. There is no chemically meaningful distinction.
Detection thresholds — orders of magnitude

Perhaps the most striking fact in flavour science: human olfactory detection thresholds span roughly ten orders of magnitude. A compound like TCA (cork taint) is perceptible at concentrations measured in parts per trillion. Ethyl acetate requires a concentration eight million times higher before it registers. Several patterns are worth noting. Sulfur compounds (shown in orange) cluster at the extreme low end — our olfactory system is extremely sensitive to them, which is plausible as a signal for both spoilage (rotten eggs, decaying meat) and desirable cooked-food aromas (coffee, garlic). Geosmin and TCA are not food compounds produced by cooking at all — they are environmental contaminants detectable at vanishingly small concentrations. Esters and terpenes, at the other end, are generally present in food in relatively large amounts and have correspondingly higher thresholds.

The practical implication: you cannot "balance out" a low-threshold off-compound by adding more desirable flavours. If a wine has TCA at 3 ng/L, no amount of additional fruit character will compensate — TCA actively suppresses the olfactory response to other compounds (see Off-flavours below). Low-threshold compounds must be eliminated, not masked.

The flavour pairing hypothesis

In the early 2000s, chef Heston Blumenthal and flavour chemist François Benzi proposed that ingredients sharing dominant aroma compounds should pair well in cooking — the so-called food pairing hypothesis. Chocolate and coffee share pyrazines and furanones; strawberry and balsamic vinegar share furaneol; white chocolate and caviar were claimed to share trimethylamine (the fishy amine compound). The hypothesis attracted significant media attention and spawned commercial software for suggesting novel pairings based on shared volatile profiles.

The empirical record is more nuanced. A 2011 analysis of 56,498 recipes across multiple cuisines (Ahn et al., Scientific Reports) found that North American and Western European cuisines do indeed tend to use ingredients that share more aroma compounds than random. But East Asian, Indian, and West African cuisines showed the opposite pattern — traditional pairings systematically avoided shared compounds.

This suggests that "shared compounds" is neither a universal principle nor a coincidence. The more likely interpretation: Western European cuisine developed around a set of ingredients (cream, butter, egg, wine) that happen to share many Maillard and dairy lactone compounds, and the hypothesis was derived from — and describes — that one regional tradition, not human flavour perception in general.

An alternative framing that accounts for more cuisines is aromatic contrast: some great pairings work not because the aromas converge, but because they provide complementary, non-competing aromatic information that together produce a richer overall experience. A Korean kimchi jjigae combining fermented, allium, and citrus/acidic compounds covers more aromatic space than any ingredient alone, even though no pair of its components shares significant volatiles.

Current status of the hypothesis: useful as an ideation tool for novel European-style pairings, and it does explain some specific combinations well. Not a universal theory. The "shared compounds" principle should be treated as one input alongside cultural context, textural contrast, acid-fat-salt balance, and general flavour complexity.
Off-flavours

Off-flavours — compounds that are not intended to be present and that degrade the product — are often more instructive than desirable flavours, because the same threshold sensitivity that makes them perceptible at trace concentrations also means they cannot be overcome by adding more "good" flavour. Understanding their chemistry allows either prevention or correction.

Oxidative rancidity — trans-2-nonenal and hexanal

Covered in depth in the Fats lesson. The short version: autoxidation of polyunsaturated fatty acids generates hydroperoxides, which decompose to short-chain aldehydes and ketones. Trans-2-nonenal (cardboard; threshold ~80 ng/L) and hexanal (paint, green; threshold ~5 µg/L) are the primary off-compounds in oxidized cooking oils, stale beer, and old wholegrain flour. Prevention: exclude oxygen, refrigerate, use antioxidants (tocopherols, rosemary extract), minimise PUFA exposure to air after grinding or opening.

TCA — cork taint

2,4,6-Trichloroanisole (TCA) is formed when environmental fungi (Botrytis, Aspergillus, Penicillium) methylate chlorophenols — compounds that form when chlorine-based sanitisers react with wood phenolics in wine corks or barrel staves. The result is a compound with a detection threshold around 1–2 ng/L in wine (parts per trillion) that smells like damp cardboard and mouldy newspaper.

Crucially, TCA does more than just add an off-smell: it suppresses olfactory receptor responses by blocking intracellular cyclic AMP signalling in olfactory neurons. A wine with TCA at sub-threshold concentrations may not smell "corky" at all — it will simply seem flat, dull, and devoid of fruit. This is why TCA is so problematic: at very low doses it acts as an olfactory suppressor rather than an identifiable off-odour, making the wine seem just unremarkable rather than visibly faulty. The taint source can also be cardboard packaging and wooden storage shelves (not just corks), so switching to screw-cap closures reduces but does not eliminate the risk.

Geosmin — earthy, beetroot, petrichor

Geosmin ((4S,4aR,8aS)-octahydro-4,8a-dimethyl-4a(2H)-naphthalenol) is a bicyclic alcohol produced by Streptomyces bacteria in soil and by some cyanobacteria in water. Detection threshold in water: approximately 4 ng/L. It is responsible for the "earthy" note in fresh beetroot, some freshwater fish (catfish, carp), and for petrichor — the characteristic smell of rain on dry earth, caused by geosmin released when raindrops impact dry soil and aerosolize surface material.

In cooking: geosmin is volatile and can be driven off by heat. It is also acid-labile — adding vinegar or citric acid during cooking converts geosmin to a non-volatile form, which is why beet preparations with vinegar taste less earthy than boiled plain beet. For freshwater fish with earthy off-notes, marinating in an acidic medium (buttermilk, citrus) before cooking significantly reduces the geosmin load.

TMA — fish odour

Marine fish muscle contains high concentrations of trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO), an odourless compound used as an osmolyte to counter the desiccating effect of seawater. When the fish dies, bacteria reduce TMAO to trimethylamine (TMA) — the compound responsible for the characteristic "fishy" smell. TMA is a volatile amine with a low detection threshold; its concentration increases with time post-slaughter and with temperature.

TMA is a weak base (pKa ~9.8). Acid protonates it: TMA + H⁺ → TMA-H⁺ (trimethylammonium ion), which is ionic, non-volatile, and odourless. This is the exact mechanism by which lemon juice "de-fishifies" fish — the acid does not remove the TMA, it simply renders it non-volatile. The effect is immediate and effective at any pH below about 7. This is also why freshwater fish (which do not use TMAO as an osmolyte) do not develop a fishy smell in the same way.

Brett — barnyard, leather, medicinal

Brettanomyces bruxellensis (Brett) is a spoilage yeast that metabolises hydroxycinnamic acids (ferulic acid, p-coumaric acid) in wine and beer into vinyl phenols and then into 4-ethylphenol (barnyard, leather, Band-Aid) and 4-ethylguaiacol (smoked meat, spice, medicinal). The individual perception threshold for 4-ethylphenol in red wine is ~420 µg/L; it is considered a fault above ~600 µg/L.

Brett is controversial because low levels can add complexity — some classic styles (Châteauneuf-du-Pape, traditional Burgundy, Belgian farmhouse ales) are associated with low-level Brett character, which becomes "rustic complexity" rather than a defect. This illustrates a general principle: off-compounds exist on a spectrum. Diacetyl is a defect in lager but a desired character in Chardonnay and some ales. Context, concentration, and cultural expectation all determine whether a compound is a flaw or a feature.

Worked examples
Example 1 — Why does lemon juice reduce the fishy smell?
A fillet of sea bass has been in the refrigerator for two days and smells mildly fishy. A cook squeezes fresh lemon juice over it immediately before serving and the fishiness largely disappears within seconds. Explain the chemistry precisely — where does the smell go, and why does acid produce this effect so rapidly?

Source of the smell:
Marine fish muscle contains trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO), an odourless osmolyte. After the fish dies, bacteria (primarily Shewanella and Pseudomonas species on the surface) reduce TMAO to trimethylamine (TMA) using it as a terminal electron acceptor. TMA is a volatile tertiary amine with a detection threshold around 1 µg/L and a very distinctive fishy smell.

The acid mechanism:
TMA is a Brønsted base, with a pKa of ~9.8 in water. At neutral pH (~7), the equilibrium strongly favours the protonated form (trimethylammonium ion, TMA-H⁺) — about 99.8% protonated — but TMA is still measurably volatile in its free base form. At pH ~2 from lemon juice, essentially 100% of TMA is protonated. The ionic form TMA-H⁺ has negligible vapour pressure: it cannot evaporate from the fish surface, so it never reaches the olfactory epithelium.

Key distinction:
The TMA is not destroyed or removed — it is still present in the fish, now as a tasteless ionic salt. The smell has been physically rendered non-volatile. If you were to neutralise the acid with baking soda, the fishy smell would partially return as TMA was re-liberated in its free base form. The effect is instantaneous because the acid-base equilibrium is fast — no reaction or degradation is required, just proton transfer.

Why only marine fish?
Freshwater fish do not use TMAO for osmoregulation (they live in hypotonic rather than hypertonic environments). They contain little to no TMAO, so no TMA is produced after death, and they do not develop the same fishy smell. "Fishiness" in freshwater species (catfish, carp) typically comes from geosmin instead, which has a different chemistry and is not reduced by acid.

Example 2 — Maximising umami in a broth
A chef wants to build the most intensely umami-rich broth possible from: dried kombu, fresh shiitake mushrooms, canned tomatoes, chicken carcasses, and dried shiitake. Design a preparation strategy that maximises umami using the chemistry of the T1R1+T1R3 receptor and the glutamate–nucleotide synergy.

Step 1 — Identify glutamate and nucleotide sources:

  • Free glutamate sources: Kombu (~2.5 g/100g dry weight — the richest known natural source), canned/roasted tomatoes (~0.14 g/100g fresh, concentrated by canning), long-simmered chicken carcasses (collagen hydrolysis releases glutamate-containing peptides; also some free glutamate).
  • Nucleotide sources: Chicken carcasses and meat (IMP — inosine monophosphate, from ATP degradation post-slaughter); dried shiitake mushrooms (GMP — guanosine monophosphate, concentrated by drying because enzymatic activity converts adenosine to GMP before the mushroom fully dries out).

Step 2 — Preparation protocols:

  • Kombu: Extract at 60–65°C for 45 minutes. Above ~80°C, kombu releases mucilaginous compounds (alginates) that produce an unpleasant slimy texture and slightly bitter notes. The glutamate extracts readily at 60°C — high temperature is not needed.
  • Chicken: Simmer the carcasses for 3–4 hours. IMP is present in the muscle from ATP degradation (a post-mortem process already complete by the time you buy the bird). Long simmering extracts it along with gelatin and small peptides.
  • Dried shiitake: Rehydrate in cold water overnight (not boiling water). Cold extraction preserves more of the GMP content. Add the mushrooms and soaking liquid to the final broth.
  • Tomatoes: Use slow-roasted or concentrated canned tomatoes rather than fresh — concentration increases free glutamate per gram significantly. Add late to avoid overcooking acidic compounds.

Step 3 — Combine for maximum synergy:
The critical insight: glutamate (from kombu + tomatoes) × nucleotides (IMP from chicken + GMP from shiitake) = synergistic amplification of 8–30×. Using both IMP and GMP simultaneously may enhance synergy further, since they appear to bind at partially distinct sites or stabilize the receptor via slightly different conformational changes. Reduction at the end concentrates all free glutamate, amplifying the effect. Do not add salt until the very end — salt suppresses bitter notes but can mask the rounded, sustained umami character if added too early during reduction.

Example 3 — Diagnosing a faulty wine
At dinner, a bottle of red Burgundy smells of damp cardboard with no fruit. The sommelier suggests it's "reduction" that will blow off with swirling. You suspect TCA cork taint. How would you distinguish the two faults, and what would you predict about whether the smell will dissipate?

The two faults:

  • Reduction (sulfur compounds): H₂S, methanethiol, or DMS produced by yeast under low-nutrient fermentation conditions. These are volatile at wine pH and do dissipate with swirling/aeration — the off-compounds evaporate off the glass. A reductive wine often also has a matchstick or struck-flint note alongside damp basement.
  • TCA cork taint: TCA is not produced by the wine — it is absorbed from tainted cork material. It is stable and does not evaporate on swirling. At sub-perceptual threshold concentrations, TCA suppresses the olfactory response to fruit aromas, making the wine smell dull and flat rather than overtly "corky."

Diagnostic protocol:

  1. Pour a small amount. Cover the glass with your palm for 60 seconds, then nose immediately. If the off-character diminished, reduction is likely — accumulation under the glass then release is characteristic of volatile sulfides. If unchanged or worse, suspect TCA.
  2. Smell the cork directly. TCA tends to be concentrated in the cork material before it migrates into the wine. A musty, damp-cardboard cork odour strongly implicates TCA.
  3. Swirl vigorously for 3–5 minutes. Reductive wines often improve noticeably. TCA wines will not.

Prediction:
If it is TCA, the wine will not improve with time or decanting. It is irreversibly tainted. The sommelier's suggestion about reduction is plausible but testable within 5 minutes at the table. A wine that shows no improvement after aggressive aeration should be returned. The important nuance: if the TCA is at a sub-threshold level, the wine may not smell actively corky — it will simply seem thin and flavourless, which can be mistaken for a bad vintage or poor storage rather than a chemical fault.

Food for thought
  • Wine tasters and perfumers can identify dozens of distinct aromatic compounds in a single sample — yet humans only have ~400 functional olfactory receptor genes, compared to a dog's ~1,000. How does expertise improve olfactory discrimination if the hardware is fixed? And what does this imply about what "detecting" a smell actually means at the neural level?
    The ~400 receptor genes are not a ceiling on discrimination — they are the sensors. What experts improve is not the sensitivity of individual receptors but the brain's ability to parse, label, and categorise the combinatorial patterns that reach the olfactory bulb. Studies of trained tasters show increased functional connectivity between the olfactory bulb and frontal cortex (the region associated with naming and working memory) compared to novices. The difference is not in the nose — it is in the cortical representation of smell patterns as labelled, discrete objects ("green pepper = 2-isobutyl-3-methoxypyrazine") rather than undifferentiated backgrounds. This is the same process as a musician hearing individual instruments in an orchestra where a novice hears only sound — the audio signal is identical, but the parsed representation is different. It also implies that smell vocabulary genuinely affects smell perception: people who have precise names for aromatic categories (trained sommeliers, perfumers, spice traders) can identify and recall them more accurately than people who lack the vocabulary, even given identical sensory hardware.
  • The flavour pairing hypothesis predicts that ingredients sharing dominant aroma compounds should pair well. Yet traditional West African, Korean, and Indian cuisines consistently pair ingredients with almost no shared volatiles — and produce highly complex, harmonious flavours. What alternative principle might explain why these pairings work, and what does the cross-cultural data tell us about the limits of a purely chemical theory of flavour?
    Several alternative principles account for what "shared compounds" cannot. First, aromatic contrast and complementarity: a cuisine built on fermented fish paste, sesame, gochugaru, and garlic covers a broader span of the aromatic space (sulfurous, nutty, spicy, pungent-allium) than any two of its ingredients share — the combination is richer precisely because the aromas don't overlap. Second, textural and temperature contrast amplifies perceived flavour complexity: the same dish hot vs. cold, with vs. without crunchy garnish, produces meaningfully different flavour experiences because proprioceptive and temperature signals integrate with olfaction in the brain's flavour perception system. Third, Maillard and fermentation products in virtually every traditional cuisine generate pyrazines, furanones, and thiols in common — the "background" of cooked food is already shared; what distinguishes cuisines is the aromatic foreground. The deeper limitation of the hypothesis is that it treats flavour as a property of individual compounds rather than of a system: aroma is perceived contextually, and the same compound can be attractive (coffee thiol) or repellent (rotten-meat thiol) depending entirely on what surrounds it.
  • Anosmia (the complete inability to smell) is a known side effect of some viral infections, head trauma, and neurological conditions. Patients with complete anosmia can still taste sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami normally. Many report that food is "flavourless" and eating becomes unpleasant or purely functional. Some develop parosmia on recovery — distorted smells where familiar foods smell rotten or chemical. What does the experience of anosmia and parosmia reveal about how the brain constructs flavour, and why might familiar foods smell especially distorted (not unfamiliar ones) during parosmia recovery?
    Anosmia confirms that taste alone is insufficient for the experience of food as pleasurable or diverse — the five basic tastes provide structure (sweet, savoury, acid) but not the specificity that makes one food different from another. The hedonic aspects of eating are heavily olfactory-dependent: food reward, appetite, and food choice are all driven by retronasal aroma in a way they are not driven by basic taste. Parosmia is more revealing about the neural architecture. The prevailing model: olfactory receptor neurons regrow after viral damage, but the new ORNs may re-innervate incorrect glomeruli in the olfactory bulb — wire to the wrong target. The brain then reads a familiar smell pattern (coffee, onion) using the wrong decoder, producing a distorted percept. Familiar foods produce parosmia more severely than unfamiliar ones because the brain has a strong, specific learned representation of what coffee or onion should smell like — the mismatch between the expected and received pattern is jarring. Completely novel odours have no stored representation to violate. This explains why parosmia patients often tolerate unfamiliar foods (novel cuisines, novel ingredients) better than their previously loved favourites.
Common pitfalls
  • Confusing taste and flavour — or smell and aroma. In culinary contexts, people use "taste" to mean the full flavour experience, and "smell" to mean orthonasal sniffing. In food science, "taste" is strictly the five basic modalities; "flavour" is the integrated percept; "aroma" is often used for retronasal olfaction specifically. These distinctions matter when diagnosing problems: a dish that "tastes flat" needs salt, acid, or aromatic complexity — not necessarily more of all three.
  • Thinking you can mask a low-threshold off-compound. Compounds like TCA and geosmin are detectable at concentrations far below any practical flavour addition. A tainted wine cannot be "improved" by adding more flavour — TCA actively suppresses olfactory signalling, making the wine duller the more you hope to detect fruit. Prevent or remove; you cannot override.
  • Treating MSG as chemically distinct from "natural" glutamate. Free L-glutamate activates the T1R1+T1R3 receptor identically regardless of whether it came from a packet of MSG, a reduction of kombu stock, or a chunk of aged Parmesan. The receptor does not know the provenance. The discussion about MSG is a social and historical one, not a chemical one.
  • Adding umami sources without considering the nucleotide synergy. Adding glutamate (soy sauce, MSG, tomato paste) to a dish with no nucleotide source (IMP/GMP) gets some umami amplification from glutamate alone but misses the 8–30× synergy. Pairing glutamate-rich ingredients with IMP sources (meat, shellfish) or GMP sources (dried mushrooms) is what produces deeply savoury, extended flavour.
  • Applying the flavour pairing hypothesis universally. The hypothesis describes tendencies in Western European cuisines; it was not derived from — and does not describe — the logic of most of the world's culinary traditions. Using it as a universal rule for recipe development will produce results that cluster in a narrow aromatic space. Use it as one heuristic, not a law.
Connects to

Heat & Browning — The Maillard reaction generates the majority of the volatile aroma compounds in cooked food: Strecker aldehydes, pyrazines, furanones, and thiols. The flavour science of roasted and baked foods is essentially applied Maillard chemistry.
Fermentation — Fermentation is the other dominant source of food aroma: esters from yeast, organic acids from LAB, sulfur compounds from bacteria, and hundreds of secondary metabolites from mould aging. The character of wine, beer, cheese, and sourdough is largely a volatile chemistry story.
Fats, Oils & Emulsions — Fats are the primary flavour carriers in food: lipophilic aroma compounds (terpenes, lactones, pyrazines) dissolve preferentially in fat and are delivered retronasal during chewing. Fat rancidity (trans-2-nonenal, hexanal) is one of the most common sources of off-flavours in stored food.
