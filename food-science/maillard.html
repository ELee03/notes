Heat & Browning

Maillard Reaction · Caramelisation · Water Activity · pH · Flavour Chemistry

A steak seared in a screaming-hot cast-iron pan. A baguette with a mahogany crust. A cup of dark-roast coffee. Roasted peanuts. Soy sauce. These foods have almost nothing in common — except that they all owe their most prized flavours and aromas to a single family of reactions: the Maillard reaction. Understanding it changes how you think about heat, moisture, and seasoning. It is arguably the most important chemical event in cooking.

What actually happens when food browns?

Browning is not a single thing. The visible colour change, the new aromas, and the flavour complexity are all chemical consequences of the same underlying reactions — but there are two completely different pathways to browning, and they are not the same reaction at different temperatures:

Maillard Reaction Caramelisation
Reactants Amino acids + reducing sugars Sugars only (no protein needed)
Onset temperature ~140°C surface temp ~160–180°C (depends on sugar type)
Flavour profile Roasty, meaty, complex, nutty Sweet, buttery, butterscotch
Key products Hundreds of volatiles + melanoidins (brown polymers) Furans, diacetyl, HMF, caramel pigments
Familiar examples Seared meat, bread crust, roasted coffee Caramel sauce, toffee, crème brûlée top

Most browned food involves both reactions to some degree. Bread crust is heavily Maillard — wheat flour contains proteins (with free amino groups) and starch-derived reducing sugars that react together. But the caramel-sweet notes in crust come from pure caramelisation of surface sugars. Crème brûlée is almost entirely caramelisation — the thin sugar layer has very little protein. Coffee roasting is heavily Maillard — coffee beans are rich in chlorogenic acids, amino acids, and sucrose (which first inverts to glucose + fructose, then participates in Maillard).

The key insight to carry forward: these are mechanistically distinct reactions, not the same reaction at different temperatures. Conflating them will cause confusion in everything that follows.

The Maillard reaction — what it is and what it needs

The reaction is named after Louis-Camille Maillard, a French physician who described it in 1912 while studying amino acid chemistry. He was not trying to explain cooking. He noticed that when amino acids were heated with sugars, a brown colour appeared — and spent years characterising the products. The cooking significance was recognised only decades later.

Two ingredients are required:

  • A reducing sugar — one with a free aldehyde (–CHO) or ketone (C=O) group. Glucose, fructose, lactose, and maltose all qualify. Sucrose does not — its aldehyde is locked in a glycosidic bond and unavailable. Sucrose-only glazes only caramelise; they don't Maillard until sucrose splits into glucose + fructose (via heat or acid).
  • A free amino group — from a free amino acid dissolved in the food, or from the side chain of a lysine residue in a protein. Lysine is particularly reactive because its side chain carries an ε-amino group that is free even when the lysine is incorporated into a protein chain.
Why does sucrose need to split first?
Table sugar (sucrose) is a disaccharide — glucose and fructose joined by a glycosidic bond at their anomeric carbons. This bond blocks both carbonyl groups from reacting with amines. Heat or acid breaks the bond (inversion), releasing free glucose and fructose. This is why acidic marinades (citrus, vinegar) on sugar-glazed food actually accelerate browning — the acid inverts the sucrose and makes the reducing sugars available.
The mechanism — a cascade of hundreds of reactions

The Maillard "reaction" is actually a cascade. The early steps are chemically understood; the later stages produce such complexity that no complete description exists. Here is the broad structure:

Stage 1 — Condensation and Amadori rearrangement (colourless)

The free amino group (–NH₂) attacks the carbonyl carbon of the reducing sugar, forming an unstable Schiff base (an N-substituted glycosylamine). This rearranges via the Amadori rearrangement into a more stable 1-amino-1-deoxy-2-ketose — the Amadori compound. No visible change yet. The food looks exactly the same.

Stage 2 — Fragmentation and Strecker degradation (most of the flavour)

Amadori compounds decompose along several pathways depending on temperature and pH:

  • At acidic conditions: 1,2-enolisation → furfural derivatives and HMF (hydroxymethylfurfural, which smells of cooked sweetness and caramel).
  • At neutral to basic conditions: 2,3-enolisation → reductones and dehydroreductones. These are highly reactive and feed back into later stages.
  • Strecker degradation: reactive dicarbonyl intermediates from Stage 2 react with amino acids, stripping off CO₂ and producing short, volatile aldehydes called Strecker aldehydes. These are among the most potent odour compounds in cooked food. Every amino acid produces a characteristic Strecker aldehyde: leucine → 3-methylbutanal (malty, cheese); cysteine → methanethiol and thiophenes (meat, roasty); proline → 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (bread crust, popcorn, jasmine rice aroma).

Stage 3 — Polymerisation → melanoidins (the colour)

Reactive intermediates undergo aldol condensations and polymerise into large, nitrogen-containing polymers called melanoidins — the brown pigments. Their exact structures are not known; they vary with starting materials and conditions. The visible browning appears here.

Why does seared beef smell different from toasted bread?
Both involve Maillard, but with different amino acid profiles. Beef is rich in cysteine (→ meaty, sulphurous Strecker aldehydes and thiazoles) and glutamic acid. Wheat flour is rich in proline (→ 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline, the bread-crust aroma). Same reaction class, profoundly different odour because the starting amino acid determines which Strecker aldehydes are produced.
What controls the rate of browning?

Four variables dominate: temperature, water activity, pH, and the type of sugar present. They are mostly independent, which means you can tune them separately.

Temperature — the dominant variable

The Maillard reaction obeys Arrhenius kinetics: rate increases exponentially with temperature. The activation energy is roughly 100–130 kJ/mol, which means the rate approximately doubles for every 10°C rise in the relevant range. Practically:

  • Below ~120°C: negligible Maillard (this is why stored dry foods brown only very slowly).
  • ~140°C: noticeably begins. A dry oven at 140°C will eventually brown bread, just slowly.
  • ~160–180°C: rapid browning. Most roasting and searing happens here.
  • Above ~200°C: risk of pyrolysis — burning and acrolein/acrylamide formation overtake Maillard. The sweet spot for controlled browning is 155–185°C surface temperature.

Crucially: this is surface temperature, not internal temperature. A water-based cooking medium (braise, poach, steam) is capped at 100°C — too cold for meaningful Maillard, regardless of how long you cook.

Water activity — the silent enemy of browning

Water inhibits the Maillard reaction in two ways simultaneously:

  1. Evaporative cooling: while liquid water is present on a surface, that surface cannot exceed 100°C. Steam production keeps it cool. No water = surface can reach 160°C+.
  2. Reaction equilibrium: the condensation step (Stage 1) releases water as a by-product. Le Chatelier's principle: excess water pushes the equilibrium backward, slowing the forward reaction.

The optimal water activity for Maillard is approximately 0.5–0.8 — moist enough for reactant mobility, dry enough for the surface temperature to climb. At aw near 1.0 (wet surface), browning is strongly suppressed. At aw near 0 (bone dry), the reactants cannot migrate to react. The sweet spot is a slightly damp hot surface that is actively drying out.

This is why patting meat dry before searing is one of the most impactful technique decisions in the kitchen. It is not about aesthetics — it is about removing the water that is physically preventing browning.

pH — alkaline conditions dramatically accelerate the reaction

At low pH, the amino group is protonated: –NH₃⁺. A protonated amine cannot act as a nucleophile — it has no lone pair available to attack the carbonyl carbon. The condensation step fails to proceed. Raising pH deprotonates the amine, freeing up the lone pair, and the reaction accelerates strongly.

  • Lye-dipped pretzels: a brief dip in NaOH solution (~3–4%, pH ≈ 13) before baking produces an extremely deep mahogany crust in minutes at 200°C. Without the lye, the same pretzel would be light golden at best.
  • Baking soda in cookies: a small addition (0.1–0.2% by flour weight) raises dough pH to ~8–9 and noticeably accelerates browning without producing a detectable soapy taste at these concentrations.
  • Egg white brushed over pastry: egg white is mildly alkaline (pH ~8.5–9) and rich in protein (lysine). Combined: excellent browning promoter.

Reducing sugar type

Not all sugars react at the same rate. Pentoses (5-carbon sugars: ribose, xylose) react fastest due to their higher proportion of open-chain form. Among hexoses: fructose > galactose > glucose. Disaccharides (lactose, maltose) react more slowly because they must partially hydrolyse first. Approximate relative rates at equivalent conditions:

Ribose (~5×)  >  Fructose (~7×)  >  Glucose (1×)  >  Lactose (~0.5×)   (relative to glucose = 1)

Honey is ~38% fructose already in free form — this is why honey glazes produce deeper browning faster than pure sucrose glazes at the same temperature. Milk contains lactose, which browns more slowly than glucose but steadily over time — visible in long-simmered milk-based sauces.

Caramelisation — browning without protein

Caramelisation is the thermal pyrolysis of sugars alone — no amino acids required. When pure sucrose is heated above ~160°C, it begins to dehydrate and fragment, producing a complex mixture of compounds:

  • Furanones and furans (caramel-like, nutty aromas)
  • Diacetyl (buttery, characteristic of fresh caramel)
  • HMF (hydroxymethylfurfural — sweet, slightly chemical)
  • Caramel polymers (the brown colour — less complex than melanoidins)

Onset temperatures by sugar type: fructose (~110°C), glucose (~160°C), sucrose (~185°C). The onset varies with heating rate and presence of acids/bases. Caramelisation flavour is markedly different from Maillard: sweeter, more butterscotch, less complex, and notably absent of the "roasty/meaty" notes that come from amino acid involvement.

In practice, most browning in the kitchen involves both reactions overlapping. A caramel sauce is dominated by caramelisation (pure sugar). Seared meat is dominated by Maillard. Bread crust sits in between — both mechanisms contribute.

Applying this in the kitchen
The rule of two: for any browning goal, ask (1) is the surface temperature above ~150°C? and (2) is the surface sufficiently dry? Both must be yes. Most browning failures trace to one of these being violated.

Searing meat

Pat the surface bone dry. Use a pan with high thermal mass (cast iron) that maintains temperature when cold meat is added. Preheat until just smoking. Don't move the meat immediately — allow the surface to dry and heat before it releases naturally. Don't crowd the pan: each piece releases moisture; too many pieces creates steam, drops pan temperature, and prevents browning. Add a small pinch of baking powder to a dry brine (alkaline) for faster, darker crust formation.

Bread baking

Steam early in the bake: this keeps the crust extensible for oven spring (the final rise burst as yeast dies and trapped CO₂ expands). Remove steam midway — the crust then dries, reaches 160°C+, and browns via Maillard from flour proteins and Strecker degradation. Egg wash = protein + sugar brush = accelerated Maillard = deep golden crust. Milk wash gives a softer, more even golden colour (lactose, which browns more slowly).

Roasted vegetables

Vegetables contain both amino acids (from proteins) and natural sugars — both Maillard reactants. They will brown if: (1) the oven or pan is hot enough, and (2) the surface can dry out rather than steam. Overcrowding a roasting tray is the most common failure: too much vegetable releases too much steam, the surface temperature stays near 100°C, and the vegetables steam-roast instead of caramelising/Maillarding. Use enough surface area. A light coat of oil helps heat transfer and promotes surface drying.

Butter vs. clarified butter

Regular butter contains ~1.5% milk proteins and ~0.5% lactose. These are Maillard reactants. Butter browns (beurre noisette) because the milk solids Maillard at the fat-phase temperature. Clarified butter (ghee) has milk solids removed — it is pure fat and does not brown on its own. It also has a smoke point of ~250°C vs. butter's ~150°C. Use clarified butter when you need high heat without burning; use whole butter when you want the nutty-milk Maillard flavour contribution.

Worked examples
Example 1 — Why a wet steak resists browning
Two identical steaks are seared in the same pan at the same temperature. One is patted bone dry; the other is placed directly from its vacuum-sealed bag (still damp). Explain the difference in browning behaviour using Maillard reaction principles. Consider both the surface temperature mechanism and the reaction equilibrium argument.

Surface temperature argument:
While liquid water is present on the steak's surface, evaporative cooling keeps that surface at approximately 100°C. The latent heat of evaporation of water is 2260 J/g — enormous compared to the heat capacity of the pan. As long as moisture is evaporating, virtually all the heat going into the surface is consumed by vaporisation rather than heating the surface itself. At 100°C, the Maillard reaction rate is negligible (roughly 10,000× slower than at 180°C by Arrhenius). The wet steak cannot brown until the surface moisture is fully driven off — which can take minutes, during which the steak is overcooking internally.

Reaction equilibrium argument:
Stage 1 of the Maillard reaction (Amadori condensation) releases water as a by-product. Le Chatelier's principle: high water concentration at the surface pushes the equilibrium toward the reverse reaction, slowing the forward Maillard pathway. Even if the surface somehow reached 150°C while still wet, the excess water would suppress the reaction thermodynamically.

The dry steak: No surface moisture means the pan's heat immediately raises the surface to 160–180°C. At 180°C, Maillard rate is dramatically faster. Browning begins within 30–60 seconds of contact.

Practical extension: Salt the steak and leave it uncovered on a wire rack in the fridge for 12–24 hours before cooking. Osmosis initially draws moisture to the surface, but refrigerator airflow evaporates it. The result: a very dry surface and lightly brined interior — optimal for both browning and seasoning.

Example 2 — The pretzel lye bath
Traditional German pretzels are briefly dipped in food-grade lye (NaOH solution, ~3–4%) before baking at 220°C. Predict and explain the effect on (a) browning rate and depth, (b) flavour, and (c) texture, compared to an untreated pretzel.

(a) Browning rate and depth:
The dip raises surface pH to approximately 12–13. At this pH: amino groups on wheat proteins (especially lysine) are fully deprotonated and available for the Amadori condensation step. The initial condensation is the rate-limiting step at moderate temperatures; removing the protonation barrier dramatically accelerates the overall cascade. Result: the pretzel achieves a deep mahogany-brown crust within 12–15 minutes at 220°C, compared to a pale golden-brown for the untreated version at the same time and temperature.

(b) Flavour:
Faster Maillard means more Strecker aldehyde production. Wheat's proline content drives production of 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (bread-crust aroma) at higher concentrations. The deep melanoidin formation also contributes slight bitterness — characteristic of proper pretzels. At alkaline conditions, certain Amadori decomposition pathways are also favoured that produce more pyrazines (nutty, roasty aromas). The result is a more complex, intensely "baked" flavour profile than an unbathed pretzel.

(c) Texture:
NaOH saponifies a small amount of surface lipids in the dough and partially gelatinises surface starch. This creates a distinct thick, chewy outer skin rather than the thin crispy crust of an egg-washed bread. The gelatinised starch also browns differently — contributing to the characteristic sheen.

Safety note: Food-grade lye at 3–4% is safe with proper handling. Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) achieves pH ~8.5–9, which gives a milder but real version of the same effect — suitable for home bakers.

Example 3 — Honey vs. sucrose glaze on roasted chicken
A recipe glazes a roasting chicken with either (A) diluted honey or (B) simple syrup (dissolved sucrose). Both are brushed on at the same time, same oven temperature (200°C). Predict which browns faster and to what degree, and explain the chemistry.

Honey (Glaze A):
Honey is approximately 38% fructose, 31% glucose, 7% other sugars, plus trace amino acids from pollen and yeast activity during nectar fermentation. The sugars are already in free reducing form — no hydrolysis step needed. Fructose reacts roughly 7× faster than glucose in the Maillard pathway. The trace amino acids provide their own Maillard reactants independent of the chicken's proteins. Expected: fast, deep browning; rich complex flavour with roasty Maillard character.

Simple syrup (Glaze B):
Pure sucrose has its aldehyde locked in a glycosidic bond — it cannot participate in Maillard without inversion. At 200°C, some thermal inversion occurs, releasing free glucose and fructose, but this is slower than honey's pre-available reducing sugars. Once inverted, it behaves similarly to honey's sugar fraction, but with no amino acid contribution. Expected: slower initial browning than honey; eventual similar caramelisation character but less Maillard complexity.

Verdict: Honey browns earlier and darker, and produces a more complex (more Maillard) flavour. Sucrose glaze produces a cleaner, sweeter, more purely caramel-like result. Recipes that call for "deep golden glaze fast" (Peking duck, glazed ham) use honey or glucose syrup specifically for this reason.

Food for thought
  • The Maillard reaction produces hundreds of distinct volatile compounds simultaneously. Why does this combinatorial complexity in reaction products tend to produce perceived complexity in flavour — what does the human olfactory system actually do with all of that?
    Humans have ~400 functional olfactory receptor types, each tuned to a range of molecules (not one molecule per receptor). Flavour perception is a combinatorial pattern across many simultaneously activated receptors, not a single match. A mixture of 200 distinct volatiles activates dozens of receptor types in a specific pattern that the brain reads as a complex "scene." A single purified Strecker aldehyde smells sharp and chemical; the same aldehyde embedded in a mixture of 200 Maillard products smells like roast chicken. The context — the pattern — creates the perception. Evolution likely tuned this system to distinguish complex mixtures (thoroughly cooked food: safe, calorie-dense, digestible) from simpler profiles (raw food, spoiled food, unripe fruit). Maillard's complexity is essentially the chemical signature of "well cooked" — a signal we are wired to find rewarding.
  • At room temperature, the Maillard reaction still proceeds — just very slowly. What are the practical consequences for food storage, and what conditions would you control to minimise unwanted browning?
    Room-temperature Maillard causes off-flavours and colour changes during storage — called "non-enzymatic browning during storage" or "storage Maillard." It is significant for: dried milk powder (develops stale/cooked off-flavours over months), dried egg products, cereal products, spray-dried coffee, and any dried food with both protein and reducing sugars. A nutritional consequence: lysine (an essential amino acid) is especially reactive in the Maillard pathway and is consumed, reducing the bioavailability of protein in stored dried milk. Control strategies: (1) refrigerate or freeze — Arrhenius means rate drops ~2× per 10°C; at 4°C vs. 25°C, the rate is ~5× slower; (2) reduce water activity to ~0.3–0.4 (below the optimal but still nonzero, which limits reactant mobility); (3) vacuum or inert atmosphere packaging (some Maillard pathways involve oxygen); (4) remove reducing sugars (expensive) or use sugar alcohols (sorbitol, xylitol) which participate weakly in Maillard. The lesson: Maillard is a kinetics problem, not a threshold phenomenon.
  • 2-Acetyl-1-pyrroline (2-AP) is the single compound most responsible for the aroma of freshly baked bread crust. It is also the primary aroma compound in jasmine rice, and appears at trace levels in basmati rice and popcorn. Speculate: why would proline-derived Maillard chemistry produce a compound present in an uncooked grain?
    This is genuinely puzzling and not fully resolved, but the current understanding: jasmine and basmati rice varieties are unusual in that they accumulate 2-AP at measurable levels even before cooking, apparently produced via an enzymatic pathway during grain development (possibly involving proline metabolism and a Δ1-pyrroline intermediate). The Maillard reaction during cooking of any proline-rich food produces 2-AP via the same Δ1-pyrroline intermediate — just by a thermal route rather than enzymatic. So the same compound can be produced by entirely different mechanisms converging on the same intermediate. This is a reminder that flavour compounds are not the exclusive product of cooking — plants and microorganisms produce many of the same volatiles via metabolic pathways, which is why some raw foods smell remarkably like their cooked counterparts.
Common pitfalls
  • Equating Maillard with caramelisation. Different reactants, different temperatures, different products, different flavour profiles. A browned onion is Maillard. A caramel sauce from pure sugar is caramelisation. Knowing which you are triggering matters for technique decisions.
  • Relying on internal temperature as a proxy for browning. Internal temperature and surface temperature are independent variables. A sous-vide steak perfectly cooked to 57°C internally still has a pale, wet surface. Browning requires a separate high-heat step — these are not the same process.
  • Overcrowding the pan. Each piece of food releases moisture as it heats. Too many pieces → collective steam → pan temperature drops → surface temperature stays near 100°C → no browning. Cook in batches or use a pan large enough that pieces are not touching.
  • Ignoring reducing sugar type in glazes. Honey and glucose syrup brown faster and at lower temperatures than sucrose. Honey-glazed items can over-brown quickly if not watched at high oven temperatures. This is not a failure of the recipe — it is a predictable consequence of fructose reactivity.
  • Thinking browning only happens at high heat. Room-temperature Maillard is real, just slow. It affects stored dried foods, causes off-flavours in powder products, and is why some baked goods stale faster at higher humidity.
