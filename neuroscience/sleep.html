Sleep, Arousal & Circadian Rhythms

Sleep Stages · EEG Correlates · Flip-Flop Switch Model · REM Circuits · Functions of Sleep · Circadian Clock · SCN · Molecular Clock

We spend roughly one third of our lives asleep. Despite a century of research, the full function of sleep remains debated — but what's clear is that it is not passive. Sleep is an active, precisely regulated brain state with distinct neural circuits controlling its timing and architecture, specific molecular events that serve the functions of memory consolidation and cellular maintenance, and a profound influence on virtually every system in the body. Understanding sleep means understanding how the brain controls its own state — a question that connects to consciousness, learning, and the treatment of psychiatric and neurological disease.

Sleep Stages

Sleep is not a single state. The electroencephalogram (EEG) — which records the summed electrical activity of millions of cortical neurons — reveals that sleep progresses through distinct stages with characteristic oscillatory patterns.

NREM (non-rapid eye movement) sleep has three stages:

  • N1 (drowsiness) — the transition from wake: alpha waves (8–12 Hz, eyes closed at rest) are replaced by lower-amplitude, mixed-frequency theta waves (4–8 Hz). Brief duration (1–7 min). Hypnic jerks can occur.
  • N2 (light sleep) — characterized by sleep spindles (bursts of 12–15 Hz activity lasting 0.5–3 s, generated by thalamo-cortical loops) and K-complexes (sharp negative deflection followed by positive component, generated by cortex in response to disturbance and during spontaneous cortical "up states"). The most time is spent in N2 (~50% of total sleep).
  • N3 (slow-wave sleep, SWS / deep sleep) — dominated by high-amplitude delta waves (<4 Hz, >75 µV). Slow oscillations alternate between "up states" (widespread cortical firing, lasting ~1 s) and "down states" (neuronal silence, lasting ~0.5 s). Deepest, most restorative sleep; hardest to awaken from. Concentrated in the first half of the night. Growth hormone is released in pulses during N3.

REM (rapid eye movement) sleep — paradoxical sleep: the EEG resembles wake (low-amplitude, high-frequency desynchronized activity), yet the person is deeply asleep and difficult to arouse. Defining features: rapid eye movements, complete skeletal muscle atonia (except for diaphragm and eye muscles), and dreaming (reported by ~80% of subjects awakened from REM). Heart rate and breathing become irregular. Most vivid, narrative dreams occur in REM.

Sleep cycles through NREM and REM roughly every 90 minutes across the night. NREM-heavy cycles dominate early in the night (more SWS), while REM-heavy cycles dominate later (longer REM periods). This architecture means that sleep deprivation preferentially eliminates late-night REM sleep if you go to bed late but wake at the same time.

Typical Sleep Architecture (Hypnogram)

Typical adult sleep over 8 hours. Deep SWS (N3) dominates early; REM periods lengthen toward morning. Each 90-minute cycle transitions through N1→N2→N3→N2→REM.

Neural Circuits of Sleep and Wake

Wakefulness is maintained by a set of ascending arousal systems in the brainstem and hypothalamus, all projecting diffusely throughout the brain. Each uses a different neurotransmitter:

  • Locus coeruleus (LC) — norepinephrine (NE); fires during wake and arousing stimuli, silent during sleep. NE promotes wakefulness and attentional alertness.
  • Dorsal raphe nucleus — serotonin (5-HT); active during wake, reduced in NREM, virtually silent in REM ("REM-off").
  • Tuberomammillary nucleus (TMN) — histamine; wake-active. Antihistamines (diphenhydramine) cause drowsiness by blocking H1 receptors in the brain.
  • Basal forebrain — acetylcholine; active during wake and REM sleep; promotes cortical activation (desynchronization).
  • Lateral hypothalamus — orexin (hypocretin); wake-promoting and stabilizing. Orexin neurons project to and reinforce all arousal systems.

Sleep is actively generated (not merely a cessation of wakefulness) by the ventrolateral preoptic area (VLPO) of the anterior hypothalamus. VLPO neurons are sleep-active: they fire maximally during NREM sleep, releasing GABA and galanin to inhibit the arousal systems (LC, raphe, TMN, basal forebrain).

The flip-flop switch model (Saper et al.): VLPO and the arousal systems mutually inhibit each other. VLPO inhibits arousal → arousal weakens → VLPO disinhibited → stronger VLPO inhibition → sleep deepens. The arousal systems inhibit VLPO → VLPO weakens → arousal systems disinhibited → wake strengthens. This mutual inhibition creates a bistable switch — the system is either in the "wake" state or the "sleep" state, with rapid transitions between them. This explains why sleep-wake transitions are abrupt, not gradual.

Orexin stabilizes the switch: Orexin neurons project to both VLPO (weak excitation) and arousal systems (strong excitation). Their net effect is to hold the system in the wake state and prevent inappropriate switches to sleep. Loss of orexin neurons (which occurs in narcolepsy) destabilizes the switch — patients have sudden transitions to sleep (sleep attacks) and direct transitions to REM without NREM (cataplexy — sudden muscle atonia triggered by strong emotion, essentially REM atonia invading wakefulness).

Homeostatic sleep pressure (Process S): adenosine accumulates in the basal forebrain during waking (a byproduct of neuronal ATP use). It inhibits wake-promoting neurons and increases the drive to sleep — the longer you're awake, the sleepier you feel. Caffeine works by blocking adenosine A1/A2A receptors, preventing adenosine from sensing accumulated sleep debt. Sleep clears adenosine (partly via the glymphatic system — see below).

REM Sleep Circuits

REM sleep is controlled by a different brainstem circuit, centred on the pontine tegmentum:

  • REM-on neurons: cholinergic and glutamatergic neurons in the PPT/LDT (pedunculopontine/laterodorsal tegmental nuclei) become active during REM. They generate ascending activation of the thalamus and cortex (producing the waking-like EEG), drive the PGO (ponto-geniculo-occipital) waves that produce the characteristic eye movements, and activate brainstem circuits that cause muscle atonia via glycinergic hyperpolarization of spinal motor neurons.
  • REM-off neurons: noradrenergic (LC) and serotonergic (raphe) neurons are virtually silent during REM, releasing their inhibition of cholinergic REM-on cells — this releases REM.

The muscle atonia of REM sleep prevents acting out dreams. REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD) occurs when atonia circuits fail (typically due to brainstem degeneration in early Parkinson's disease or Lewy body dementia): patients physically act out their dreams, sometimes injuring themselves or bed partners. RBD is now recognized as a prodromal marker for synucleinopathies — it can precede overt Parkinson's by 10+ years.

Functions of Sleep

Memory consolidation: The hippocampal sharp-wave ripple (100–200 Hz oscillation in CA1, occurring during NREM N2 and N3) coordinates with thalamic sleep spindles and cortical slow oscillations in a precise temporal sequence: hippocampal ripples are nested within spindles, which are nested within the up-states of slow oscillations. This three-way nesting synchronizes hippocampal replay with windows of cortical excitability, allowing hippocampal traces to drive cortical synaptic changes (systems consolidation, Lesson 8). Optogenetic disruption of ripples selectively impairs spatial memory tested after sleep.

Synaptic homeostasis hypothesis (SHY) (Tononi and Cirelli): During wake, cortical synapses are continuously strengthened by activity-dependent LTP (learning). Over a waking day, total synaptic strength increases. Sleep (particularly slow-wave sleep) globally downscales synaptic weights (synaptic homeostasis), reducing total synaptic strength back toward a baseline. This renormalisation removes noise (weakly learned, spurious connections) while preserving the relative differences (the learned signal is in the ratio of weights, not their absolute values). SHY explains why sleep restriction impairs learning, why cortical slow oscillations in SWS might reflect synaptic renormalisation, and why we feel "refreshed" after sleep. It contrasts with the memory consolidation hypothesis: where consolidation says sleep selectively strengthens specific memories, SHY says sleep broadly downscales, which passively enhances the signal-to-noise of the remaining network.

Glymphatic clearance: During NREM sleep, brain cells shrink by ~60%, expanding the extracellular space. CSF flows through perivascular spaces, driving interstitial fluid exchange and clearing metabolic waste — including β-amyloid and tau (the pathological proteins of Alzheimer's disease). Sleep deprivation increases β-amyloid accumulation in the interstitium. This suggests that chronic sleep deprivation may contribute to Alzheimer's pathology by impairing the nightly clearance of toxic aggregates — one of the most striking recent connections between sleep neuroscience and neurological disease.

Sleep also serves hormonal regulation (growth hormone, cortisol follow sleep-dependent rhythms), immune function (cytokine release, T-cell trafficking), metabolic regulation (glucose homeostasis — sleep deprivation causes insulin resistance within days), and emotional regulation (REM sleep reduces the emotional charge of memories, possibly by replaying them in a low-NE/NE-silent environment).

The Circadian Clock

Sleep timing is controlled by two interacting processes: the homeostatic sleep drive (Process S, adenosine) and the circadian clock (Process C). The circadian clock generates an approximately 24-hour rhythm in virtually every cell in the body, coordinated by the master pacemaker in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus.

The SCN receives light input directly from the retina via the retinohypothalamic tract, carried by intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) that express the photopigment melanopsin (peak sensitivity ~480 nm, blue light). Unlike rod and cone photoreceptors, ipRGCs respond to sustained illumination and encode ambient light levels for circadian entrainment, not image formation.

The molecular clock operates in virtually every cell — it is a cell-autonomous transcription-translation feedback loop:

  1. CLOCK and BMAL1 proteins heterodimerize and drive transcription of Period (Per1, Per2, Per3) and Cryptochrome (Cry1, Cry2) genes.
  2. PER and CRY proteins accumulate, dimerize, and enter the nucleus to inhibit CLOCK:BMAL1 transcriptional activity — negative feedback.
  3. As PER/CRY proteins are degraded (via ubiquitination; FBXL3 is a key ubiquitin ligase for CRY), the inhibition is relieved, allowing CLOCK:BMAL1 to restart transcription.
  4. The loop period: ~24 hours, determined by the degradation rates and nuclear transport delays.

Mutations in clock genes shift or shorten/lengthen the period: familial advanced sleep phase syndrome (ASPS, the "lark" extreme) is associated with Per2 mutations; delayed sleep phase syndrome (DSPS, extreme "owls") involves abnormal Per3 or CRY1 variants. Complete clock disruption in SCN-lesioned animals destroys circadian rhythmicity, though the animals still sleep the same total amount — confirming that the clock controls timing but not the homeostatic need for sleep.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Narcolepsy and orexin
A patient presents with excessive daytime sleepiness, sudden muscle weakness triggered by strong emotion (laughter, surprise), and direct entry into REM at sleep onset. Why do these symptoms occur together, and what is the likely pathology?

This is classic narcolepsy with cataplexy (Type 1 narcolepsy), caused by selective loss of orexin-producing neurons in the lateral hypothalamus (autoimmune destruction, usually triggered in childhood).

Loss of orexin destabilizes the flip-flop switch between wake and sleep — without orexin's reinforcement of the wake state, the brain makes inappropriate sudden transitions to sleep (sleep attacks) even during activities. The REM atonia system can intrude into wakefulness (cataplexy: triggered by emotion because emotional circuits directly activate limbic inputs to the brainstem REM circuits, which are no longer held in check by orexin). Direct sleep-onset REM (SOREM) reflects the same destabilization: without proper NREM before REM, the system skips to REM. The CSF orexin level in Type 1 narcolepsy is undetectable (<110 pg/mL). Treatment: modafinil/solriamfetol (promote wakefulness), sodium oxybate (consolidates nocturnal sleep), venlafaxine (suppresses REM/cataplexy).

Food for Thought
  • The SHY hypothesis and the memory consolidation hypothesis both predict that sleep is important for learning, but for different reasons. Is there a way to distinguish them experimentally?
    Several predictions differ. SHY predicts that sleep selectively renormalizes synaptic weights globally — you'd expect post-sleep EEG to show reduced delta slow-wave amplitude (reflecting lower average synaptic strength). Consolidation predicts that specific reactivated memories are strengthened. You can test these by (a) using SWS suppression while leaving total sleep intact — SHY predicts this should impair all learning; consolidation predicts only declarative memory should suffer; (b) targeting ripple disruption during sleep — only consolidation theory predicts this should impair specific recent memories while leaving remote ones intact. Current evidence supports elements of both: synaptic renormalization occurs, AND specific memory reactivation and strengthening occurs during sleep. They may operate in sequence or in different circuits.
  • Blue light (480 nm) from screens suppresses melatonin and delays circadian phase. Why does this happen at the molecular level, and why is the effect stronger in the evening than in the morning?
    ipRGCs expressing melanopsin (peak ~480 nm) project to the SCN. Evening light delays the molecular clock by driving Per gene expression at a clock phase when it would normally decline — pushing the free-running cycle later. Morning light advances the clock (same light, different phase of the molecular feedback loop). This phase-dependent response is described by the Phase Response Curve (PRC) — it is an intrinsic property of the CLOCK:BMAL1/PER feedback loop, and the SCN's current clock phase determines whether light advances, delays, or has no effect on the rhythm. This is also why jet lag is asymmetric: westward travel (phase advance by evening light) is easier to adapt to than eastward travel (phase delay required).
Common Pitfalls
  • Thinking REM sleep is the only sleep stage that matters for memory. NREM SWS is critical for declarative memory consolidation (hippocampal replay during ripples). REM sleep is particularly important for procedural and emotional memory consolidation. Both stages contribute; they serve different memory types.
  • Thinking sleep is passive (the brain is "off"). Sleep is actively generated by VLPO. During sleep, the brain shows organized oscillations (slow waves, spindles, ripples) that serve specific functions. The thalamus in burst mode drives cortical slow oscillations. Metabolic activity falls only ~10–15% during sleep compared to relaxed waking.
  • Conflating circadian rhythms and homeostatic sleep pressure. These are independent processes that summate. You can be circadianly awake but homestatically sleepy (jet lag). You can have accumulated sleep debt but be temporarily overridden by peak circadian alertness in the late afternoon (the "wake maintenance zone"). Melatonin is a circadian signal (from SCN → pineal), not a direct sleep inducer; it works by reinforcing the circadian signal, not by increasing homeostatic pressure.
