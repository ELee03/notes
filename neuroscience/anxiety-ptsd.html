Anxiety & PTSD

Fear vs Anxiety · Amygdala-PFC Circuit · GAD · Panic · Social Anxiety · PTSD as Memory Disorder · Extinction Failure · Treatment

Anxiety disorders are the most common category of psychiatric condition — lifetime prevalence around 30% in the general population. They share a core biology: the fear circuit running in excess, or failing to be adequately regulated by prefrontal cortex. PTSD is covered here because its core mechanism — extinction failure — is a specific dysfunction of the same amygdala-PFC circuitry, though PTSD is now classified separately from anxiety disorders in DSM-5.

Fear vs Anxiety: A Useful Distinction

Fear is a response to a present, identifiable threat. It is adaptive — it mobilizes the body for fight or flight and terminates when the threat passes. Anxiety is anticipatory concern about a future or ambiguous threat. Normal anxiety is also adaptive: it motivates preparation, vigilance, and avoidance of genuinely risky situations.

Anxiety disorders arise when this response is disproportionate to the actual threat, persists beyond the threat context, or generalizes to situations that are not genuinely dangerous. The key neurological change: the amygdala's threat-detection system is activated too readily, and prefrontal circuits that normally regulate and inhibit amygdala activity are insufficiently engaged.

The main anxiety disorders share this biology but differ in what triggers the response:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD): pervasive, difficult-to-control worry about multiple domains of life (health, finances, relationships, work). Chronic muscle tension, fatigue, sleep disruption, difficulty concentrating. The distinguishing feature is that the worry is not tied to specific triggers — it shifts and generalizes.
  • Panic Disorder: recurrent unexpected panic attacks (sudden intense fear with physical symptoms — heart racing, shortness of breath, derealization, fear of dying or losing control) plus persistent worry about having further attacks. Panic attacks are driven by a mis-firing interoceptive threat response — the body's signals (elevated heart rate, breathlessness) are catastrophically misinterpreted as life-threatening.
  • Social Anxiety Disorder: intense fear of social or performance situations, driven by fear of negative evaluation. Distinct from shyness — it causes clinically significant impairment and avoidance.
  • Specific Phobias: fear tied to specific objects or situations (heights, spiders, blood, flying). The fear is recognized as disproportionate but the emotional response cannot be controlled by knowing this.
  • Agoraphobia: fear of situations where escape would be difficult or help unavailable if a panic attack occurred — often develops as a secondary consequence of panic disorder.
The Amygdala-PFC Circuit

The core circuit is covered in detail in the Emotion lesson. Briefly: the basolateral amygdala (BLA) receives sensory input and computes threat associations via fear conditioning; the central amygdala (CeA) drives fear responses (autonomic activation, freezing, HPA axis); the prefrontal cortex — particularly the ventromedial PFC (vmPFC) and infralimbic cortex — inhibits amygdala output during extinction (when a previously dangerous cue is no longer dangerous).

In anxiety disorders:

  • The amygdala is hyperreactive — responds to a wider range of stimuli, at lower thresholds
  • vmPFC inhibitory control over the amygdala is reduced — the extinction circuit is weak
  • The anterior insula and dorsal ACC show heightened activity, particularly during interoceptive threat and worry

This profile produces the characteristic pattern: threat responses that are triggered too easily, and cannot be terminated by top-down reasoning ("I know this is irrational") because the inhibitory circuit is not the cognitive knowledge circuit. Knowing you should not be afraid does not engage the same prefrontal pathways that suppress amygdala activity during extinction.

PTSD: Anxiety Disorder or Memory Disorder?

PTSD follows exposure to traumatic events (threat to life, serious injury, sexual violence) and involves:

  • Re-experiencing: intrusive memories, nightmares, flashbacks — involuntary re-activation of the traumatic memory in a way that feels present rather than remembered
  • Avoidance: of trauma-related cues, thoughts, and feelings
  • Negative cognitions and mood: persistent distorted beliefs ("I am to blame," "the world is completely dangerous"), emotional numbing, persistent negative emotions
  • Hyperarousal: hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, sleep disruption, irritability and aggression

PTSD is usefully understood as a memory pathology as much as an anxiety disorder. Traumatic memory is encoded differently from ordinary memory: the high noradrenergic and cortisol state during a traumatic event strengthens amygdala-dependent emotional memory encoding while impairing hippocampal contextual encoding. The result is a fragmented, intensely emotional memory trace with weak contextual information — which is why trauma memories are vivid and emotionally overwhelming but often lack coherent narrative structure, and why they are triggered by sensory cues (a smell, a sound) that share features with the original event.

The core maintenance mechanism is extinction failure. In healthy fear extinction, the vmPFC learns that the CS (conditioned stimulus) no longer predicts threat, and suppresses CeA output. In PTSD, this extinction learning is impaired — the hippocampus and vmPFC are hypoactive (particularly the hippocampus, which is reduced in volume in PTSD, likely due to glucocorticoid toxicity), while the amygdala remains hyperreactive. Exposure-based therapies work precisely by driving extinction learning: they bring the patient into contact with trauma-related cues in a safe context, giving the vmPFC-hippocampus system the opportunity to learn new safety associations.

Memory reconsolidation is another active treatment target: when a memory is retrieved, it becomes temporarily labile (reconsolidation window). Pharmacological interference during this window (propranolol blocks noradrenergic reconsolidation) or pairing retrieval with extinction learning (EMDR may partially work this way) can update or weaken the original fear trace. The science is promising but the clinical translation remains in progress.

Treatment

Psychotherapy is first-line for all anxiety disorders and PTSD:

  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): challenges catastrophic thinking patterns; behavioural experiments test feared outcomes
  • Exposure therapy: systematic exposure to feared stimuli, either gradually (systematic desensitization) or intensively (flooding/prolonged exposure for PTSD). The most powerful intervention for specific phobias and PTSD. Works by driving extinction learning.
  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing): originally developed for PTSD; bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tapping) during trauma memory processing. Has good empirical support. Mechanism disputed — may work via reconsolidation disruption or attentional dual-tasking effects.

Pharmacotherapy: SSRIs and SNRIs are first-line for GAD, social anxiety, and PTSD. They reduce amygdala reactivity and facilitate extinction learning over weeks. Benzodiazepines rapidly reduce acute anxiety but are not recommended for long-term anxiety disorder treatment — they do not facilitate extinction (they may actually impair it), carry dependence risk, and often worsen anxiety when discontinued. They are appropriate for short-term use (acute panic) or specific situations (flight phobia, procedural anxiety).

Beta-blockers (propranolol) for performance anxiety (social anxiety, situational): reduce the somatic symptoms (heart racing, tremor) that fuel the catastrophic interpretation cycle in panic. No evidence for long-term anxiety treatment.

Common Pitfalls
  • "Just don't worry about it / face your fears." The cognitive knowledge that a fear is irrational does not engage the prefrontal extinction circuit. Exposure therapy works because it drives extinction learning at the circuit level — simply telling yourself not to be anxious does not.
  • "Benzodiazepines are the right treatment for anxiety." They reduce acute anxiety but impair extinction learning, leading to long-term maintenance of the anxiety disorder. They are appropriate for acute use, not as ongoing treatment.
  • "PTSD means you're weak / didn't handle trauma well." PTSD is a neurobiological consequence of overwhelming stress — the hippocampus is literally smaller in PTSD. Vulnerability depends on genetic factors, prior trauma history, and the nature of the trauma, not character strength.
  • "Flashbacks are just vivid memories." Flashbacks involve a qualitatively different experience from ordinary remembering — the traumatic event is re-experienced as if happening now, with full physiological arousal, not recalled as a past event. This reflects the abnormal encoding of trauma memory.
