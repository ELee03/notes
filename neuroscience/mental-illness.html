Mental Illness: An Introduction

What Psychiatry Studies · Incomplete Biology · Reverse-Engineered Theories · Epistemic Honesty · Reading the Condition Lessons

Psychiatric disorders are disorders of brain circuits — and the preceding lessons in this cluster have been building exactly the foundation needed to understand them. Reward dysfunction, fear circuit dysregulation, prefrontal-limbic imbalance, synaptic plasticity gone wrong: the neuroscience of mental illness is continuous with the neuroscience of normal function.

But before diving into individual conditions, it is worth being honest about where the field stands — because the popular picture of psychiatric neuroscience is substantially more confident than the actual science warrants.

The Honest State of the Field

Almost everything we think we know about the biology of mental illness was reverse-engineered from drugs that were discovered by accident. Chlorpromazine — the first antipsychotic — was an antihistamine being tested for surgical sedation. Imipramine — the first antidepressant — was being tested as another antihistamine. Lithium was found to calm guinea pigs. None of these were designed to treat psychiatric conditions. They worked, and we then built theories of illness from their mechanisms of action.

This matters because reverse-engineered theories have a structural bias: they tell you about the receptors and pathways that happen to be targeted by the drugs we stumbled upon, not necessarily about the primary causes of the conditions. The fact that blocking D2 receptors reduces hallucinations does not mean hallucinations are caused by excess dopamine signalling, any more than the fact that aspirin reduces fever means fever is caused by insufficient aspirin.

The consequences of this are visible everywhere in the field:

  • The serotonin hypothesis of depression — the "chemical imbalance" story — is probably not correct as stated. Large studies do not find consistently lower serotonin in depressed people. SSRIs raise serotonin within hours; clinical benefit takes weeks. The story is incomplete at best.
  • The dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia captures something real — striatal dopamine is clearly involved — but it cannot explain negative and cognitive symptoms, which are often more disabling than the hallucinations the drugs treat.
  • We do not have a single psychiatric condition for which we have a clear, validated causal biological mechanism from first principles. Every model is a hypothesis, most are decades old, and none fully predicts treatment response.

This is not a reason to dismiss biological psychiatry — it has produced real treatments that genuinely help people. It is a reason to hold the models with appropriate humility, and to be alert to the difference between "this drug works and targets X" and "this condition is caused by insufficient/excessive X."

What Biology Does Show

Despite the caveats, the neuroscience of psychiatric conditions has produced genuine knowledge:

  • These are brain conditions. Structural and functional differences in psychiatric disorders are observable in brain imaging, genetics, and post-mortem tissue. They are not products of weak character, bad parenting, or insufficient willpower.
  • Heritability is real and high. Schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and ADHD are among the most heritable conditions in medicine — more heritable than most physical diseases. The genetic architecture is complex (many variants of small effect), but the biological basis is not in doubt.
  • Circuit models are useful even if incomplete. Understanding the amygdala-PFC fear circuit helps understand anxiety and PTSD. Understanding cortico-striato-thalamic loops helps understand OCD. The circuit models don't fully explain causation, but they organize the phenomena and guide treatment targets.
  • Psychotherapy and medication converge on the same circuits. Brain imaging shows that effective CBT and effective pharmacotherapy both normalize the same overactive or underactive circuits — just via different input channels. This suggests the biology is real even when the intervention is psychological.
Scope of the Following Lessons

The lessons that follow cover seven conditions in depth. The organizing principle for each is:

  1. Phenomenology first — what the condition actually is like from the inside, including the features that popular descriptions miss or distort.
  2. What the neuroscience shows — circuit models, genetics, neuroimaging findings, with explicit labelling of what is well-established versus contested.
  3. Treatment — what works, why, and what the limitations are.
  4. What we don't know — the honest gaps.

These lessons are written from a neuroscience perspective — the biology is the primary focus. They are not comprehensive clinical references. For each condition, the phenomenology section aims to give enough of the lived reality that the biology feels like an explanation of something real, not an abstract circuit diagram.

A note on language: these lessons use both clinical ("disorder," "symptoms," "diagnosis") and neurodiversity ("presentation," "profile," "trait") framings where appropriate. ADHD and autism especially are conditions where many people have strong and legitimate feelings about how they are described. The lessons try to represent the science accurately and the human reality honestly without treating clinical labels as the only valid frame.
The Conditions
  • Schizophrenia — a severe psychotic disorder involving hallucinations, delusions, and cognitive impairment; one of the most misrepresented conditions in popular culture
  • Depression — the world's leading cause of disability; not sadness, not weakness, and not a simple chemical imbalance
  • Bipolar Disorder — characterized by episodes of mania and depression; frequently misdiagnosed and poorly understood
  • Anxiety & PTSD — fear circuits running too hot or failing to extinguish; the most common category of psychiatric condition
  • OCD — intrusive thoughts and compulsive rituals; not a personality trait, not perfectionism
  • Autism Spectrum — a heterogeneous neurodevelopmental condition affecting social communication and sensory processing
  • ADHD — attention dysregulation (not deficit), executive dysfunction, and time blindness; not a children's condition
