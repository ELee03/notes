Mental Illness

Schizophrenia · Dopamine Hypothesis · Depression · Monoamine Theory · Bipolar Disorder · Anxiety · OCD · Autism · ADHD · Psychopharmacology

Psychiatric disorders are disorders of brain circuits — the same circuits covered throughout this cluster. Understanding them requires both the biology of normal function and how that function can become dysregulated. This lesson covers the major categories of mental illness from a neuroscience perspective: what is known about their neural substrates, what the leading biological hypotheses are, and what treatments follow from those hypotheses. A recurrent theme: our best biological theories of mental illness were reverse-engineered from the drugs that happen to work, not derived from first principles — which means they are probably incomplete.

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a severe psychiatric disorder affecting ~1% of the population. It typically onset in late adolescence or early adulthood. Core features:

  • Positive symptoms (added behaviours): hallucinations (most commonly auditory — "hearing voices"), delusions (fixed false beliefs), disorganized thinking and speech.
  • Negative symptoms (lost behaviours): flat affect, alogia (poverty of speech), avolition (loss of motivation), social withdrawal, anhedonia.
  • Cognitive symptoms: impaired working memory, attention, and executive function — often more predictive of functional outcome than positive symptoms.

The dopamine hypothesis is the oldest and most influential biological theory of schizophrenia. It arose from two observations made in the 1950s–60s:

  1. Antipsychotic drugs (chlorpromazine, haloperidol) that relieve positive symptoms all block dopamine D2 receptors — and their clinical potency correlates with their D2 receptor affinity.
  2. Drugs that increase dopamine (amphetamine, L-DOPA, cocaine) can induce psychosis-like symptoms including hallucinations and paranoid delusions in healthy people.

The refined version: mesolimbic dopamine is hyperactive (excess dopaminergic signalling in the striatum → delusions and hallucinations; too much salience attribution to irrelevant stimuli), while mesocortical dopamine is hypoactive (insufficient dopaminergic input to prefrontal cortex → cognitive symptoms and negative symptoms). This "two-component" version explains why D2 blockers help positive symptoms but often worsen negative and cognitive symptoms.

PET imaging confirms: patients with schizophrenia show elevated striatal dopamine synthesis and release, particularly in the presynaptic terminal. The excess dopamine in the striatum is thought to generate aberrant prediction errors — the brain incorrectly signals "this is important!" to irrelevant stimuli, which may be the mechanism of delusion formation (unusual significance attached to coincidences).

Glutamate hypothesis: A competing and complementary hypothesis holds that NMDA receptor hypofunction is the primary deficit. Basis: NMDA antagonists (PCP, ketamine) produce the full range of schizophrenia symptoms — positive, negative, and cognitive — in healthy people, more faithfully than amphetamine does. Moreover, NMDA hypofunction on GABAergic interneurons (particularly parvalbumin-positive fast-spiking interneurons in PFC) would disinhibit excitatory neurons, leading to disorganized firing and the cognitive and negative symptoms. Downstream consequence: disturbed dopamine regulation (NMDA hypofunction → reduced prefrontal inhibition of mesolimbic dopamine → dopamine excess in striatum).

The current synthesis: NMDA receptor dysfunction in cortical interneurons is possibly the upstream cause; the resulting dysregulated dopamine transmission is the proximate mechanism of positive symptoms and the target of current medications.

Genetics: Heritability of schizophrenia is ~80%. But no single gene has large effect — hundreds of common variants each contribute tiny risk (polygenic architecture). Several rare copy number variants (CNVs) have larger effects: 22q11 deletion (DiGeorge syndrome) carries ~30% lifetime risk. Candidate genes cluster around synaptic function (DISC1, DTNBP1), NMDA receptor function (GRM3, GRIN2A), and immune function (C4A — a complement protein expressed in synapses, involved in synaptic pruning).

Neurodevelopmental model: Schizophrenia is now primarily understood as a neurodevelopmental disorder. The trigger for onset in early adulthood may be the final wave of synaptic pruning in prefrontal cortex, which is excessive in genetically vulnerable individuals (possibly driven by complement-mediated over-pruning via C4A). The underlying circuit vulnerability was established much earlier — possibly prenatally.

Major Depression

Major depressive disorder (MDD) is the world's leading cause of disability. Lifetime prevalence ~15–20%. Core features: persistent low mood, anhedonia, cognitive disturbances (concentration, memory, executive function), somatic symptoms (sleep, appetite, energy), and suicidality.

Monoamine hypothesis: Antidepressants were discovered by accident in the 1950s (iproniazid, a tuberculosis drug, elevated mood; imipramine, an antihistamine, had antidepressant effects). Both were found to increase synaptic monoamine levels (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine). This led to the hypothesis that depression is caused by monoamine deficiency. The modern version: selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) block the serotonin transporter SERT, increasing synaptic serotonin. They are the most widely prescribed class of antidepressants.

The monoamine hypothesis is almost certainly incomplete:

  • SSRIs increase synaptic serotonin within hours, but clinical antidepressant effects take 2–6 weeks. The delayed response requires synaptic plasticity and circuit remodeling, not just monoamine increase.
  • Tryptophan depletion (which lowers serotonin) does not cause depression in healthy people — only in people with a history of depression or family history. Monoamine deficiency alone is insufficient.
  • ~30% of patients do not respond to any monoamine-based antidepressant (treatment-resistant depression).

Neuroplasticity hypothesis: Chronic stress reduces BDNF expression in the hippocampus, causes hippocampal atrophy (dendritic retraction, reduced neurogenesis), and impairs prefrontal-limbic regulation. Antidepressants restore BDNF, promote neurogenesis, and facilitate synaptic remodeling — and this plasticity, not the immediate monoamine change, may be the mechanism of therapeutic efficacy. SSRI-induced BDNF upregulation takes 2–4 weeks, matching the clinical timeline.

Ketamine and fast-acting antidepressants: Ketamine (NMDA antagonist) produces antidepressant effects within hours, even in treatment-resistant patients. Mechanism: ketamine disinhibits mTOR signaling in prefrontal neurons, rapidly upregulating AMPA receptors and strengthening synaptic connections — bypassing the slow BDNF-neurogenesis pathway. This discovery transformed understanding of depression mechanisms and led to esketamine (Spravato) approval for treatment-resistant depression.

Stress and the HPA axis: Chronic stress dysregulates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Cortisol levels are elevated in ~50% of MDD patients, and hypercortisolemia is neurotoxic to hippocampal neurons (glucocorticoid receptors in hippocampus normally provide negative feedback; when this feedback is disrupted, cortisol remains elevated). Childhood adversity permanently increases HPA axis reactivity (epigenetic programming of glucocorticoid receptor gene expression), explaining why early-life stress is a major risk factor for adult MDD.

Circuit models: fMRI studies consistently implicate: hyperactivity of the subgenual anterior cingulate cortex (sgACC/area 25) and amygdala (associated with rumination, negative bias, emotional hyperreactivity); hypoactivity of PFC (impaired regulatory control); and abnormal connectivity between PFC, ACC, amygdala, and striatum. Deep brain stimulation of sgACC can induce rapid antidepressant effects in treatment-resistant patients, confirming its causal role.

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder is characterized by episodes of mania (elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, grandiosity, impulsivity, increased goal-directed activity) alternating with depressive episodes. Lifetime prevalence ~2%. High heritability (~80%); genetic risk overlaps substantially with schizophrenia (shared GWAS loci).

The biological substrate is less well understood than for unipolar depression. Key findings:

  • Lithium, the gold-standard mood stabilizer, inhibits glycogen synthase kinase-3β (GSK-3β) and inositol monophosphatase among other targets. Its mechanism likely involves modulation of circadian rhythm gene expression (CLOCK, BMAL1) and neuroplasticity signals. Circadian clock dysfunction is increasingly recognized as central to bipolar disorder — most episodes are triggered by disrupted sleep-wake cycles.
  • Dopamine: manic states involve elevated dopaminergic signalling in the mesolimbic system (elevated reward sensitivity, impulsive decision-making, grandiosity), while depressive phases show the opposite. Antipsychotics (D2 blockers) are effective mood stabilizers in mania.
  • Mitochondrial function: Emerging evidence implicates mitochondrial dysfunction and altered energy metabolism in bipolar disorder. Valproate (another mood stabilizer) affects mitochondrial function.

Structural imaging: reduced grey matter in prefrontal cortex (especially orbital and medial PFC) and alterations in amygdala volume, similar to but distinct from changes in unipolar depression and schizophrenia.

Anxiety Disorders and OCD

Anxiety disorders (generalized anxiety, panic disorder, social anxiety, PTSD, specific phobia) share a core feature: excessive or inappropriate fear/anxiety responses. OCD was historically classed with anxiety disorders but is now in its own category (obsessive-compulsive and related disorders).

Circuit model of fear and anxiety: The amygdala is central. The basolateral amygdala (BLA) receives sensory input and computes threat associations via learned fear conditioning (see Emotion lesson). The central amygdala (CeA) generates fear responses (autonomic arousal, freezing, HPA activation). In anxiety disorders, this circuit is hyperreactive: the amygdala responds excessively to threat-related stimuli, and prefrontal extinction circuits fail to suppress it adequately.

PTSD specifically involves failure of extinction: the traumatic memory is not extinguished because the fear circuit is abnormally strengthened at encoding (high noradrenergic/cortisol state during trauma) and deficient in extinction consolidation (reduced hippocampal and PFC function). First-line treatments (prolonged exposure therapy, EMDR) are operationally extinction-based. Pharmacological augmentation with propranolol (β-blocker, reduces noradrenaline signalling during traumatic memory reactivation) is being investigated to reduce reconsolidation of fear memories.

OCD circuit: Obsessive-compulsive disorder involves intrusive thoughts (obsessions) that are neutralized by repetitive behaviours (compulsions). The neural correlate: hyperactivity in the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) loop — particularly the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), caudate nucleus, and thalamus. In healthy subjects, OFC detects errors and generates a "something is wrong" signal; the caudate then filters this signal, allowing appropriate behavioural response. In OCD, OFC-caudate connectivity is dysregulated — the error signal cannot be shut off, leading to persistent distress and compulsive behaviour to neutralize it.

SSRIs reduce OCD symptoms (at higher doses than required for depression), likely by modulating serotonergic inputs to the OFC-striatum circuit. Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) with exposure and response prevention is equally effective. Neuroimaging shows that both pharmacological and CBT treatment normalize the hyperactive CSTC circuit — biological and psychological interventions converge on the same circuit.

Neurodevelopmental Disorders: Autism and ADHD

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is characterized by deficits in social communication and interaction, and restricted/repetitive behaviours. Prevalence ~1–2% (with broad diagnostic criteria). Heritability ~70–80%; no single gene explains more than ~1% of cases (highly polygenic).

Neural hypotheses:

  • E/I imbalance: Many ASD-associated genes (SHANK3, NRXN1, CNTNAP2, NLGN) encode synaptic scaffolding proteins at excitatory synapses or regulate GABA inhibitory circuits. The hypothesis: an excess of excitatory relative to inhibitory tone ("E/I imbalance") disrupts normal circuit function, possibly by generating sensory hypersensitivity and disrupting the oscillatory patterns needed for cognitive binding.
  • Disrupted social brain circuits: fMRI shows reduced activation of "social brain" regions (superior temporal sulcus, fusiform face area, medial PFC) during social tasks. These regions are normally coordinated in a network for mentalizing and social reward; their reduced activity and connectivity may explain social communication deficits.
  • Altered connectivity: DTI and fMRI show reduced long-range connectivity but increased local connectivity in ASD brains — "underconnected" between regions, "overconnected" within regions. This pattern may explain both social deficits (require long-range integration) and repetitive/detail-focused behaviours (reflect strong local processing).

ASD is clearly neurodevelopmental — structural and functional differences are present in brain imaging at 6–12 months, before behavioural diagnosis. No environmental cause (vaccines, parenting style) has been established; the robust evidence is for genetic and early neurodevelopmental origins.

Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is characterized by inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity, onset in childhood, with impaired executive function as the core deficit. Prevalence ~5–7% of children. Heritability ~75%. Key genes involve dopamine and norepinephrine signalling (DRD4, DAT1, ADRA2A).

The neural model: hypofunctionality of the mesocortical dopamine and norepinephrine systems projecting to prefrontal cortex, reducing PFC's ability to regulate attention and inhibitory control. Stimulant medications (methylphenidate, amphetamine) block dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake, increasing catecholamine levels specifically in PFC (where the baseline level is insufficient). This "paradoxical calming" effect on hyperactivity is actually predictable from the PFC model: PFC normally suppresses subcortical motor circuits; stimulants restore PFC function, enabling better regulation.

Structural imaging: ADHD brains show delayed cortical maturation — the wave of cortical grey matter thickening and thinning that normally peaks at ~7–8 years is delayed by ~3 years in ADHD. The right prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate, and basal ganglia show the largest delays. Many children "outgrow" ADHD symptoms as their prefrontal maturation catches up.

Psychopharmacology Overview

The major drug classes and their mechanisms:

Drug class Examples Mechanism Use
SSRIs Fluoxetine, sertraline Block SERT (serotonin transporter) Depression, anxiety, OCD
SNRIs Venlafaxine, duloxetine Block SERT + NET Depression, GAD, neuropathic pain
Typical antipsychotics Haloperidol, chlorpromazine D2 receptor antagonist Schizophrenia (positive symptoms); EPS side effects
Atypical antipsychotics Clozapine, olanzapine, quetiapine D2 + 5-HT2A antagonism (and others) Schizophrenia (all symptoms), bipolar mania; lower EPS
Mood stabilizers Lithium, valproate, lamotrigine Multiple (GSK-3β, sodium channels, GABA) Bipolar disorder
Benzodiazepines Diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam Positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A receptor Acute anxiety, seizures; dependence risk
Stimulants Methylphenidate, amphetamine Block DAT/NET; amphetamine also reverses transporter ADHD; narcolepsy
NMDA antagonists Ketamine, esketamine NMDA receptor block → mTOR/AMPA plasticity Treatment-resistant depression (fast-acting)

A recurring pattern in psychopharmacology: drugs with clean single-target mechanisms often have limited efficacy; the most clinically effective drugs (clozapine, lithium, valproate, ketamine) have multiple or incompletely understood mechanisms. This may reflect the complexity of psychiatric disorders — they are not single-neurotransmitter deficits, and effective treatment requires modulation of complex circuits in ways we don't fully understand yet.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Why do antipsychotics take weeks to work despite immediate D2 blockade?
Haloperidol occupies >70% of striatal D2 receptors within hours of the first dose. Yet clinical improvement in schizophrenia takes 2–6 weeks. If the dopamine hypothesis is correct, why the delay?

Immediate D2 blockade does not directly reduce the presynaptic hyperactivity that is thought to drive positive symptoms — it blocks postsynaptic signalling but the dopaminergic neurons themselves are initially unaffected and may even increase firing (via disrupted autoreceptor feedback).

Over weeks of continuous D2 blockade, several adaptive changes occur:

  1. Presynaptic dopaminergic neuron depolarization block: Sustained loss of dopaminergic signalling eventually causes mesolimbic dopamine neurons to become tonically depolarized (inactivated) rather than firing phasically. This depolarization block — studied in rodent models — reduces the dopamine cells' responsiveness to salient stimuli, which is the state thought to be therapeutic.
  2. Receptor downregulation: Chronic D2 blockade leads to downstream signalling adaptations in postsynaptic neurons.
  3. Cortical circuit remodeling: Reduced mesolimbic dopamine input alters corticostriatal circuit dynamics over time.

This delayed mechanism explains why antidepressants and antipsychotics both require weeks — the immediate neurochemical change triggers a cascade of synaptic plasticity and circuit adaptation that constitutes the actual therapeutic effect. The monoamine change is the input to the process, not the process itself.

Example 2 — The CSTC loop in OCD: surgical and stimulation targets
Treatment-resistant OCD can be treated with deep brain stimulation (DBS). The most effective DBS targets include the anterior limb of the internal capsule (ALIC), nucleus accumbens, and subthalamic nucleus. Given the CSTC model, explain why stimulation at these sites might relieve OCD.

The CSTC loop in OCD: OFC sends hyperactive error/alarm signals → striatum (caudate nucleus) → globus pallidus/substantia nigra → thalamus → back to OFC. The loop is stuck in a positive feedback state — the "error" signal cannot be shut off.

The ALIC contains fibers connecting OFC to thalamus (part of the hyperactive CSTC loop). DBS at ALIC disrupts abnormal synchronous oscillatory activity in this circuit — the high-frequency stimulation (130 Hz) functionally inhibits or desynchronizes the loop. This was the rationale for the older ablative surgery (anterior capsulotomy, which cuts these same fibers).

The nucleus accumbens (ventral striatum) is the interface between limbic/cortical input and motor output, and is dysregulated in OCD. DBS here modulates dopamine and serotonin release in the striatum, potentially normalizing the reward/aversion signalling that drives compulsive behaviour.

The subthalamic nucleus (STN) is part of the hyperdirect cortico-subthalamic pathway — a "brake" on motor output that is relevant in OCD (compulsive behaviours may reflect failure of this brake). DBS of STN is primarily used for Parkinson's disease but also shows efficacy in severe OCD.

Importantly: DBS is not "curing" OCD — it is modulating the overactive circuit to reduce its pathological output, allowing behavioural therapy to then work on the underlying fear associations. The combination of DBS with CBT is more effective than either alone.

Food for Thought
  • All our major psychiatric drug classes were discovered by serendipity — chlorpromazine (an antihistamine), imipramine (another antihistamine), lithium (a salt found in guinea pigs), iproniazid (a tuberculosis drug). We then reverse-engineered theories of disease from their mechanisms. What are the limitations of this approach, and how might theory-driven drug discovery work differently?
    The reverse-engineering approach has given us useful heuristics (dopamine in schizophrenia, monoamines in depression) but the theories are probably too simple because they were derived from imprecise tools that affect many systems. The drugs work for some people, not all, because the diseases are heterogeneous — "depression" or "schizophrenia" is a clinical description, not a single biological entity. Theory-driven discovery would start from known circuit deficits (identified via genetics, imaging, circuit tracing) and design drugs that specifically correct those deficits. Ketamine is the closest to a mechanistically-motivated therapy (NMDA hypofunction hypothesis → block NMDA → trigger downstream plasticity); its success validates circuit-based reasoning. The next generation of psychiatric drugs will likely be circuit-specific neuromodulators rather than monoamine transporter blockers.
  • Autism and schizophrenia appear superficially opposite — ASD involves social withdrawal and sensory hypersensitivity from early in life; schizophrenia involves positive symptoms and typically onsets in young adulthood. Yet their genetic risks overlap (22q11 deletion increases risk of both; DISC1 is associated with both). How could the same genes produce such different phenotypes?
    The same gene can have very different effects depending on when in development it acts, which cell types it is expressed in, and what other genetic and environmental background it operates against. 22q11 deletion affects over 40 genes; which of those, expressed in which developmental context, produces the schizophrenia phenotype vs. the ASD phenotype likely depends on other genetic modifiers and developmental timing. Both disorders involve disrupted synaptic development — but if excitatory synapses are reduced differently in social vs. frontal circuits, or at different developmental stages, the outcome differs. Additionally, the clinical phenotypes may not be as distinct as they seem: ASD and schizophrenia both involve social cognition deficits, and a meaningful fraction of people with 22q11 deletion have features of both. The categorical boundary between them may be an artifact of DSM classification rather than a sharp biological boundary.
  • Deep brain stimulation, transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), and transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) all modulate psychiatric symptoms by directly altering neural activity — bypassing pharmacology entirely. Does their efficacy support or undermine the monoamine/neurotransmitter hypotheses of mental illness?
    Neither supports nor undermines the neurotransmitter hypotheses directly — it just adds evidence that circuit activity, not just chemical concentrations, matters. TMS/DBS alters neural firing patterns and synchrony, which secondarily alters neurotransmitter release (stimulation of reward circuits releases dopamine; sgACC DBS presumably alters serotonin and glutamate dynamics in connected circuits). So the two levels of description — transmitter and circuit — are complementary, not competing. The more interesting implication: if you can achieve the same therapeutic outcome by modulating circuit activity directly (stimulation) as by chemical modulation (drugs), it suggests the final common mechanism is the circuit state, not the specific transmitter concentration. This argues for circuit-level models of mental illness over transmitter-deficiency models.
Common Pitfalls
  • "Depression is a serotonin deficiency." This is the popular version of the monoamine hypothesis, but it is probably wrong as a direct cause. Brain imaging does not show reduced serotonin levels in depressed patients. SSRIs likely work by triggering neuroplasticity changes through their effect on serotonin signalling, not by correcting a deficiency. The "chemical imbalance" framing oversimplifies — and may harm by implying that depression is purely biological and that behavioural/psychological factors don't matter.
  • "Stimulants are dangerous for ADHD patients because they are addictive." At therapeutic doses, taken as prescribed (not crushed/snorted), methylphenidate and amphetamine do not produce the rapid dopamine spike in reward circuits that drives addiction. They gradually raise baseline dopamine tone in prefrontal circuits. Long-term follow-up studies find that treated ADHD patients have lower rates of substance abuse than untreated ADHD patients (who may self-medicate).
  • "Antipsychotics treat schizophrenia." They treat the positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) but often worsen or have little effect on negative and cognitive symptoms, which are actually more predictive of functional outcomes. Managing schizophrenia effectively requires addressing all three symptom domains — medication handles one.
  • "Psychiatric disorders are caused by bad parenting or trauma." While adverse childhood experiences are genuine risk factors for depression, PTSD, and some personality disorders, the major psychotic disorders (schizophrenia, bipolar) are strongly heritable neurodevelopmental conditions. No evidence supports "refrigerator mother" theories of autism or psychogenic theories of schizophrenia. Stigmatizing families by implying they caused the disorder delays diagnosis and treatment.
  • "Psychotherapy and medication treat different things." This distinction is artificial. Both effective psychotherapy (CBT, DBT, prolonged exposure) and effective pharmacotherapy normalize the same overactive or underactive circuits — demonstrated by neuroimaging. They reach the same endpoint via different input channels (one changes behaviour, one changes receptor activity), but converge on the same circuit correction. For many disorders, combined treatment is more effective than either alone.
