Autism Spectrum Disorder

Heterogeneity · Neurodiversity · Social Communication · Sensory Processing · Masking · E/I Balance · Genetics · No Vaccine Link

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental condition characterized by differences in social communication and interaction, and by restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities. The word "spectrum" is important: ASD is not a gradient from mild to severe along a single dimension, but a genuinely heterogeneous group of profiles that share core features while varying enormously in presentation, support needs, cognitive profile, and the degree to which the differences are experienced as disabling.

Vaccines do not cause autism. This has been studied exhaustively across millions of children in multiple countries. The original 1998 paper claiming a link (Wakefield et al.) was fraudulent — the data were fabricated, the paper was retracted, and the author lost his medical license. Every subsequent large-scale study has found no association between the MMR vaccine (or any vaccine component) and autism. Continuing to treat this as a live controversy does real harm: it discourages vaccination and does nothing to support autistic people.
What ASD Involves

The DSM-5 diagnostic criteria centre on two domains:

1. Social communication and interaction:

  • Difficulty with the reciprocal back-and-forth of social conversation — not necessarily from lack of interest, but from differences in how social signals are processed
  • Reduced or atypical nonverbal communication (eye contact, facial expression, gesture, body language)
  • Difficulty developing and maintaining friendships; differences in understanding and navigating unwritten social rules

2. Restricted, repetitive behaviours, interests, or activities:

  • Repetitive motor movements or speech (stimming — self-stimulatory behaviour such as hand-flapping, rocking; echolalia)
  • Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines; significant distress at changes
  • Highly focused, intense interests — often in specific domains, pursued with depth unusual for peers
  • Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input — sounds, textures, lights, tastes may be overwhelming or may be sought out

What the criteria do not capture well:

  • Sensory processing differences are often the most immediately distressing feature. Many autistic people describe environments — busy open-plan offices, supermarkets, crowded social events — as physically painful or cognitively overwhelming. This is not a metaphor.
  • Executive function difficulties — task initiation, cognitive flexibility, working memory — are common and often more functionally impairing than the social features.
  • Masking (also called camouflaging): actively suppressing or hiding autistic traits to appear neurotypical. Masking is exhausting, depletes cognitive and emotional resources, and is strongly associated with burnout, anxiety, and depression. It is more common in women and girls, and is a major reason ASD is underdiagnosed in women — their social camouflage hides the diagnostic features in clinical encounters.
The Heterogeneity Problem

"Spectrum" is genuinely true and genuinely challenging. The ASD diagnosis encompasses:

  • Non-speaking individuals with intellectual disability and high support needs
  • Highly verbal, intellectually gifted individuals who work in demanding professional careers and whose differences are invisible to most people
  • Everything between

These people share some features (differences in social processing, sensory sensitivity, restricted interests) but their lived experiences, support needs, and what they need from the world are radically different. A single diagnostic label covering this range creates both clinical challenges (what does "autism research" even generalize to?) and political tensions within the autistic community (people with high support needs and their families sometimes feel that neurodiversity advocacy built around high-functioning autistic people erases their reality).

Intellectually, this heterogeneity suggests that "autism" is not a single condition but a cluster of related conditions with partly distinct biological substrates. Efforts to identify subtypes (by genetic cause, cognitive profile, or neurobiological signature) have had limited success so far.

Neurodiversity

The neurodiversity framework — which holds that neurological differences like autism are natural human variation rather than deficits to be cured — is meaningful and has produced significant positive change: reducing the shame and self-hatred many autistic people experience, advocating for accommodation rather than normalization, and challenging harmful "therapies" (particularly Applied Behaviour Analysis as historically practiced, which focused on suppressing autistic behaviours regardless of their function).

It is also incomplete as a full account. Many autistic people, particularly those with higher support needs, experience significant suffering that is not simply the result of a neurotypical world failing to accommodate them — sensory overwhelm, difficulty communicating needs, social isolation — and for whom better support, not just acceptance, is needed. These perspectives are not in conflict: both "accept and accommodate difference" and "reduce genuine suffering" are valid goals, and they apply differently to different people on the spectrum.

The neuroscience can remain neutral on the normative question. What the biology shows is that ASD involves genuine neural differences with real functional consequences — it is not a choice, a parenting failure, or a social construct. What those differences mean for the individual, and what they need from the world, requires listening to the individuals themselves.

Neuroscience

Heritability is ~70–80%, one of the highest in psychiatry. No single gene explains more than ~1% of cases. The genetic architecture is highly heterogeneous: hundreds of rare variants and de novo mutations affecting synaptic genes (SHANK3, NRXN1, CNTNAP2, NLGN), alongside common variants of tiny effect. Different rare variants likely produce ASD through different mechanisms, contributing to the clinical heterogeneity.

E/I imbalance hypothesis: Many ASD-associated genes encode synaptic proteins at excitatory synapses or regulate inhibitory GABA circuits. The hypothesis: an excess of excitation relative to inhibition in local cortical circuits disrupts the precise timing and coordination of neural activity, producing sensory hypersensitivity, disrupted cortical oscillations (particularly gamma-band, implicated in perceptual binding), and the reduced social brain network engagement seen in imaging. Animal models of ASD gene knockouts often show E/I imbalance that can be pharmacologically corrected.

Connectivity: fMRI consistently shows reduced long-range connectivity (between distant brain regions) with increased local connectivity (within regions) in ASD. This "underconnected-overconnected" pattern may explain both social deficits (which require long-range integration of social brain networks) and the intense, detailed processing style and deep interests (which may reflect strong local processing).

Social brain network: Regions involved in mentalizing and social cognition — fusiform face area (face processing), superior temporal sulcus (biological motion, gaze), TPJ (theory of mind), medial PFC (self-referential and other-referential thinking) — show reduced activation and connectivity during social tasks in ASD. This is not "not caring about people" — it is a different neural architecture for processing social information.

ASD is neurodevelopmental: structural and functional differences are present in brain imaging in infancy (6–12 months), before behavioural diagnosis. Prenatal brain development — neural migration, synaptogenesis, circuit formation — is where the relevant differences arise.

Gender and Diagnosis

Historically, ASD was diagnosed in boys roughly 4:1 over girls. Current estimates of the true ratio are closer to 2:1 or 3:2 — still a real difference, but much smaller than the diagnostic ratio suggests. Girls and women are significantly underdiagnosed for several reasons:

  • Early diagnostic criteria and research were conducted on male samples
  • Girls show stronger masking/camouflaging behaviour, hiding autistic traits in clinical encounters
  • Girls' restricted interests are more likely to be socially typical (animals, literature, media fandoms) and therefore less obviously autistic
  • Social difficulties are more apparent in male-typical social environments (competitive, hierarchical) than in female-typical ones (cooperative, more accommodating of difference)

Many women receive an ASD diagnosis in adulthood after decades of being told they have anxiety, depression, borderline personality disorder, or are "just shy." The experience of late diagnosis is mixed — relief at an explanation, and grief at what might have been different with earlier support.

Common Pitfalls
  • "Vaccines cause autism." No. Thoroughly debunked, the original paper was fraudulent. See the callout at the top of this lesson.
  • "Autistic people lack empathy." This is wrong. Research shows autistic people can have strong emotional empathy (feeling others' emotions) but may have differences in cognitive empathy (inferring what others are thinking or feeling, theory of mind). "Lack of empathy" has also been used harmfully to dismiss autistic people's emotional experiences entirely.
  • "Autism is a childhood condition." Autistic people do not stop being autistic at 18. They develop coping strategies, support needs may change, but the neurological differences persist.
  • "High-functioning means low support needs." "High-functioning" (and conversely "low-functioning") are imprecise labels. An autistic person who is verbal and employed may have severe sensory sensitivities, significant mental health burden from masking, and high support needs in some domains. Function varies by context and domain.
  • "ABA therapy is the gold standard." Traditional ABA focused on suppressing autistic behaviours (including stimming) through operant conditioning. This approach has been criticized extensively by autistic adults, many of whom experienced it as traumatic. Modern support approaches focus on quality of life, communication, and accommodation rather than normalization of behaviour.
