Depression

Anhedonia · Beyond the Chemical Imbalance · Neuroplasticity Hypothesis · HPA Axis · Ketamine · Psychotherapy · Heterogeneity

Major depressive disorder is the leading cause of disability worldwide. It is not sadness. Sadness is a normal emotional response to loss; it is proportionate, it comes and goes, and it does not prevent you from functioning. Depression is a state in which the capacity to feel motivation, pleasure, and connection is not just reduced but often abolished — in which the future seems genuinely without prospect and the present without meaning, independent of actual circumstances. Understanding the distinction matters because "just cheer up" is not just unhelpful advice; it reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of what the condition is.

What It Is Like

The diagnostic criteria require either persistent low mood or anhedonia — loss of interest or pleasure in activities that were previously enjoyable — for at least two weeks, along with several other symptoms. Anhedonia is arguably the more specific feature: it is the inability to feel rewarded, not just the presence of bad feelings. Many severely depressed people describe not sadness but a kind of affective flatness — a grey absence where feeling used to be.

Associated features include:

  • Cognitive symptoms: difficulty concentrating, memory impairment, slowed thinking (psychomotor retardation), ruminative negative thinking that is difficult to interrupt
  • Somatic symptoms: sleep disruption (typically early-morning waking and inability to return to sleep; sometimes hypersomnia), appetite and weight changes, fatigue, loss of libido
  • Sense of worthlessness, guilt disproportionate to circumstances
  • In severe cases: psychotic features (delusions of guilt, illness, or poverty), and suicidality

"Depression is not just sadness" is a useful corrective but also incomplete. Depression is heterogeneous — the clinical diagnosis covers a range of presentations from the melancholic (profound anhedonia, early waking, psychomotor changes, no reactivity to positive events) to the atypical (mood brightens temporarily in response to positive events, hypersomnia, rejection sensitivity, leaden paralysis in limbs). These may be biologically distinct conditions sharing a diagnostic label. Treatment response varies accordingly.

The Chemical Imbalance Story: What It Gets Wrong

The popular understanding of depression as a "chemical imbalance" — specifically insufficient serotonin — is wrong as a direct causal account, and the field largely knows this even though the story persisted in public health messaging for decades.

What the evidence shows:

  • Tryptophan depletion studies — which lower serotonin — do not reliably cause depression in healthy people. They can trigger relapse in people who are already in remission on SSRIs, suggesting serotonin is involved in maintaining the treated state, not in causing the untreated condition.
  • Antidepressants raise synaptic serotonin within hours. Clinical benefit takes 2–6 weeks. If depression were simply low serotonin, this gap would not exist.
  • Multiple antidepressant classes work via different mechanisms (SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, TCAs, bupropion, mirtazapine, agomelatine) — they converge on symptom relief through different neurotransmitter targets, suggesting no single transmitter deficiency is the cause.
  • A 2022 umbrella review (Moncrieff et al.) found no consistent evidence for lower serotonin activity in depression, reigniting debate. The review was somewhat overstated in media coverage — it did not prove serotonin is irrelevant, only that the simple "deficiency" model is not supported.

The "chemical imbalance" framing was not entirely cynical — it helped destigmatize depression by framing it as biological rather than characterological. But it has also produced harm: patients who think they have a simple chemical deficiency may be less inclined to engage with psychotherapy, which is equally effective for mild-to-moderate depression, and may feel that symptoms returning mean their brain chemistry is "off again" rather than that they have identifiable psychological patterns to work on.

The Neuroplasticity Hypothesis

The current leading hypothesis is that antidepressants work not primarily by correcting a transmitter deficiency, but by promoting synaptic plasticity — restoring the brain's capacity for adaptation that stress and depression have impaired.

Key evidence:

  • Chronic stress reduces BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) expression in the hippocampus and PFC, causes dendritic retraction, and reduces adult neurogenesis in the dentate gyrus. These structural changes correlate with depression symptoms in animal models and are partly reversed by antidepressants.
  • All effective antidepressants (across different mechanisms) increase BDNF expression after 2–4 weeks of treatment — matching the clinical timeline. Blocking BDNF signalling prevents antidepressant effect in animal models.
  • Blocking adult neurogenesis in rodents partially blocks antidepressant behavioural effects (though this finding is not consistently replicated).

On this view, depression involves a brain that has lost plasticity — stuck in maladaptive patterns, unable to update. Antidepressants restore plasticity, making the brain responsive to learning and environmental input again. This explains why therapy combined with medication is more effective than either alone: the drug opens a window of plasticity; therapy provides the new learning that fills it.

Stress, the HPA Axis, and Early Adversity

Chronic stress dysregulates the HPA axis: sustained cortisol elevation damages hippocampal neurons (which express glucocorticoid receptors and normally provide negative feedback), impairing the shutdown of the stress response. This creates a positive feedback loop — stress damages the hippocampus, damaged hippocampus fails to terminate cortisol, cortisol damages further.

Childhood adversity (abuse, neglect, household dysfunction) is one of the strongest risk factors for adult depression. The mechanism appears to involve epigenetic programming of glucocorticoid receptor expression in the hippocampus — early-life stress methylates the GR gene promoter, permanently reducing GR expression and increasing HPA reactivity. The brain learns, during a sensitive period, that the world is dangerous and the stress response should be calibrated high. This calibration persists into adulthood and predisposes to depression under stress.

Circuit Model

Neuroimaging consistently implicates:

  • Subgenual anterior cingulate cortex (sgACC, area 25): hyperactive in depression; generates rumination, negative self-referential thinking, and the subjective sense of hopelessness. Deep brain stimulation of sgACC produces rapid antidepressant effects in treatment-resistant patients, confirming its causal role.
  • Amygdala: hyperreactive to negative stimuli; reduced threshold for threat detection; contributes to the negative cognitive bias of depression.
  • Prefrontal cortex: hypoactive; reduced ability to regulate sgACC and amygdala; impaired cognitive control of rumination.
  • Hippocampus: reduced volume in chronic depression, consistent with stress-induced neuronal damage and reduced neurogenesis.
  • Ventral striatum / nucleus accumbens: reduced reward responsivity; directly underlies anhedonia.
Ketamine and the Fast-Acting Revolution

Ketamine (an NMDA antagonist) produces antidepressant effects within hours — including in treatment-resistant patients who have failed multiple SSRIs. This was one of the most significant discoveries in psychiatry in decades. Esketamine (Spravato) is now FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression and major depression with suicidal ideation.

The mechanism is distinct from SSRIs. Ketamine's NMDA blockade triggers a rapid burst of glutamate transmission in PFC (via disinhibition of pyramidal neurons), activating AMPA receptors. This activates mTORC1 signalling and rapidly increases synthesis of synaptic proteins — AMPA receptor subunits, BDNF — restoring dendritic spine density and synaptic strength that chronic stress had eroded. The effect is rapid precisely because it bypasses the slow BDNF-mediated pathway and directly triggers synaptogenesis.

Ketamine does not work for everyone, effects can be transient (days to weeks for a single infusion), and repeated use raises questions about cognitive side effects and dissociative experiences during administration. It is not a replacement for conventional antidepressants but a genuinely different option for people who do not respond to them.

Treatment

Psychotherapy is as effective as medication for mild-to-moderate depression and more durable (lower relapse rates). Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) targets maladaptive thought patterns and behaviours. Behavioural Activation (a component of CBT) addresses the withdrawal and inactivity that perpetuate depression. Interpersonal therapy targets relationship patterns. All produce measurable changes in brain activity in the same circuits that medication targets.

Pharmacotherapy: SSRIs are first-line due to tolerability. SNRIs (add norepinephrine reuptake inhibition) are alternative first-line. For non-responders: augmentation (add aripiprazole, lithium, or thyroid hormone), switch to different class, or consider MAOIs (more effective for atypical depression but dietary restrictions). Tricyclics are effective but have narrow therapeutic windows and dangerous overdose profiles.

Treatment-resistant depression (failure of ≥2 adequate antidepressant trials) affects roughly 30% of patients. Options include ketamine/esketamine, ECT (electroconvulsive therapy — stigmatized but one of the most effective treatments in psychiatry, particularly for severe or psychotic depression), TMS, and DBS in the most severe cases.

Lifestyle factors with meaningful evidence: aerobic exercise (effect sizes comparable to antidepressants for mild-moderate depression), sleep regularization, and social connection. These are not trivial additions — they operate on the same neuroplasticity mechanisms.

Common Pitfalls
  • "Depression is just sadness / just think positively." Depression involves neurobiological changes to reward processing, stress response, and cognition. It is not addressable by attitude adjustment any more than diabetes is addressable by trying harder to produce insulin.
  • "SSRIs fix the chemical imbalance." The mechanism of antidepressants is not correcting a serotonin deficiency. They work — but probably by restoring synaptic plasticity, not topping up a depleted neurotransmitter.
  • "If antidepressants worked, they'd work immediately." The weeks-long delay is real, but it is not evidence against efficacy — it reflects the time required for synaptic remodelling. The misconception leads people to discontinue medication prematurely.
  • "Therapy is for people who don't want real treatment." Psychotherapy is a real biological treatment that produces measurable changes in brain circuits. For mild-moderate depression it is as effective as medication, with better durability.
