Sex and the Brain

Sexual Differentiation · Gonadal Hormones · Hypothalamic Sex Differences · Sexual Motivation · Sex Differences in Brain and Cognition · Puberty and Plasticity

Every brain develops from the same embryonic blueprint. What makes a brain develop differently in males and females is largely the action of sex hormones — particularly testosterone and its derivatives — during critical developmental windows. The resulting sex differences in brain structure and function are real but consistently modest in magnitude, heavily overlapping between groups, and often poorly understood in terms of causal mechanisms. This lesson covers the neuroscience of sexual differentiation — what we know, what remains contested, and why it matters for understanding reproductive behaviour, cognition, and neurological vulnerability.

Sexual Differentiation of the Brain

The mammalian default developmental trajectory, in the absence of gonadal hormones, is broadly female-typical. Male sexual differentiation requires active hormonal induction. The key findings come from classic work in rodents and primates.

Gonadal differentiation precedes brain differentiation. In males, the SRY gene on the Y chromosome triggers the bipotential gonad to differentiate into testes (around week 6–7 of human embryonic development). Testes begin secreting testosterone (and the related androgen dihydrotestosterone, DHT) by week 8–10. This organizational phase of testosterone action permanently alters the developing brain's architecture.

Testosterone can act directly on androgen receptors (AR), but much of its masculinizing effect on the brain is mediated by its local conversion to estradiol (E2) via aromatase in the developing brain. This is counterintuitive — estrogen masculinizes the male brain during development. The female brain is protected from circulating estradiol by alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), which binds estradiol in the bloodstream but does not bind testosterone. So: testosterone (from the testes) enters the male brain, gets converted to estradiol, and activates estrogen receptors to masculinize brain structure. Female brains have lower circulating testosterone and AFP binding further reduces any estradiol access.

Later, activational hormone effects operate in adulthood. Gonadal hormones (testosterone in males, estradiol and progesterone cycling in females) continue to regulate brain function reversibly — they modulate the expression of pre-existing circuits rather than creating new anatomy. The distinction: organizational effects are early, permanent, and structure-building; activational effects are later, reversible, and function-modulating.

The Hypothalamus and Reproductive Circuits

The hypothalamus is the primary brain structure controlling reproductive function and the site of the most robust, replicated sex differences in brain anatomy.

The sexually dimorphic nucleus of the preoptic area (SDN-POA) is 3–8 times larger in male rats than female rats. It contains more neurons and more neuropil. Analogous nuclei have been identified in humans (INAH-1 through INAH-4 — the interstitial nuclei of the anterior hypothalamus). INAH-3 is 2–3 times larger in males than females in postmortem studies; it is also larger in people who are sexually attracted to women than those attracted to men, regardless of biological sex (a finding from Simon LeVay's 1991 study that sparked enormous controversy).

The SDN-POA/INAH-3 is not a direct "control centre" for sexual orientation or behaviour — its function is not fully understood. It may be involved in regulating the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, which controls sex hormone secretion, or in organizing motor patterns related to reproductive behaviour.

The HPG axis itself shows a sex difference in its organization. In females, gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) neurons in the preoptic area control cyclic LH/FSH release from the pituitary, driving the menstrual/estrous cycle. In males, the GnRH → LH → testosterone axis is tonic (continuous, not cyclic). This difference in feedback regulation is organized perinatally by testosterone exposure — male GnRH neurons are permanently set to tonic mode.

Kisspeptin neurons in the arcuate nucleus and anteroventral periventricular nucleus (AVPV) are critical regulators of GnRH release and show sex differences: AVPV kisspeptin neurons are more numerous in females and drive the LH surge at ovulation. Arcuate kisspeptin neurons in both sexes drive the pulsatile GnRH secretion underlying the HPG axis basal activity.

Sexual motivation and behaviour circuits: The medial preoptic area (mPOA) in males integrates sensory, hormonal, and reward signals to coordinate male-typical copulatory behaviour. Testosterone acting on mPOA neurons (via androgen receptors and aromatization to estradiol) is necessary for libido and male sexual behaviour in many species. Dopamine release in the mPOA increases during sexual activity and encodes reward and motivation. The ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH) is critical for female-typical receptivity (lordosis in rodents); estrogen and progesterone act on VMH neurons to enable this behaviour.

Sex Differences in Brain Structure

Beyond the hypothalamus, sex differences in human brain anatomy are reported throughout the literature. The key context for interpreting them: effect sizes are consistently small, distributions heavily overlap, and the brain is not straightforwardly "male" or "female" — it is mosaic.

A 2015 study by Joel et al. analysed ~1,400 MRI scans and found that while some brain regions showed mean sex differences, almost no individual had a brain entirely at the "male" or "female" end of the distribution across all regions. Most people had a mosaic of features — some regions closer to the male mean, others closer to the female mean. A purely binary "male brain / female brain" is not supported by the data.

Reported sex differences include:

  • Total brain volume: Males average ~10–15% larger brains, but this difference largely disappears when controlling for body size. There is no evidence that larger brains confer cognitive advantage in this range of variation.
  • Corpus callosum: Some studies report sex differences in callosal subregions (particularly the splenium), but the literature is inconsistent and many early claims have not replicated with larger samples and better methodology.
  • Amygdala: Larger in males after controlling for total brain volume; may contribute to reported sex differences in emotional processing and certain psychopathologies (PTSD, anxiety disorders).
  • Hippocampus: Inconsistent sex differences; some reports of slightly larger hippocampus relative to brain volume in females, potentially relevant to different risk profiles for depression.
  • White matter microstructure: DTI studies show sex differences in several tracts, but results vary across studies. Some report greater within-hemisphere connectivity in males and greater between-hemisphere connectivity in females, but this is not robustly replicated.

A critical caution: many brain imaging sex-difference papers have been underpowered, have not corrected for total brain volume, have not accounted for hormonal variation within sexes, and have not been replicated. The field has been systematically affected by publication bias toward positive findings. Large pre-registered replication studies tend to find smaller effects than original reports.

Sex Differences in Cognition

This is among the most politically fraught areas in neuroscience. The empirical summary: there are a small number of cognitive domains with reliable, replicated mean sex differences, all with small effect sizes and enormous within-sex variability. These differences do not justify differential treatment of individuals.

Reliably replicated differences (meta-analytic, large N):

  • Mental rotation: Males score higher on average on 3D object rotation tasks (Cohen's d ≈ 0.5–0.9). This is the largest cognitive sex difference reported and is present cross-culturally, though its magnitude varies with culture, suggesting substantial environmental contribution.
  • Verbal fluency and some aspects of verbal memory: Females score slightly higher on average (d ≈ 0.2–0.4). Not a difference in general verbal ability — IQ measures show no sex difference — but in specific tasks like listing words beginning with a letter or remembering word lists.

Not reliably different: General intelligence, mathematical ability (historical gaps have closed substantially in countries with gender equity), working memory capacity, most executive function measures.

Explaining the cause of observed differences is where things get complicated. Candidate mechanisms for mental rotation differences: prenatal testosterone exposure (supported by congenital adrenal hyperplasia studies), different spatial experience (boys' toys, video games, navigation practices), stereotype threat (aware of the expectation of worse performance, women score lower; remove the threat and the gap shrinks substantially). All three likely contribute.

Hormonal modulation of cognition in adults: Estradiol has positive effects on verbal memory and fine motor control in women; testosterone may enhance spatial performance. These effects are real but small. They help explain within-sex variation (e.g., cognitive performance across the menstrual cycle in some studies) but do not explain between-sex differences alone.

Puberty and Neuroplasticity

Puberty is the second great wave of sex hormone action on the brain — the first being the perinatal organizational period. It begins with the reactivation of the GnRH pulse generator (which is suppressed during childhood) at around ages 8–12 in girls and 9–14 in boys.

GnRH pulses stimulate LH and FSH release, which stimulate gonadal sex steroid production. Rising estradiol in girls drives breast development, uterine growth, and the eventual onset of menstruation. Rising testosterone in boys drives genital development, muscle growth, voice deepening, and spermatogenesis.

On the brain:

  • Grey matter volume follows an inverted-U trajectory: increases in childhood through puberty, then decreases in adolescence as synaptic pruning refines circuits. This trajectory is slightly earlier in girls than boys. The prefrontal cortex is the last region to fully mature (mid-20s), regardless of sex.
  • White matter continues myelinating through adolescence and into the mid-20s.
  • Reward system maturation is asynchronous: the limbic system (nucleus accumbens, amygdala) becomes more reactive to reward under pubertal hormones before the prefrontal cortex has fully developed its regulatory control. This mismatch is proposed to explain heightened adolescent risk-taking, sensation-seeking, and sensitivity to peer influence — the "imbalance model" of adolescent brain development.
  • Sex differences in this asynchrony: boys show more pronounced risk-taking during adolescence; girls show higher rates of anxiety and depression onset, associated with changes in stress-reactivity circuitry.

Adolescence is also a period of social brain development: mentalizing networks (medial PFC, TPJ) become more active in social cognition tasks, peer relationships become paramount, and identity formation involves extensive prefrontal-limbic interaction. Many psychiatric disorders (depression, anxiety, psychosis, eating disorders) first manifest during this period, consistent with puberty and adolescent brain plasticity as a vulnerability window.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Congenital adrenal hyperplasia and the organizational hypothesis
Girls with congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) have a genetic mutation in 21-hydroxylase, causing impaired cortisol synthesis and compensatory overproduction of androgens (including testosterone) from the adrenal cortex. CAH girls have been exposed to elevated prenatal testosterone. What does the organizational hypothesis predict about their behaviour, and what do studies show?

The organizational hypothesis predicts that elevated prenatal testosterone will masculinize the brain during critical developmental windows, leading to more male-typical patterns of behaviour and cognition.

Studies on girls with CAH generally find:

  • Toy and play preference: CAH girls show preference for male-typical toys (vehicles, construction) and rough-and-tumble play relative to control girls. Effect is replicated across cultures.
  • Spatial cognition: CAH girls score somewhat higher on 3D mental rotation tasks than control girls.
  • Gender role identity: Most CAH girls identify as female; a higher proportion than controls report some gender nonconformity. A small but elevated proportion of adults with CAH identify as male or non-binary.
  • Sexual orientation: Higher rates of non-heterosexual attraction in women with CAH compared to controls.

CAH studies are the strongest human evidence for prenatal androgen effects on behaviour, since the prenatal hormone environment is abnormal due to a known genetic cause rather than researchers manipulating hormones (which is unethical). However, confounds include: CAH is a serious medical condition requiring lifelong management, girls are raised with genital ambiguity that may affect parental behaviour, and effect sizes for behavioural outcomes are moderate rather than complete masculinization.

Example 2 — Kisspeptin and the LH surge
In female rodents, a surge of estradiol in late follicular phase triggers a massive LH surge that causes ovulation. Explain the neural mechanism, and why this positive feedback only occurs in females.

Normally, estradiol exerts negative feedback on the HPG axis: high estradiol suppresses GnRH and LH. But in females, rising estradiol at mid-cycle switches to positive feedback, stimulating a massive GnRH/LH surge.

The mechanism: kisspeptin neurons in the anteroventral periventricular nucleus (AVPV) express estrogen receptors (ERα). When estradiol reaches a critical threshold, AVPV kisspeptin neurons are stimulated (positive feedback) and fire synchronously, releasing a large pulse of kisspeptin onto GnRH neurons → large GnRH surge → pituitary LH surge → ovulation.

In males, the AVPV kisspeptin population is much smaller (a structural difference organized by perinatal testosterone exposure). The arcuate nucleus kisspeptin neurons (which drive tonic pulsatile GnRH in both sexes) still show normal negative feedback; but the AVPV positive-feedback circuit is absent or minimal. Therefore males cannot generate a preovulatory LH surge even if given high doses of estradiol.

This sex difference in the AVPV was organized perinatally: if female rats are given testosterone at birth, their AVPV kisspeptin population is masculinized and they cannot generate an LH surge as adults — even after ovarian transplant.

Food for Thought
  • If most cognitive sex differences are small and distributions heavily overlap, at what point does a population-level mean difference justify different treatment or expectations for individuals — in medicine, in education, in occupational contexts?
    This is fundamentally a question about inference from population statistics to individuals, not a neuroscience question per se. Even a real average difference with d=0.5 means the distributions overlap enormously — around 69% of the female distribution overlaps with the male distribution in that case. Treating an individual based on their group's mean is statistically unjustified unless individual data is unavailable and the base rate difference is large (as in some disease risk contexts). In education and employment, where individual assessment is possible and cheap, relying on group mean differences is both statistically unsound and ethically problematic. In medicine, sex is a relevant variable for dosing (pharmacokinetics differ), disease risk, and symptom presentation — these are legitimate clinical applications of population-level differences.
  • The mismatch model of adolescent brain development proposes that subcortical reward regions mature before prefrontal regulatory regions, explaining adolescent risk-taking. But adolescent risk-taking is not uniformly elevated — it is specifically elevated in peer contexts. What does this tell you about the circuit model?
    A simple "subcortical overactivation due to immature PFC" model predicts that adolescents should make risky decisions consistently regardless of context. The fact that risk-taking is specifically heightened in peer presence (while alone or with adults, adolescents may show quite adult-like judgment) suggests the critical variable is social reward salience rather than general impulsivity. Peers engage the social reward system (OFC, ventral striatum) in a way that is particularly strong during adolescence — peers' approval signals activate dopaminergic reward circuits more powerfully. A more complete model: both the reward system's responsiveness to social signals and the prefrontal regulatory network's ability to override that signal are age-dependent, but the dominant effect is specifically about social-affective input, not general executive impairment.
  • Alpha-fetoprotein protects the developing female brain from estradiol in rodents. Humans have much lower AFP levels and different timing. Does this mean the aromatization hypothesis of human brain masculinization is wrong?
    The aromatization mechanism is well-established in rodents but its applicability to humans is uncertain and debated. In humans, AFP levels are lower and the timing of sexual differentiation is different (much of it may occur in the first trimester when AFP is not yet highly expressed). Several observations complicate direct extrapolation: girls with aromatase deficiency have relatively normal female development; boys with androgen receptor mutations (complete androgen insensitivity) develop female-typical external genitalia but their brain development and reported gender identity tends female. This latter finding is sometimes taken as evidence that testosterone acting via AR (not aromatized to estradiol) is critical for human brain masculinization — opposite to the rodent story. The honest answer is that we don't know the relative contributions of AR-mediated testosterone effects vs. aromatized estradiol effects in human brain development, and extrapolating from rodents is unreliable on this specific question.
Common Pitfalls
  • "Males and females have different brains." This is technically true in a small number of regions (especially hypothalamic nuclei), but implies a more binary difference than the data supports. The brain is mosaic — most individuals have features spanning the spectrum. "Different brains" is better understood as "different mean distributions with large overlap" rather than two distinct types.
  • "The menstrual cycle impairs cognitive performance." Large-scale studies with rigorous within-individual designs find no reliable cognitive impairment across the menstrual cycle. Early studies reporting cycle effects used small samples, between-subjects designs, and were susceptible to expectancy effects. The ovarian hormone fluctuations do affect mood and energy in a subset of people (premenstrual syndrome; PMDD), but do not impair general cognitive performance.
  • "Estrogen feminizes; testosterone masculinizes." In adult activational effects, this is roughly right. In developmental organizational effects, it is wrong: estrogen (aromatized from testosterone) masculinizes the male rodent brain. The same molecule can have opposite effects depending on timing and receptor context.
  • "Sex differences in cognition are caused by nature or nurture — not both." The evidence clearly shows both contribute. Prenatal androgens affect behavioural traits (CAH studies); socialization and gender-differentiated experience also affect trait development. Nature and nurture are not competing explanations — they interact at every level.
Connects To
  • Emotion & the Limbic System (L14): The amygdala shows sex differences in volume and connectivity; shows differential hormonal regulation. Stress response (HPA axis) is sexually dimorphic — females are generally more stress-responsive — which contributes to higher rates of PTSD and depression in women.
  • Neural Development (L11): The perinatal critical period for testosterone's organizational effects on the brain uses the same mechanisms as critical periods in other sensory systems — hormone-dependent synaptic pruning and structural remodeling. Adolescent puberty is a second critical period for social brain development.
  • Reward & Neuromodulation (L13): Sex hormones modulate dopaminergic reward circuit function — ovarian hormones alter striatal dopamine release and receptor expression. This contributes to sex differences in addiction vulnerability and the fluctuating reward sensitivity across the menstrual cycle.
