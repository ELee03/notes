Schizophrenia

Psychosis · Positive/Negative/Cognitive Symptoms · Dopamine Hypothesis · NMDA Hypofunction · Neurodevelopmental Origins · Antipsychotics

Schizophrenia is among the most severe psychiatric conditions, profoundly disrupting a person's ability to perceive reality, think coherently, and function in daily life. It is also one of the most misrepresented conditions in popular culture — most commonly confused with dissociative identity disorder (DID, "split personality"), with which it has nothing in common. Understanding schizophrenia requires understanding three distinct symptom clusters, two competing but complementary biological hypotheses, and a neurodevelopmental model that has fundamentally changed how the condition is conceptualized.

What It Is Like

Schizophrenia is not split personality. The name ("schizo" = split, "phrenia" = mind) referred to a splitting of mental functions — thought, perception, emotion — from each other, not a splitting of identity into multiple people. This confusion has caused enormous harm.

The experience of schizophrenia is organized around three clusters:

Positive symptoms (things added to normal experience) are the most dramatic and the most treatable:

  • Hallucinations — perceptions without external stimuli. Auditory hallucinations (voices) are most common: voices that comment on the person's behaviour, argue with each other, give commands, or provide a running commentary. Crucially, these are experienced as externally real, not as thoughts. Visual, tactile, and olfactory hallucinations occur but are less common.
  • Delusions — fixed false beliefs held with unshakeable conviction despite contradictory evidence. Persecutory delusions (being watched, plotted against) are most common. Ideas of reference (coincidences carry personal meaning: that newscast is sending me a message), thought insertion, thought withdrawal, and grandiose delusions also occur.
  • Disorganized thinking — loosening of associations, tangential speech, word salad in severe cases. Thinking that does not follow logical chains.

Negative symptoms (things subtracted from normal experience) are less dramatic but more persistently disabling:

  • Flat affect — reduced emotional expressiveness, monotone speech, expressionless face
  • Alogia — poverty of speech; brief, empty replies
  • Avolition — profound loss of motivation; difficulty initiating and sustaining goal-directed activity
  • Anhedonia — reduced capacity for pleasure
  • Social withdrawal

Cognitive symptoms — impairments in working memory, attention, processing speed, and executive function — are often the most predictive of long-term functional outcomes (employment, independence, relationships). Antipsychotics treat positive symptoms effectively but have minimal impact on negative and cognitive symptoms, which is why many people with schizophrenia remain significantly impaired even when their psychosis is controlled.

The typical course: onset in late adolescence or early adulthood (late teens/early 20s for men; somewhat later for women), often preceded by a prodromal period of social withdrawal, odd ideas, and declining function. Acute psychotic episodes punctuate a chronic course that varies considerably between individuals — some have few episodes and recover well, others have persistent symptoms despite treatment.

The Dopamine Hypothesis

The dopamine hypothesis is the oldest and most empirically supported biological theory of schizophrenia. It arose from two observations in the 1950s–60s:

  1. All effective antipsychotic drugs block D2 dopamine receptors. Their clinical potency correlates with D2 affinity.
  2. Dopamine-releasing drugs (amphetamine, cocaine, L-DOPA) can induce psychosis-like states in healthy people, including paranoid delusions and hallucinations.

The refined version distinguishes two components:

  • Mesolimbic dopamine hyperactivity: excess presynaptic dopamine release in the striatum generates aberrant salience — the brain incorrectly signals "this is important" to neutral stimuli. This may be the mechanism by which coincidences acquire the personal significance of delusions, and by which internal neural noise acquires the external reality of voices. PET imaging confirms elevated striatal dopamine synthesis and release in unmedicated schizophrenia.
  • Mesocortical dopamine hypoactivity: insufficient dopaminergic input to prefrontal cortex impairs PFC function, producing the cognitive and negative symptoms. This is why D2 blockers treat positive symptoms but often worsen or are inert for negative and cognitive symptoms — they reduce the mesolimbic excess but do not address the cortical deficit.

Why antipsychotics take weeks despite blocking D2 receptors within hours: immediate D2 blockade alone is not therapeutic. Over weeks of sustained blockade, mesolimbic dopamine neurons enter depolarization block — a state of tonic inactivation where they no longer generate phasic firing responses to salient stimuli. This normalization of dopamine dynamics, not simply the receptor blockade itself, is thought to underlie clinical improvement.

The Glutamate/NMDA Hypothesis

A second, complementary hypothesis centres on NMDA receptor hypofunction. The key evidence: NMDA antagonists (PCP, ketamine) produce the full syndrome of schizophrenia — positive, negative, and cognitive symptoms — more completely than amphetamine does. They also worsen schizophrenia in people who already have it.

The proposed mechanism: NMDA receptors on GABAergic interneurons (particularly fast-spiking parvalbumin-positive cells in PFC) are hypofunction. Since these interneurons normally inhibit excitatory pyramidal neurons, their hypofunction leads to disinhibited, disorganized pyramidal cell firing — consistent with the cognitive and negative symptom profile. NMDA hypofunction in prefrontal interneurons also disrupts the tightly regulated dopamine release in subcortical regions, potentially explaining the mesolimbic dopamine excess as a downstream consequence of cortical NMDA dysfunction.

The current synthesis: NMDA receptor dysfunction in cortical interneurons is a plausible upstream cause; dysregulated dopamine transmission is the proximate mechanism of positive symptoms. Neither hypothesis alone is complete.

Genetics and Neurodevelopmental Origins

Schizophrenia is highly heritable (~80%). The genetic architecture is diffuse — hundreds of common variants of small effect, plus rare CNVs with larger effects (22q11 deletion carries ~30% lifetime risk). Candidate genes cluster around glutamatergic signalling (GRIN2A, GRM3), synaptic scaffolding (DISC1), and immune function (C4A).

C4A is particularly interesting: it is a complement protein expressed at synapses that contributes to synaptic pruning during adolescent brain development. Overexpression of C4A may drive excessive synaptic elimination in PFC during the adolescent pruning period, potentially explaining both the neurodevelopmental origins and the late-adolescent onset. This connects schizophrenia to the neural development literature on critical periods and synaptic refinement.

The neurodevelopmental model holds that the vulnerability is established early (possibly prenatally) but only manifests clinically when the final wave of PFC maturation and pruning occurs in late adolescence. The trigger for the first psychotic episode is often stress, cannabis use, or sleep disruption — not causes, but precipitants in a vulnerable brain.

Treatment

Antipsychotics divide into:

  • Typical (first generation): primarily D2 antagonists (haloperidol, chlorpromazine). Effective for positive symptoms. High rate of extrapyramidal side effects (motor symptoms: parkinsonism, akathisia, tardive dyskinesia from chronic D2 blockade in basal ganglia).
  • Atypical (second generation): D2 antagonism plus 5-HT2A antagonism and other targets (clozapine, olanzapine, quetiapine, risperidone). Lower EPS risk. Clozapine is the most effective antipsychotic overall — it helps treatment-resistant cases — but carries risk of agranulocytosis requiring blood monitoring.

The limitations are significant: antipsychotics address positive symptoms in most patients but leave negative and cognitive symptoms largely untreated. Side effects (metabolic syndrome, sedation, EPS) reduce adherence. Non-adherence is the most common cause of relapse. Long-acting injectable formulations help adherence.

Psychosocial interventions — cognitive behavioural therapy for psychosis (CBTp), family therapy, supported employment — add meaningful benefit beyond medication and address the functional impairment that drugs don't reach.

Common Pitfalls
  • "Schizophrenia = split personality." Completely wrong. Split personality (now called Dissociative Identity Disorder) involves distinct identity states, not psychosis. The confusion has been harmful for decades.
  • "People with schizophrenia are violent." The association between schizophrenia and violence is small and largely explained by comorbid substance use and social circumstances. Most violence in society is committed by people without mental illness. People with schizophrenia are far more likely to be victims of violence than perpetrators.
  • "Antipsychotics cure schizophrenia." They suppress positive symptoms; they do not cure the condition and have minimal impact on negative and cognitive symptoms, which are often the primary determinants of functional outcomes.
