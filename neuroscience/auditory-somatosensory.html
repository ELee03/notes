Auditory & Somatosensory Systems

Cochlea · Tonotopy · Hair Cells · Auditory Pathway · Sound Localization · Mechanoreceptors · Dorsal Column Pathway · Spinothalamic Tract · Pain

Hearing and touch are fundamentally mechanical senses — they detect deformations. The ear transforms pressure waves in air into neural signals encoding frequency, amplitude, and location. The somatosensory system converts skin deformation, limb position, and tissue damage into parallel streams of tactile, proprioceptive, and pain signals. Both systems share the core principle seen in vision: peripheral transduction by specialized receptors, followed by parallel processing streams that extract different features and converge on topographically organized cortical maps.

The Cochlea

The cochlea is a snail-shaped fluid-filled structure in the inner ear that converts sound into neural signals. It contains three fluid-filled ducts separated by membranes: the scala vestibuli and scala tympani (filled with perilymph, high-Na⁺, low-K⁺) and the scala media (filled with endolymph, high-K⁺ — maintained by the stria vascularis). The endocochlear potential (+80 mV in the scala media, generated by active K⁺ pumping) is critical for hair cell function.

Sound waves enter via the oval window, displacing the basilar membrane that runs the length of the cochlea (35 mm in humans). The basilar membrane is not uniform — it is wide and floppy at the apex, narrow and stiff at the base. This gradient causes a travelling wave of displacement to propagate from base to apex with frequency-dependent peak amplitude: high frequencies peak at the base, low frequencies at the apex. This is tonotopy — the spatial encoding of frequency along the basilar membrane, a mechanical Fourier transform of the acoustic stimulus.

Sitting on the basilar membrane is the organ of Corti, containing the hair cells. There are ~3,500 inner hair cells (IHCs) — the primary sensory cells, each sending ~10 afferent fibres to the auditory nerve — and ~12,000 outer hair cells (OHCs), which are electromotile and act as a mechanical amplifier (cochlear amplifier), increasing the sensitivity and sharpening the frequency tuning of the basilar membrane response by up to 50 dB.

Mechanotransduction in hair cells: The stereocilia (hair-like projections) on IHCs are deflected by basilar membrane movement. Tip links connect adjacent stereocilia; when the bundle deflects toward the tallest stereocilium, tip links pull open mechanically-gated K⁺ channels (MET channels) at the tips. K⁺ flows in from the endolymph (which is at high K⁺ concentration and +80 mV relative to the cell) — the electrochemical gradient is enormous, driving a large K⁺ influx that depolarizes the hair cell. This opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels at the base, triggering glutamate release onto afferent neurons.

The auditory nerve (cranial nerve VIII) contains ~30,000 fibres, each tuned to a best frequency corresponding to its position along the basilar membrane. Each fibre encodes the amplitude envelope of its best-frequency component via its firing rate, and temporal fine structure via phase-locking — precisely timed spikes synchronized to the sound waveform, up to ~4–5 kHz.

The Auditory Pathway

The auditory pathway is more complex than the visual pathway — it has more obligatory synaptic stages and extensive bilateral projections at every level:

Cochlear Nucleus (CN) → Superior Olivary Complex (SOC) → Inferior Colliculus (IC) → Medial Geniculate Nucleus (MGN) of thalamus → Primary Auditory Cortex (A1)

The cochlear nucleus (brainstem) receives all auditory nerve input and immediately splits into parallel processing streams with different cell types extracting different features (onsets, sustained firing, pitch, timing).

The superior olivary complex is the first site of binaural convergence — neurons here receive input from both ears and are the first to compute the inter-ear differences used for sound localization.

The inferior colliculus (midbrain) is the major convergence point of the ascending auditory pathway. It integrates inputs from numerous brainstem nuclei and projects to the MGN.

In primary auditory cortex (A1), tonotopy is preserved — neurons are arranged in isofrequency bands. Beyond pure frequency, A1 neurons are sensitive to complex temporal patterns, combinations of frequencies, and the onset/offset of sounds. Higher auditory areas (belt and parabelt regions) process speech, music, and auditory object recognition.

Sound Localization

Localizing sound in space requires comparing the signal arriving at the two ears. The brain uses two primary cues:

Interaural time difference (ITD) — sound from a source to the left arrives at the left ear slightly before the right ear. The maximum ITD for a human head (~175 mm width) is approximately 650 µs. The medial superior olive (MSO) detects ITDs using coincidence detector neurons with delay lines — axons from the two cochlear nuclei arrive with different conduction delays, and the MSO neuron fires maximally when the delays are equal (a neural implementation of the Jeffress model). ITDs encode azimuth (left-right position) and are most useful for low-frequency sounds that can be phase-locked.

Interaural level difference (ILD) — high-frequency sounds (above ~1.5 kHz) are attenuated by the acoustic shadow cast by the head; the more distant ear receives a softer sound. The lateral superior olive (LSO) computes ILDs by comparing ipsilateral excitation to contralateral inhibition (via the MNTB as an inhibitory relay). ILDs encode azimuth for high-frequency sounds.

Elevation and front-back ambiguity are resolved by spectral filtering by the pinnae (outer ear) — the pinna's ridges produce direction-specific frequency notches (head-related transfer function, HRTF) that the auditory system has learned to use.

Somatosensory Receptors

The skin and deep tissues contain specialized receptor endings that transduce different mechanical properties. The four primary mechanoreceptor types in glabrous (non-hairy) skin:

Receptor Adapts Receptive field Detects
Merkel disc (SA1)Slowly Small, sharpForm, texture, edges
Ruffini ending (SA2)Slowly Large, diffuseSkin stretch, hand shape
Meissner corpuscle (RA1)Rapidly Small, sharpSlip, flutter, grip control
Pacinian corpuscle (RA2)Rapidly Large, diffuseVibration (100–300 Hz)

Slowly adapting (SA) receptors maintain firing as long as a stimulus is present — they signal sustained touch. Rapidly adapting (RA) receptors fire only at the onset (and offset) of a stimulus — they signal change.

Proprioceptors in muscles and joints inform the CNS of body position: muscle spindles (Ia and II afferents) detect muscle length and stretch velocity; Golgi tendon organs (Ib afferents) detect muscle tension; joint capsule receptors detect joint angle.

Somatosensory Pathways

Two major ascending pathways carry somatosensory information to the cortex:

Dorsal column–medial lemniscal (DCML) pathway — carries fine touch, vibration, and proprioception. First-order neurons from skin and deep tissue enter the spinal cord and ascend ipsilaterally in the dorsal columns all the way to the brainstem, where they synapse in the dorsal column nuclei (cuneate for upper body, gracile for lower body). Second-order neurons cross to the contralateral side (internal arcuate fibres) and ascend as the medial lemniscus to the thalamus (VPL nucleus). Third-order neurons project to primary somatosensory cortex (S1). Fast, precise, somatotopically organized.

Spinothalamic tract — carries pain, temperature, and crude touch. First-order neurons enter the spinal cord and synapse within 1–2 segments. Second-order neurons cross immediately in the anterior commissure and ascend contralaterally to the VPL. This rapid crossing is clinically important: a unilateral spinal cord lesion (e.g., Brown-Séquard syndrome) causes loss of fine touch ipsilateral to the lesion and loss of pain/temperature contralateral.

Somatosensory cortex (S1) in the postcentral gyrus is organized as a somatotopic map — the cortical homunculus, drawn by Penfield from electrical stimulation of cortex in awake neurosurgical patients. The body is mapped systematically, but with area proportional to innervation density: the hand, fingers, lips, and tongue have disproportionately large representations because they have the highest receptor density and finest discrimination (smallest two-point discrimination threshold).

Pain

Pain (nociception) is the sensory system's warning of actual or potential tissue damage. It is not simply a more intense version of touch — it has a distinct receptor type, distinct afferent fibres, and a distinct psychological quality.

Nociceptors are free nerve endings that respond to tissue-damaging stimuli — mechanical (sharp), thermal (hot or very cold), or chemical (bradykinin, prostaglandins, H⁺, capsaicin via TRPV1 channels). Two fibre types:

  • Aδ fibres (thinly myelinated, 5–30 m/s) — produce first pain: sharp, well-localized, immediate.
  • C fibres (unmyelinated, 0.5–2 m/s) — produce second pain: dull, burning, diffuse, prolonged. "Hit your thumb with a hammer" — you feel the initial sharp pain (Aδ), then a wave of throbbing pain (C).

Gate control theory (Melzack and Wall, 1965): Pain transmission to the brain is gated by a spinal circuit in the dorsal horn. Substantia gelatinosa (SG) interneurons inhibit transmission cells (T cells) that relay pain signals to the brain. SG cells are excited by large-diameter myelinated fibres (Aβ, for touch) and inhibited by small-diameter pain fibres (C). Touch activity "closes the gate"; pain fibre activity alone "opens the gate." This explains why rubbing an injury temporarily reduces pain. Descending pathways from brainstem (periaqueductal grey → raphe → spinal cord) modulate the gate via serotonin and enkephalins — the neurochemical basis of stress analgesia and placebo effects.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Tonotopy and place coding
The basilar membrane is ~35 mm long. A 20 Hz tone activates the apical tip; a 20,000 Hz tone activates the basal end. Approximately where does a 1,000 Hz tone activate the basilar membrane, given that frequency maps approximately logarithmically?

The Greenwood function maps position (in mm from apex) to frequency approximately as $f = A(10^{ax} - k)$ with $a \approx 0.06$ mm⁻¹ in humans. More intuitively, the logarithmic scale means each octave (factor of 2 in frequency) spans a roughly equal distance along the membrane (~3–4 mm).

From 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz is $\log_2(20000/20) = \log_2(1000) \approx 10$ octaves spread across ~35 mm. Each octave spans ~3.5 mm.

From 20 Hz to 1,000 Hz is $\log_2(1000/20) = \log_2(50) \approx 5.6$ octaves. Measured from the apex: $5.6 \times 3.5 \approx 20$ mm from the apex = ~15 mm from the base. A 1 kHz tone activates the middle-to-basal region of the basilar membrane.

Food for Thought
  • Congenital deafness from hair cell loss can be treated with cochlear implants — arrays of electrodes that directly stimulate auditory nerve fibres at tonotopic positions. Why do cochlear implant users report that speech sounds "robotic" initially, and how does the brain adapt over months of use?
    Cochlear implants provide only ~22 electrode channels (vs. ~30,000 auditory nerve fibres), giving coarse spectral resolution and no fine temporal structure. The missing fine structure is what makes voices sound natural. Over months, the brain recalibrates — cortical auditory maps reorganize in response to the new spectral representation, and users learn to extract speech information from the coarser signal. This plasticity is greater in early-implanted children, who can achieve near-normal speech perception, than in post-lingually deafened adults.
  • Phantom limb pain — patients who have lost a limb often experience pain "in" the missing limb. Gate control theory involves peripheral and spinal mechanisms. What does phantom limb pain suggest about where pain is ultimately constructed?
    Phantom pain occurs with no peripheral nociceptor input at all, demonstrating that pain is constructed by the brain, not simply transmitted from the periphery. The cortical map of the missing limb becomes "invaded" by adjacent representations (e.g., face input maps onto hand area), and spontaneous activity in the reorganized cortex is interpreted as coming from the phantom. This supports the view that pain is a brain construct — an inference about body state — not a direct readout of tissue damage. Mirror therapy (which creates visual feedback of the phantom limb moving) can relieve phantom pain by providing input to the body map.
Common Pitfalls
  • Confusing outer hair cells and inner hair cells. IHCs (~3,500) are the primary sensory cells sending 90–95% of afferent fibres to the brain. OHCs (~12,000) are electromotile amplifiers that sharpen tuning and increase sensitivity but do not directly drive the auditory nerve. Noise-induced hearing loss typically destroys OHCs first, broadening frequency tuning and reducing sensitivity before IHC loss causes profound deafness.
  • Forgetting that the spinothalamic tract crosses immediately. Pain/temperature fibres cross within 1–2 spinal segments of entry. A right-sided spinal lesion causes loss of pain/temperature on the LEFT side below the lesion (because those fibres had already crossed), but loss of touch/proprioception on the RIGHT (DCML fibres haven't crossed yet). Students often get this backwards.
Connects to

Bioelectricity — mechanotransduction in hair cells (MET channels) and nociceptors (TRPV1, TRPA1 channels) are voltage-gated channels with specific biophysical properties. The endocochlear potential generated by stria vascularis is a specialized electrochemical gradient system.

Motor Systems (Lesson 6) — proprioceptive signals from muscle spindles and Golgi tendon organs are the feedback signals for spinal reflex circuits and cerebellar error correction. The somatosensory system is as much about position and movement as about touch.
