Psychedelics & the Brain

5-HT2A Agonism · Default Mode Network · REBUS Model · Neuroplasticity · MDMA · Therapeutic Applications · Safety

Psychedelics are among the most pharmacologically fascinating substances known — small molecules that can, within an hour, produce the most profound alterations in conscious experience a human being can undergo short of a near-death event, and then resolve cleanly with no obvious tissue damage. For decades they were cultural objects and political liabilities before they were serious science. They are now the centre of one of the most productive research programmes in psychiatry, offering genuine therapeutic promise for conditions that have resisted treatment for generations — depression, PTSD, addiction, end-of-life anxiety. Understanding how they work illuminates fundamental questions about consciousness, predictive coding, and the relationship between brain dynamics and subjective experience.

The Major Classes

The term "psychedelic" is used loosely. Three mechanistically distinct drug classes are commonly grouped under it:

Classic psychedelics (serotonergic psychedelics) are the core of the category: LSD (lysergic acid diethylamide), psilocybin (the prodrug in magic mushrooms, dephosphorylated in vivo to psilocin), DMT (N,N-dimethyltryptamine, the active compound in ayahuasca and found endogenously in trace amounts), and mescaline (from peyote). These all act as agonists at the 5-HT2A serotonin receptor. Despite their different structures — LSD is an ergoline, psilocin an indole, mescaline a phenethylamine — their phenomenology overlaps substantially because they converge on the same receptor.

Dissociatives — ketamine, PCP (phencyclidine), and MXE — produce a dissociative state through NMDA receptor antagonism. The phenomenology is distinct from classic psychedelics (more anesthetic, "out-of-body") but ketamine is increasingly grouped with psychedelic-assisted therapies because of overlapping therapeutic applications. Covered in more depth in the Depression lesson.

Entactogens / empathogens — principally MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine) — produce profound prosocial, empathogenic effects via massive monoamine release rather than 5-HT2A agonism. The subjective experience differs markedly from classic psychedelics (warmth, emotional openness, less perceptual distortion), but MDMA-assisted therapy is a serious therapeutic programme, so it belongs in this lesson.

The 5-HT2A Mechanism

Classic psychedelics are partial or full agonists at the 5-HT2A receptor, a Gq-coupled metabotropic receptor for serotonin. Crucially, endogenous serotonin also binds 5-HT2A, so the question is not why a foreign molecule hits the receptor — it is what is different about how psychedelics activate it.

The key concept is biased agonism. GPCRs signal through multiple downstream pathways: the canonical G-protein pathway (Gq → PLC → IP3 → Ca²⁺ → PKC) and the β-arrestin pathway. Psychedelics are biased toward the β-arrestin pathway compared to serotonin, and specifically toward a signalling complex involving β-arrestin and Src kinase. This distinct signalling profile — not just the receptor binding — appears responsible for the psychedelic effect. Non-psychedelic 5-HT2A agonists (like the appetite suppressant lorcaserin) bind the same receptor but do not produce hallucinogenic effects.

Where does this matter in the brain? The highest density of 5-HT2A receptors is on the apical dendrites of layer V pyramidal neurons in the cortex — the cells that provide the main output from cortex to subcortical structures. When these neurons are activated by a psychedelic, they depolarize more easily and fire in response to inputs they would normally filter. The result is a breakdown of normal hierarchical signal processing: excitatory noise that would ordinarily be suppressed as irrelevant reaches higher-order areas and enters awareness. This is the neural basis of perceptual distortions, pattern-seeing, and the intrusion of normally subthreshold imagery into consciousness.

Psychedelics also trigger glutamate release in prefrontal cortex via these layer V neurons, activating AMPA receptors and mTOR signalling — the same pathway ketamine uses, but through a different entry point. This glutamate surge is thought to underlie both the acute neuroplastic effects and some of the longer-lasting structural changes described below.

The Default Mode Network and Ego Dissolution

The default mode network (DMN) is a set of cortical midline structures — medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, angular gyrus, and precuneus — that are active at rest, during self-referential thought, mental time travel (imagining past and future), and social cognition. The DMN is essentially the brain's "self-model" — the neural substrate of the narrative self, the story you tell yourself about who you are across time.

Psychedelics reliably and profoundly suppress DMN activity and, crucially, disrupt the normal anticorrelation between the DMN and task-positive networks. Normally, when you are engaged with the external world, the DMN quiets and task-positive networks activate; they are mutually inhibitory. Psychedelics break this gating — the DMN and other networks become coupled in abnormal patterns, and the boundaries between normally distinct networks dissolve.

The subjective correlate of DMN suppression is ego dissolution — the attenuation or complete loss of the sense of being a bounded, continuous self. This ranges from mild (reduced self-referential rumination, sense of merging with environment) to complete (the self simply ceases to be experienced; what remains is experience without an experiencer). Ego dissolution is not simply sedation or confusion — it can occur in the context of fully alert, lucid experience. The degree of ego dissolution correlates directly with the degree of DMN suppression on fMRI.

The therapeutic relevance is significant. Many psychiatric conditions — depression, addiction, OCD, PTSD, end-of-life anxiety — involve an overactive, rigid self-narrative: ruminative loops, rigid self-identification with trauma, inability to disengage from threat-related thinking. Temporarily suspending the neural machinery that generates this narrative may create a window in which beliefs, habits, and self-concepts that feel immovable become revisable.

The REBUS Model

Robin Carhart-Harris and Karl Friston proposed the REBUS model (Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics, 2019) as a unified account of how psychedelics alter conscious experience, grounded in the predictive coding / active inference framework of brain function.

In the predictive coding account, perception is not passive recording — it is active inference. The brain constructs a generative model of the world and continuously generates top-down predictions about sensory inputs. Sensory areas report the discrepancy between what was predicted and what arrived (the prediction error). Higher areas use these errors to update their model. Crucially, the influence of predictions versus sensory signals is weighted by their relative precision — the brain decides how much to trust its priors versus incoming sensory data.

REBUS proposes that psychedelics, by activating 5-HT2A receptors on layer V pyramidal neurons, flatten the cortical hierarchy by increasing the gain on bottom-up prediction errors relative to top-down predictions. The result: the brain's high-level priors — including the deep, entrenched priors constituting the self-model — exert less suppression on lower-level sensory and associative processing. Normally subthreshold signals bubble up; normally rigid beliefs become fluid. Hallucinatory percepts arise not from inserting novel imagery but from freeing internal generative models (especially in early visual areas) to run unconstrained by top-down correction.

The therapeutic implication: deeply entrenched maladaptive beliefs — about the self, about safety, about the future — are high-level priors. Under psychedelics, these priors become temporarily less binding. Combined with a therapeutic context designed to direct this opened state toward belief revision, the result can be lasting change that months of conventional therapy fail to achieve. The question REBUS raises is whether the therapeutic benefit comes primarily from the pharmacology, the psychological content of the experience (the mystical experience), or the interaction between them — an open empirical question.

Neuroplasticity: Psychedelics as Psychoplastogens

One of the most important recent discoveries is that classic psychedelics are psychoplastogens — they rapidly and robustly promote structural neuroplasticity. David Olson's lab showed that LSD, DMT, and psilocin increase dendritic spine density, dendritic arbour complexity, and synapse number in prefrontal cortex within 24 hours, in rodents. The effect sizes are large and persist for weeks after a single dose.

The mechanism involves TrkB, the receptor for BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor). Psychedelics — particularly LSD and psilocin — directly bind TrkB at a transmembrane site distinct from the BDNF binding site, acting as allosteric modulators. This activates the BDNF-TrkB signalling cascade (PLCγ → IP3 → Ca²⁺; PI3K → Akt → mTOR) and drives synaptic protein synthesis. The convergence with ketamine is notable: both drugs ultimately converge on mTOR-driven synaptogenesis in PFC, via different receptor mechanisms. This pathway is the opposite of what chronic stress produces — stress reduces dendritic spine density and BDNF expression in PFC, and depression is associated with these reductions. Psychedelics appear to rapidly reverse them.

The clinical correlate: psilocybin produces antidepressant effects that can last months after a single or double dose in trials — far longer than the drug is pharmacologically active (psilocin's half-life is ~3 hours). The persistent benefit cannot be explained by the acute receptor pharmacology alone; it is likely sustained by structural synaptic changes. This is why the therapeutic protocol matters — the plasticity window may be an opportunity for re-learning, and what gets learned during or after the session may determine the durability of benefit.

MDMA: A Different Mechanism

MDMA is not a 5-HT2A agonist. It works by entering monoamine nerve terminals via the monoamine transporters (SERT, DAT, NET) and then reversing transporter direction, causing massive efflux of serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine into the synapse. The serotonin release is quantitatively dominant (roughly 3× the dopamine release). MDMA also inhibits monoamine oxidase, extending the monoamine presence, and promotes the release of oxytocin from the hypothalamus via 5-HT1A activation.

The phenomenological result — warmth, empathy, reduced social anxiety, emotional openness, reduced defensive responding to threatening stimuli — is largely serotonin-mediated. The amygdala's threat response is suppressed; emotional memories become accessible without triggering the usual defensive arousal. This is why MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD is conceptually compelling: PTSD involves emotional memories that are inaccessible in ordinary therapy because their retrieval triggers overwhelming fear responses. Under MDMA, the memory can be re-accessed and processed in a state of reduced threat, potentially allowing the fear association to undergo extinction learning or reconsolidation to a less aversive trace.

Phase 3 trials (MAPS, 2021–2023) showed that two to three MDMA-assisted therapy sessions produced PTSD remission in ~67% of participants versus ~32% with therapy alone. These are extraordinary numbers for a condition notorious for treatment resistance. The FDA declined to approve esketamine-like approval in 2024, citing concerns about trial design (blinding is difficult when participants know whether they received MDMA), but the therapeutic signal is real and the research continues.

MDMA carries genuine neurotoxicity risks at high doses or with repeated use — particularly serotonergic axon terminal damage detectable in imaging of heavy recreational users. Therapeutic dosing (75–120 mg, two to three sessions lifetime) is far below the recreational-binge range where toxicity has been observed, but this remains an open safety question for any broader rollout.

Therapeutic Applications

The modern psychedelic therapy renaissance, based at centres including Johns Hopkins, Imperial College London, and NYU, has produced controlled trial evidence for:

  • Treatment-resistant depression (TRD): Psilocybin (two doses, 25 mg) produced rapid, large antidepressant effects in multiple trials, including the Imperial College randomized controlled trial (2021) that found effects comparable to escitalopram at 6 weeks. Effect sizes are large; a meaningful fraction of TRD patients achieve remission.
  • End-of-life anxiety and existential distress: Single-dose psilocybin in patients with life-threatening illness (cancer) produces lasting reductions in depression and anxiety and improvements in meaning and acceptance — effects still detectable at 4.5-year follow-up in some studies. The mystical experience quality of the session predicts outcome.
  • Addiction: Open-label and pilot data for psilocybin in tobacco cessation and alcohol use disorder are striking (80% abstinence at 6 months in the tobacco study). The mechanism is speculative — possibly related to disruption of habit circuits, reduction of craving through DMN disruption, or the perspective shift of the experience itself.
  • PTSD: MDMA-assisted therapy, as described above — the most mature clinical programme, with Phase 3 data.
  • OCD: Early open-label data for psilocybin, consistent with the CSTC loop disruption hypothesis. Understudied.

The trial format matters. Psychedelic-assisted therapy is not just "take drug, feel better" — it involves preparation sessions, extended dosing sessions with trained therapists present, and integration sessions afterward. The drug creates a window; the therapeutic work determines what gets built in it. This is fundamentally different from antidepressants, which work regardless of whether the patient does anything particular during treatment.

Microdosing — taking sub-perceptual doses (1/10 to 1/20 of a full dose) regularly — has attracted enormous popular interest. Controlled trials have mostly found that the reported cognitive and mood benefits do not exceed placebo when blinding is adequate. The expectation effect is very large in this population. Microdosing probably does not produce the neuroplastic and DMN-disrupting effects that underlie full-dose therapeutic benefit.

Safety and Risk

Physiological safety: Classic psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, DMT, mescaline) have extraordinarily low physiological toxicity. No established lethal dose exists from receptor-mediated effects in humans — there are no receptors that, when fully occupied, produce a fatal outcome. LSD's estimated human lethal dose, extrapolated from animal data, is orders of magnitude above a psychoactive dose. Cardiovascular effects (mild tachycardia and hypertension from serotonergic activation) exist but are rarely clinically significant in healthy adults. They are not physically addictive — 5-HT2A receptor downregulation produces rapid tolerance within 3–4 days of repeated dosing, making compulsive daily use self-limiting.

Psychological risks are real and not trivial:

  • Difficult experiences / "bad trips": Acute psychological distress, panic, paranoia, and confusion occur, particularly with high doses, adverse set (mindset) and setting, or unacknowledged psychological material. In a supervised therapeutic context, distressing experiences are manageable and sometimes therapeutically productive. In unsupervised recreational settings, they can be dangerous — particularly because impaired judgment can lead to accidents.
  • HPPD (Hallucinogen Persisting Perception Disorder): A minority of users report persistent visual disturbances (geometric patterns, tracers, halos) that remain after the drug has cleared. The prevalence is hard to estimate — surveys find 0.5–4% of users report some persistent perceptual changes, but clinically distressing HPPD is much rarer. Mechanisms are unknown.
  • Psychosis risk in vulnerable individuals: Classic psychedelics can precipitate or accelerate the onset of psychotic illness in people with a personal or strong family history of psychotic disorders (schizophrenia, bipolar I). This is a genuine contraindication, not a myth. The causal direction is debated — some of these episodes may represent an underlying vulnerability that would have manifested anyway — but the association is real and the risk is not acceptable. Psychiatric screening is part of responsible clinical trial protocols.
  • Serotonin syndrome risk: Combining serotonergic psychedelics with other serotonergic drugs (MAOIs, lithium, SSRIs) can produce serotonin syndrome, a potentially dangerous excess of serotonin activity. Ayahuasca contains both DMT and an MAOI; this combination is intentional and generally safe in its traditional dose range, but combining with other serotonergic medications is dangerous.
Common Pitfalls
  • "Psychedelics cause hallucinations by adding serotonin." Psychedelics are 5-HT2A agonists, not serotonin releasers. Endogenous serotonin also binds 5-HT2A without producing psychedelic effects. The effect depends on biased agonism and specific downstream signalling, not on raising serotonin levels.
  • "They're completely safe because they're natural / non-addictive." Non-addictive does not mean safe for everyone. The psychological risks — particularly for people with predisposition to psychosis — are real. "Natural" is irrelevant to pharmacology.
  • "Microdosing has the same therapeutic effects as full doses." There is no controlled evidence for meaningful cognitive or mood enhancement from microdosing beyond placebo. The neuroplastic and DMN-disrupting effects that drive full-dose therapy require full doses.
  • "The therapeutic benefit is just from the subjective experience / insight." The synaptic remodelling evidence suggests that pharmacological neuroplasticity — independent of subjective experience — plays a role. Non-psychedelic structural analogues that preserve plasticity-promoting effects without perceptual effects are under active development (tabernanthalog, analogue series from the Olson lab).
  • "SSRIs block psychedelic effects." Chronic SSRI use does reduce the subjective effects of classic psychedelics — probably through 5-HT2A receptor downregulation — but does not eliminate them entirely. The interaction is real, clinically relevant for patients considering psilocybin therapy, and under-studied.
