Bipolar Disorder

Mania vs Hypomania · Bipolar I vs II · Circadian Disruption · Lithium · Mood Stabilizers · Misdiagnosis

Bipolar disorder is characterized by episodes of mania or hypomania alternating with episodes of depression. The name and the "mood swings" characterization underplay what the condition involves — mania is not just elevated mood, and the disorder is not about rapid emotional fluctuation in daily life. It is a condition of episodic, dramatic shifts in brain state that can be profoundly disabling and dangerous, and that is frequently misdiagnosed as unipolar depression, personality disorder, or in the hypomanic state, not recognized as pathological at all.

What Mania Is — and Isn't

Mania is not "being really happy." The manic state involves:

  • Elevated, expansive, or irritable mood — often a mixture of euphoria and irritability rather than pure happiness
  • Grandiosity — inflated self-esteem, sense of special ability or mission, unrealistic plans
  • Decreased need for sleep — not insomnia, but genuinely feeling rested after 2–3 hours and having enormous energy
  • Pressured speech — rapid, difficult to interrupt, jumping between topics
  • Racing thoughts / flight of ideas
  • Increased goal-directed activity — starting many projects, hypersexuality, high productivity
  • Impulsive, risky behaviour — spending sprees, reckless driving, sexual indiscretion, poor business decisions made with extreme confidence

The distinguishing feature of mania versus depression is that it is often ego-syntonic in its early stages — the person feels great and may resist intervention. This is one reason bipolar disorder is underdiagnosed: the depressive episodes bring people to treatment, but the hypomanic/early manic periods are often not reported because they feel like finally feeling well. Clinicians who only see the depressive presentation may diagnose unipolar depression and prescribe antidepressants, which can trigger a manic episode in bipolar disorder.

Hypomania is a less severe form — the same qualitative features but not severe enough to cause marked impairment, not requiring hospitalization, and without psychotic features. Hypomania can feel productive and pleasurable, which further reduces reporting. Bipolar I requires at least one full manic episode; Bipolar II requires hypomania and major depression but no full mania. Cyclothymia involves milder fluctuations below the threshold for either pole.

Psychotic features (grandiose or persecutory delusions, hallucinations) occur in severe mania and can be confused with schizophrenia. The distinction: in bipolar disorder, psychosis is mood-congruent (grandiose delusions during mania) and episodic; in schizophrenia, psychosis tends to be mood-incongruent and more persistent.

Neuroscience

Bipolar disorder is highly heritable (~75–80%). The genetic risk overlaps substantially with schizophrenia (shared GWAS loci), consistent with a shared neurodevelopmental substrate that produces different phenotypes depending on genetic background. Lithium-response, circadian gene variants (CLOCK, TIMELESS), and calcium channel genes (CACNA1C) are among the most replicated findings.

Circadian disruption is increasingly recognized as central to bipolar disorder rather than a side effect of it. Bipolar disorder involves dysregulation of the same clock mechanisms (CLOCK/BMAL1 heterodimer, PER/CRY feedback loops) described in the Sleep lesson. Most manic episodes are precipitated by sleep disruption — shift work, jet lag, a night of missed sleep. Maintaining strict sleep schedules is one of the most evidence-based behavioural interventions.

The neuroimaging profile overlaps with depression: reduced prefrontal volume and activity, amygdala hyperreactivity. Distinctive features in bipolar: exaggerated ventral striatal reward responsivity (correlates with manic risk), and reduced OFC control over limbic structures in both poles.

The dopaminergic model of mania: excess mesolimbic dopamine activity, producing heightened reward sensitivity, drive, and grandiosity — the same system that is excessive in schizophrenia's positive symptoms but here recruited in a context of intact reality testing (in hypomania) or mood-congruent distortion (in full mania). This is why antipsychotics (D2 blockers) are effective mood stabilizers in acute mania.

Treatment

Lithium remains the gold-standard mood stabilizer after 70 years. It reduces the frequency and severity of both manic and depressive episodes and is the only medication with strong evidence for reducing suicide risk in bipolar disorder. Its mechanism is incompletely understood — it inhibits GSK-3β (affecting circadian gene expression and neuroplasticity signalling), inositol monophosphatase, and several other targets. Its therapeutic window is narrow; toxicity monitoring (blood levels, renal and thyroid function) is required.

Anticonvulsants — valproate (sodium valproate), lamotrigine, carbamazepine — are effective mood stabilizers. Valproate is particularly effective for mixed episodes and rapid cycling. Lamotrigine is more effective for the depressive pole. Valproate carries significant teratogenic risk (neural tube defects) and should be used with caution in women of childbearing age.

Atypical antipsychotics — quetiapine, olanzapine, aripiprazole — are approved for acute mania and maintenance. Quetiapine is also effective for bipolar depression.

Antidepressants in bipolar are controversial. In bipolar I, antidepressants without a mood stabilizer can trigger mania and are generally avoided. In bipolar II, the evidence is mixed. Current guidelines generally recommend against antidepressant monotherapy in bipolar.

Psychosocial interventions — particularly Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT), which focuses on regularizing daily routines and sleep schedules — have evidence for reducing episode frequency. CBT adapted for bipolar helps with relapse prevention and managing the psychological consequences of the condition.

Common Pitfalls
  • "Bipolar means mood swings." The condition involves episodic shifts lasting days to months, not rapid emotional reactivity within a day. Emotional lability (rapidly shifting emotions in response to events) is not what defines bipolar disorder.
  • "Hypomania is fine, it's productive." Hypomania can feel productive but it impairs judgement, disrupts relationships and finances, and reliably precedes deeper episodes in most people with bipolar II. Not recognizing or reporting hypomania leads to inadequate treatment.
  • "Treating depression is the main goal." Treating the depressive pole without mood stabilization risks inducing mania. Antidepressants prescribed without a mood stabilizer for what turns out to be bipolar II depression is one of the most common diagnostic errors in psychiatry.
  • "Lithium is old and crude." Lithium has a stronger evidence base for long-term mood stabilization and suicide prevention than any of the newer alternatives. "Newer" does not mean better in this case.
