Discrete-Time Systems
z-Transform · Pulse Transfer Functions · Sampling · ZOH · Tustin · Digital PID
Every control loop implemented in software runs on a computer that reads sensors and computes actuator commands at discrete time instants — once every $T_s$ seconds. The continuous-time theory of Lessons 1–14 assumes signals exist at every moment; a digital controller breaks this assumption. Discrete-time control is not a minor implementation detail. It introduces its own stability conditions (the unit circle replaces the left half-plane), its own design tools (the z-transform replaces Laplace), and its own failure modes (aliasing, quantisation, inter-sample ripple). Half of practical modern control is digital — this lesson covers the tools needed to design and analyse digital controllers rigorously.

Why discrete-time analysis matters
A continuous-time plant is connected to a digital controller through two operations: an analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) that samples the plant output at intervals $T_s$, and a digital-to-analogue converter (DAC) that holds the computed control signal constant until the next sample (a zero-order hold, ZOH).
The key effects introduced by sampling:
  • Aliasing. If the plant signal contains frequency components above $f_s/2$ (the Nyquist frequency), they fold back into the sampled signal as lower-frequency imposters — aliasing. An anti-aliasing low-pass filter must remove these components before sampling.
  • Phase lag. The ZOH introduces a time delay of approximately $T_s/2$ in the effective loop. This phase lag reduces the phase margin compared to the continuous-time design, potentially destabilising a system that was stable in continuous time.
  • Stability boundary changes. In continuous time, stability requires poles in the left half-plane. In discrete time, it requires poles inside the unit circle $|z| < 1$. A stable continuous-time pole at $s = \sigma + j\omega$ ($\sigma < 0$) maps to $z = e^{sT_s}$, which is inside the unit circle — but the mapping is nonlinear, and high-gain or fast-sampling effects can push poles outside.
  • Finite word length. Digital controllers implement arithmetic with finite precision. Coefficient quantisation and limit cycles (oscillations due to rounding) are real concerns in fixed-point implementations.
The z-transform
The z-transform is to difference equations what the Laplace transform is to differential equations. For a causal discrete-time signal $x[k]$: $$X(z) = \mathcal{Z}\{x[k]\} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty x[k]\,z^{-k}$$ The key property is the time-shift theorem: $\mathcal{Z}\{x[k-1]\} = z^{-1}X(z)$. This is the discrete-time analogue of multiplying by $s$ in Laplace. A delay of $n$ samples corresponds to multiplication by $z^{-n}$.

The final value theorem for discrete-time systems: if $x[k] \to x_\infty$ as $k\to\infty$, then $x_\infty = \lim_{z\to 1}(1-z^{-1})X(z) = \lim_{z\to 1}\frac{z-1}{z}X(z)$. This is the exact analogue of the Laplace final value theorem $\lim_{s\to 0} sX(s)$.
Sampling theorem and aliasing
Shannon-Nyquist sampling theorem. A continuous-time signal with no frequency content above $f_\text{max}$ Hz can be perfectly reconstructed from samples taken at rate $f_s \geq 2f_\text{max}$. The frequency $f_s/2$ is the Nyquist frequency.
Why aliasing happens

Sampling at rate $f_s$ is equivalent to multiplying the signal by an impulse train $\sum_k \delta(t - kT_s)$. In the frequency domain, this is convolution with another impulse train at multiples of $f_s$. The spectrum of the sampled signal is: $$X_s(f) = f_s \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty X(f - nf_s)$$ Each term is a copy of $X(f)$ shifted by $nf_s$. If $X(f) = 0$ for $|f| > f_s/2$, the copies don't overlap and $X(f)$ can be recovered by low-pass filtering. If the signal has energy above $f_s/2$, the copies overlap — the high-frequency content folds back into $[0, f_s/2]$ and is indistinguishable from a genuine lower-frequency signal. This is aliasing. $\blacksquare$

Choosing $T_s$ in practice. The Nyquist criterion gives a lower bound. In practice, sample at least $10\times$ the closed-loop bandwidth — this keeps the ZOH phase lag small compared to the system dynamics and leaves room for the anti-aliasing filter roll-off. For a bandwidth of $\omega_{bw}$: $$f_s \geq \frac{10\,\omega_{bw}}{2\pi}$$
Pulse transfer functions
The pulse transfer function $G(z)$ is the z-domain input-output relationship of a discrete-time system, analogous to $G(s)$ in continuous time. For a difference equation: $$y[k] + a_1 y[k-1] + \cdots + a_n y[k-n] = b_0 u[k] + b_1 u[k-1] + \cdots + b_m u[k-m]$$ Taking the z-transform and dividing: $G(z) = Y(z)/U(z) = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + b_m z^{-m}}{1 + a_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + a_n z^{-n}}$.
Stability: the unit circle. A discrete-time system is stable if and only if all poles of $G(z)$ lie strictly inside the unit circle: $|z_i| < 1$ for all poles $z_i$. Compare to continuous time: $\text{Re}(s_i) < 0$.
The mapping $z = e^{sT_s}$ connects the s-plane to the z-plane:
  • Left half-plane ($\text{Re}(s) < 0$) $\to$ inside unit circle ($|z| < 1$). Stable in both.
  • Imaginary axis ($\text{Re}(s) = 0$) $\to$ unit circle ($|z| = 1$). Marginally stable in both.
  • Right half-plane ($\text{Re}(s) > 0$) $\to$ outside unit circle ($|z| > 1$). Unstable in both.
  • The strip $-\pi/T_s < \text{Im}(s) < \pi/T_s$ (the primary strip) maps to the entire z-plane. Frequencies above $\omega_s/2$ alias to lower frequencies — another view of the aliasing problem.
Discretisation: ZOH and Tustin methods
Given a continuous-time plant $G_p(s)$, we need a discrete-time equivalent $G_p(z)$ for analysis and simulation. Two main methods:
Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) discretisation — the exact discretisation that matches the physical setup (the DAC holds the input constant between samples): $$G_p(z) = \mathcal{Z}\!\left\{\frac{1-e^{-sT_s}}{s}\,G_p(s)\right\} = (1-z^{-1})\,\mathcal{Z}\!\left\{\frac{G_p(s)}{s}\right\}$$ ZOH discretisation exactly reproduces the continuous-time step response at the sample instants. It's the standard for converting a plant model to discrete time.
Tustin (bilinear) method — the standard for converting a continuous-time controller $C(s)$ to discrete time. It uses the approximation $s \approx \frac{2}{T_s}\frac{z-1}{z+1}$, substituted into $C(s)$: $$C(z) = C(s)\big|_{s = \frac{2}{T_s}\frac{z-1}{z+1}}$$ The bilinear mapping preserves the left-half-plane $\leftrightarrow$ unit circle relationship exactly (no aliasing in the frequency response) and preserves passivity. The frequency response of $C(z)$ matches $C(s)$ up to a frequency warping — frequencies are distorted by $\omega_a = \frac{2}{T_s}\tan(\omega T_s / 2)$.
Pre-warping for Tustin. If the controller has a critical frequency $\omega_c$ (e.g. a notch filter or resonance), apply Tustin with pre-warping: replace $s \to \frac{\omega_c}{\tan(\omega_c T_s/2)}\frac{z-1}{z+1}$ to ensure the digital controller matches $C(j\omega_c)$ exactly at $\omega_c$.
Digital PID
The continuous-time PID controller $C(s) = K_p + \frac{K_i}{s} + K_d s$ must be discretised for implementation. Using Tustin with $s \to \frac{2}{T_s}\frac{z-1}{z+1}$:
Proportional term: $K_p \to K_p$ (unchanged).
Integral term: $\frac{K_i}{s} \to \frac{K_i T_s}{2}\frac{z+1}{z-1}$. This is the trapezoidal (bilinear) integration rule. In the time domain: $$I[k] = I[k-1] + \frac{K_i T_s}{2}(e[k] + e[k-1])$$
Derivative term: $K_d s \to K_d \frac{2}{T_s}\frac{z-1}{z+1}$. In practice, the pure derivative is also low-pass filtered at frequency $N/T_s$ (where $N \approx 5$–$20$) to reduce noise sensitivity: $$C_D(z) = K_d \frac{N(z-1)}{z - e^{-NT_s/T_s}}$$
The complete digital PID update equations (velocity form — avoids integrator reset on mode change): 
// Velocity (incremental) form — update ΔU at each sample
e_k = r[k] - y[k]                   // current error
dU = Kp*(e_k - e_prev)              // P increment
   + Ki*Ts*e_k                       // I increment (backward Euler, simpler)
   + Kd/Ts*(e_k - 2*e_prev + e_pprev) // D increment (second difference)
u[k] = u[k-1] + dU
e_pprev = e_prev
e_prev = e_k
Worked examples
Example 1 — z-transform of a first-order system
A continuous-time plant $G_p(s) = \frac{1}{s+2}$ is sampled with $T_s = 0.1$ s using ZOH discretisation. Find the pulse transfer function $G_p(z)$.
ZOH formula: $G_p(z) = (1-z^{-1})\,\mathcal{Z}\!\left\{\frac{G_p(s)}{s}\right\}$. First, $\frac{G_p(s)}{s} = \frac{1}{s(s+2)} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{s} - \frac{1}{s+2}\right)$. z-transform: $\mathcal{Z}\!\left\{\frac{1}{s}\right\} = \frac{z}{z-1}$ and $\mathcal{Z}\!\left\{\frac{1}{s+2}\right\} = \frac{z}{z-e^{-2T_s}} = \frac{z}{z - e^{-0.2}}$. So $\mathcal{Z}\!\left\{\frac{G_p(s)}{s}\right\} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{z}{z-1} - \frac{z}{z-e^{-0.2}}\right)$. Multiply by $(1-z^{-1}) = \frac{z-1}{z}$: $$G_p(z) = \frac{z-1}{z} \cdot \frac{z}{2}\cdot\frac{(z-e^{-0.2}) - (z-1)}{(z-1)(z-e^{-0.2})} = \frac{1 - e^{-0.2}}{2(z - e^{-0.2})}$$ With $e^{-0.2} \approx 0.8187$: $G_p(z) = \frac{0.0907}{z - 0.8187}$. The pole at $z = 0.8187$ is inside the unit circle — stable, as expected (the continuous-time pole at $s = -2$ is stable).
Example 2 — Tustin discretisation of a PI controller
A PI controller $C(s) = 3 + \frac{1.5}{s}$ with $T_s = 0.05$ s. Convert to discrete time using Tustin. Write the difference equation.
Substitute $s = \frac{2}{T_s}\frac{z-1}{z+1} = 40\frac{z-1}{z+1}$: $$C(z) = 3 + \frac{1.5}{40\frac{z-1}{z+1}} = 3 + \frac{1.5(z+1)}{40(z-1)} = 3 + \frac{0.01875(z+1)}{z-1}$$ Combining over a common denominator: $$C(z) = \frac{3(z-1) + 0.01875(z+1)}{z-1} = \frac{(3+0.01875)z + (0.01875-3)}{z-1} = \frac{3.01875z - 2.98125}{z-1}$$ In the time domain ($U(z)/E(z) = C(z)$, multiply both sides by $(z-1)$): $$(z-1)U = (3.01875z - 2.98125)E$$ $$u[k] - u[k-1] = 3.01875\,e[k] - 2.98125\,e[k-1]$$ $$u[k] = u[k-1] + 3.01875\,e[k] - 2.98125\,e[k-1]$$ This is the velocity form of the PI controller. Only one delay of stored state ($u[k-1]$ and $e[k-1]$) is needed.
Food for thought
Reflection questions
  1. The ZOH introduces a phase lag of approximately $T_s/2$ per radian. For a system with phase margin $45°$, what is the maximum sampling period (as a fraction of the crossover frequency period) that keeps the system stable with at least $10°$ phase margin to spare?
    The ZOH phase lag is $\omega \cdot T_s/2$ radians at frequency $\omega$. At the gain crossover frequency $\omega_c$, this equals $\omega_c T_s/2$ radians. To keep $10°$ of margin: $45° - \omega_c T_s/2 \cdot (180°/\pi) \geq 10°$, so $\omega_c T_s/2 \leq 35° \cdot \pi/180° \approx 0.61$ rad. Thus $T_s \leq 1.22/\omega_c$. Since the crossover period is $2\pi/\omega_c$, the maximum $T_s \approx 0.19 \times 2\pi/\omega_c$ — about 19% of the crossover period. This is why the rule "sample at least 5–10 times the bandwidth" exists.
  2. Why does Tustin (bilinear) discretisation preserve the stability boundary (left half-plane ↔ unit circle), while forward Euler ($s \to (z-1)/T_s$) does not guarantee this?
    Tustin maps $s = j\omega$ (imaginary axis) to $z = (1 + j\omega T_s/2)/(1 - j\omega T_s/2)$, which has $|z| = 1$ for all $\omega$ — the imaginary axis maps exactly to the unit circle. This means the left half-plane maps to the unit disk. Forward Euler maps $s = -\sigma$ (stable, $\sigma > 0$) to $z = 1 - \sigma T_s$, which is inside the unit circle only if $\sigma T_s < 2$ — for fast poles or large $T_s$, a stable continuous-time system can produce an unstable discrete-time system with forward Euler. Tustin is always stability-preserving; forward Euler is not.
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Sampling too slowly and ignoring the ZOH phase lag. The rule of thumb is $f_s \geq 10 f_{bw}$. Slower sampling increases ZOH phase lag, reducing phase margin — a continuous-time design with $45°$ phase margin can become unstable when implemented with $f_s = 3 f_{bw}$.
  • Skipping the anti-aliasing filter. Without a low-pass filter before the ADC, high-frequency noise above $f_s/2$ aliases into the control bandwidth and looks like plant disturbances. The anti-aliasing filter should roll off by at least $40$ dB before $f_s/2$.
  • Using forward Euler for controller discretisation. Forward Euler is simple but does not preserve the stability boundary. Use Tustin (bilinear) for controllers, and ZOH for plant models.
  • Forgetting integrator windup in digital PID. The discrete integrator accumulates without bound if the actuator saturates. Always implement anti-windup (e.g. conditional integration — freeze the integrator when the actuator is saturated and the error has the same sign as the integrator state).
