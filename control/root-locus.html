5 · Root Locus
Closed-Loop Poles · Evans' Rules · Gain Design · Stability Boundary
Notation used in this lesson
$L(s)$Open-loop transfer function $C(s)P(s)$; factored as $K\,G(s)$ where $G$ is monic
$p_i,\,z_i$Open-loop poles and zeros (roots of $L$ denominator and numerator)
$n,\,m$Number of open-loop poles and zeros ($n \ge m$ for proper $G$)
$\sigma_A,\,\phi_A$Asymptote centroid (real axis intercept) and asymptote angles
$s_d$Desired closed-loop pole location (design specification)
$K_{cr}$Critical gain where root locus crosses the imaginary axis (same as Routh's $K_{cr}$)
In Lesson 4, Routh-Hurwitz answered a binary question: for a given $K$, is the system stable? Root locus asks a richer question: as $K$ varies continuously from $0$ to $\infty$, how do the closed-loop poles move through the complex plane? The root locus is the set of all possible closed-loop pole locations, parameterized by $K$. It is a picture — drawn once, for a given plant-controller structure — that tells you immediately whether you can achieve any desired pole location, and what gain is required to put the poles there.
What does the gain do to the poles?
A unity-feedback system with open-loop $L(s) = K\,G(s)$ has the characteristic equation $$1 + K\,G(s) = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad K\,G(s) = -1$$ At $K = 0$: $G(s) = \infty$ at the open-loop poles → the closed-loop poles start at the open-loop poles. As $K \to \infty$: $G(s) = 0$ at the open-loop zeros → the closed-loop poles end at the open-loop zeros (or go to infinity along asymptotes if there are more poles than zeros).
So the root locus connects the open-loop poles ($K=0$) to the open-loop zeros ($K\to\infty$), through paths determined entirely by the pole-zero structure of $G(s)$. Evans (1948) discovered a set of geometric rules for drawing these paths without solving the characteristic equation — a remarkable result that made the root locus a practical tool even before computers.
The angle condition
The characteristic equation $K\,G(s) = -1$ has both a magnitude and a phase condition. Write $G(s)$ in terms of its poles and zeros: $$G(s) = \frac{(s-z_1)(s-z_2)\cdots(s-z_m)}{(s-p_1)(s-p_2)\cdots(s-p_n)}$$ For $s = s^*$ to be on the root locus, $K\,G(s^*) = -1$, so:
$$\underbrace{|K\,G(s^*)| = 1}_{\text{magnitude condition}} \qquad\text{and}\qquad \underbrace{\angle G(s^*) = (2k+1)\cdot 180°,\; k\in\mathbb{Z}}_{\text{angle condition}}$$
The angle condition is the key: a point $s^*$ is on the root locus if and only if the sum of angles from all zeros to $s^*$, minus the sum of angles from all poles to $s^*$, equals an odd multiple of $180°$. This is a purely geometric condition that determines which points are on the locus. The magnitude condition then tells you the value of $K$ required for $s^*$ to be the closed-loop pole.
How to use this in design: Suppose you want a closed-loop pole at $s_d = -2 + 2j$ (chosen for a desired settling time and damping ratio). Check whether $s_d$ lies on the root locus using the angle condition. If it does, compute $K$ from the magnitude condition: $K = 1/|G(s_d)|$. This is the design gain. If $s_d$ is not on the locus, no value of $K$ can place a pole exactly there — you need to modify the controller structure (e.g., add a lead compensator, Lesson 8).
Evans' construction rules
The following rules let you sketch the root locus quickly by hand, without computing any roots. Apply them in order — each one fills in more of the picture.
Rule 1 · Number of branches
The locus has $n$ branches (one for each closed-loop pole). The degree $n$ equals the number of open-loop poles.
Rule 2 · Start points ($K = 0$)
The $n$ branches start at the $n$ open-loop poles $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n$.
Rule 3 · End points ($K \to \infty$)
$m$ branches end at the $m$ finite open-loop zeros $z_1, \ldots, z_m$. The remaining $n - m$ branches go to infinity along asymptotes.
Rule 4 · Real-axis segments
A point on the real axis is on the locus if and only if the total number of real poles and real zeros to its right is odd. (The imaginary parts cancel by conjugate symmetry, so only real poles/zeros count.)
Rule 5 · Asymptotes
The $n-m$ asymptotes all originate from the centroid: $$\sigma_A = \frac{\sum p_i - \sum z_i}{n - m}$$ The asymptote angles are: $$\phi_A = \frac{(2k+1)\cdot 180°}{n-m}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-m-1$$ For example, if $n-m=2$: angles are $90°$ and $270°$ (straight up/down). If $n-m=3$: $60°$, $180°$, $300°$.
Rule 6 · Breakaway and break-in points
Where two branches meet on the real axis and then leave (or converge from complex plane to real axis), the gain $K$ has a local extremum. Find these by solving $dK/ds = 0$ where $K = -1/G(s)$ (evaluated on the real axis where the locus is real). Equivalently, solve $\frac{d}{ds}\ln|G(s)| = 0$, or: $$\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{s - p_i} = \sum_{j=1}^m \frac{1}{s - z_j}$$
Rule 7 · Angles of departure (from complex poles)
The angle at which a branch leaves a complex pole $p_k$ is: $$\phi_{\text{dep}} = 180° + \angle G(s)\big|_{s \to p_k \text{ from existing terms}}$$ Specifically: sum all angles from other poles to $p_k$, subtract sum of angles from all zeros to $p_k$, add to $180°$. This pins the departure direction precisely.
Rule 8 · Imaginary-axis crossing
Where the locus crosses the imaginary axis marks the boundary between stable and unstable closed-loop systems. Find this by the Routh criterion (Lesson 4): set the $s^1$ row element to zero to find $K_{cr}$, then use the auxiliary polynomial to find the crossing frequency $\omega_{cr}$.
Reading the locus for design
Once you have the locus, you can extract design information directly:
  • Stability range: The gain $K$ is stable for all values where all closed-loop poles are in the left half-plane. This is the range $0 < K < K_{cr}$ where $K_{cr}$ is the imaginary-axis crossing gain.
  • Overshoot specification: A second-order system with damping ratio $\zeta$ has poles at angle $\cos^{-1}\zeta$ from the negative real axis. Desired overshoot → desired $\zeta$ → desired pole angle → draw that ray from the origin and find where the locus intersects it → that's the design gain $K$.
  • Settling time specification: Settling time $t_s \approx 4/\sigma$ where $\sigma$ is the real part of the dominant poles. Draw a vertical line at $\sigma = -4/t_{s,\text{desired}}$ and find where the locus intersects it.
  • Sensitivity to gain variation: A locus that stays nearly vertical near the design point is robust to gain variations — the pole location doesn't change much as $K$ changes. A locus that curves strongly toward the imaginary axis is gain-sensitive — small increases in $K$ move the pole rapidly toward instability.
Worked examples
Example 1: Two poles at the origin (double integrator). $G(s) = 1/s^2$. Poles: $s=0$ (multiplicity 2). Zeros: none. $n=2$, $m=0$.
  • Branches: 2, both start at $s=0$.
  • Asymptotes: $n-m=2$. Centroid: $\sigma_A = (0+0)/2 = 0$. Angles: $90°$ and $270°$.
  • Real axis: to the left of $s=0$ (one pole to the right is odd?). The count at any real $\sigma < 0$ is 2 poles to the right — even — so no real-axis segments.
  • Locus: two branches leave the double pole at $s=0$ and go straight up and down along the imaginary axis. The system is marginally stable for all $K>0$ — the poles are always on the imaginary axis. A plain proportional controller can never stabilize a double integrator. (This is why double-integrator plants require derivative action.)
Example 2: Three-pole system (the running example from Lesson 4). $G(s) = 1/[s(s+1)(s+3)]$. Poles: $0, -1, -3$. No zeros. $n=3$, $m=0$.
  • Asymptotes: $n-m=3$. Centroid: $\sigma_A = (0-1-3)/3 = -4/3 \approx -1.33$. Angles: $60°$, $180°$, $300°$.
  • Real axis: On segment $(-1, 0)$: 1 pole to the right ($s=0$) — odd → on the locus. On $(-\infty, -3)$: 3 poles to the right — odd → on the locus. On $(-3, -1)$: 2 poles to the right — even → not on the locus.
  • Breakaway: On the real-axis segment $(-1,0)$ between poles 0 and $-1$, two branches collide and leave as a conjugate pair. The breakaway point satisfies $1/s + 1/(s+1) + 1/(s+3) = 0$, giving a cubic — numerically, $s \approx -0.42$.
  • Imaginary-axis crossing: From Lesson 4: $K_{cr} = 12$ at $\omega_{cr} = \sqrt{3}$. At $K=12$, the conjugate branch pair crosses the imaginary axis at $\pm j\sqrt{3}$.
  • Stability: System stable for $0 < K < 12$.
Root locus for $G(s) = 1/[s(s+1)(s+3)]$. Green = locus; orange dashed = asymptotes; red circles = $j\omega$ crossing at $K_{cr}=12$.
Example 3: Adding a zero (lead compensation preview). Add a zero at $z = -2$ to the previous plant: $G(s) = (s+2)/[s(s+1)(s+3)]$. The zero "attracts" nearby locus branches.
  • $n=3$, $m=1$. Asymptotes: $n-m=2$, centroid $\sigma_A = (0-1-3-(-2))/2 = -1$, angles $90°$ and $270°$.
  • Real axis: segment $(-1,0)$: 1 pole, 0 zeros to the right (odd) → on locus. Segment $(-2,-1)$: 2 poles, 0 zeros to the right (even) → not on locus. Wait: zero at $-2$ counts too. Segment $(-3,-2)$: 2 poles, 1 zero = 3 OA objects → odd → on locus. Segment $(-\infty,-3)$: 3 poles + 1 zero = 4 (even) → not on locus.
  • The asymptotes now go straight up and down from $\sigma_A=-1$, and the locus stays further to the left. Adding the zero improves stability — a smaller portion of the locus lies in the RHP for any practical gain.

  2. The root locus shows only the paths for $K > 0$ (positive gain). What would you sketch for $K < 0$ (negative gain, or positive feedback)? The angle condition becomes $\angle G(s^*) = 0°$ instead of $180°$ — how does this change the real-axis rule and the asymptote angles?
  3. Rule 4 says a real-axis point is on the locus if there are an odd number of real poles+zeros to its right. Prove this from the angle condition: for a real test point, all complex poles and zeros come in conjugate pairs whose angle contributions cancel in pairs. The only surviving angle contributions are from the real poles and zeros.
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Counting poles and zeros of the wrong thing. The root locus is drawn using the open-loop poles and zeros of $L(s)=C(s)P(s)$. The branches end at $K\to\infty$ at the open-loop zeros — not the open-loop poles. Confusing which are poles and which are zeros — especially when $C(s)$ has both — leads to a completely wrong locus.
  • Applying Rule 4 using complex poles. Complex poles and zeros come in conjugate pairs. Their angle contributions to any real-axis test point cancel exactly (the angles from $p = -a + jb$ and $p = -a - jb$ to any real point sum to $0°$ or $360°$). Only real poles and zeros affect the real-axis rule. Counting complex poles leads to the wrong real-axis segments.
  • Treating the locus as continuous in all directions. Locus branches can intersect on the real axis (at breakaway/break-in points), but complex branches do not generically cross each other. Two branches approaching from complex-plane directions repel each other — they don't pass through the same point. (Mathematically, two distinct eigenvalue trajectories of a matrix can't cross in general position.) If your sketch has two complex branches crossing, you've likely made an error.
  • Designing at a point that "looks like" it's on the locus. The sketching rules give you approximate locus paths. Before committing to a design point $s_d$, always verify it using the angle condition: compute $\angle G(s_d)$ and check that it equals $(2k+1)\cdot180°$ to within a degree or two. If it's off, $s_d$ is not actually on the locus and no gain places the pole exactly there.
Connects to
  • Lesson 4 (Routh-Hurwitz) — Routh gives the stability boundary ($K_{cr}$) as an algebraic result; root locus makes the same boundary visible geometrically as the imaginary-axis crossing. They agree exactly. Use Routh when you need the precise gain value; use root locus when you need to understand the full picture of pole movement.
  • Lesson 8 (Lead-Lag Compensators) — When the root locus of a plain proportional controller doesn't pass through your desired pole location, a lead compensator adds a zero-pole pair that bends the locus toward the desired region. The design procedure is entirely graphical: place the compensator zero and pole to satisfy the angle condition at $s_d$.
  • Lesson 12 (State Feedback & Pole Placement) — State feedback $u = -Kx$ assigns all $n$ closed-loop eigenvalues simultaneously, not just a dominant pair. Root locus is a single-input single-output gain parameter method; state feedback is the multi-dimensional generalization. Ackermann's formula from Lesson 12 gives the gain vector to place all poles at any specified locations.
