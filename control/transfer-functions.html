Every physical system around you — a motor, an RC circuit, a heated room — is governed by differential equations. But differential equations are cumbersome to manipulate: cascading two systems means convolving two impulse responses, a painful operation in time. The Laplace transform converts derivatives into multiplication by $s$, turning the ODE into algebra and the cascade into a product. The result — the transfer function — is the single most important object in classical control theory. Everything in this cluster, from Bode plots to root locus to PID design, is manipulation of transfer functions.
From differential equations to transfer functions
Consider a simple first-order linear system modeled by the ODE
$$\tau \dot{y}(t) + y(t) = K\, u(t)$$
where $y$ is the output, $u$ is the input, $\tau > 0$ is the time constant, and $K$ is the DC gain. Taking the Laplace transform of both sides (with zero initial conditions):
$$\tau s\, Y(s) + Y(s) = K\, U(s) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad (\tau s + 1)\,Y(s) = K\,U(s)$$
Solving for $Y(s)/U(s)$ gives the transfer function:
$$\boxed{G(s) = \frac{Y(s)}{U(s)} = \frac{K}{\tau s + 1}}$$
This is defined only for zero initial conditions — the transfer function captures how the system responds to inputs, not to initial conditions. In general, a linear time-invariant (LTI) system with input $u$ and output $y$ satisfying an $n$th-order ODE has a rational transfer function:
$$G(s) = \frac{b_m s^m + b_{m-1}s^{m-1} + \cdots + b_0} {s^n + a_{n-1}s^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0} = \frac{N(s)}{D(s)}$$
The degree $n$ of the denominator equals the order of the ODE. For a physically realizable system (the output can't respond before the input), we require $m \le n$ (the transfer function is proper).
Why zero initial conditions? The transfer function is defined for the forced response only. If initial conditions are nonzero, the full response is $Y(s) = G(s)U(s) + (\text{terms from ICs})$. We keep the two parts separate: $G(s)U(s)$ handles inputs; the IC terms are handled by partial fractions and the homogeneous solution. Control design only concerns the forced response, so the transfer function is what matters.
Useful Laplace pairs (you need these constantly):

Poles, zeros, and what they tell you
Factor both numerator and denominator of $G(s) = N(s)/D(s)$:
$$G(s) = K_0 \frac{(s - z_1)(s - z_2)\cdots(s - z_m)} {(s - p_1)(s - p_2)\cdots(s - p_n)}$$
  • Poles $p_i$ are the roots of $D(s) = 0$. They are the values of $s$ at which $|G(s)| \to \infty$. Poles determine the natural modes of the system — what the system does with no input after a perturbation.
  • Zeros $z_i$ are the roots of $N(s) = 0$. They are the values of $s$ at which $G(s) = 0$ exactly — the system blocks sinusoidal inputs at those frequencies.
  • $K_0$ is the gain (or zero-frequency scaling factor). Not the same as the DC gain unless you evaluate at $s=0$.
The pole locations in the complex $s$-plane tell you everything about stability and transient behavior:
× = pole location. Left half-plane poles → decaying modes (stable). Imaginary axis → sustained oscillation. Right half-plane → growing (unstable).
Reading poles:
  • Real pole at $s = -a$ ($a > 0$): contributes a decaying exponential mode $e^{-at}$ to the time response. Faster decay as $|a|$ increases. The time constant is $\tau = 1/a$.
  • Complex conjugate poles at $s = -\sigma \pm j\omega_d$ ($\sigma > 0$): contributes an oscillatory decaying mode $e^{-\sigma t}\sin(\omega_d t)$. The oscillation frequency is $\omega_d$; the decay rate is $\sigma$.
  • Poles on the imaginary axis ($\sigma = 0$): marginally stable — sustained oscillation, neither decaying nor growing. Any perturbation oscillates forever.
  • Poles in the right half-plane ($\sigma < 0$, i.e., positive real part): unstable — the corresponding mode grows exponentially. Even a tiny perturbation diverges.
Dominant poles. In a system with multiple poles, the dominant poles are the ones closest to the imaginary axis — they decay the slowest and therefore dominate the long-term transient behavior. Poles far to the left decay so quickly they barely affect the response. This is why a 10th-order system often "behaves like" a second-order system: the 8 fast poles die out in the first few milliseconds and the two dominant poles govern the rest.
First-order systems
The prototypical first-order system is:
$$G(s) = \frac{K}{\tau s + 1} = \frac{K/\tau}{s + 1/\tau}$$
One pole at $s = -1/\tau$ (stable for $\tau > 0$); no finite zeros. To find the step response, apply a unit step input $U(s) = 1/s$:
$$Y(s) = G(s)\cdot\frac{1}{s} = \frac{K}{\tau s + 1}\cdot\frac{1}{s} = \frac{K}{s} - \frac{K\tau}{(\tau s + 1)} = \frac{K}{s} - \frac{K}{s + 1/\tau}$$
Inverse Laplace transform (using the table above):
$$\boxed{y(t) = K\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \quad t \ge 0}$$
This is the first-order step response. It starts at 0, rises toward the final value $K$, and never overshoots. Key time-domain characteristics:
Characteristic Value Meaning
Time constant $\tau$ $y(\tau) = K(1-e^{-1}) \approx 0.632\,K$ Reaches 63.2% of final value
Rise time $t_r$ $\approx 2.2\tau$ (10%→90%) How fast the output rises
Settling time $t_s$ $\approx 4\tau$ (within 2%) When the response is essentially done
DC gain $K$ $G(0) = K$ Final value for unit step
Overshoot 0% First-order systems never overshoot
The initial slope is $\dot{y}(0^+) = K/\tau$. A tangent line from the origin with this slope reaches the final value exactly at $t = \tau$ — a useful graphical check for reading $\tau$ off an experimental step response.
Physical examples of first-order systems:
  • RC circuit: $\tau = RC$, $K = 1$. Output voltage $V_C(s)/V_{in}(s) = 1/(RCs+1)$.
  • Thermal system (room + heater): $\tau = $ thermal capacitance / thermal conductance, $K = $ steady-state temperature rise per unit power input.
  • DC motor (torque-to-speed, electrical lag neglected): $\omega(s)/V(s) = K/(Js + b)$ where $J$ is moment of inertia and $b$ is viscous friction — same form with $\tau = J/b$, $K_{\text{dc}} = K/b$.
System type and steady-state error
Closed-loop performance depends critically on how many integrators are in the open-loop transfer function $L(s) = C(s)P(s)$. Write:
$$L(s) = \frac{K_{\text{loop}}\, (s+z_1)(s+z_2)\cdots}{s^N (s+p_1)(s+p_2)\cdots}$$
The integer $N$ is the system type — the number of poles at the origin. Each integrator multiplies the loop transfer function by $1/s$, which is the Laplace transform of a unit ramp. More integrators means better steady-state tracking for polynomial inputs, at the cost of reduced phase margin (integrators contribute $-90°$ of phase each).
System Type $N$ Step error $e_{ss}$ Ramp error $e_{ss}$ Example plant
Type 0 $\dfrac{1}{1+K_p}$ $\infty$ Thermal system, RC circuit
Type 1 $0$ $\dfrac{1}{K_v}$ Position servo, integrating plant
Type 2 $0$ $0$ Double integrator (robotic arm acceleration)
Here $K_p = \lim_{s \to 0} L(s)$ (position constant) and $K_v = \lim_{s \to 0} s\,L(s)$ (velocity constant). These are the error constants from the final-value theorem. For a unity-feedback system, the steady-state error to a step input is:
$$e_{ss} = \lim_{t\to\infty} e(t) = \lim_{s\to 0} \frac{s\,R(s)}{1 + L(s)}$$
With $R(s) = 1/s$ (unit step) and a Type 0 system, this gives $1/(1+K_p)$. With a Type 1 system, $L(s) \to \infty$ as $s \to 0$, so $e_{ss} = 0$.
Adding an integrator reduces steady-state error — for free? Not quite. Adding a pole at $s=0$ to the controller $C(s)$ shifts $90°$ of phase lag into the loop at low frequencies. Unless compensated, this eats into the phase margin and can destabilize the system. PID controllers (Lesson 7) include an integrator (the "I" action) precisely to eliminate steady-state error, but the integral gain must be tuned carefully to preserve stability.
Worked examples
Example 1 — RC circuit: first-order system
A series RC circuit with $R = 1\,\text{k}\Omega$, $C = 100\,\mu\text{F}$. The voltage across $C$ satisfies $RC\dot{V}_C + V_C = V_\text{in}$. Find the transfer function, time constant, and step response.
Taking the Laplace transform: $(RCs + 1)V_C(s) = V_\text{in}(s)$. $$G(s) = \frac{V_C(s)}{V_\text{in}(s)} = \frac{1}{RCs + 1} = \frac{1}{0.1s + 1}$$
  • Time constant: $\tau = RC = 0.1$ s.
  • Pole at $s = -1/\tau = -10$.
  • DC gain: $G(0) = 1$ (capacitor fully charges to input).
  • Step response: $V_C(t) = 1 - e^{-10t}$. Reaches 63.2% at $t = 0.1$ s, settled (2%) at $t \approx 0.4$ s.
Example 2 — DC motor: Type 1 plant
A DC motor has velocity transfer function $\omega(s)/V(s) = K_m/(Js+b)$. Derive the transfer function for position control (input = voltage, output = angle).
Position is the integral of velocity: $\theta = \int \omega\,dt \Rightarrow \theta(s) = \omega(s)/s$. $$P(s) = \frac{\theta(s)}{V(s)} = \frac{K_m}{s(Js + b)} = \frac{K_m/b}{s\!\left(\dfrac{J}{b}s + 1\right)}$$
  • Pole at $s=0$: the integrator from velocity to position. Type 1 plant.
  • Pole at $s = -b/J$: mechanical time constant $\tau_m = J/b$.
  • A proportional controller gives zero SSE to a step position reference — the plant integrator ensures this without needing an integral controller term.
Example 3 — Reading poles, zeros, and DC gain
For $G(s) = \dfrac{5(s+2)}{(s+1)(s+4)}$, identify poles, zeros, DC gain, and dominant behavior.
  • Zero: $s = -2$.
  • Poles: $s = -1$ and $s = -4$. Both in the LHP → stable.
  • DC gain: $G(0) = 5 \cdot 2 / (1 \cdot 4) = 2.5$.
  • Dominant pole: $s = -1$ (closest to the imaginary axis, slowest mode). The step response is primarily governed by $e^{-t}$ with time constant $\tau = 1$ s. The $e^{-4t}$ mode settles in $\approx 1$ s and barely affects the overall response.

Food for thought
Reflection questions
  1. The DC gain is $G(0) = K$. What does $G(j\omega)$ represent physically? If you drive the system with a sinusoidal input $u(t) = A\sin(\omega t)$, what is the steady-state output? (Hint: the magnitude is $A|G(j\omega)|$ and the phase shift is $\angle G(j\omega)$.) What happens to $|G(j\omega)|$ as $\omega \to \infty$ for the first-order system?
    Answer
    $G(j\omega)$ is the frequency response — it describes how the system responds to sinusoidal inputs at frequency $\omega$. For a stable LTI system driven by $u(t) = A\sin(\omega t)$, the steady-state output is: $$y_{ss}(t) = A|G(j\omega)|\sin(\omega t + \angle G(j\omega))$$ The magnitude $|G(j\omega)|$ scales the amplitude; the phase $\angle G(j\omega)$ shifts the sinusoid in time. For the first-order system $G(s) = K/(\tau s + 1)$: $|G(j\omega)| = K/\sqrt{(\tau\omega)^2+1} \to 0$ as $\omega \to \infty$. The system attenuates high-frequency inputs — it is a low-pass filter. At $\omega = 1/\tau$ (the corner frequency), the output is $K/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707K$ (−3 dB), and the phase is −45°.
  2. Two first-order systems in series: $G_1(s) = 1/(\tau_1 s + 1)$ and $G_2(s) = 1/(\tau_2 s + 1)$. The combined transfer function is $G_1 G_2 = 1/((\tau_1 s+1)(\tau_2 s+1))$. Is this still "first-order"? How does its step response differ from a single first-order system with $\tau = \tau_1 + \tau_2$? Does it still have no overshoot?
    Answer
    No, $G_1 G_2$ is second-order (denominator degree 2), not first-order. It has two real poles at $-1/\tau_1$ and $-1/\tau_2$. The step response is $y(t) = 1 - \dfrac{\tau_1 e^{-t/\tau_1} - \tau_2 e^{-t/\tau_2}}{\tau_1 - \tau_2}$ (for $\tau_1 \neq \tau_2$). This has an S-shape: it starts with zero slope (unlike a single first-order system which starts with maximum slope), then rises, then levels off. No, there is no overshoot — both poles are real and negative, giving a monotone non-oscillatory step response. However, the rise and settling times are longer than a single first-order system with time constant $\tau_1 + \tau_2$: the two-stage system is slower, and the initial slope is zero, not $K/\tau$.
  3. The final value theorem says $\lim_{t\to\infty} y(t) = \lim_{s\to 0} s\,Y(s)$ (when the limit exists). Use this to derive the steady-state error formula $e_{ss} = \lim_{s\to 0} s \cdot E(s)$ for the closed-loop system, where $E(s) = R(s)/(1+L(s))$. When does the final-value theorem fail to give the correct answer?
    Answer
    For a unit step input $r(t) = 1(t)$, $R(s) = 1/s$. The error signal is $E(s) = R(s)/(1+L(s))$. Applying the FVT: $$e_{ss} = \lim_{s\to 0} s \cdot E(s) = \lim_{s\to 0} s \cdot \frac{1/s}{1+L(s)} = \frac{1}{1 + L(0)}$$ For a Type 0 loop (no integrator), $L(0) = K_p$ (position constant), so $e_{ss} = 1/(1+K_p)$. For a Type 1 loop, $L(0) \to \infty$, so $e_{ss} = 0$. When the FVT fails: The FVT requires all poles of $sY(s)$ to be in the open LHP (strictly stable). It fails when: (1) the closed-loop is unstable (poles in the RHP or on the imaginary axis — $y(t)$ doesn't converge); (2) the input $R(s)$ has poles on the imaginary axis (e.g., a ramp $1/s^2$ combined with an unstable closed-loop); (3) the system oscillates at steady state ($y(t)$ doesn't settle to a constant). In all these cases, the formula gives a number that doesn't correspond to any physical limit.
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Confusing poles of $G(s)$ with closed-loop poles. The plant transfer function $P(s)$ has poles that determine the plant's open-loop behavior. The closed-loop poles are the roots of $1+C(s)P(s)=0$, which are completely different. A plant with a pole at $s=-2$ might have a closed-loop pole at $s=-10$ (much faster) or at $s=+1$ (unstable), depending on the controller. Always be clear which system's poles you're discussing.
  • Pole-zero cancellation. If the controller has a zero at the same location as a plant pole, they appear to "cancel" in the product $C(s)P(s)$. But the cancelled pole is still present in the closed-loop system — it just becomes unobservable or uncontrollable. If that cancelled pole is unstable (positive real part), the closed-loop system contains a hidden unstable mode that can destabilize when perturbed. Never cancel an unstable pole with a controller zero.
  • The final value theorem requires stability. $\lim_{t\to\infty} y(t) = \lim_{s\to 0} sY(s)$ only holds if $y(t)$ has a finite limit — i.e., if the system is stable. If there's a pole at $s=0$ (or right of it) in $Y(s)$, the formula gives a meaningless number. Always check stability before applying the final-value theorem.
  • Treating all time constants as equal. A second-order system $(s+1)(s+100)$ has one pole at $-1$ and one at $-100$. The dominant behavior is set by the slow pole at $-1$; the fast pole at $-100$ is gone in about $0.04$ s. But you cannot simply ignore the fast pole when it is inside the feedback loop — it contributes $-90°$ of phase at the crossover frequency, which can push the phase margin negative.
