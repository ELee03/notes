Linear Quadratic Regulator
Optimal Control · Quadratic Cost · Riccati Equation · Q/R Tuning · Stability Margins
Pole placement (Lesson 12) gives complete freedom: any eigenvalues you want, anywhere. But complete freedom comes with a burden — you must choose well, and there's no principle telling you what "good" pole locations look like except intuition and experience. The Linear Quadratic Regulator resolves this by framing control as an optimisation problem: minimise a cost that quantifies both how badly the states deviate and how much control effort you expend. The answer is a state-feedback law, and the optimal gain falls out of a single matrix equation.
Notation recap
$Q$State cost matrix ($n \times n$, positive semidefinite $Q \geq 0$)
$R$Input cost matrix ($m \times m$, positive definite $R > 0$)
$P$Solution to the algebraic Riccati equation ($n \times n$, positive definite)
$K^*$Optimal feedback gain $R^{-1}B^TP$
$J$Total cost (infinite-horizon quadratic integral)
The Cost Function
Start with the linear system $\dot{x} = Ax + Bu$ and ask: what control signal $u(t)$ minimises the following integral from now until infinity?
$$J = \int_0^\infty \bigl(x(t)^T Q\, x(t) + u(t)^T R\, u(t)\bigr)\,dt$$
Each term has a physical meaning:
  • $x^TQx$ — the state cost. When $Q$ is diagonal, $Q = \text{diag}(q_1,\ldots,q_n)$, this equals $\sum_i q_i x_i^2$. Each $q_i$ weights how expensive it is for state component $x_i$ to deviate from zero. Larger $q_i$ means "keep this state small."
  • $u^TRu$ — the input cost. When $R = r\cdot I$, this is $r\|u\|^2$. Large $r$ means "actuator effort is expensive." Small $r$ means "effort is cheap, use as much as you need."
The ratio $Q/R$ is what matters — not their absolute values. Doubling both $Q$ and $R$ leaves the optimal gain unchanged; only the relative weighting between state penalty and control effort determines the design.
The cost $J$ must be finite for the problem to be meaningful. A sufficient condition is that $u = K^*x$ makes the closed-loop stable (all eigenvalues of $A - BK^*$ in the left half-plane, so $x \to 0$ and the integral converges).
The Optimal Control Law
The remarkable result of LQR theory is that the minimising control is linear state feedback — the same structure as Lesson 12, but now the gain is determined by solving an optimisation rather than by picking desired poles:
$$u^*(t) = -K^* x(t), \qquad K^* = R^{-1}B^T P$$
where $P$ is the unique symmetric positive-definite solution to the algebraic Riccati equation (ARE):
$$\boxed{A^T P + PA - PBR^{-1}B^T P + Q = 0}$$
The closed-loop system under this control is $\dot{x} = (A - BK^*)x$, and the minimum achievable cost is $J^* = x(0)^T P\, x(0)$ — it depends only on the initial state and the Riccati solution $P$.
The Algebraic Riccati Equation
The ARE is a nonlinear matrix equation in the unknown $P$. Despite being nonlinear (the $PBR^{-1}B^TP$ term is quadratic in $P$), it has a unique positive-definite solution under mild conditions:
Existence and uniqueness. If $(A, B)$ is stabilisable and $(A, C_Q)$ is detectable (where $Q = C_Q^T C_Q$), then the ARE has a unique positive-semidefinite solution $P \geq 0$, and the closed-loop $A - BK^*$ is asymptotically stable.
For fully controllable and fully observable systems (the common case), the conditions are satisfied and $P > 0$ is strictly positive definite.
How to solve it. In practice you never solve the ARE by hand — use a software routine: 
% MATLAB
[K, P, e] = lqr(A, B, Q, R)    % K = gain, P = Riccati solution, e = closed-loop eigenvalues

# Python (scipy)
from scipy.linalg import solve_continuous_are
P = solve_continuous_are(A, B, Q, R)
K = np.linalg.solve(R, B.T @ P)  # K = R^{-1} B^T P
Internally these routines use the Hamiltonian matrix approach: the $2n \times 2n$ Hamiltonian $$\mathcal{H} = \begin{bmatrix}A & -BR^{-1}B^T \\ -Q & -A^T\end{bmatrix}$$ has $n$ eigenvalues in the left half-plane and $n$ in the right half-plane (when the stabilisability/detectability conditions hold). The solution $P$ is constructed from the stable eigenspace of $\mathcal{H}$.
Derivation — Completing the Square
The cleanest path to the ARE and the optimal gain uses a matrix analogue of completing the square. It's worth following through — the argument is short and reveals exactly why the Riccati equation is the right equation to solve.
⟶ Derivation (complete the square)
Step 1. Suppose there exists a symmetric $P > 0$ such that for all $x$ and $u$: $$x^TQx + u^TRu + \frac{d}{dt}(x^TPx) = (u + R^{-1}B^TPx)^T R\,(u + R^{-1}B^TPx) + x^T\underbrace{(A^TP + PA - PBR^{-1}B^TP + Q)}_{\text{if = 0, this vanishes}}x$$ This identity follows from expanding $\frac{d}{dt}(x^TPx) = \dot{x}^TPx + x^TP\dot{x} = (Ax+Bu)^TPx + x^TP(Ax+Bu)$.

Step 2. Assume $P$ satisfies the ARE, so the last term vanishes: $$x^TQx + u^TRu + \frac{d}{dt}(x^TPx) = (u + R^{-1}B^TPx)^T R\,(u + R^{-1}B^TPx) \geq 0$$
Step 3. Integrate from $0$ to $\infty$ (assuming $x(\infty) = 0$): $$J = \int_0^\infty (x^TQx + u^TRu)\,dt = x(0)^TPx(0) + \int_0^\infty (u + K^*x)^TR(u + K^*x)\,dt$$ where $K^* = R^{-1}B^TP$.

Step 4. The integral on the right is non-negative, so $J \geq x(0)^TPx(0)$. Equality holds when $u = -K^*x$. This is the optimal control law, and the minimum cost is $J^* = x(0)^TPx(0)$.
The derivation shows two things simultaneously: the ARE tells us what $P$ must be for the argument to work, and once $P$ satisfies the ARE, the optimal gain follows immediately as $K^* = R^{-1}B^TP$.
Connection to reinforcement learning. The cost $J = x_0^T P x_0$ is the value function of the LQR problem. The ARE is the Bellman equation evaluated at steady state — exactly the continuous-time analogue of the DP value iteration from Lesson 5 of the RL cluster. LQR is, in fact, a solved RL problem with a known quadratic structure that makes the optimisation tractable.
Tuning $Q$ and $R$
The engineer's job is to choose $Q$ and $R$ to express design intent. Two frameworks help:
Bryson's rule. A pragmatic starting point: set the diagonal entries of $Q$ and $R$ to be the inverse of the square of the maximum acceptable value for each state and input:
$$Q_{ii} = \frac{1}{x_{i,\max}^2}, \qquad R_{jj} = \frac{1}{u_{j,\max}^2}$$
This normalises all states and inputs to a common scale before comparing them. If position can swing ±0.1 m but velocity can swing ±1 m/s, you might set $Q_{11} = 100$ and $Q_{22} = 1$ so both contribute equally when at their limits.
Scalar tradeoff. For many designs, a convenient starting point is $Q = I$, $R = \rho\,I$ and sweep $\rho$:
$\rho$ small ($\ll 1$)
Effort cheap — aggressive control. Large $K$, fast poles pushed far left. Fast settling, large inputs.
$\rho = 1$
Balanced. Equal weight on states and inputs. Moderate speed, moderate effort.
$\rho$ large ($\gg 1$)
Effort expensive — conservative control. Small $K$, slow poles near imaginary axis. Slow settling, small inputs.
In practice: start with Bryson's rule, simulate the step response, and adjust the ratio of $Q$ to $R$ until the settling time and peak control signal are both within acceptable limits. There's always a tradeoff — faster settling requires more actuator effort.
Guaranteed Stability Margins
One of the most remarkable properties of LQR is that it provides guaranteed stability margins — something arbitrary pole placement cannot offer.
LQR stability margin theorem (single input). For a single-input system with optimal gain $K^*$, the closed-loop system remains stable for any loop gain $\alpha \in [0.5,\,\infty)$: that is, replacing $u = -K^*x$ with $u = -\alpha K^* x$ is stable for all $\alpha \geq 0.5$. In terms of classical margins:
  • Gain margin: $[−6\,\text{dB},\,+\infty\,\text{dB}]$ (a factor of 0.5 down to $\infty$ up)
  • Phase margin: $\geq 60°$
This is proven using the return-difference inequality. The key identity is: $$\|I + K^*(j\omega I - A)^{-1}B\|_2 \geq 1 \quad \forall\,\omega$$ which means the Nyquist plot of $K^*(j\omega I - A)^{-1}B$ never enters a unit circle centred at $-1 + 0j$, guaranteeing $\geq 60°$ phase margin and $\geq 6\,\text{dB}$ gain margin for gain reduction.
Important caveat. These guarantees apply to the continuous-time LQR loop gain in the original, ideal model. They vanish when:
  • The system is discretised (digital implementation introduces phase lag)
  • An observer is added (the separation principle preserves stability but not margins)
  • The plant has unmodelled dynamics (model uncertainty)
In practice, LQR-designed gains are often robust, but the theoretical margin guarantee should not be taken as a substitute for robustness analysis under the actual implementation.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Double integrator, analytic LQR
Given $A = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{bmatrix}$, $B = \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}$, $Q = I$, $R = 1$. Solve the ARE analytically and find the optimal gain $K^*$ and minimum cost $J^*$ from $x_0 = [1,\,0]^T$.
Let $P = \begin{bmatrix}p & q \\ q & s\end{bmatrix}$ (symmetric). The ARE gives three equations:
  • $(1,1)$: $-q^2 + 1 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; q = 1$
  • $(1,2)$: $p - qs = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; p = s$
  • $(2,2)$: $2q - s^2 + 1 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; s^2 = 3 \;\Rightarrow\; s = \sqrt{3}$
So $P = \begin{bmatrix}\sqrt{3} & 1 \\ 1 & \sqrt{3}\end{bmatrix}$. The optimal gain: $$K^* = R^{-1}B^TP = [0,\;1]\begin{bmatrix}\sqrt{3}&1\\1&\sqrt{3}\end{bmatrix} = [1,\;\sqrt{3}]$$ Closed-loop: $A - BK^* = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-1&-\sqrt{3}\end{bmatrix}$, eigenvalues $s = -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \pm \tfrac{1}{2}j$ (underdamped, $\zeta = \sqrt{3}/2 \approx 0.866$, $\omega_n = 1$). Minimum cost: $J^* = x_0^T P x_0 = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.73$.
Example 2 — First-order motor, closed-form solution
A motor velocity system: $\dot{x} = -5x + 10u$. With scalar weights $Q = q$, $R = r$, find the optimal gain $K^*$ and evaluate for $q = r = 1$.
The ARE reduces to a scalar quadratic (since $n=1$, $P = p$): $$A^T p + pA - pBR^{-1}B^T p + Q = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; -10p - \frac{100p^2}{r} + q = 0$$ Rearranging: $\frac{100}{r}p^2 + 10p - q = 0$. Taking the positive root: $$p = \frac{r}{200}\!\left(-10 + \sqrt{100 + \frac{400q}{r}}\right)$$ Optimal gain: $K^* = R^{-1}B^T p = 10p/r$. For $q = r = 1$: $p = \tfrac{1}{200}(-10 + \sqrt{500}) \approx 0.062$, so $K^* \approx 0.62$. Closed-loop pole: $-5 - 10(0.62) = -11.2$, well to the left of the open-loop pole $-5$.
Example 3 — How LQR poles move as $\rho = R/Q$ varies
For the double integrator with $Q = I$, $R = \rho$, use the analytic solution $k_1 = 1/\!\sqrt{\rho}$, $k_2 = \sqrt{(1+2\sqrt{\rho})/\rho}$ to describe how the optimal closed-loop poles behave as $\rho \to 0$ (cheap effort) and $\rho \to \infty$ (expensive effort).
The closed-loop poles are at $s = -k_2/2 \pm j\sqrt{k_1 - k_2^2/4}$.

$\rho \to 0$ (aggressive): $k_1 \approx \rho^{-1/2} \to \infty$, $k_2 \approx \rho^{-1/4}\sqrt{2} \to \infty$. Poles move far into the left half-plane at $|s| \sim \rho^{-1/4}$ — fast settling, large $K$, large actuator effort.

$\rho \to \infty$ (conservative): $k_1 \to 0$, $k_2 \to 0$. Poles approach the imaginary axis but remain in the left half-plane for all finite $\rho > 0$ — the optimal solution is always stable. (At $\rho = \infty$, effort is infinitely expensive, so $u = 0$ and the "poles" are those of the open-loop system at $s = 0$.)

Key insight: LQR poles never cross into the right half-plane no matter how $\rho$ is tuned — stability is built into the formulation.
Food for Thought
  • The minimum cost is $J^* = x_0^T P x_0$. What does it mean that the cost depends on the initial condition? If the system starts near the origin, is the cost always low regardless of the gain?
    Answer
    $J^* = x_0^T P x_0$ is a quadratic form — it measures the "size" of the initial condition in the metric defined by $P$. Starting near the origin means $\|x_0\|$ is small, so $J^*$ is small regardless of $P$'s magnitude. However, this is only the optimal cost under $K^*$. A suboptimal or bad gain could produce a much larger cost from the same initial condition — the value $x_0^T P x_0$ is a lower bound, achieved only by the LQR controller. So yes, near the origin the cost is always small under the LQR gain, but that's also when control is easiest.
  • LQR theory says: if $Q = 0$ (no state penalty), then $u = 0$ is "optimal" in some trivial sense. What does this tell you about how to set $Q$? Can you always set $Q = 0$ and still get a meaningful design?
    Answer
    With $Q = 0$, the cost is purely $\int u^T R u\,dt$ — the optimizer minimises effort with no regard for where $x$ ends up, so $u = 0$ trivially wins. The system is "optimally controlled" by doing nothing, which is useless for regulation. You cannot set $Q = 0$ for states you actually need to regulate. At minimum, $Q$ must be positive on the subspace of states you care about. The requirement is that $(A, C_Q)$ is detectable where $Q = C_Q^T C_Q$ — if any unobservable mode of $(A, C_Q)$ is unstable, the ARE has no stabilising solution.
  • The stability margin result says LQR tolerates a gain reduction to $0.5K^*$. When an observer is added (Lesson 14), this margin disappears in general. Why does adding the observer degrade the robustness guarantees?
    Answer
    The LQR margin proof relies on a specific algebraic inequality for the loop gain $L(j\omega) = K^*(j\omega I - A)^{-1}B$ — namely $\|I + L(j\omega)\| \geq 1$ for all $\omega$. This holds because the feedback path is the identity (direct state measurement). When an observer is inserted, the actual loop gain becomes $K^*(j\omega I - A + LC)^{-1}L \cdot C(j\omega I - A)^{-1}B$, which involves the observer dynamics and introduces additional phase lag. The return-difference inequality no longer holds in general, and the phase margin can be arbitrarily close to 0°. This is why LQG (LQR + Kalman filter) requires loop transfer recovery (LTR) or $H_\infty$ methods to restore robustness.
Common Pitfalls
  • Choosing $Q = 0$. Setting $Q = 0$ means no state cost — the optimizer doesn't care where the state is, only about minimising input. The result is $u = 0$, which is trivially "optimal" but useless for regulation. Always give $Q$ at least some small weight on the states you care about.
  • Forgetting $N_r$. LQR designs a regulator ($x \to 0$). If tracking a reference $r \neq 0$, the feedforward gain $N_r$ from Lesson 12 is still needed, or integral augmentation must be used.
  • Treating the stability margins as unconditional. The $60°$ phase margin and $[−6\,\text{dB}, \infty]$ gain margin assume a continuous-time implementation with exact state measurement and perfect model. Digital control, observers, and model uncertainty all erode these guarantees.
  • Confusing $Q \geq 0$ and $R > 0$. $Q$ needs to be positive semidefinite (non-negative eigenvalues) — it's fine for some state combinations to be unpenalised. But $R$ must be strictly positive definite — a zero $R$ makes the problem ill-posed (infinite control effort is allowed and the solution degenerates).
  • Tuning only $\rho = R/Q$ without considering physical limits. Even if $J$ is minimised, the resulting control signal $u(t)$ may exceed actuator limits. After finding $K^*$, always simulate the worst-case initial condition to verify that peak control effort stays within physical bounds.
