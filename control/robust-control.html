Introduction to Robust Control
Plant Uncertainty · H∞ Norm · Small Gain Theorem · Sensitivity Functions · μ-Analysis Preview
Every controller in Lessons 1–15 was designed for a specific plant model $G_p(s)$. Real plants are never exactly their model. Parameters drift with temperature. Flexible structures have unmodelled resonances. High-frequency dynamics are truncated in the model. Linearisation throws away nonlinear terms. The question robust control asks is: how do you design a controller that guarantees stability and performance not just for the nominal model $G_p(s)$, but for an entire family of plants $G_p(s) + \Delta(s)$ where $\Delta$ represents all possible model errors?
Notation recap
$\|G\|_\infty$H∞ norm of $G$: $\sup_\omega \sigma_\text{max}(G(j\omega))$ = peak singular value over all frequencies
$\Delta$Uncertainty: perturbation to the nominal plant $G_p$
$S(s)$Sensitivity function $= (1 + GC)^{-1}$: maps disturbances to output errors
$T(s)$Complementary sensitivity function $= GC(1+GC)^{-1}$: closed-loop transfer function
$L(s)$Loop transfer function $= G(s)C(s)$
Why nominal design is not enough
Classical gain and phase margins (Lesson 6) are robustness measures — they quantify how much the loop gain can vary before instability. But they are one-dimensional. A plant with $45°$ phase margin is robust to a phase shift of $45°$ at the gain crossover frequency. It may be fragile to:
  • High-frequency unmodelled resonances. The model is valid up to some frequency $\omega_\text{max}$; the real plant has resonances above this that were omitted. If the loop has gain above $\omega_\text{max}$, the real resonance can cause instability.
  • Parametric variations. The plant gain changes $\pm 20\%$ with temperature. The mass of a robotic arm changes as it picks up different payloads. The controller must handle all of these.
  • Structured perturbations. A flexible structure's resonant frequency shifts depending on the load, but the mode shape stays the same. This is structured uncertainty — not all perturbations are equally likely.
Robust control provides a systematic framework for quantifying these uncertainties and designing controllers that are guaranteed to stabilise and achieve performance across the entire uncertainty set.
The H∞ norm
The H∞ norm of a stable transfer matrix $G(s)$ is: $$\|G\|_\infty = \sup_{\omega \in \mathbb{R}} \bar\sigma(G(j\omega))$$ where $\bar\sigma$ denotes the largest singular value. For a SISO system, $\bar\sigma(G(j\omega)) = |G(j\omega)|$, so the H∞ norm is simply the peak of the Bode magnitude plot.
Input-output interpretation. The H∞ norm is the worst-case gain of the system from input to output, over all frequencies and all unit-energy inputs: $$\|G\|_\infty = \sup_{\|u\|_2 = 1} \|Gu\|_2$$ where $\|\cdot\|_2$ is the $L^2$ (energy) norm. A small H∞ norm means the system attenuates all inputs; a large H∞ norm means some frequency of sinusoidal input gets amplified.
Why is this the right norm for robust control? Because the feedback loop is an interconnection of transfer functions, and the gain of the interconnection is bounded by the product of the gains. If the uncertainty $\Delta$ has small gain and the nominal transfer function seen by $\Delta$ also has small gain, the closed-loop remains stable.
Sensitivity and complementary sensitivity
For a feedback loop with plant $G$ and controller $C$, two fundamental closed-loop transfer functions capture performance and robustness:
Sensitivity function: $$S(s) = \frac{1}{1 + L(s)}, \quad L = GC$$ $S$ maps reference-to-error and disturbance-to-output. $\|S\|_\infty$ is the peak sensitivity — how much the system amplifies disturbances at the worst-case frequency. Good disturbance rejection requires $|S(j\omega)|$ to be small across the low-frequency control bandwidth.
Complementary sensitivity function: $$T(s) = \frac{L(s)}{1 + L(s)} = 1 - S(s)$$ $T$ is the closed-loop transfer function from reference to output. $\|T\|_\infty$ quantifies how well the output tracks the reference, and how much high-frequency noise is fed through (since large $|T|$ at high frequencies amplifies sensor noise). Sensor noise rejection requires $|T(j\omega)|$ to roll off at high frequencies.
The fundamental trade-off: $S + T = 1$. Since $S + T = 1$ identically, making $S$ small (good disturbance rejection) and $T$ small (good noise rejection) simultaneously is impossible — they can't both be small at the same frequency. This is the waterbed effect: pushing $|S|$ below 1 in one frequency band raises it above 1 somewhere else (Bode's integral theorem). Any controller has to balance disturbance rejection and noise sensitivity.
The Small Gain Theorem
The Small Gain Theorem is the central stability theorem of robust control. It gives a sufficient condition for a feedback interconnection to be stable when one of the systems represents uncertainty.
Small Gain Theorem. Consider a feedback loop with two stable systems $M$ and $\Delta$ in a feedback interconnection. The closed-loop is stable if: $$\|M\|_\infty \cdot \|\Delta\|_\infty < 1$$ Equivalently: $\bar\sigma(M(j\omega)) \cdot \bar\sigma(\Delta(j\omega)) < 1$ for all $\omega$.
Proof sketch — Small Gain Theorem

Let the closed-loop signals be $e = d - \Delta Mw$ and $w = Me$. The system is well-posed and stable if the map from disturbance $d$ to internal signals $(e, w)$ is bounded.

Taking $L^2$ norms and applying the triangle inequality: $$\|w\|_2 \leq \|M\|_\infty \|e\|_2 \leq \|M\|_\infty(\|d\|_2 + \|\Delta\|_\infty\|w\|_2)$$ Rearranging: $\|w\|_2(1 - \|M\|_\infty\|\Delta\|_\infty) \leq \|M\|_\infty\|d\|_2$. If $\|M\|_\infty\|\Delta\|_\infty < 1$, then $(1 - \|M\|_\infty\|\Delta\|_\infty) > 0$, so: $$\|w\|_2 \leq \frac{\|M\|_\infty}{1 - \|M\|_\infty\|\Delta\|_\infty}\|d\|_2$$ The map from $d$ to $w$ is bounded — the closed-loop is stable. $\blacksquare$

Uncertainty representations
Uncertainty can be modelled in different ways depending on what is known about the class of possible perturbations:
  • Unstructured additive uncertainty: $G_\text{true} = G + \Delta_A$ where $|\Delta_A(j\omega)| \leq W_A(\omega)$ for a known weighting function $W_A$. Models absolute additive perturbations — e.g. unmodelled high-frequency resonances.
  • Unstructured multiplicative uncertainty: $G_\text{true} = G(1 + \Delta_M)$ where $|\Delta_M(j\omega)| \leq W_M(\omega)$. Models relative (percentage) perturbations — e.g. gain variation of $\pm 20\%$.
  • Structured uncertainty: $\Delta = \text{diag}(\delta_1 I, \delta_2 I, \ldots)$ where each $\delta_i$ is an uncertain real or complex parameter. Models physical parametric uncertainty — e.g. uncertain mass, stiffness, or time constant.
For multiplicative uncertainty, the robust stability condition from the Small Gain Theorem becomes: $$\|W_M T\|_\infty < 1 \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad |T(j\omega)| < \frac{1}{|W_M(j\omega)|} \;\forall\,\omega$$ This says: the complementary sensitivity must roll off faster than the uncertainty weight grows. Large uncertainty at high frequency (as is typical for unmodelled resonances) requires $|T|$ to roll off before those frequencies.
H∞ control design (overview)
The H∞ control problem asks: find the controller $C$ that minimises $\|T_{zw}\|_\infty$, where $T_{zw}$ is the closed-loop transfer function from exogenous inputs $w$ (disturbances, noise, uncertainty) to performance outputs $z$ (errors, actuator signals).
The design is set up by choosing weighting functions $W_1$ (on $S$, penalising disturbance rejection) and $W_2$ (on $T$, penalising noise sensitivity and robustness): $$\min_C \left\|\begin{bmatrix}W_1 S \\ W_2 T\end{bmatrix}\right\|_\infty$$ A good $H_\infty$ design makes $|W_1 S|$ and $|W_2 T|$ both small over their respective frequency ranges — disturbance rejection in the low-frequency band, noise rejection in the high-frequency band.
Solving H∞ problems. The H∞ optimal controller is computed by solving two coupled algebraic Riccati equations (similar to the LQR ARE). In MATLAB: hinfsyn(P, nmeas, ncont) where $P$ is the augmented plant (including weights). The solution gives the optimal $\gamma^* = \min_C \|T_{zw}\|_\infty$ and the corresponding controller.
μ-analysis preview
The Small Gain Theorem is conservative for structured uncertainty — it treats all $\Delta$ with $\|\Delta\|_\infty \leq 1$ as possible, but structured uncertainty has only certain entries non-zero. Structured singular value (μ) analysis tightens the bound by exploiting the structure of $\Delta$.
The structured singular value $\mu_\Delta(M)$ is the smallest $\bar\sigma(\Delta)$ that makes the feedback loop singular, given that $\Delta$ must have the prescribed structure. The robust stability condition becomes $\mu_\Delta(M(j\omega)) < 1$ for all $\omega$ — much less conservative than the unstructured small gain bound $\|M\|_\infty < 1/\|\Delta\|_\infty$.
Computing $\mu$ exactly is NP-hard for general structures; in practice, upper and lower bounds are computed via convex optimisation (linear matrix inequalities). MATLAB's Robust Control Toolbox implements this via mussv.
Worked examples
Example 1 — Robust stability with multiplicative uncertainty
Plant: $G(s) = \frac{1}{s+1}$. Multiplicative uncertainty: $|\Delta_M(j\omega)| \leq W_M(\omega) = \frac{2\omega}{10}$ (growing linearly with frequency, reaching 1 at $\omega=5$ rad/s). A PI controller gives $T(j\omega)$ with $|T| \approx 1$ for $\omega < 3$ rad/s and $|T| \approx 0.1$ at $\omega = 10$ rad/s. Is the closed loop robustly stable?
Robust stability requires $|W_M(j\omega)||T(j\omega)| < 1$ for all $\omega$. Check at $\omega = 5$ rad/s: $W_M = 2\cdot5/10 = 1$. If $|T(j5)| \approx 0.3$, then $|W_M T| = 0.3 < 1$. ✓ Check at $\omega = 3$ rad/s: $W_M = 2\cdot3/10 = 0.6$. $|T(j3)| \approx 1$, so $|W_M T| = 0.6 < 1$. ✓ The robust stability condition is satisfied at the checked frequencies. The binding constraint is near $\omega \approx 3$–$5$ rad/s where both $W_M$ and $|T|$ are significant. To achieve a larger stability margin, we would reduce $|T|$ at these frequencies by reducing the controller bandwidth.
Example 2 — The waterbed effect
For a minimum-phase stable single-input single-output system, Bode's sensitivity integral states: $\int_0^\infty \ln|S(j\omega)|\,d\omega = 0$ (for a plant with no right-half-plane poles or zeros). If a controller achieves $|S(j\omega)| = 0.1$ ($-20$ dB) for all $\omega \leq 1$ rad/s, what does this imply about $|S|$ at higher frequencies?
Bode's integral: $\int_0^\infty \ln|S(j\omega)|\,d\omega = 0$. The low-frequency contribution: $\int_0^1 \ln(0.1)\,d\omega = 1 \cdot (-2.303) = -2.303$. This negative contribution must be compensated by positive values (i.e. $\ln|S| > 0 \Rightarrow |S| > 1$) at higher frequencies. The area under $\ln|S|$ above 1 rad/s must equal $+2.303$. If $|S(j\omega)| = 1.5$ ($+3.5$ dB) for $1 \leq \omega \leq 4$ rad/s: $\int_1^4 \ln(1.5)\,d\omega = 3 \times 0.405 = 1.22$. Still $1.08$ short — the sensitivity must exceed 1 even further at higher frequencies. The message: pushing $|S|$ below 1 in a band of width $W$ forces $|S| > 1$ elsewhere, with the total "violation" proportional to $W \cdot \ln(1/\min|S|)$.
Food for thought
Reflection questions
  1. LQR provides guaranteed stability margins (≥60° phase margin, ≥6 dB gain margin) but no robustness guarantees against unstructured uncertainty. H∞ provides guarantees against arbitrary bounded uncertainty. What is the fundamental difference in what these two theories optimise, and why does LQR not directly address unstructured uncertainty?
    LQR optimises a quadratic cost function assuming the nominal plant model is exact — it is an optimal nominal controller with good stability margins as a side effect. It does not account for model uncertainty at all. H∞ explicitly minimises the worst-case gain from uncertainty input to performance output — it bakes the uncertainty description into the design objective. LQR's stability margins follow from the Kalman inequality (a property of the ARE solution), not from any explicit robustness optimization. For an uncertain plant, LQR may give poor performance or even instability if the real plant differs significantly from the model; H∞ guarantees stability and performance for all plants within the uncertainty set.
  2. The small gain theorem gives a sufficient condition for stability, not a necessary one. Sketch a feedback loop where the small gain condition $\|M\|_\infty\|\Delta\|_\infty < 1$ is violated, yet the loop is stable. What makes this possible?
    Suppose $M(j\omega) = 2$ (constant gain of 2) and $\Delta(j\omega) = 0.7e^{j\phi(\omega)}$ — a phase-shifting uncertainty with magnitude 0.7. The small gain product is $2 \times 0.7 = 1.4 > 1$, violating the condition. But if the phase of $\Delta$ is such that $M\Delta$ never encircles $-1$ in the Nyquist plot, the loop can still be stable. The small gain theorem only looks at magnitudes; it ignores the beneficial phase relationships that can keep the Nyquist plot away from $-1$ even when the magnitude product exceeds 1. This is why μ-analysis can be less conservative — it accounts for the structure of $\Delta$ including possible phase constraints.
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Treating H∞ design as a black box. The H∞ solution depends critically on the choice of weighting functions $W_1$ and $W_2$. Poor weights give a mathematically optimal controller that performs badly in practice. Understanding what each weight penalises (and why) is more important than knowing how to call hinfsyn.
  • Conflating H∞ norm with H2 norm. The H2 norm ($\|G\|_2 = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty \text{tr}[G(j\omega)^*G(j\omega)]\,d\omega}$) is the RMS gain for white noise inputs — it arises in LQG/Kalman filter design. H∞ is the peak gain over all frequencies and sinusoidal inputs. LQG optimises H2; robust control targets H∞.
  • Using gain/phase margins alone for high-order systems. Gain and phase margins are defined at the single gain crossover frequency. A system can have good classical margins but poor robustness to high-frequency resonances. The H∞ norm of the sensitivity function provides a more complete robustness picture.
