1 · The Control Problem
Feedback · Open-Loop vs Closed-Loop · Block Diagrams · Stability · Disturbance Rejection
Everything around you is being controlled. The temperature in the room stays within a comfortable range even as the sun moves. Your car's engine maintains a steady speed as the road tilts. Your body keeps its blood glucose at roughly 5 mmol/L through a network of hormonal feedback loops. Control theory is the mathematical framework for designing these systems: given a physical process (called the plant), design an algorithm (the controller) that makes it behave as desired despite uncertainty, disturbances, and things you can't measure directly. This lesson is the conceptual foundation — the vocabulary and mental models that everything else in the cluster builds on.
What is a control system?
A control system is an arrangement of components that maintains some quantity of interest — temperature, speed, position, concentration, voltage — at a desired value or trajectory, despite the influence of external disturbances and internal imperfections.
Three things are always present:
  • The plant — the physical process to be controlled. A motor, a furnace, a chemical reactor, a spacecraft. The plant takes an input (voltage, fuel flow, actuator force) and produces an output (shaft speed, temperature, composition, position). We can often model the plant mathematically, but the model is never perfect.
  • The actuator — the mechanism that applies the input to the plant. A valve, a servo, a heating element, a thruster. Often modeled as part of the plant for analysis purposes.
  • The sensor — the mechanism that measures the output (or some related quantity). A thermometer, a tachometer, a GPS receiver. Sensors are noisy and imperfect.
A controller is the algorithm that decides what input to apply to the plant based on some information about the desired output and the actual output. The question is: what information should the controller use?
Open-loop control
The simplest approach: compute the required input from the desired output, apply it, and never look at what actually happens. This is open-loop control.
Example: a toaster. You set the darkness level (the reference), and the timer determines how long the heating elements stay on (the input). The toaster doesn't measure whether your toast is actually dark or pale — it just runs for the preset time. If the bread is thin, it burns; if the voltage is lower than expected, it stays pale. No measurement, no correction.
Open-loop control works when:
  • The plant model is accurate
  • There are no significant disturbances
  • Precise output is not critical
It fails when any of these conditions don't hold. A slightly wrong model, a gust of wind, a worn actuator — all produce uncorrected errors that persist indefinitely.
Closed-loop feedback control
The key insight of feedback control: measure what is actually happening and use the discrepancy to correct the input.
The key elements in the closed-loop block diagram:
  • Reference $r(t)$ — the desired output (setpoint). "I want the temperature to be 22°C."
  • Error $e(t) = r(t) - y(t)$ — the discrepancy between what you want and what you have. The summing junction computes this.
  • Controller — takes the error and computes the control input $u(t)$. This is where the engineering happens.
  • Plant — the physical process. Produces output $y(t)$ in response to input $u(t)$ and disturbances $d(t)$.
  • Disturbance $d(t)$ — unwanted inputs to the plant (wind, load changes, power fluctuations). Feedback allows these to be corrected.
  • Measurement noise $n(t)$ — sensor errors that corrupt the feedback signal. A real constraint on how aggressively you can control.
Why feedback wins
Feedback is not just "better" than open-loop — it is qualitatively different. Consider three failure modes of open-loop control, and how feedback handles each:
Model uncertainty. Your model of the plant is wrong — the gain is 10% higher than you assumed. Open-loop: the output is 10% off forever. Closed-loop with high gain: the error drives the input until the output matches the reference, regardless of the exact plant gain. Feedback desensitizes the system to plant variations.
Disturbances. A load is suddenly applied to the motor. Open-loop: the speed drops and stays low until you manually increase the input. Closed-loop: the speed drop creates an error, which increases the control input, which brings the speed back. Feedback rejects disturbances automatically.
Unstable plants. Some physical systems are inherently unstable — an inverted pendulum, an unstable aircraft, a chemical reactor with positive feedback. Open-loop control cannot stabilize an unstable plant (any perturbation grows without bound). Feedback control can stabilize plants that are fundamentally unstable by continuously correcting errors before they grow large.
The fundamental trade-off. Feedback introduces a new problem: the feedback signal carries measurement noise $n(t)$. If you control very aggressively (high gain), you react quickly to errors — but you also react to sensor noise, potentially causing the actuator to chatter and the output to oscillate. The entire art of control design is choosing how to balance these competing concerns: fast enough to reject disturbances, gentle enough to not amplify noise. This trade-off runs through every topic in this cluster.
Block diagram notation
Block diagrams are the language of control systems. Each block represents a transformation from input to output, described by a transfer function $G(s)$ (introduced in Lesson 2). A few rules:
  • Cascade (series): Two blocks in series have combined transfer function $G_1(s) \cdot G_2(s)$. The output of the first feeds the input of the second.
  • Summing junction: A circle with a $+$ or $-$ at each input. Sums (or subtracts) signals. The negative sign at the feedback path input is the "negative feedback" that makes error = reference − output.
  • Parallel paths: Two blocks in parallel with outputs summed have combined transfer function $G_1(s) + G_2(s)$.
  • Closed-loop transfer function: For the standard unity-feedback loop with plant $P(s)$ and controller $C(s)$: $$T(s) = \frac{C(s)P(s)}{1 + C(s)P(s)}$$ This is the transfer function from reference $r$ to output $y$. The denominator $1 + C(s)P(s)$ is the characteristic polynomial — its roots (the closed-loop poles) determine stability and response speed.
⟶ Worth understanding: Derive the closed-loop transfer function $T(s) = Y(s)/R(s)$ from the block diagram. Start from $Y = P(U)$ and $U = C(R - Y)$. Substitute and solve for $Y/R$. This algebraic manipulation is the foundation of all closed-loop analysis — once you can do it for simple loops, you can handle multi-loop systems by the same algebra.
Performance objectives
What does "good" control mean? Several objectives, often in tension:
Objective What it means Metric
Stability The system doesn't blow up (outputs remain bounded for bounded inputs) All closed-loop poles in the left half-plane
Tracking The output follows the reference closely and quickly Rise time, settling time, overshoot, steady-state error
Disturbance rejection External disturbances have small effect on the output Disturbance transfer function magnitude
Robustness System remains stable and performs well even if the plant model is wrong Gain margin, phase margin
Noise attenuation Sensor noise is not amplified into the actuator or output High-frequency gain of sensitivity function
These objectives conflict. Faster tracking requires higher controller gain, which reduces robustness and amplifies noise. Rejecting disturbances requires high gain at low frequencies, but this tends to make the system less stable at high frequencies. Control design is the process of navigating these trade-offs within the constraints set by the application.
Examples across engineering and biology
Thermostat. Plant: building heating system. Reference: desired temperature. Measurement: thermometer. Control: on/off relay. Disturbance: outdoor temperature changes, doors opening. This is on/off (bang-bang) control — crude but sufficient for a slow, lossy plant where exact tracking is unnecessary.
Cruise control. Plant: car engine and drivetrain. Reference: target speed. Measurement: wheel speed sensor. Control: throttle position. Disturbance: hills, headwinds, passengers boarding. The PI (proportional-integral) controller common in cruise control eliminates steady-state error when climbing hills.
Inverted pendulum. Plant: a stick balanced on a cart. This is open-loop unstable — without feedback, any perturbation causes it to fall. Feedback control measures the angle and continuously drives the cart to keep the pendulum upright. The same principle applies to balancing rockets (SpaceX Falcon 9 vertical landing) and two-wheeled robots.
Blood glucose regulation. The pancreas secretes insulin (reduces glucose) and glucagon (raises glucose) based on blood glucose measurements — a biological feedback controller that has been running for hundreds of millions of years. Diabetes is, in part, a failure of this feedback loop. Artificial pancreas devices (closed-loop insulin pumps) replicate it using engineering control theory.
Operational amplifier. A standalone op-amp has enormous gain (10⁵–10⁷) but is unstable and sensitive to temperature. By wrapping it in a feedback network (resistors from output to inverting input), the closed-loop gain becomes precisely set by the resistor ratio and is nearly independent of the op-amp's exact gain. This is the same principle as Lesson 2's closed-loop formula applied to analog circuits.

  2. Feedback can stabilize an unstable plant. But can feedback make a stable plant unstable? Under what conditions would a feedback controller make things worse? (Think about high gain and time delays.)
  3. The closed-loop transfer function is $T(s) = CP/(1+CP)$. What happens to $T(s)$ when $CP \gg 1$? What does this tell you about what the output does at frequencies where the loop gain is large?
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Thinking more gain is always better. Increasing controller gain improves tracking and disturbance rejection — up to a point. Beyond a critical gain, the system oscillates or becomes unstable. Every control design problem has a gain limit set by the phase lag in the loop (delays, parasitic dynamics, sensor lag). Routh-Hurwitz (Lesson 4) and Nyquist (Lesson 6) make this precise.
  • Confusing the plant and the closed-loop system. When someone asks "is this system stable?", they always mean the closed-loop system (plant + controller + feedback). An unstable plant can be stabilized by feedback; a stable plant can be destabilized. The plant's poles are not the same as the closed-loop poles.
  • Ignoring the negative in negative feedback. The standard feedback loop subtracts the measured output from the reference: $e = r - y$. If you accidentally wire positive feedback ($e = r + y$), the error grows without bound. In practice this happens with sign errors in sensor wiring or software, and the system immediately goes unstable.
  • Assuming the model is good enough. Real plants have dynamics that aren't in the model: actuator saturation, time delays, high-frequency resonances. A controller designed for the nominal model may fail badly when these unmodeled dynamics interact with the feedback loop. Robustness (gain and phase margins, Lesson 6) quantifies how much margin you have before unmodeled dynamics cause instability.
Connects to
  • Differential Equations — Every block in a block diagram corresponds to an ODE or a transfer function derived from one. The plant $P(s)$ is the Laplace transform of the plant's impulse response, which comes from the plant's differential equation. Lesson 2 builds this connection.
  • RL · The RL Problem (Lesson 1 of RL cluster) — The RL agent-environment loop is formally analogous to the closed-loop control diagram. The agent is the controller; the environment is the plant; the reward signal is an analog of the error. The key difference: RL learns the controller from data rather than designing it analytically from a plant model.
  • Neuroscience — Biological motor control is feedback control: the cerebellum acts as a predictive controller (open-loop component), while the spinal reflexes provide fast feedback loops. Parkinson's disease can be modeled as a change in the loop gain of the basal ganglia feedback circuit.
