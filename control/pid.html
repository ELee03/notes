7 · PID Control
Proportional · Integral · Derivative · Tuning · Anti-Windup

$K_p$Proportional gain

Over 90% of industrial control loops are PID controllers. Not because PID is optimal — it usually isn't — but because it is simple, intuitive, and works well for a vast range of plants. Every chemical plant, refinery, power station, HVAC system, and industrial robot uses thousands of PID loops. Understanding PID is understanding the vast majority of deployed control systems in the world. This lesson explains why each term works, derives the transfer function, and gives you the tools to tune it.
The three actions
A PID controller computes its output $u(t)$ from the error signal $e(t) = r(t) - y(t)$ using three independent terms: $$u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int_0^t e(\tau)\,d\tau + K_d \frac{de(t)}{dt}$$
Proportional (P)
Action: $K_p e(t)$ — output proportional to current error. Effect: Faster response, reduced (but nonzero) steady-state error for most plants. Analogy: You are steering. The larger the deviation from the desired path, the harder you turn. The output is always proportional to how far off you are right now.
Integral (I)
Action: $K_i \int e\,dt$ — output proportional to accumulated past error. Effect: Eliminates steady-state error completely (for step references and constant disturbances). Adds a pole at $s=0$ to the loop — Type 1 system. Analogy: If you've been consistently off course to the right for the past minute, you apply extra left correction to account for the accumulated drift. The integral "remembers" all past errors.
Derivative (D)
Action: $K_d \dot{e}(t)$ — output proportional to rate of change of error. Effect: Anticipates future errors; adds damping; allows higher P gain without oscillation. Adds a zero to the open-loop transfer function. Analogy: If the error is currently zero but increasing rapidly, you apply a corrective output before the error becomes large. Derivative is predictive. Problem: Amplifies high-frequency noise (derivative of noise is large). Always used with a low-pass filter in practice.
PID transfer function and forms
Taking the Laplace transform of the PID control law gives the parallel form:
$$\boxed{C(s) = K_p + \frac{K_i}{s} + K_d s = K_p\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{T_i s} + T_d s\right)}$$
In the standard (ISA) form, $K_p$ is a single gain and $T_i$, $T_d$ are time constants: $K_i = K_p/T_i$ and $K_d = K_p T_d$. This form is physically intuitive: $T_i$ is the time the integrator takes to add a contribution equal to the P term, and $T_d$ is the prediction horizon.
The PID transfer function can also be written as a product of factors: $$C(s) = \frac{K_p}{T_i s}\,\left(T_i s + 1\right)\!\left(T_d s + 1\right)$$ (valid when $T_i T_d s^2$ is grouped appropriately — the exact form depends on the algebraic grouping used, but the poles and zeros are the same.) In either form, the PID controller has:
  • One pole at $s=0$ (integrator from I action) — gives Type 1 loop.
  • Two zeros at $s = -1/(2T_d)\pm\sqrt{1/(4T_d^2)-1/(T_d T_i)}$ — these are the PD correction zeros.
  • For the open-loop $L(s) = C(s)P(s)$: the integrator in $C(s)$ adds $-90°$ phase lag; the two zeros add phase lead near the crossover frequency — this is how derivative action improves the phase margin.
Effect on the Bode plot: Adding a PID controller to a first-order plant $P(s)=K/(τs+1)$:
  • P action: scales the whole curve upward → raises $\omega_c$.
  • I action: adds $+20$ dB/dec slope at low frequencies (and $-90°$ phase there) → low-frequency gain goes to $\infty$ → zero steady-state error.
  • D action: adds $+20$ dB/dec slope at high frequencies (and $+90°$ phase lead there) → compensates the phase lag near crossover → larger phase margin.
Ziegler-Nichols tuning
Ziegler and Nichols (1942) gave two empirical tuning methods that require no plant model — just simple experiments. These are not optimal, but they give a useful starting point and illustrate the relationship between the PID parameters and the plant's dynamics.
Method 1: Ultimate Oscillation (closed-loop).
  1. Set $K_i = 0$ and $K_d = 0$ (P-only control).
  2. Increase $K_p$ from zero until the closed-loop system just begins to sustain oscillation. Record this ultimate gain $K_{cr}$.
  3. Record the period of the sustained oscillation $T_{cr}$.
  4. Use the Ziegler-Nichols table to set the PID parameters.
Controller $K_p$ $T_i$ $T_d$
P $0.5\,K_{cr}$ — —
PI $0.45\,K_{cr}$ $T_{cr}/1.2$ —
PID $0.6\,K_{cr}$ $T_{cr}/2$ $T_{cr}/8$
Method 2: Step Response (open-loop). Apply a step input to the plant in open-loop. For plants that look like $K e^{-L s}/(Ts+1)$ (common in process control): read off the dead time $L$ and time constant $T$ from the S-shaped step response. Then $K_p = 1.2T/(KL)$, $T_i = 2L$, $T_d = 0.5L$.
Z-N gives $PM \approx 30°$ — adequate but not great. Process control engineers often detune by a factor of 2–3 (lower $K_p$, higher $T_i$) to get more robustness. For precision motion control or medical devices, use analytical methods (root locus, pole placement) rather than Z-N tuning.
Practical issues: derivative kick and integral windup
Derivative kick. When the reference $r(t)$ changes as a step, the error $e(t) = r(t) - y(t)$ also jumps. Its derivative is theoretically infinite — the derivative term produces an enormous spike in the control output. This is called derivative kick.
Fix: Apply the derivative to the measurement only, not the error: $$u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int e\,dt - K_d \frac{dy(t)}{dt}$$ Since $\dot{e} = \dot{r} - \dot{y}$, this removes the contribution from $\dot{r}$. A step in $r$ no longer causes a derivative spike.
Integral windup. When the actuator saturates (the control output $u$ hits a physical limit — valve fully open, motor at max torque), the plant is no longer receiving the commanded signal. But the integrator keeps accumulating error, building up an enormous integral term ("windup"). When the saturation eventually lifts, the controller has a huge integral bias that causes the output to overshoot badly or remain stuck at the limit.
Anti-windup strategies:
  • Clamping: Stop integrating when the output saturates. $\dot{I} = 0$ whenever $u = u_{max}$ and $e > 0$ (or $u = u_{min}$ and $e < 0$). Simple to implement.
  • Back-calculation: Feed back the difference between the commanded and actual (saturated) output to reduce the integrator state. More principled and gives smoother recovery.
  • Conditional integration: Only integrate when the error is small enough that saturation is unlikely. Not recommended — can cause steady-state error.
Worked examples
Example 1: PI control of a first-order plant. Plant: $P(s) = 1/(2s+1)$. Design a PI controller to track step references with zero steady-state error and $PM \approx 60°$.
PI controller: $C(s) = K_p(1 + 1/(T_i s)) = K_p(T_i s + 1)/(T_i s)$. Open loop: $L(s) = K_p(T_i s + 1)/[T_i s (2s+1)]$. Choose $T_i = 2$ (cancel the plant pole at $s = -1/2$, called PI-cancellation): $L(s) = K_p / (2s^2)$ — wait, that's wrong; let me redo. $T_i = 2$: $L(s) = K_p(2s+1)/(2s(2s+1)) = K_p/(2s)$. This is a Type 1, first-order loop.
  • Phase of $L(j\omega) = K_p/(2j\omega)$: always $-90°$. $PM = 180° - 90° = 90°$ regardless of $K_p$. Excellent margin.
  • Gain crossover: $|K_p/(2\omega_c)| = 1$ → $\omega_c = K_p/2$. Choose $\omega_c = 1$ rad/s for a time constant of $\approx 1$ s → $K_p = 2$.
  • Final controller: $C(s) = 2(1 + 1/(2s)) = 2 + 1/s$. Zero steady-state error to step; 90° phase margin.
Example 2: Effect of derivative on a second-order plant. Plant: $P(s) = 1/(s(s+1))$ (position servo, Type 1). Proportional control alone with $K_p = 1$: characteristic polynomial $s^2+s+1$, $\zeta=0.5$, 16% overshoot. Adding $K_d=1$ (PD control): $C(s)P(s) = (s+1)/s(s+1) = 1/s$. Characteristic polynomial $s^2 + s + 1$ → wait, $1+C(s)P(s) = 1 + (1+s)/s(s+1) = 1 + 1/s$... let me redo cleanly.
With $K_p=1$, $K_d=1$: $C(s) = 1+s$, $C(s)P(s) = (1+s)/[s(s+1)] = 1/s$. Closed-loop: $T(s) = (1/s)/(1+1/s) = 1/(s+1)$. First-order, no overshoot, $\tau=1$ s. The derivative zero at $s=-1$ exactly cancelled the plant pole at $s=-1$ — a convenient example showing how derivative action can dramatically change the closed-loop behavior.

Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Applying raw derivative to the error signal. The classic PID formula differentiates $e(t) = r(t) - y(t)$. When $r(t)$ is a step, the derivative term produces an instantaneous infinite spike. Always differentiate only the output $y(t)$ in the derivative term (or apply a low-pass filter to $e(t)$). This is called "derivative-on-measurement" and is standard practice.
  • Ignoring integral windup in simulation and implementation. A PID that works perfectly in simulation may fail catastrophically in hardware because the simulation doesn't model the actuator limits. Whenever your actuator has any limit (and every real actuator does), you must include anti-windup logic. Test explicitly: apply a large step reference that drives the actuator to saturation and verify that the recovery is smooth.
  • Thinking more D is always better for damping. Derivative action does add damping — but it amplifies high-frequency noise by $|s K_d|$. Unfiltered derivative on a noisy measurement (e.g., a velocity encoder with electrical noise) produces violent actuator chatter. Always include a first-order low-pass filter on the derivative: replace $K_d s$ with $K_d s/(N^{-1} s + 1)$ where $N \approx 10$–$20$ limits the high-frequency gain.
  • Tuning in the frequency domain and forgetting about actuator constraints. Frequency-domain design (phase margin, bandwidth) gives the right linear behavior, but real systems have nonlinear limits: actuator saturation, rate limits, dead zones. A beautifully tuned PID in the linear regime may oscillate badly when the actuator saturates. Non-linear phenomena require separate analysis beyond the linear PID design.
