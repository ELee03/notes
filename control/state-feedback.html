State Feedback & Pole Placement
Full-State Feedback · Eigenvalue Assignment · Ackermann's Formula · Reference Tracking
Suppose you could measure every state variable of a system at every instant. Then instead of an external controller operating only on the output, you could close the loop through the full internal state — and the dynamics of the closed-loop system become yours to choose. Pole placement is the exact answer to the question: if controllability guarantees we can reach any state, what gain K turns that freedom into freely-chosen eigenvalues?
Notation recap
$A, B, C$System matrices (state, input, output)
$K$State-feedback gain vector (row vector, $1\times n$ for SISO)
$A_{cl}$Closed-loop system matrix $A - BK$
$\mathcal{C}$Controllability matrix $[B\ AB\ \cdots\ A^{n-1}B]$
$\varphi_d(s)$Desired closed-loop characteristic polynomial
$N_r$Reference feedforward gain (for steady-state tracking)
Full-State Feedback
In classical control we fed back only the output $y = Cx$ — a scalar signal. In state-space control we feed back the entire state vector $x(t)$, which contains everything the system "knows" about its own history. The control law is simply:
$$u(t) = -Kx(t) + N_r\, r(t)$$
where $K \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times n}$ is a row vector of gains and $r(t)$ is the reference (set-point). For the moment ignore the $N_r$ term and set $r = 0$ to focus on stabilisation. The closed-loop state equation becomes:
$$\dot{x} = Ax + Bu = Ax + B(-Kx) = (A - BK)\,x$$
The dynamics are now governed by the closed-loop matrix $A_{cl} = A - BK$. The behaviour of the system — stability, speed, damping — is determined entirely by the eigenvalues of $A - BK$. The design question becomes: choose $K$ so that $\text{eig}(A - BK)$ equals a desired set of poles.
Why subtract? The sign convention $u = -Kx$ makes positive gains stabilising for most plants (like a negative-feedback loop). If the $i$-th state is positive and is destabilising, a positive $k_i$ feeds a negative correction back into the input.
Eigenvalue Assignment Theorem
Controllability from Lesson 11 was about reachability — the ability to drive the state anywhere with an appropriate input. It turns out this is exactly the condition needed for arbitrary pole placement:
Theorem — Pole Placement
The eigenvalues of $A - BK$ can be assigned arbitrarily (any values, subject only to complex poles appearing in conjugate pairs) if and only if $(A, B)$ is controllable.
The "only if" direction is intuitive: an uncontrollable mode cannot be affected by any input, so its eigenvalue is fixed regardless of $K$. The "if" direction says that controllability is not just sufficient but exactly sufficient — you get complete freedom over the spectrum of the closed-loop system.
This is one of the central results of modern control theory. In classical frequency-domain design (root locus, PID) you tune a scalar gain or a few parameters and hope the poles end up where you want. With full-state feedback you can place every pole exactly where you specify.
Direct Coefficient Matching
For low-order systems ($n = 2$ or $3$) the quickest route to $K$ is direct coefficient matching: write the characteristic polynomial of $A - BK$ in terms of the unknown gains, set it equal to the desired polynomial, and solve.
Example — double integrator. The double integrator $\ddot{y} = u$ has state $x = [y,\,\dot{y}]^T$ and:
$$A = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}$$
With gain $K = [k_1,\;k_2]$ the closed-loop matrix is:
$$A_{cl} = A - BK = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 \\ -k_1 & -k_2\end{bmatrix}$$
Its characteristic polynomial is: $$\det(sI - A_{cl}) = s^2 + k_2\,s + k_1$$ Suppose the desired closed-loop characteristic polynomial is $\varphi_d(s) = s^2 + 2\zeta\omega_n s + \omega_n^2$. Matching coefficients gives: $$k_1 = \omega_n^2, \qquad k_2 = 2\zeta\omega_n$$ The state-feedback gain directly programs the natural frequency and damping ratio of the closed-loop. For $\omega_n = 2\,\text{rad/s}$ and $\zeta = 0.7$: $k_1 = 4$, $k_2 = 2.8$.
Direct matching works cleanly for $n = 2$ and $n = 3$ but becomes tedious for larger systems. Ackermann's formula automates it.
Ackermann's Formula
For a single-input system of order $n$, Ackermann's formula gives $K$ directly from the controllability matrix and the desired polynomial:
$$\boxed{K = \mathbf{e}_n^T\,\mathcal{C}^{-1}\,\varphi_d(A)}$$
where:
  • $\mathcal{C} = [B,\;AB,\;\ldots,\;A^{n-1}B]$ is the $n \times n$ controllability matrix
  • $\varphi_d(s) = (s - \lambda_1)(s - \lambda_2)\cdots(s - \lambda_n)$ is the desired characteristic polynomial
  • $\varphi_d(A) = A^n + \alpha_{n-1}A^{n-1} + \cdots + \alpha_1 A + \alpha_0 I$ is the polynomial evaluated at the matrix $A$
  • $\mathbf{e}_n^T = [0,\;0,\;\ldots,\;0,\;1]$ is the $n$-th standard basis row vector
The formula assumes $(A,B)$ is controllable (so $\mathcal{C}$ is invertible). The product $\mathbf{e}_n^T \mathcal{C}^{-1}$ selects the last row of $\mathcal{C}^{-1}$; right-multiplying by $\varphi_d(A)$ produces the gain vector.
Derivation — Ackermann's formula

Step 1: Controllable canonical form. For any controllable $(A, B)$, define the change of basis $T = \mathcal{C}\,W$ where $W$ is the companion matrix built from the open-loop characteristic polynomial coefficients $p(\lambda) = \lambda^n + a_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0$: $$W = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & \cdots & a_{n-1} & 1 \\ a_2 & \cdots & 1 & 0 \\ \vdots & \iddots & & \vdots \\ 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ In the new coordinates $\tilde{x} = T^{-1}x$, the system becomes $\tilde{A} = T^{-1}AT$, $\tilde{B} = T^{-1}B$, which are exactly the controllable canonical form (CCF): $\tilde{B} = e_n$ (last standard basis vector), and $\tilde{A}$ has the open-loop polynomial coefficients $-a_0, \ldots, -a_{n-1}$ in its last row.

Step 2: Design in CCF. The closed-loop matrix is $\tilde{A} - \tilde{B}\tilde{K}$. Since $\tilde{B} = e_n$, subtracting $\tilde{K}$ only modifies the last row of $\tilde{A}$. Choosing $\tilde{K} = [\tilde{k}_1, \ldots, \tilde{k}_n]$ so the last row becomes $[-\alpha_0, \ldots, -\alpha_{n-1}]$ gives the desired characteristic polynomial $\varphi_d$. So $\tilde{K} = e_n^T\,\varphi_d(\tilde{A})$ (the last row of $\varphi_d(\tilde{A})$ extracts exactly the required shifts).

Step 3: Transform back. The original gain is $K = \tilde{K}T^{-1} = e_n^T\,\varphi_d(\tilde{A})\,T^{-1}$. Using $T = \mathcal{C}W$ and the fact that $\varphi_d(\tilde{A}) = T^{-1}\varphi_d(A)T$ (polynomials commute with similarity transforms): $$K = e_n^T\,T^{-1}\varphi_d(A)T\,T^{-1} = e_n^T\,T^{-1}\varphi_d(A) = e_n^T\,W^{-1}\mathcal{C}^{-1}\varphi_d(A)$$ Since $e_n^T W^{-1} = e_n^T$ (the last row of $W^{-1}$ is $e_n^T$ by construction of $W$): $$\boxed{K = e_n^T\,\mathcal{C}^{-1}\,\varphi_d(A)}\ \blacksquare$$

Example — verify the double integrator. With the double-integrator matrices above: $$\mathcal{C} = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix},\quad \mathcal{C}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix}$$ $$\varphi_d(A) = A^2 + 2\zeta\omega_n A + \omega_n^2 I = \begin{bmatrix}-\omega_n^2 & 2\zeta\omega_n \\ 0 & -\omega_n^2 + 0\end{bmatrix} \ldots$$ Working it through (the algebra is straightforward) recovers $K = [\omega_n^2,\;2\zeta\omega_n]$, exactly matching the direct method result. ✓
MATLAB / NumPy. K = acker(A, B, desired_poles) (MATLAB) or implement in Python: 
import numpy as np
from numpy.linalg import matrix_power, inv

def ackermann(A, B, poles):
    n = A.shape[0]
    # Controllability matrix
    C = np.hstack([matrix_power(A, i) @ B for i in range(n)])
    # Desired characteristic polynomial coefficients
    phi = np.poly(poles)          # returns [1, alpha_{n-1}, ..., alpha_0]
    # Evaluate polynomial at A
    phiA = np.zeros_like(A, dtype=float)
    for i, c in enumerate(phi):
        phiA += c * matrix_power(A, n - i)
    en = np.zeros((1, n)); en[0, -1] = 1.0
    return en @ inv(C) @ phiA
Choosing Desired Poles
Pole placement gives you complete freedom to put eigenvalues wherever you like in the left half-plane. That freedom is only useful if you know where to put them. A few principles guide the choice:
Dominant second-order poles. For an $n$-th-order system, often the simplest strategy is to designate two poles as dominant — placed at the complex location that gives the desired $\zeta$ and $\omega_n$ (from the second-order specifications of Lesson 3): $$\lambda_{1,2} = -\zeta\omega_n \pm j\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}$$ The remaining $n - 2$ poles are placed well to the left (say, $5\times$ to $10\times$ farther from the imaginary axis), so they produce fast transients that decay before the dominant poles matter. The closed-loop then behaves approximately like the second-order system defined by the dominant pair.
Speed vs. control effort. Pushing poles further left speeds up the response but demands larger $K$, which requires larger control signals. Real actuators saturate. A gain $K$ that looks fine in simulation may be physically unrealizable if it requires, say, a 50 V input to a 5 V system. This is the central limitation of arbitrary pole placement — and the reason LQR (Lesson 13) provides a principled tradeoff between speed and effort.
Real poles only. If you want no oscillation (overdamped response), place all poles on the negative real axis. For tracking problems this is often conservative; some damped oscillation ($\zeta \approx 0.7$) gives faster settling without excessive overshoot.
Ill-conditioning warning. When desired poles are widely spread, $\mathcal{C}^{-1}$ in Ackermann's formula can be numerically ill-conditioned. For large $n$ or widely-spaced poles, use the more numerically stable Bass-Gura or Heymann–method implementations. MATLAB's place() (distinct from acker()) uses a robust algorithm.
Reference Tracking
State feedback $u = -Kx$ is a regulator — it drives $x \to 0$, which means $y \to 0$. For a servo problem — tracking a nonzero reference $r$ — we need to add reference input explicitly.
Feedforward gain $N_r$. The simplest fix is: $$u = -Kx + N_r\,r$$ With this input the closed-loop dynamics are: $$\dot{x} = (A - BK)x + BN_r\,r, \quad y = Cx$$ At steady state ($\dot{x} = 0$, $r = \text{const}$): $$x_{ss} = -(A - BK)^{-1}\,BN_r\,r$$ $$y_{ss} = -C(A - BK)^{-1}BN_r\,r$$ For $y_{ss} = r$ we need:
$$N_r = \frac{1}{-C(A - BK)^{-1}B} = \frac{1}{C(BK - A)^{-1}B}$$
$N_r$ is a scalar (for single-output systems) computed from the closed-loop DC gain. It works well when the plant model is accurate; any model error creates a steady-state offset.
Integral action. A more robust strategy adds an integrator state $z$ that accumulates the tracking error $e = r - y$: $$\dot{z} = r - y = r - Cx$$ Augment the state to $\tilde{x} = [x^T,\;z]^T$ and design $K$ for the augmented system $(\tilde{A}, \tilde{B})$: $$\tilde{A} = \begin{bmatrix}A & 0 \\ -C & 0\end{bmatrix},\quad \tilde{B} = \begin{bmatrix}B \\ 0\end{bmatrix}$$ The gain $\tilde{K} = [K_x,\;K_z]$ drives both the original states and the integral error: $u = -K_x x - K_z z + \text{(reference feedforward)}$. The integrator guarantees zero steady-state error even with constant disturbances and model uncertainty — for the same reason integral action in PID eliminates steady-state error (Lesson 7).
Which to use? $N_r$ is simple and works when the model is good. Integral augmentation is more robust but increases system order by 1 and requires care to avoid integrator windup. For disturbance rejection, integral action is usually the better choice.
Comparison to Root Locus
Root locus (Lesson 5) and pole placement are related but answer different questions:
Property Root Locus Pole Placement (State Feedback)
Parameter Scalar gain $K$ Gain vector $\mathbf{K}$ ($n$ free parameters)
Degrees of freedom 1 (slide along a fixed locus) $n$ (place all $n$ poles independently)
Achievable poles Points on the root locus only Anywhere in $\mathbb{C}$ (if controllable)
Information required Transfer function $G(s)$ Full state $x(t)$ at every instant
Design approach Graphical; read gain from locus Algebraic; solve for $K$ directly
Main limitation Limited freedom over pole locations Requires full state measurement
The requirement to measure every state is the key practical limitation of full-state feedback. In many systems not all states are directly measurable — temperature inside a wall, velocity of a gear train shaft. This motivates the observer (Lesson 14), which reconstructs the state from output measurements, allowing state feedback without direct state sensing.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Stabilising an unstable inverted pendulum
The inverted pendulum linearised about the upright position: $\dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\6&0\end{bmatrix}x + \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}u$. The open-loop poles are $s = \pm\sqrt{6} \approx \pm 2.45$ — one unstable. Place the closed-loop poles at $-3 \pm 3j$.
Step 1 — Controllability. $\mathcal{C} = [B,\;AB] = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$, $\det\mathcal{C} = -1 \neq 0$. ✓

Step 2 — Closed-loop char poly. $K = [k_1,\;k_2]$: $$A_{cl} = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\6-k_1&-k_2\end{bmatrix} \;\Rightarrow\; \chi(s) = s^2 + k_2 s + (k_1-6)$$ Step 3 — Desired polynomial. Poles $-3 \pm 3j$: $(s+3)^2 + 9 = s^2 + 6s + 18$.

Step 4 — Match coefficients. $k_2 = 6$, $k_1 - 6 = 18 \Rightarrow k_1 = 24$. So $\boxed{K = [24,\;6]}$.

Check: Closed-loop poles $s = -3 \pm 3j$ — both in left half-plane. ✓ The feedback has overcome the unstable open-loop pole at $+\sqrt{6}$.
Example 2 — Third-order system via Ackermann's formula
$$A = \begin{bmatrix}0&1&0\\0&0&1\\-1&-2&-3\end{bmatrix},\quad B = \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}$$ Place poles at $-2,\,-3,\,-4$ using Ackermann's formula.
Desired polynomial. $(s+2)(s+3)(s+4) = s^3 + 9s^2 + 26s + 24$.

Key observation. $A$ is already in controllable canonical form (the last row contains the negative of the open-loop char poly coefficients $1, 2, 3$). In this form $\mathcal{C} = I$ (up to column permutation) and Ackermann's formula simplifies: $K$ is just the difference between the desired and existing last-row coefficients.

Open-loop char poly: $s^3 + 3s^2 + 2s + 1$.
Desired char poly: $s^3 + 9s^2 + 26s + 24$.
$$\boxed{K = [24-1,\;26-2,\;9-3] = [23,\;24,\;6]}$$ Verify: $A-BK$ has last row $[-1-23,\;-2-24,\;-3-6] = [-24,\;-26,\;-9]$, giving char poly $s^3 + 9s^2 + 26s + 24$. ✓
Example 3 — Computing $N_r$ for a DC motor
A DC motor position system: $A = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\0&-5\end{bmatrix}$, $B = \begin{bmatrix}0\\10\end{bmatrix}$, $C = [1,\;0]$. State feedback $K = [4,\;1.5]$ has been designed. Find $N_r$ so that the closed-loop DC output matches a constant reference.
Recall: $N_r = 1\,/\,\bigl[C(BK - A)^{-1}B\bigr]$, equivalently $N_r = -1\,/\,\bigl[C(A-BK)^{-1}B\bigr]$.

$$A_{cl} = A-BK = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-40&-20\end{bmatrix},\quad \det(A_{cl}) = 40$$ $$(A_{cl})^{-1} = \frac{1}{40}\begin{bmatrix}-20&-1\\40&0\end{bmatrix}$$ $$C(A_{cl})^{-1}B = [1,0]\cdot\frac{1}{40}\begin{bmatrix}-20&-1\\40&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0\\10\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{40}[{-10}] = -0.25$$ $$N_r = \frac{1}{-(-0.25)} = \boxed{4}$$ With $u = -Kx + 4r$, a unit step reference produces unit steady-state output position.
Food for Thought
  • If a system is controllable, you can place its poles anywhere. Does this mean you can make a physical system respond arbitrarily fast? What stops you in practice?
    Theoretically yes — controllability says you can place eigenvalues arbitrarily far left. But poles far left require large gains $K$, which require large control signals $u = -Kx$. Real actuators saturate (a motor has a maximum torque; a valve can't open more than 100%). Once saturated, the system is no longer linear and the pole placement design is invalid. Additionally, large gains amplify sensor noise and excite unmodelled high-frequency dynamics (structural resonances, etc.) that the model ignores. In practice, achievable bandwidth is limited by actuator authority and model validity, not by controllability theory.
  • State feedback with $N_r$ eliminates steady-state error only for constant references and only if the model is exact. Why does adding an integrator state (integral action) fix both limitations?
    The integrator accumulates tracking error $\dot z = r - y$ over time. At steady state $\dot z = 0$ requires $r = y$ exactly — this holds regardless of the specific model parameters, because the integrator forces the error to zero by construction (internal model principle: a system that contains a model of the disturbance/reference dynamics can reject/track it). For time-varying references, the integral state adds a "memory" of past errors that naturally corrects for ramp-like drift. The cost is one extra state, which makes the design a $(n+1)$-dimensional pole-placement problem, and the risk of integrator windup under actuator saturation (same issue as PID).
  • For the double integrator, $K = [\omega_n^2,\; 2\zeta\omega_n]$. The gains grow with $\omega_n$. If you want the system to settle 10× faster, how do the gains scale? What does this imply for the maximum achievable bandwidth?
    Scaling $\omega_n \to 10\omega_n$: $k_1 = \omega_n^2 \to 100\omega_n^2$ (scales as $\omega_n^2$), $k_2 = 2\zeta\omega_n \to 20\zeta\omega_n$ (scales as $\omega_n$). The position gain grows by 100×, the velocity gain by 10×. The control signal at the initial state $x_0 = [1, 0]^T$ is $u = -k_1 \cdot 1 = -100 k_1$, a factor of 100 larger. If the actuator saturates at $|u| \leq u_{\max}$, the maximum bandwidth is approximately $\omega_n \leq \sqrt{u_{\max} / |x_{0,\max}|}$. Beyond this the linear design breaks down, and you'd need anti-windup or a nonlinear control strategy to manage saturation.
Common Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to check controllability first. If the system is not controllable, Ackermann's formula will either throw a singular-matrix error or, worse, silently return a wrong answer. Always verify rank$(\mathcal{C}) = n$ before proceeding.
  • Placing poles too aggressively. Choosing poles very far left increases $K$ dramatically. Large gains amplify sensor noise, cause saturation, and can destabilise the system through unmodelled high-frequency dynamics. LQR (Lesson 13) makes this tradeoff systematic.
  • Forgetting $N_r$. A common error: design $K$ to place poles correctly, then apply $u = -Kx + r$ without computing $N_r$. The system is stable but the output will not track the reference — it tracks $r$ divided by the closed-loop DC gain, which is generally not 1. Always compute $N_r$ or use integral augmentation.
  • Real poles ≠ no oscillation in the output. Even with all real closed-loop eigenvalues, if the system has zeros, the step response can exhibit oscillation or undershoot. Eigenvalue placement controls the poles, not the zeros — both shape the response.
  • Assuming full state is available. Pole placement requires $x(t)$ at every instant. If you only measure the output $y$, you cannot compute $Kx$ directly. The observer in Lesson 14 reconstructs $x$ from $y$, making output-feedback design possible.
