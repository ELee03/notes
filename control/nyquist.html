6 · Nyquist Criterion & Stability Margins
Frequency Response · Nyquist Plot · Encirclement · Gain Margin · Phase Margin
Notation used in this lesson
$L(j\omega)$Open-loop frequency response: $L(s)$ evaluated on $s=j\omega$
$|L(j\omega)|$Magnitude (gain) of open-loop at frequency $\omega$
$\angle L(j\omega)$Phase angle of open-loop at frequency $\omega$
$\omega_c$Gain crossover frequency: $|L(j\omega_c)| = 1$ (0 dB)
$\omega_\phi$Phase crossover frequency: $\angle L(j\omega_\phi) = -180°$
$GM$Gain margin: $GM = 1/|L(j\omega_\phi)|$ (how much gain increase causes instability)
$PM$Phase margin: $PM = 180° + \angle L(j\omega_c)$ (how much phase delay causes instability)
$N$Number of counterclockwise encirclements of $-1$ by the Nyquist plot
Routh-Hurwitz and root locus work from the characteristic polynomial — they require you to know $L(s)$ analytically. In practice, you often don't have a perfect plant model; you have measured frequency response data: sweep a sine wave through your system and record how the amplitude and phase change at each frequency. The Nyquist criterion extracts stability information from exactly this data. And it gives you something the other methods don't: a quantitative measure of how stable you are — the gain margin and phase margin. These are the two most practically important numbers in classical control design.
Frequency response
For a stable LTI system $G(s)$, the frequency response is the steady-state output to a sinusoidal input. If $u(t) = A\sin(\omega t)$, the steady-state output is: $$y_{ss}(t) = A\,|G(j\omega)|\,\sin\!\left(\omega t + \angle G(j\omega)\right)$$ The output has the same frequency but different amplitude and phase. $G(j\omega) = G(s)|_{s=j\omega}$ is a complex number at each frequency $\omega$.
The Bode plot (Lesson 3) is one representation: magnitude $|G(j\omega)|$ in dB and phase $\angle G(j\omega)$ in degrees, both plotted vs. $\log_{10}\omega$. The Nyquist plot is another: $G(j\omega)$ traced in the complex plane as $\omega$ goes from $0$ to $\infty$ (or $-\infty$ to $\infty$). Same data, different picture.
The Nyquist plot
The Nyquist plot of $L(s)$ is the image of the Nyquist contour (the imaginary axis from $-j\infty$ to $+j\infty$, with a small detour around any poles on the axis) under the mapping $s \mapsto L(s)$. For practical purposes, it's the polar plot of $L(j\omega)$ as $\omega$ goes from $0$ to $\infty$ (the upper semicircle), plus its complex conjugate reflection for $\omega < 0$.
The key feature: the critical point $-1 + j0$ on the real axis. If the Nyquist plot passes through $-1$, then $L(j\omega)=-1$ for some $\omega$, meaning $1+L(j\omega)=0$ — the closed-loop system has a pole on the imaginary axis (marginally stable). If the plot encircles $-1$, the system is unstable.
Nyquist plot for a stable Type-1 open-loop. Solid = $\omega > 0$, dashed = $\omega < 0$ (mirror). The plot does not encircle $-1$.
The Nyquist encirclement theorem
Cauchy's argument principle gives the mathematical foundation. For a contour that encircles no poles of $f(s)$, the number of times $f(s)$ encircles the origin equals the number of zeros of $f(s)$ inside the contour. Applied to $f(s) = 1+L(s)$ with the Nyquist contour:
Nyquist Stability Criterion. Let $P$ = number of open-loop poles of $L(s)$ in the right half-plane, and $N$ = number of counterclockwise (positive) encirclements of the point $-1$ by the Nyquist plot of $L(s)$. Then the number of closed-loop poles in the right half-plane is: $$Z = P - N$$ The closed-loop system is stable if and only if $Z = 0$, i.e., $N = P$.
In the most common case ($P = 0$: stable plant, no controller poles in RHP), the condition simplifies to: stable iff $N = 0$ (no encirclements of $-1$).
Encirclement direction convention: As $\omega$ increases from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$, a counterclockwise encirclement of $-1$ counts as $+1$; a clockwise encirclement counts as $-1$.
For an open-loop-stable plant ($P=0$): The Nyquist plot must not encircle $-1$ at all for the closed-loop to be stable. Intuitively, if the loop gain is exactly $-1$ at some frequency (magnitude = 1, phase = $-180°$), the feedback reinforces the signal at that frequency instead of canceling it — positive feedback at $\omega$ — and the output grows without bound.
Gain margin and phase margin
The margins quantify how close the Nyquist plot is to encircling $-1$. Two independent measures — one for gain uncertainty, one for phase uncertainty:
Gain margin (GM): At the phase crossover frequency $\omega_\phi$ (where $\angle L(j\omega_\phi) = -180°$), how much can the gain increase before the plot reaches $-1$? $$GM = \frac{1}{|L(j\omega_\phi)|} \quad \text{or in dB:} \quad GM_{dB} = -20\log_{10}|L(j\omega_\phi)|$$ For stability: $GM > 1$ ($GM_{dB} > 0$). Typical target: $GM \ge 2$ ($\ge 6$ dB).
Phase margin (PM): At the gain crossover frequency $\omega_c$ (where $|L(j\omega_c)| = 1$, i.e., 0 dB), how much additional phase lag would cause instability? $$PM = 180° + \angle L(j\omega_c)$$ For stability: $PM > 0$. Typical target: $PM \ge 30°$–$45°$.
Physical meaning of PM: A pure time delay $e^{-\tau s}$ contributes phase lag $-\omega\tau$ at frequency $\omega$. At the gain crossover, the phase lag is $-\omega_c \tau$. This equals the phase margin when $\omega_c \tau = PM$ in radians, so the maximum allowable delay before instability is $\tau_{max} = PM/\omega_c$. Phase margin is the control designer's primary robustness metric.
PM and damping ratio. For a standard second-order loop with $L(s) = \omega_n^2 / [s(s+2\zeta\omega_n)]$, there is an approximate relationship: $$PM \approx \arctan\!\left(\frac{2\zeta}{\sqrt{\sqrt{1+4\zeta^4}-2\zeta^2}}\right) \approx 100\zeta \text{ degrees (for } \zeta < 0.7\text{)}$$ So $PM = 30°$ corresponds to roughly $\zeta \approx 0.3$ (oscillatory); $PM = 60°$ corresponds to $\zeta \approx 0.6$ (well-damped). Targeting $PM \approx 45°$ typically gives a well-damped step response with modest overshoot.
Reading margins from the Bode plot
The Nyquist criterion is best understood from the Nyquist plot, but margins are most easily read from the Bode plot:
  • Find $\omega_c$ (gain crossover): the frequency where the magnitude plot crosses 0 dB. Phase margin = the phase at $\omega_c$, plus $180°$. Positive PM → stable at this gain.
  • Find $\omega_\phi$ (phase crossover): the frequency where the phase plot crosses $-180°$. Gain margin = the negative of the magnitude at $\omega_\phi$, in dB. Positive $GM_{dB}$ → stable at this gain.
A system with $\omega_c < \omega_\phi$ is typically stable (the gain has dropped below 0 dB before the phase reaches $-180°$). If $\omega_c > \omega_\phi$, the gain is still greater than unity when the phase passes through $-180°$ — the Nyquist plot encircles $-1$ — system is unstable.
Worked examples
Example 1 — Gain and phase margins for a Type-1 third-order loop
Compute the gain margin (GM) and phase margin (PM) for $L(s) = \dfrac{3}{s(s+1)(s+3)}$.
Phase crossover frequency $\omega_\phi$ (where $\angle L = -180°$): $$-90° - \arctan(\omega) - \arctan(\omega/3) = -180°$$ $$\arctan(\omega) + \arctan(\omega/3) = 90°$$ Using $\arctan(a)+\arctan(b) = 90°$ iff $ab = 1$: $\omega \cdot (\omega/3) = 1 \Rightarrow \omega_\phi = \sqrt{3}$ rad/s. Gain margin: $|L(j\sqrt{3})| = 3\big/\!\left[\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{1+3}\cdot\sqrt{9+3}\right] = 3\big/\!\left[\sqrt{3}\cdot 2\cdot 2\sqrt{3}\right] = 3/12 = \tfrac{1}{4}$. $$\text{GM} = \frac{1}{1/4} = 4 \quad (\approx 12 \text{ dB})$$ Gain crossover frequency $\omega_c$ (where $|L|=1$): Numerically $\omega_c \approx 0.74$ rad/s. Phase at $\omega_c$: $-90° - \arctan(0.74) - \arctan(0.247) \approx -90° - 36.4° - 13.9° = -140.3°$. $$\text{PM} = 180° - 140.3° = 39.7° \approx 40°$$ Both margins are acceptable (GM $\geq 6$ dB, PM $\approx 40°$), indicating reasonable robustness.
Example 2 — Delay margin: how much transport delay is tolerable?
Using the same loop $L(s) = 3/[s(s+1)(s+3)]$, add a transport delay $e^{-\tau s}$. Find the maximum delay $\tau_\text{max}$ before the system becomes unstable.
A pure delay $e^{-\tau s}$ has unit gain at all frequencies ($|e^{-j\omega\tau}| = 1$), so it does not change the gain crossover frequency $\omega_c \approx 0.74$ rad/s. It does add phase lag: $\angle e^{-j\omega\tau} = -\omega\tau$ radians. PM goes to zero when the additional lag at $\omega_c$ equals the existing PM: $$\omega_c \,\tau_\text{max} = \text{PM}_\text{original} \cdot \frac{\pi}{180°} = 39.7° \cdot \frac{\pi}{180°} \approx 0.693 \text{ rad}$$ $$\tau_\text{max} = \frac{0.693}{\omega_c} \approx \frac{0.693}{0.74} \approx 0.94 \text{ s}$$ Any transport delay above $\approx 0.94$ s will destabilize this loop. This is the delay margin — a direct measure of how sensitive the system is to communication or sensing latency.

Food for thought
Reflection questions
  1. The Nyquist criterion handles open-loop unstable plants by counting encirclements relative to the number of RHP open-loop poles ($Z=P-N$). Sketch qualitatively what the Nyquist plot must look like for a closed-loop system with one open-loop unstable pole ($P=1$) to be stable. How many counterclockwise encirclements of $-1$ are required?
    The Nyquist stability criterion: $Z = P - N$, where $Z$ = closed-loop RHP poles, $P$ = open-loop RHP poles, $N$ = net clockwise encirclements of $-1$. For stability, $Z = 0$, so we need $N = -P$ — that is, $P$ counterclockwise encirclements. For $P = 1$: the Nyquist plot must encircle $-1$ exactly once counterclockwise ($N = -1$, so $Z = 1 - (-1) = 0$). This looks like a loop that wraps around the $-1$ point from the left side — the plot starts outside the $-1$ point, goes around it counterclockwise, and ends outside again. A classic example: a single unstable pole $1/(s-1)$ in the loop requires active feedback to stabilize, and the stabilizing feedback produces exactly this wrapping behavior in the Nyquist plot.
  2. The interactive shows that higher $K$ reduces phase margin. But what if you could add phase to the loop — a "phase lead" network — at the gain crossover frequency? How would this change the Bode phase plot and the PM? What does this suggest about what a lead compensator (Lesson 8) would do?
    Adding a phase lead network $C(s) = (s+z)/(s+p)$ with $p > z > 0$ inserts positive phase around a frequency band centered near $\sqrt{zp}$. On the Bode phase plot, this appears as a "hump" of positive phase centered at the lead frequency. If this hump is placed at the gain crossover frequency $\omega_c$, the phase at crossover increases — directly increasing PM. This is exactly what a lead compensator (Lesson 8) does: it is a phase-lead network designed to add positive phase at $\omega_c$. The magnitude also increases slightly (the lead has a high-pass character), which shifts $\omega_c$ upward — this is why you have to account for the new $\omega_c$ when designing the compensator, not just the original one. The overall effect is: more phase margin, higher bandwidth, slightly more noise sensitivity.
  3. Gain and phase margins are two separate robustness measures. Is it possible to have a large GM but a small PM? A large PM but a small GM? What physical scenario would produce each? (Think about where on the Nyquist plot the system comes close to $-1$ in each case.)
    Large GM, small PM: The Nyquist plot crosses the negative real axis far from $-1$ (large GM), but passes close to the unit circle at some angle near $-180°$ (small PM). This happens when the gain rolls off quickly but the phase stays near $-180°$ for a wide frequency range. Example: a system with a very lightly damped resonant mode near $\omega_c$ — the magnitude peak at the resonance is moderate (GM OK) but the rapid phase drop through the resonance brings the phase close to $-180°$ near crossover (small PM). The system is robustly gain-stable but sensitive to phase perturbations. Large PM, small GM: The Nyquist plot has a comfortable phase margin at crossover (passes far from the negative real axis in terms of angle) but crosses the real axis very close to $-1$ at a higher frequency (small GM). This typically occurs with a high-gain system where the loop gain at the phase crossover frequency is close to 1. Lightly-damped higher-frequency resonances can cause this. In practice, both must be checked. The $M_s = \max|S(j\omega)|$ (sensitivity peak) is a single measure that reflects both simultaneously — it equals the inverse of the minimum distance from the Nyquist plot to the $-1$ point.
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Applying the simplified criterion to unstable plants. "Stable iff no encirclements" is only valid when $P=0$ (the open-loop is stable). If the plant has RHP poles (e.g., an inverted pendulum), you need the full criterion $Z = P - N = 0$, meaning you need exactly $P$ counterclockwise encirclements. Missing this distinction leads to declaring an unstable closed-loop as "stable."
  • Confusing gain crossover and phase crossover. Gain margin is measured at the phase crossover frequency (where phase $= -180°$), not at the gain crossover. Phase margin is measured at the gain crossover (where gain $= 0$ dB). The names are confusingly swapped — GM involves the phase crossover; PM involves the gain crossover.
  • Treating large margins as always better. Very large gain margin (far from instability) usually means low loop gain at all frequencies — the controller is very conservative. This typically means poor disturbance rejection and slow tracking. Margins are a robustness measure, not a performance measure. Typical good margins: GM $\approx 6$–$12$ dB, PM $\approx 40°$–$60°$.
  • Forgetting that the Bode margin reading assumes minimum-phase plants. The rule "stable iff $\omega_c < \omega_\phi$" (gain crosses 0 dB before phase crosses $-180°$) applies only to open-loop-stable, minimum-phase systems. For plants with right-half-plane zeros (non-minimum phase) or pure time delays, the Bode test can give a wrong answer — use the full Nyquist criterion instead.
Connects to

  • Lesson 8 (Lead-Lag Compensators) — Lead compensators are designed specifically to increase phase margin at the gain crossover frequency. The design procedure uses the Bode plot: measure how much phase you need to add, then design a compensator zero-pole pair that provides it.
