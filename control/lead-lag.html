Lead-Lag Compensators
Frequency-Domain Design · Phase Lead · Steady-State Accuracy · Bandwidth Shaping
You've seen that adding gain increases bandwidth and improves steady-state error, but degrades phase margin. PID fixes steady-state error unconditionally — but only by adding an integrator that brings windup risk and tuning complexity. Is there a more surgical option? Lead-lag compensators reshape the frequency response directly: a lead section injects positive phase exactly where you need it (near the gain crossover), while a lag section quietly boosts DC gain without touching the bandwidth. No integrator, no windup, and the design procedure is a short series of Bode calculations.
The compensator idea
Every closed-loop property we care about — phase margin, bandwidth, steady-state error — is encoded in the open-loop transfer function $L(s) = C(s)G(s)$. We've been using $C(s) = K$ (pure gain), which scales the Bode magnitude uniformly. Gain alone can move the gain crossover frequency up or down, but it cannot change the shape of the phase curve. That's the job of a dynamic compensator.
A compensator is a transfer function $C(s)$ with non-trivial dynamics — poles and zeros placed deliberately to reshape the magnitude and phase of $L(j\omega)$. The two canonical shapes are:
  • Phase lead: a zero-pole pair $C(s) = (s+z)/(s+p)$ with $p > z > 0$ contributes positive (leading) phase in the frequency band between $z$ and $p$. Place that band around $\omega_c$ to boost phase margin and bandwidth.
  • Phase lag: the same form with $0 < p < z$ attenuates high frequencies relative to DC. The DC gain is $z/p > 1$, boosting the low-frequency loop gain and improving steady-state accuracy.
These can be cascaded — a lead section to fix the phase margin, a lag section to fix the steady-state error — giving the lead-lag compensator. Each section targets a different part of the frequency axis, so they can be designed nearly independently.
The lead compensator
The lead compensator has the form:
$$C_\text{lead}(s) = K_c \frac{s + z}{s + p}, \qquad p > z > 0$$
Define $\alpha = z/p < 1$ (the ratio of zero to pole location). The compensator can be written in a normalized form that makes its Bode behavior explicit:
$$C_\text{lead}(s) = K_c\alpha \cdot \frac{Ts + 1}{\alpha T s + 1}, \qquad T = \frac{1}{z}$$
Bode magnitude: At low frequencies ($\omega \ll z$), $|C| \approx K_c$. Between $z$ and $p$, the zero adds +20 dB/dec but the pole hasn't contributed yet, so the magnitude rises. Above $p$, both contribute and the gain flattens at $K_c/\alpha$. The net high-frequency boost is a factor of $p/z = 1/\alpha > 1$.
Bode phase: Both the zero and the pole are in the left half-plane, so each contributes phase in a specific range: the zero adds $+90°$ total (ramping from $0°$ at low freq to $+90°$ at high freq), and the pole subtracts $90°$. But their break frequencies differ ($z < p$), so there's a frequency band where the zero has contributed phase but the pole hasn't yet cancelled it. This produces a positive phase hump between $z$ and $p$.
The maximum phase contribution of a lead compensator and the frequency at which it occurs are: $$\phi_\text{max} = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{1 - \alpha}{1 + \alpha}\right), \qquad \omega_\text{max} = \sqrt{zp} = \frac{1}{T\sqrt{\alpha}}$$ The magnitude of $C_\text{lead}$ at $\omega_\text{max}$ (with $K_c = 1$) is $1/\sqrt{\alpha} > 1$.
The table below shows what $\alpha$ value is needed for common phase-lead targets. Note how quickly $\alpha$ approaches zero — very aggressive lead ($\alpha < 0.1$) comes with a large magnitude hump that can push the new gain crossover into a region with less phase than expected.

Lead design procedure
The strategy: choose $\alpha$ to provide the needed phase boost, then position $\omega_\text{max}$ so the peak phase coincides with the new gain crossover.
1
Determine required phase addition. Compute the uncompensated phase margin $PM_0$ and the desired PM. Add 5°–10° extra to account for the phase shift from moving the crossover: $$\phi_\text{max} = PM_\text{desired} - PM_0 + \delta, \qquad \delta \approx 5°\text{–}10°$$
2
Compute $\alpha$: $$\alpha = \frac{1 - \sin\phi_\text{max}}{1 + \sin\phi_\text{max}}$$ If $\alpha < 0.1$ (more than about 55° of lead), a single lead stage is likely insufficient — use two cascaded lead sections.
3
Find $\omega_\text{max}$ — the new gain crossover frequency. With $K_c = 1$, the compensator magnitude at $\omega_\text{max}$ is $1/\sqrt{\alpha}$. For this to be a crossover ($|C \cdot G| = 1$), we need $|KG(j\omega_\text{max})| = \sqrt{\alpha}$. On the Bode magnitude plot, find the frequency where the uncompensated loop is $20\log_{10}(1/\sqrt{\alpha}) = -10\log_{10}(1/\alpha)$ dB below 0 dB.
4
Compute the zero and pole: $$T = \frac{1}{\omega_\text{max}\sqrt{\alpha}}, \qquad z = \frac{1}{T} = \omega_\text{max}\sqrt{\alpha}, \qquad p = \frac{z}{\alpha} = \frac{\omega_\text{max}}{\sqrt{\alpha}}$$
5
Adjust $K_c$ if needed to meet a specific velocity constant or DC gain requirement. This may shift the crossover slightly — verify.
6
Verify the compensated PM by computing $\angle L(j\omega_c)$ for the full compensated loop. Iterate if the result differs from the target by more than a few degrees.
Why add 5°–10° extra in step 1? Moving the gain crossover to a higher frequency exposes the plant's phase curve at a frequency where it's slightly more negative than at the original $\omega_{c0}$. The 5°–10° buffer accounts for this. Neglecting it is the single most common reason a designed compensator falls short of the PM target when implemented.
The lag compensator
The lag compensator has the same algebraic form but with the zero and pole swapped:
$$C_\text{lag}(s) = \frac{s + z}{s + p}, \qquad 0 < p < z, \qquad \beta = \frac{z}{p} > 1$$
Bode magnitude: At $\omega \to 0$ the gain is $z/p = \beta > 1$ (above 1). Between $p$ and $z$ it rolls off at $-20$ dB/dec, then flattens at 1 above $z$. The lag compensator is a low-pass filter: it passes DC at gain $\beta$ and high frequencies at gain 1.
Bode phase: The phase is 0° at DC, becomes negative (lagging) in the band between $p$ and $z$, reaching its most negative value at $\omega_\text{mid} = \sqrt{pz}$, and returning to 0° above $z$.
The phase lag is the danger — if it falls at or near $\omega_c$, it degrades the phase margin. The design rule is to place both break frequencies well below $\omega_c$ so the phase contribution at crossover is negligible (typically < 5°). A safe choice: $z \approx \omega_c / 10$.
Steady-state effect of a lag compensator. The lag compensator multiplies the error constant by $\beta$:
  • Type 0 system: position constant $K_p \to \beta K_p$, step SSE reduces by factor $\approx\beta$.
  • Type 1 system: velocity constant $K_v \to \beta K_v$, ramp SSE reduces by factor $\beta$.
This improvement happens because the lag compensator boosts the DC loop gain by $\beta$ without moving the crossover frequency or degrading PM significantly.
The intuition: the lag compensator is doing what adding extra gain does (boosting DC gain → better SSE), but without the full gain boost at all frequencies. By attenuating the HF region back down to unity, it avoids the bandwidth increase and PM degradation that a flat gain boost would cause. The "free lunch" region is DC to $p$; you pay nothing in PM because the phase effect is confined well below $\omega_c$.
Lag design procedure
1
Check that PM is already acceptable. The lag compensator does not improve PM — it slightly degrades it. If PM is insufficient, add a lead section first (or use PID).
2
Determine $\beta$ from the steady-state requirement. If you need the error constant to increase by a factor of $\beta$, that's your target ratio $z/p$.
3
Place the zero a decade below the crossover frequency: $z = \omega_c / 10$. Then compute $p = z / \beta$.
4
Account for the PM penalty. The lag contributes a phase of approximately $\arctan(\omega_c/z) - \arctan(\omega_c/p)$ (negative) at $\omega_c$. With $z = \omega_c/10$: $\arctan(10) - \arctan(10\beta) \approx -84° + 84° \approx -5°$ for large $\beta$. This is small and predictable — just verify it doesn't push PM below the requirement.
5
Large $\beta$ warning: for $\beta > 10$, the pole $p = z/\beta$ moves very close to the origin. Physically this creates a very slow mode in the closed loop that can make transient performance sluggish even if PM is fine. If $\beta$ needs to be large, consider adding an integrator (PID) instead.
Combined lead-lag
When a plant needs both better PM and better steady-state accuracy, the two compensators are cascaded:
$$C(s) = K_c \cdot \underbrace{\frac{s+z_1}{s+p_1}}_\text{lead: $p_1>z_1$} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{s+z_2}{s+p_2}}_\text{lag: $p_2
The lead section operates near $\omega_c$ ($z_1$ and $p_1$ close to crossover), while the lag section operates well below $\omega_c$ ($z_2$ and $p_2$ far to the left). Because the two sections occupy different frequency bands, their effects are nearly decoupled, and you can design them sequentially:
  1. Design the lead section to achieve the desired PM and bandwidth, treating the lag as absent. Compute $z_1, p_1$.
  2. Design the lag section to achieve the desired error constant improvement, treating the lead as a flat gain of $K_c \approx 1$. Compute $z_2, p_2$.
  3. Verify the combined system. The lead introduces a slight gain boost at $\omega_c$ (magnitude $\approx 1/\sqrt{\alpha}$ there), so the lag's effect on PM is usually still negligible. Iterate once if needed.
The combined lead-lag is roughly analogous to the "PD + PI" decomposition of a full PID, but the implementation is simpler and avoids the integrator: two stable RC networks in cascade are sufficient.
Comparison to PID
Both PID and lead-lag are classical controller structures designed in the frequency domain. Their key structural difference is the integrator: PID has a pole at the origin; lead-lag does not.
Property Lead Lag Lead-Lag PID
Improves phase margin Yes Slight degradation Yes (lead part) Yes (D term)
Increases bandwidth Yes No Yes (lead part) Yes (D term)
Improves SSE to step No (type unchanged) Yes (factor $\beta$) Yes (lag part) Yes (eliminates it)
Eliminates SSE to ramp No No No Yes (with I action, type 0→1)
Windup risk None None None Yes (needs anti-windup)
Implementation Passive RC, 2 components Passive RC, 2 components Two RC stages Op-amp circuit or digital
Tuning Systematic (Bode steps) Systematic (choose $\beta, z$) Sequential design Ziegler-Nichols or iterative
When to use each: Use a lead compensator when the plant has acceptable steady-state performance but insufficient PM or bandwidth. Use a lag when PM is already fine but SSE is too large. Use PID when you need guaranteed zero SSE (integrator action) or when the plant has no existing integrators and the lag compensator can't deliver the required gain boost. Lead-lag is the middle ground: cleaner than PID, more powerful than either alone.
One practical advantage of lead-lag in analog implementations: the compensator can be realized with a single passive RC network (lead) or a voltage divider plus capacitor (lag). PID requires active circuitry for the differentiator. For motor drives, hydraulic servos, and other high-reliability systems, the simplicity of the passive realization is a genuine benefit.

Worked examples
Example 1 — Lead design for a second-order plant.
Plant: $G(s) = 1/((s+1)(s+5))$, K = 15. Required: PM $\geq$ 50°.
Step 1 — Uncompensated system. $L_0(s) = 15/((s+1)(s+5))$. DC gain = $15/5 = 3$ (9.5 dB). Phase: $\angle L_0(j\omega) = -\arctan(\omega) - \arctan(\omega/5)$. Find $\omega_{c0}$: $|L_0(j\omega)| = 15/\sqrt{(\omega^2+1)(\omega^2+25)} = 1$ → $(\omega^2+1)(\omega^2+25) = 225$. At $\omega = 2$: $(5)(29) = 145 \neq 225$. At $\omega = 1.5$: $(3.25)(27.25) = 88.6 \neq 225$. At $\omega = 2.5$: $(7.25)(31.25) = 226.6 \approx 225$. So $\omega_{c0} \approx 2.49$ rad/s. Phase at $\omega_{c0} = 2.49$: $-\arctan(2.49) - \arctan(0.498) = -68.2° - 26.5° = -94.7°$. PM$_0$ = $180° - 94.7° = 85.3°$. (Very comfortable — the plant is inherently well-damped.)
Result: PM = 85.3° — already above 50°. No lead compensator is needed here. This example shows why you must always compute PM first before designing a compensator. Adding a lead would only increase bandwidth and HF gain with no benefit.
Example 2 — Lead design for a type-1 plant.
Plant: $G(s) = 1/(s(s+2)(s+8))$, $K = 20$. Required: PM $\geq 45°$.
Uncompensated analysis. $L_0(s) = 20/(s(s+2)(s+8))$. Velocity constant $K_v = \lim_{s\to 0}\, s L_0(s) = 20/16 = 1.25$. $|L_0(j\omega)| = 20 / (\omega\sqrt{\omega^2+4}\sqrt{\omega^2+64})$. Searching for $|L_0| = 1$: at $\omega = 0.8$: $20/(0.8 \cdot \sqrt{4.64} \cdot \sqrt{64.64}) \approx 20/(0.8 \cdot 2.154 \cdot 8.040) \approx 20/13.85 \approx 1.44$. At $\omega = 1.0$: $20/(1 \cdot \sqrt{5} \cdot \sqrt{65}) \approx 20/18.03 = 1.11$. At $\omega = 1.1$: $20/(1.1 \cdot \sqrt{5.21} \cdot \sqrt{65.21}) \approx 20/20.2 = 0.99$. So $\omega_{c0} \approx 1.08$ rad/s. Phase at $\omega = 1.08$: $-90° - \arctan(1.08/2) - \arctan(1.08/8) = -90° - 28.3° - 7.7° = -126°$. PM$_0 = 54°$. Already meets 45° — but let's say we want 60° and need more PM.
Lead design targeting PM = 60°.
Need $\phi_\text{max} = 60° - 54° + 8° = 14°$ (with 8° extra). $\alpha = (1 - \sin 14°)/(1 + \sin 14°) = (1 - 0.242)/(1 + 0.242) = 0.758/1.242 = 0.610$. Need $|L_0(j\omega_\text{max})| = \sqrt{0.610} = 0.781$, i.e., $-2.1$ dB. At $\omega = 0.9$: $|L_0| \approx 1.22$; at $\omega = 1.1$: $|L_0| \approx 0.99$; at $\omega = 1.15$: $\approx 0.90$; at $\omega = 1.3$: $\approx 0.74$. Interpolating: $\omega_\text{max} \approx 1.22$ rad/s. $T = 1/(1.22\sqrt{0.610}) = 1/(1.22 \times 0.781) = 1.05$. $z = 1/T = 0.952$, $p = z/\alpha = 0.952/0.610 = 1.56$. Compensator: $C(s) = (s + 0.95)/(s + 1.56)$. Verify: at $\omega = 1.22$, $\angle C = \arctan(1.22/0.95) - \arctan(1.22/1.56) = \arctan(1.28) - \arctan(0.782) = 52.0° - 38.0° = 14°$ ✓. Compensated PM $\approx 54° + 14° - (\text{small correction}) \approx 60°$.
Example 3 — Lag design to improve velocity constant.
Plant: $G(s) = 4/(s(s+4))$ with $K = 1$. Current $K_v = \lim_{s\to 0} s \cdot G(s) = 4/4 = 1$ (rad/s steady-state ramp error = 1). Required: $K_v \geq 5$, PM unchanged.
Uncompensated. $|L_0(j\omega)| = 4/(\omega\sqrt{\omega^2+16})$. At $\omega = 0.97$: $4/(0.97\sqrt{16.94}) \approx 4/(0.97 \times 4.116) = 1.00$. So $\omega_{c0} \approx 0.97$ rad/s. PM$_0 = 180° - 90° - \arctan(0.97/4) = 90° - 13.6° = 76.4°$. Excellent PM.
Lag design. Need $\beta = K_v^\text{new}/K_v^\text{old} = 5/1 = 5$. Place zero at $z = \omega_{c0}/10 = 0.097$. Pole at $p = z/\beta = 0.097/5 = 0.0194$. Compensator: $C_\text{lag}(s) = (s + 0.097)/(s + 0.0194)$. PM check. At $\omega = 0.97$, the lag contributes: $\angle C = \arctan(0.97/0.097) - \arctan(0.97/0.0194) = \arctan(10) - \arctan(50)$ $= 84.3° - 88.9° = -4.6°$. New PM $\approx 76.4° - 4.6° = 71.8°$ — still excellent, $K_v$ now = 5. ✓
Food for thought
  1. A lead compensator increases bandwidth. Higher bandwidth means the system responds faster, but it also means it responds to high-frequency noise faster. When does increasing bandwidth become counterproductive, and how does this shape the practical upper limit on $\phi_\text{max}$ (i.e., how small $\alpha$ can be)?
  2. A lag compensator boosts DC gain by $\beta$ relative to HF gain. Why doesn't this simply allow you to substitute it for a gain increase of $\beta$? What does a flat gain increase of $\beta$ do that the lag compensator does not?
  3. Consider a plant with a RHP zero (non-minimum phase). The phase of a RHP zero is $+180° - \arctan(\omega/z)$, which is negative (lagging) — it removes phase as frequency increases. How does this constrain the achievable phase margin and bandwidth, and why do both lead and lag compensators become much less effective in this case?
Common pitfalls
  • Forgetting the phase correction factor. Moving the gain crossover to $\omega_\text{max} > \omega_{c0}$ exposes a slightly more negative plant phase at the new crossover. Skipping the 5°–10° buffer in step 1 routinely results in a compensated PM 5–10° below target. Always verify the compensated PM numerically.
  • Using a lag to improve an already-low PM. The lag compensator cannot add phase margin — it only degrades it (slightly). If PM is already borderline, adding a lag pushes it lower. The right tool is lead, PD, or a full PID.
  • Placing the lag break frequencies too close to $\omega_c$. The "decade below" rule ($z = \omega_c/10$) is not conservative paranoia — it's the threshold below which the phase contribution at $\omega_c$ stays small (< 6°). Placing $z$ at $\omega_c/3$, say, can degrade PM by 15°–20°, defeating the purpose of the design.
  • Confusing lead and lag pole/zero ordering. Lead: $p > z$ (pole further left, zero closer to origin). Lag: $p < z$ (pole closer to origin, zero further left). Swapping them reverses the effect — you'd add phase lag where you wanted lead.
  • Expecting a lag compensator to eliminate SSE from a type-0 plant. A lag compensator multiplies the position constant by $\beta$ — it reduces SSE but does not make it zero. Only an integrator (PID I action) eliminates type-0 steady-state error to a step entirely.
