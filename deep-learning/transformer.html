6 · The Transformer
Scaled Dot-Product Attention · Multi-Head Attention · Positional Encoding · Encoder · Decoder · Masked Attention
In 2017, Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, Uszkoreit, Jones, Gomez, Kaiser, and Polosukhin published "Attention Is All You Need." The title was a provocation: recurrence, they argued, was unnecessary. Convolutions were unnecessary. The entire model could be built from attention and feed-forward layers, stacked identically. The result was the Transformer — an architecture that achieved state of the art in machine translation with a fraction of the training time, and that would go on to underpin every major language model released in the seven years that followed: BERT, GPT-2/3/4, T5, LLaMA, Claude, Gemini, and GPT-4o are all Transformers. This lesson builds the Transformer from scratch, component by component, exactly as Vaswani et al. specified it. By the end you will be able to read the original paper directly.
The paper
"Attention Is All You Need" targets machine translation: translating a source sentence into a target sentence of a different language. The prior state of the art (Wu et al. 2016's Google Neural Machine Translation system) used deep LSTM encoder-decoders with attention. The Transformer matches and exceeds its quality on WMT English-German and English-French benchmarks while training 8× faster. The key properties of the Transformer that make this possible:
  • No recurrence. Processing is fully parallel across sequence positions. All positions are processed simultaneously — no sequential dependency.
  • Constant path length. Any two positions can interact directly in a single layer via attention. In an RNN, positions $i$ and $j$ must communicate through $|i - j|$ hidden state transitions — a path length of $O(n)$. In the Transformer, the path length is $O(1)$, regardless of distance.
  • Stacked identical blocks. The architecture is clean and regularised: stack $N$ identical encoder blocks and $N$ identical decoder blocks. Each block contains the same types of sublayers. This simplicity made scaling straightforward.
Architecture overview
The Transformer follows the encoder-decoder structure from seq2seq:
  • Encoder: reads the full source sentence and produces a sequence of contextualised representations — one vector per source token. All source tokens are processed in parallel.
  • Decoder: generates the target sentence one token at a time. At each step it attends to the encoder's output (cross-attention) and to the previously generated tokens (causal self-attention).

Scaled dot-product attention
The atomic operation in the Transformer (derived fully in Lesson 5). Summary here for reference: $$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$ Inputs: $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n_q \times d_k}$, $K \in \mathbb{R}^{n_k \times d_k}$, $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n_k \times d_v}$. Output: $\mathbb{R}^{n_q \times d_v}$ — one contextualised vector per query position. The $n_q \times n_k$ matrix $QK^\top / \sqrt{d_k}$ gives all pairwise compatibility scores; softmax normalises each row to a distribution over keys; that distribution weights the values.
Multi-head attention
A single attention head computes one weighted mixture. But a sentence has many simultaneous relationships: syntactic dependencies, semantic similarity, coreference, positional adjacency, topic coherence. Multi-head attention runs $h$ attention heads in parallel, each with its own projections, then concatenates and projects the results:
Multi-head attention — full specification

For each head $i \in \{1, \ldots, h\}$, define projection matrices: $W_i^Q \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_k}$, $W_i^K \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_k}$, $W_i^V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_v}$. For the base model: $d_k = d_v = d_{\text{model}}/h = 64$.

Compute each head independently:

$$\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(Q W_i^Q,\; K W_i^K,\; V W_i^V)$$

Each head is $\in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_v}$.

Concatenate all heads along the feature dimension:

$$\text{MultiHead}(Q, K, V) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h)\, W^O$$

where $W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{h d_v \times d_{\text{model}}}$ is a final output projection. The concatenated vector is $\in \mathbb{R}^{n \times hd_v} = \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\text{model}}}$; the output projection maps it back to $\mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\text{model}}}$.

Parameter count for multi-head attention:

  • Q projections: $h \cdot d_{\text{model}} \cdot d_k = h \cdot d_{\text{model}} \cdot (d_{\text{model}}/h) = d_{\text{model}}^2$
  • K projections: $d_{\text{model}}^2$ (same reasoning)
  • V projections: $d_{\text{model}}^2$
  • Output projection $W^O$: $h d_v \cdot d_{\text{model}} = d_{\text{model}}^2$
  • Total: $4 d_{\text{model}}^2 = 4 \times 512^2 \approx 1.05\text{M}$ per attention layer

Computational cost. Each of the $h$ heads independently computes $QK^\top$, where $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k}$ and $K \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k}$. The $h$ heads together compute the same total as one attention with $d_k \cdot h = d_{\text{model}}$. Cost: $O(n^2 d_{\text{model}})$ for the attention weights plus $O(n d_{\text{model}}^2)$ for the projections. For $n < d_{\text{model}}$ (short sequences), projections dominate; for $n > d_{\text{model}}$ (long sequences), the $n^2$ attention term dominates.

What different heads learn. Visualisations of trained Transformers (Vaswani et al. 2017, Clark et al. 2019, Kovaleva et al. 2019) reveal that different attention heads specialise. Some heads track syntactic dependencies (subject-verb agreement). Some head track positional adjacency (attend to the immediate neighbour). Some track coreference. Some are "global" heads that attend uniformly across all positions. No two heads are identical — the different projections push them to different subspaces and hence different relationship types. This specialisation emerges from gradient descent without any supervision about what each head should attend to.
Positional encoding
Attention is permutation-equivariant: if you shuffle the input sequence, the output is shuffled in exactly the same way. In other words, there is no notion of order in the attention mechanism itself. The model cannot distinguish "dog bites man" from "man bites dog" based on attention alone. This is in stark contrast to RNNs, which process tokens sequentially and therefore implicitly encode position via recurrence.
The fix: add a positional encoding to each input embedding before the first layer. The positional encoding is a vector of the same dimension $d_{\text{model}}$ that uniquely identifies each position. Vaswani et al. use fixed sine and cosine encodings:
Sinusoidal positional encoding

For position $\text{pos} \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$ and dimension $i \in \{0, 1, \ldots, d_{\text{model}}-1\}$:

$$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i) = \sin\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$$ $$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i+1) = \cos\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$$

Even dimensions use sine; odd dimensions use cosine. The frequency decreases geometrically with dimension index: dimension 0 has the highest frequency (period $2\pi \approx 6.28$ steps), dimension $d_{\text{model}} - 2$ has the lowest (period $10000 \cdot 2\pi \approx 62{,}832$ steps).

The input to the first layer is the sum of the word embedding and the positional encoding: $$\mathbf{x}'_{\text{pos}} = \mathbf{x}_{\text{pos}} + \text{PE}(\text{pos})$$

Why sinusoids? Three properties make them useful:

  1. Unique per position: the vector $\text{PE}(\text{pos}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$ is distinct for every position, providing an identity.
  2. Relative position information: by the angle addition formula, $\text{PE}(\text{pos} + k)$ is a fixed linear function of $\text{PE}(\text{pos})$ for any fixed offset $k$. Specifically: $\sin(\text{pos} + k) = \sin(\text{pos})\cos(k) + \cos(\text{pos})\sin(k)$ — a linear combination of $\sin(\text{pos})$ and $\cos(\text{pos})$. This means the model can, in principle, learn to attend to "position $+k$ relative to position $\text{pos}$" via linear operations on the encodings.
  3. Generalises to unseen lengths: sinusoids are defined for any position, so the model can generalise to sequences longer than those seen during training (within limits).

Alternative: learned positional embeddings. Instead of fixed sinusoids, learn a separate embedding for each position (a lookup table, like word embeddings). Vaswani et al. find nearly identical results for both approaches. GPT-2, BERT, and many subsequent models use learned positional embeddings for simplicity. More recent models use relative positional encodings (RoPE, ALiBi) which embed position information directly into the attention score computation rather than the input, giving better length generalisation — but that is beyond the original Transformer specification.

Encoder block
The Transformer encoder is a stack of $N = 6$ identical blocks. Each block contains exactly two sublayers:
Transformer encoder block

Input to block $l$: $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\text{model}}}$ (one row per token).

Sublayer 1 — Multi-head self-attention with residual + LayerNorm:

$$Z = X + \text{Dropout}\big(\text{MultiHead}(X, X, X)\big)$$ $$X' = \text{LayerNorm}(Z)$$

The queries, keys, and values all come from the same matrix $X$ — this is self-attention. Every token attends to every other token in the source sequence.

Sublayer 2 — Position-wise feed-forward network with residual + LayerNorm:

$$F = X' + \text{Dropout}\big(\text{FFN}(X')\big)$$ $$X'' = \text{LayerNorm}(F)$$

where the FFN is applied identically and independently to each position: $$\text{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) = W_2 \,\text{ReLU}(W_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2$$ with $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{ff} \times d_{\text{model}}}$, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_{ff}}$, $d_{ff} = 2048$. The FFN expands dimension 4× (to 2048), applies ReLU, then projects back to $d_{\text{model}} = 512$.

Parameter count per encoder block:

  • Multi-head attention: $4 d_{\text{model}}^2 = 4 \times 512^2 \approx 1.05\text{M}$
  • FFN: $2 \times d_{\text{model}} \times d_{ff} = 2 \times 512 \times 2048 \approx 2.10\text{M}$
  • LayerNorm (2×): $2 \times 2 \times d_{\text{model}} = 2048$ parameters (negligible)
  • Total per block: ≈3.15M. For 6 blocks: ≈18.9M
Why LayerNorm, not BatchNorm? BatchNorm normalises across the batch dimension: for a given feature, it normalises using the batch mean and variance. This requires a large batch size (statistics are noisy for small batches) and doesn't work at test time for single-example inference unless running statistics are maintained. LayerNorm normalises across the feature dimension: for a given token, it normalises across the $d_{\text{model}}$ features using that token's own mean and variance. It is independent of batch size and works identically at train and test time. For a token representation $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$: $$\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \gamma \odot \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sigma + \epsilon} + \beta$$ where $\mu = \frac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i$ and $\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{d}\sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2$ are scalar statistics of that specific token's representation, and $\gamma, \beta \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are learned scale and shift parameters.
The "Add & Norm" shorthand. In the paper, each sublayer is described as $\text{LayerNorm}(x + \text{Sublayer}(x))$. This is the post-layer norm convention: apply the sublayer, add the residual, then normalise. Modern practice often uses pre-layer norm: $x + \text{Sublayer}(\text{LayerNorm}(x))$ — apply LayerNorm to the input before the sublayer. Pre-LN trains more stably for very deep models (GPT-2 uses pre-LN) because the gradient flows through the residual stream without passing through the LayerNorm normalisation — recall the analysis of pre-activation ResNets from Lesson 1.
Decoder block
The decoder is also a stack of $N = 6$ identical blocks, but each block has three sublayers instead of two:
Transformer decoder block

Input to decoder block $l$: $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d_{\text{model}}}$ (the target tokens seen so far), and encoder output $\text{Enc} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\text{model}}}$.

Sublayer 1 — Masked causal self-attention:

$$Z_1 = Y + \text{Dropout}\big(\text{MaskedMultiHead}(Y, Y, Y)\big)$$ $$Y_1 = \text{LayerNorm}(Z_1)$$

"Masked" means the attention score matrix has the upper triangle set to $-\infty$ before softmax — token $t$ cannot attend to tokens at positions $t+1, t+2, \ldots$ This enforces autoregressive generation: the decoder can only use what it has already generated.

Sublayer 2 — Cross-attention (encoder-decoder attention):

$$Z_2 = Y_1 + \text{Dropout}\big(\text{MultiHead}(Y_1, \text{Enc}, \text{Enc})\big)$$ $$Y_2 = \text{LayerNorm}(Z_2)$$

Queries come from the decoder state $Y_1$; keys and values come from the encoder output $\text{Enc}$. This is where the decoder "reads" the source sentence: each decoder position selectively attends to source encoder positions, deciding which source words are relevant to generating the current target word. There is no causal mask here — the decoder can attend to any source position.

Sublayer 3 — Feed-forward (same as encoder):

$$Z_3 = Y_2 + \text{Dropout}\big(\text{FFN}(Y_2)\big)$$ $$Y_3 = \text{LayerNorm}(Z_3)$$

Identical to the encoder FFN: position-wise, independent across positions, $d_{\text{model}} \to d_{ff} \to d_{\text{model}}$.

Parameter count per decoder block: Three attention sublayers (causal self-attention, cross-attention, each $\approx 1.05\text{M}$) + FFN ($\approx 2.10\text{M}$) = ≈4.2M. For 6 blocks: ≈25.2M.

Intuition for the three sublayers.
  1. Causal self-attention: integrate information from all previously generated target tokens. "Given what I've generated so far, what context do I have?"
  2. Cross-attention: use that context as a query to retrieve relevant information from the source. "Given my current decoding context, which source tokens are relevant?"
  3. FFN: process the retrieved information with position-wise transformations. "Given what I retrieved, what is the right output?"
Full architecture
Putting it all together:
  1. Input embedding + positional encoding: tokenise the source sentence into token IDs, look up a learned embedding for each ($W_{\text{emb}} \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d_{\text{model}}}$, where $V$ is vocabulary size), add sinusoidal positional encodings. In the paper, the embedding weights are also scaled: $\mathbf{e} \leftarrow \mathbf{e} \sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$ to keep the embedding scale comparable to the positional encoding.
  2. Encoder stack: pass the embedded source sequence through $N = 6$ encoder blocks. Output: contextualised source representations $\text{Enc} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\text{model}}}$.
  3. Decoder stack: at each generation step $t$, pass the target tokens generated so far ($y_0, \ldots, y_{t-1}$, embedded and position-encoded) through $N = 6$ decoder blocks, with each block receiving $\text{Enc}$ for cross-attention.
  4. Output projection: project the decoder's last-layer output at position $t$ to vocabulary logits: $W_{\text{out}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times V}$ (in the paper, $W_{\text{out}} = W_{\text{emb}}^\top$ — the embedding and output weight matrices are tied, halving the parameter count here). Apply softmax to get $p(y_t \mid y_{
  5. Training: teacher forcing — all $m$ decoder positions are processed simultaneously with the causal mask, producing all $m$ target token predictions in one forward pass. Cross-entropy loss averaged over all target positions.
Total parameter count (base model): Encoder 6 blocks ≈18.9M + Decoder 6 blocks ≈25.2M + Embedding $37,000 \times 512 \approx 19\text{M}$ (shared with output projection) ≈ 65M total. This is small by modern standards — GPT-2 has 1.5B parameters, GPT-3 has 175B, LLaMA-3-70B has 70B — but the architecture is identical in principle.
Why Transformers beat RNNs
Vaswani et al. provide a systematic comparison across four criteria:
Criterion Self-attention RNN Conv ($k$ window)
Layer complexity $O(n^2 d)$ $O(n d^2)$ $O(n k d^2)$
Sequential ops $O(1)$ $O(n)$ $O(1)$
Max path length $O(1)$ $O(n)$ $O(n/k)$
Long-range dependencies direct in 1 layer through recurrence ($n$ steps) through stacking ($n/k$ layers)
Path length is the key metric. The path between two positions $i$ and $j$ is the number of operations through which the gradient must flow for position $i$ to affect position $j$'s prediction. In an RNN, information at position 1 must travel through $n-1$ hidden state transitions to reach position $n$ — path length $O(n)$. In the Transformer, any two positions interact directly in one attention operation regardless of distance — path length $O(1)$.
Short path length has two benefits: (1) gradient signal is stronger — no exponential decay through $n$ recurrent steps; (2) optimization is easier — the model learns what to attend to rather than learning to preserve information through $n$ recurrent state transitions.
Parallelism. Because the Transformer has $O(1)$ sequential operations (all positions are processed simultaneously in each layer), it can exploit GPU/TPU parallelism fully. The RNN's $O(n)$ sequential operations are a fundamental bottleneck — you cannot compute $\mathbf{h}_t$ until $\mathbf{h}_{t-1}$ is ready. For $n = 100$ tokens on a GPU with 1000 parallel cores, the RNN uses 1 core per step and takes 100 time steps; the Transformer uses all 1000 cores simultaneously and completes in a handful of matrix multiplications. This difference in training speed is why GPT-3 (175B parameters) can be trained at all — training an equivalently sized LSTM would be prohibitively slow.
Training details
Vaswani et al. introduce several training choices that matter for Transformer stability:
Adam optimiser with custom learning rate schedule. The original Transformer uses Adam ($\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.98$, $\epsilon = 10^{-9}$) with a warmup-then-decay learning rate schedule: $$\text{lr} = d_{\text{model}}^{-0.5} \cdot \min\!\left(\text{step}^{-0.5},\; \text{step} \cdot \text{warmup\_steps}^{-1.5}\right)$$ For $\text{warmup\_steps} = 4000$. This increases linearly from 0 for the first 4000 steps, then decreases proportionally to the inverse square root of step number. Warmup is critical for Transformers trained from scratch — at the start of training, the Adam second-moment estimate $v_t$ is near zero, causing the effective step size to be large. Warmup keeps the LR low while Adam accumulates reliable gradient statistics.
Label smoothing. Instead of training with hard targets (0 or 1), use $\epsilon_{ls} = 0.1$ label smoothing: the target distribution assigns probability $1 - \epsilon_{ls} = 0.9$ to the correct token and $\epsilon_{ls} / (V - 1)$ to all others. This reduces the model's confidence (it can never achieve zero cross-entropy loss), which acts as a regulariser and improves calibration.
Dropout. Applied after every sublayer before the residual addition ($p = 0.1$). Also applied to attention weights ("attention dropout") and to the sum of embeddings + positional encodings.
Decoding. At test time, the decoder generates autoregressively. Greedy decoding: take the argmax at each step. Beam search (beam size 4–6) maintains the top-$k$ partial hypotheses, expanding each and keeping the top-$k$ at every step. Beam search consistently produces higher BLEU scores than greedy decoding.
Worked examples
Example 1 — Multi-head attention: dimension flow

Walk through the dimension transformation for multi-head attention in the base Transformer ($d_{\text{model}} = 512$, $h = 8$, $d_k = d_v = 64$) for a batch of 1 sequence of length $n = 10$ tokens. Trace input and output shapes through every step.

Input: $X \in \mathbb{R}^{10 \times 512}$ (10 tokens, each embedded to 512 dimensions)

For head $i$, compute projections:

  • $Q_i = X W_i^Q$: $(10 \times 512) \times (512 \times 64) = \mathbb{R}^{10 \times 64}$
  • $K_i = X W_i^K$: $\mathbb{R}^{10 \times 64}$
  • $V_i = X W_i^V$: $\mathbb{R}^{10 \times 64}$

Attention scores: $Q_i K_i^\top / \sqrt{64}$: $(10 \times 64) \times (64 \times 10) = \mathbb{R}^{10 \times 10}$

Softmax + weighted values: $\text{softmax}(\ldots) \times V_i$: $(10 \times 10) \times (10 \times 64) = \mathbb{R}^{10 \times 64}$. This is $\text{head}_i$.

Concatenate 8 heads: $[\text{head}_1; \ldots; \text{head}_8] \in \mathbb{R}^{10 \times 512}$

Output projection: $\mathbb{R}^{10 \times 512} \times W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{512 \times 512} = \mathbb{R}^{10 \times 512}$

The output is the same shape as the input: $\mathbb{R}^{10 \times 512}$. This is the residual-compatible shape — we can add it back to the input in the skip connection.

Example 2 — Positional encoding values

For $d_{\text{model}} = 4$, compute the positional encoding vectors for positions 0, 1, and 2.

With $d_{\text{model}} = 4$, the dimensions are $i \in \{0, 1\}$ giving pairs $(2i, 2i+1) \in \{(0,1), (2,3)\}$.

Frequencies: dimension pair 0 → frequency $= 10000^{0/4} = 1$; dimension pair 1 → frequency $= 10000^{2/4} = 100$.

Position 0: $[sin(0), cos(0), sin(0), cos(0)] = [0, 1, 0, 1]$

Position 1: $[sin(1), cos(1), sin(0.01), cos(0.01)] \approx [0.841, 0.540, 0.010, 1.000]$

Position 2: $[sin(2), cos(2), sin(0.02), cos(0.02)] \approx [0.909, -0.416, 0.020, 1.000]$

Observe: dimensions 0–1 (high frequency) change rapidly between positions — they encode fine-grained positional distinctions. Dimensions 2–3 (low frequency) change slowly — for these positions they are nearly constant, but at much larger position values (e.g., position 1000: $\sin(10) \approx -0.544$) they will have changed significantly. Different dimensions encode different resolution levels — together they uniquely identify every position.

Example 3 — Information flow in encoder-decoder cross-attention

When translating "The cat sat" (3 source tokens) to "Le chat s'est assis" (4 target tokens), describe what cross-attention computes at decoder step 3 (generating "s'est"). Which encoder states does the decoder query, and what Q/K/V roles do encoder and decoder play?

At decoder step 3, the causal self-attention sublayer has already integrated information from generated tokens "Le" (pos 0), "chat" (pos 1), and "s'est" is about to be generated. The decoder's hidden state at position 2 (0-indexed) encodes the query: "given that I've generated 'Le chat', what source information do I need to generate the next word?"

In cross-attention:

  • Q (decoder): $Y_2 W^Q$ — the decoder's self-attention output at position 2. This is the query: "what am I looking for?" It encodes the current decoding context ("generating a reflexive past tense verb in French").
  • K (encoder): $\text{Enc}_j W^K$ for each source position $j$. The encoder's contextualised representation of each source token acts as a key: "what I offer." Position 2 in the encoder ("sat") has a key that should score high with the decoder query for the verb.
  • V (encoder): $\text{Enc}_j W^V$ — what gets retrieved. If "sat" scores high, its value representation flows to the decoder.

The expected attention pattern: $\alpha_{2,2} \approx$ high (attending to "sat"), low attention to "The" and "cat" (already handled). The decoder retrieves semantic content about the source verb, which helps predict "s'est" (the reflexive auxiliary that corresponds to "sat" in this French translation).

No positional constraint is imposed on cross-attention — the decoder position 2 can attend to any source position 0, 1, or 2 freely.

Food for thought
  • The Transformer encoder is "bidirectional" in the sense that every token attends to every other token (past and future) simultaneously. The Transformer decoder is "unidirectional" (causal mask). BERT uses only an encoder (bidirectional); GPT uses only a decoder (causal). Why would you choose one over the other?
    The choice depends on the task. Bidirectional encoding (BERT-style) is ideal when you need to produce a representation of the full input, and the full input is available at inference time — e.g., classification, named entity recognition, question answering, reading comprehension. Every token sees its full context, so representations are maximally informative. You cannot use a bidirectional encoder for generation because generation is inherently sequential: you must produce token $t$ before you know token $t+1$. Causal decoding (GPT-style) is necessary when you need to generate sequences autoregressively — language modelling, text generation, code completion, dialogue. The causal mask enforces the autoregressive constraint. The downside: each position's representation is built from only left context, which is less informative than full context for classification tasks. A full encoder-decoder (original Transformer) is used for seq2seq tasks where input and output are different sequences: translation, summarisation, T5-style text-to-text. The encoder reads the full source bidirectionally; the decoder generates the target causally, reading the encoder's output via cross-attention.
  • The positional encoding is added to the embedding: $\mathbf{x}'_{\text{pos}} = \mathbf{x}_{\text{pos}} + \text{PE}(\text{pos})$. Why add rather than concatenate? Concatenation would preserve them separately; addition mixes them. Is this a problem?
    Adding mixes positional and semantic information into a single $d_{\text{model}}$-dimensional vector. This means the model cannot explicitly disentangle "what this token means" from "where this token appears." At first this seems problematic — ideally the query "what does this token mean at this position?" should use both components without conflating them. Concatenation would give $2d_{\text{model}}$ dimensions and preserve the separation. In practice, addition works for several reasons: (1) the Q/K/V projection matrices $W^Q$, $W^K$, $W^V$ have learned to project the sum into a subspace where positional and semantic information can be recovered via the linear projection; (2) the model has many heads and layers to recover this information; (3) concatenation doubles the dimension and quadruples the attention computation cost ($O(n^2 \cdot 2d)$ instead of $O(n^2 \cdot d)$). Addition is the cheaper choice and works well enough empirically. More recent models (RoPE, ALiBi) address the conceptual awkwardness by not adding positional encodings to the input at all — instead they modify the attention score computation directly (multiplying $Q$ and $K$ by rotation matrices based on position, for RoPE). This is theoretically cleaner: content and position are never mixed in the representation; position only affects how queries and keys interact.
  • The FFN sublayer applies the same MLP independently to each position: $\text{FFN}(\mathbf{x}_i) = W_2\,\text{ReLU}(W_1 \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2$. If attention already mixes information across positions, what role does the FFN serve? Why not use all-attention?
    This is an important and not entirely settled question. The most compelling interpretation (Geva et al. 2021, "Transformer Feed-Forward Layers Are Key-Value Memories") is that FFN layers function as memories for factual knowledge. The first layer of the FFN can be seen as a key-value store: each row of $W_1$ is a "key" that pattern-matches against the current token's representation; when a row has high inner product with the input (so its ReLU is active), the corresponding column of $W_2$ ("value") is added to the output. This gives the FFN a role complementary to attention: attention routes and contextualises information across positions; FFN recalls and refines at the single-token level using stored patterns. Practically, models without FFN sublayers ("attention-only Transformers") perform significantly worse. The FFN's larger inner dimension ($d_{ff} = 4 d_{\text{model}}$) provides extra capacity to store associations. The all-attention alternative would require larger attention layers (more heads or larger $d_k$), increasing the $O(n^2)$ term — the FFN cost scales as $O(n d^2)$ rather than $O(n^2 d)$, which is better for long sequences.
Common pitfalls
  • Applying the causal mask in the wrong place. The mask must zero out positions $j > i$ by setting $e_{ij} = -\infty$ before the softmax. Post-softmax masking (setting $\alpha_{ij} = 0$ then re-normalising, or not renormalising) is incorrect and produces different (wrong) distributions. The correct implementation: add a matrix of zeros and $-\infty$ to the score matrix before calling softmax.
  • Using post-LN instead of pre-LN for deep Transformers. The original paper uses post-LN ($\text{LayerNorm}(x + \text{Sublayer}(x))$). For the original 6-layer model, this trains fine. For $\geq 24$-layer models (e.g., large language models), post-LN causes instability and requires very careful warmup. GPT-2, GPT-3, LLaMA, and most modern models use pre-LN. If training loss explodes early in training for deep models, switch to pre-LN before changing anything else.
  • Forgetting the $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling. Without it, large $d_k$ (64 or larger) causes peaky softmax and near-zero gradients. This was covered in Lesson 5 but bears repeating — it is the most common bug in hand-coded attention.
  • Not sharing encoder/decoder embedding weights. The paper ties the source embedding, target embedding, and output projection matrix ($W_{\text{emb}} = W_{\text{out}}^\top$), saving ≈19M parameters for the 37K-token vocabulary. If you forget this tie and train three separate matrices, the model is overcapacitated and may not match reported results (the tied version provides implicit regularisation).
  • Evaluating generation quality only on perplexity. The Transformer is trained to minimise perplexity (cross-entropy on next-token prediction). Lower perplexity does not guarantee better text quality. Use BLEU (for translation), ROUGE (for summarisation), human evaluation, or downstream task accuracy as the final quality metric. BLEU has its own well-known limitations (not sensitive to fluency, heavily n-gram-based), but it is the standard for MT comparison.
Connects to
BERT & GPT (Lesson 7) — both are direct descendants of the Transformer. BERT uses only the encoder stack with bidirectional self-attention, pretrained via masked language modelling. GPT uses only the decoder stack with causal self-attention, pretrained via autoregressive language modelling. Lesson 7 covers what happens when you scale these pretraining recipes.
Deep Networks & Residual Connections (Lesson 1) — the Transformer's "Add & Norm" is exactly the pre/post-activation residual block. The residual stream through the stacked blocks provides gradient highways in the depth direction. Layer normalisation plays the same stabilising role that batch normalisation played in ResNets, adapted to the sequence setting.
Training Neural Networks (SL10) — the Adam optimiser, warmup schedules, dropout, and label smoothing are all covered there. The Transformer's training recipe is a specific instance of those general techniques, with the inverse-square-root LR schedule being the distinctive addition.
