9 · Generative Models
VAEs · GANs · Diffusion (DDPM) · Score Matching · Classifier-Free Guidance · Latent Diffusion
Discriminative models answer "given data $\mathbf{x}$, what is the label $y$?" Generative models answer "given a latent variable or noise, how do I produce new data $\mathbf{x}$ that looks like it came from the same distribution as the training set?" This is a harder problem: you need to model the full density $p(\mathbf{x})$ rather than the conditional $p(y | \mathbf{x})$. Modern generative models for images — the most visible being Stable Diffusion (2022) and DALL-E — are the result of twenty years of progress through three distinct paradigms: VAEs (latent variable models with variational inference), GANs (game-theoretic implicit density models), and diffusion models (iterative denoising). This lesson derives each from first principles, explains why diffusion models have largely displaced VAEs and GANs for image generation, and traces how classifier-free guidance and latent compression make large-scale text-to-image systems tractable.
Taxonomy of generative models
Generative models can be classified by how they represent and sample from $p(\mathbf{x})$:
Paradigm How $p(\mathbf{x})$ is represented Exact likelihood? Sampling speed
VAE Variational lower bound on $\log p(\mathbf{x})$; latent $\mathbf{z}$ No (ELBO) One forward pass
Normalising flows Exact via change of variables (invertible transforms) Yes One forward pass
GAN Implicit (discriminator-trained generator); no explicit density No One forward pass
Autoregressive (GPT) Product of conditionals $\prod_t p(x_t | x_{<t})$ Yes Sequential (slow)
Diffusion (DDPM) Iterative denoising; tractable ELBO No (ELBO) Many steps (but distillable)
Variational Autoencoders
A VAE (Kingma & Welling 2013) is a latent variable model: we posit a prior $p(\mathbf{z})$ (usually $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$) and a decoder $p_\theta(\mathbf{x} | \mathbf{z})$ (a neural network). We want to maximise the log-likelihood $\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$, but marginalising out $\mathbf{z}$: $$\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \log \int p_\theta(\mathbf{x} | \mathbf{z}) p(\mathbf{z})\, d\mathbf{z}$$ is intractable for high-dimensional $\mathbf{z}$. The key idea: introduce an encoder $q_\phi(\mathbf{z} | \mathbf{x})$ (the approximate posterior, also called the recognition model) to make this tractable.
ELBO derivation
Evidence Lower BOund (ELBO) derivation

Starting from the log-likelihood, add and subtract $\log q_\phi(\mathbf{z} | \mathbf{x})$ inside the expectation over $q_\phi$:

$$\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \log \int p_\theta(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})\, d\mathbf{z} = \log \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}\left[\frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}\right]$$

By Jensen's inequality ($\log \mathbb{E}[X] \geq \mathbb{E}[\log X]$, since $\log$ is concave):

$$\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}\left[\log \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}\right] = \mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi; \mathbf{x})$$

Expanding $\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}) = \log p_\theta(\mathbf{x} | \mathbf{z}) + \log p(\mathbf{z})$:

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}[\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})]}_{\text{reconstruction}} - \underbrace{D_\text{KL}(q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{z}))}_{\text{regularisation}}$$

The gap between $\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ and $\mathcal{L}$ is exactly $D_\text{KL}(q_\phi \| p_\theta(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})) \geq 0$. Maximising the ELBO both increases the marginal likelihood and forces the approximate posterior $q_\phi$ toward the true posterior $p_\theta(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$.

Encoder choice: $q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}), \text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\phi(\mathbf{x})))$. The KL term is then analytic: $$D_\text{KL}(\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2) \| \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_j \left(\mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - \log \sigma_j^2 - 1\right)$$

Reparameterisation trick: Sampling $\mathbf{z} \sim q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ is not differentiable w.r.t. $\phi$. Reparametrise: $\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$. Now $\mathbf{z}$ is a deterministic function of $(\phi, \boldsymbol{\epsilon})$, so gradients flow through $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi$.

VAE properties and limitations. VAEs produce blurry reconstructions because the reconstruction term $\mathbb{E}[\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})]$ with a Gaussian decoder reduces to pixel-wise MSE, which averages over uncertainty — sharp details are "averaged out." The KL regularisation forces the posterior toward a smooth Gaussian prior, limiting the amount of information the latent can carry. Posterior collapse (entire dimensions of $\mathbf{z}$ ignored by the decoder) is a common failure mode. VAEs are excellent as encoders for downstream tasks, but their image generation quality was consistently surpassed by GANs and later diffusion models.
Generative Adversarial Networks
GAN minimax objective (Goodfellow et al. 2014)

Two networks: a generator $G_\theta: \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{X}$ mapping noise $\mathbf{z} \sim p(\mathbf{z})$ to generated samples, and a discriminator $D_\phi: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]$ estimating the probability that a sample is real. The objective:

$$\min_\theta \max_\phi\; \mathcal{V}(G_\theta, D_\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\text{data}}[\log D_\phi(\mathbf{x})] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p(\mathbf{z})}[\log(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))]$$

For fixed $G$, the optimal discriminator is: $$D^*(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{p_\text{data}(\mathbf{x})}{p_\text{data}(\mathbf{x}) + p_G(\mathbf{x})}$$ Substituting back, the generator objective becomes: $$\mathcal{V}(G, D^*) = -\log 4 + 2\, D_\text{JS}(p_\text{data} \| p_G)$$ where $D_\text{JS}$ is the Jensen-Shannon divergence. The global minimum is at $p_G = p_\text{data}$, where $D_\text{JS} = 0$ and $D^*(\mathbf{x}) = 1/2$ everywhere — the discriminator can no longer distinguish real from generated.

Practical training:

  1. Update discriminator: maximise $\mathcal{V}$ w.r.t. $\phi$ for $k$ steps (real vs. fake classification).
  2. Update generator: minimise $\log(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))$ w.r.t. $\theta$. In practice, maximise $\log D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))$ instead ("non-saturating" loss) to avoid vanishing gradients when the discriminator is strong.

GAN training dynamics and failure modes
Mode collapse is the canonical GAN failure: the generator learns to produce only a few high-quality samples (or even just one) that fool the discriminator, ignoring most of the data distribution. From the divergence perspective: minimising $D_\text{JS}$ does not penalise the generator for missing modes, only for generating distinguishable samples. Wasserstein GAN (WGAN, Arjovsky et al. 2017) replaces $D_\text{JS}$ with the Wasserstein-1 distance, which measures the minimum "earth mover's cost" to transform one distribution into another. The Wasserstein distance has well-defined gradients everywhere — even when $p_G$ and $p_\text{data}$ have disjoint support (which causes $D_\text{JS}$ gradients to vanish). WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al. 2017) enforces the 1-Lipschitz constraint needed for WGAN with a gradient penalty instead of weight clipping.
StyleGAN, BigGAN, and progressive growing refined GAN training with architectural innovations: progressive growing (start at low resolution, add layers), style injection via AdaIN (StyleGAN), spectral normalisation, and truncation tricks for quality/diversity tradeoffs. Peak GAN quality (StyleGAN2, 2020) was stunning but required careful per-dataset tuning. Training instability and mode collapse were persistent problems that diffusion models largely eliminated.
Diffusion models (DDPM)
DDPM — Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (Ho et al. 2020)

A diffusion model defines a Markov chain that adds noise to data over $T$ steps (the forward process), then trains a neural network to reverse that process (the reverse process).

Forward process (fixed, not learned): $$q(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_t;\, \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,\mathbf{x}_{t-1},\, \beta_t \mathbf{I})$$ Each step adds Gaussian noise with variance schedule $\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_T$. Let $\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t$ and $\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t \alpha_s$. Then the distribution of $\mathbf{x}_t$ given $\mathbf{x}_0$ is analytic (by reparameterisation): $$q(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_0) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_t;\, \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0,\, (1-\bar{\alpha}_t)\mathbf{I})$$ So $\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$. For $T = 1000$ with an appropriate schedule, $\mathbf{x}_T \approx \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ (pure noise).

Reverse process (learned): $$p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1} | \mathbf{x}_t) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_{t-1};\, \boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t),\, \sigma_t^2 \mathbf{I})$$ We train a neural network $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ to predict the noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ that was added. The ELBO simplifies to a mean-squared error in noise space: $$\mathcal{L}_\text{simple} = \mathbb{E}_{t, \mathbf{x}_0, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}}\left[\|\boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \epsilon_\theta(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\boldsymbol{\epsilon},\, t)\|^2\right]$$ At inference, start from $\mathbf{x}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ and iteratively denoise: $\mathbf{x}_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\left(\mathbf{x}_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\right) + \sigma_t \mathbf{z}$ where $\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ (or $\mathbf{z} = 0$ at final step).

Score matching and DDIM
Score function connection. The score function of a distribution is $\nabla_\mathbf{x} \log p(\mathbf{x})$ — the gradient of the log-density. The noise prediction $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ is directly related to the score of the noisy distribution $p_t(\mathbf{x}_t)$: $$\nabla_{\mathbf{x}_t} \log p_t(\mathbf{x}_t) = -\frac{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}$$ Denoising score matching (Vincent 2011; Song & Ermon 2019) shows that training $\epsilon_\theta$ to predict added noise is equivalent to learning the score function of the noise-perturbed data distribution. This connects diffusion models to a deep family of energy-based models and stochastic differential equations (SDEs). Song et al. (2020) formulated both the forward and reverse processes as SDEs, giving a continuous-time framework that unifies DDPM, SMLD (noise-conditional score networks), and many other approaches.
DDIM — deterministic sampling (Song et al. 2020). DDPM's reverse process is stochastic (adds Gaussian noise at each step). DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models) derives a non-Markovian variant whose reverse process is deterministic: $$\mathbf{x}_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}}\underbrace{\left(\frac{\mathbf{x}_t - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\epsilon_\theta}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}}\right)}_{\text{predicted } \mathbf{x}_0} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}}\,\epsilon_\theta$$ Since the process is deterministic, you can skip steps: instead of $T = 1000$ steps, use $T' = 20$–$50$ steps by subsampling the timestep schedule. DDIM gives the same trained model faster generation at the cost of slightly different sample quality — a quality/speed tradeoff controlled by the number of steps.
Classifier-free guidance
Classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans 2021)

Text-to-image generation requires conditioning the model on a text prompt $\mathbf{c}$. The conditional model $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{c})$ generates images from the conditional distribution $p(\mathbf{x} | \mathbf{c})$. But conditioning alone often produces images that match the prompt loosely. We want to generate from a "sharpened" distribution that strongly matches $\mathbf{c}$.

Classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol 2021): use a separately trained classifier $p_\phi(c | \mathbf{x})$ to steer the sampling via: $$\tilde{\epsilon}(\mathbf{x}_t, t) = \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\nabla_{\mathbf{x}_t} \log p_\phi(c | \mathbf{x}_t)$$ The gradient of the classifier w.r.t. the noisy image pushes the denoising trajectory toward regions that the classifier predicts as matching $c$. This requires a separate noisy-domain classifier — inconvenient.

Classifier-free guidance (CFG) eliminates the need for an explicit classifier by jointly training the model with and without conditioning: with probability $p_\text{uncond}$ (e.g., 0.1), replace $\mathbf{c}$ with a null token $\emptyset$. The model learns both $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{c})$ and $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \emptyset)$. At inference, use a convex combination: $$\tilde{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \emptyset) + w \cdot [\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{c}) - \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \emptyset)]$$ The guidance scale $w > 1$ amplifies the conditional signal beyond what the model would generate with conditioning alone. By the implicit score representation: $$\tilde{\epsilon} \propto -\nabla_{\mathbf{x}_t} \log p(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{c})^w p(\mathbf{x}_t)^{1-w}$$ — the sampled distribution is $p(\mathbf{x} | \mathbf{c})$ raised to the power $w$, a sharpened conditional. Typical values: $w = 7$–$12$ for text-to-image. Large $w$ gives images that strongly match the prompt but sacrifice diversity and sometimes realism (over-saturation, artifacts). Small $w$ gives more diversity but looser adherence.

Latent diffusion models (Stable Diffusion)
Running diffusion in pixel space for 512×512 images is extremely expensive — each denoising step operates on a $512 \times 512 \times 3 = 786{,}432$-dimensional tensor, and you need hundreds of steps. Latent diffusion models (Rombach et al. 2022, "High-Resolution Image Synthesis with Latent Diffusion Models") solve this with a two-stage approach:
Stage 1 — perceptual compression (pre-trained, not updated during diffusion training): Train a VAE encoder $\mathcal{E}$ to compress images to a low-dimensional latent space: $\mathbf{z} = \mathcal{E}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{h/f \times w/f \times c}$ where $f$ is the downsampling factor (e.g., $f=8$: a 512×512 image → 64×64×4 latent). A decoder $\mathcal{D}$ reconstructs: $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{z})$. The VAE is regularised with a KL penalty (small regularisation keeps latent close to a Gaussian) plus a perceptual loss and patch-based adversarial loss to prevent blur. This latent space is 48× smaller in total dimensionality than pixel space.
Stage 2 — diffusion in latent space: The diffusion model $\epsilon_\theta$ is a U-Net that operates entirely in the compressed latent space $\mathbf{z}$. Cross-attention layers in the U-Net integrate the text conditioning: text is encoded by CLIP (or a text encoder) into embeddings, which serve as keys and values in the cross-attention ($Q$ from the U-Net spatial features, $K$/$V$ from the text encoder). Generation: sample $\mathbf{z}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$, iteratively denoise with $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t, \mathbf{c})$ using DDIM, then decode with $\mathcal{D}$. The full pipeline: noise → latent → pixel.
This architecture is used by Stable Diffusion (open-source, ~860M parameters), DALL-E 2 (the second stage, prior + diffusion decoder), and Imagen (pixel-space cascaded diffusion, different). The key insight: most of the "semantic" work happens in the low-dimensional latent space; the decoder handles pixel-level reconstruction independently. This separation of semantic generation from perceptual rendering is what makes training tractable.
Worked examples
Example 1 — VAE ELBO decomposition by hand

A VAE has a 2D latent space. After encoding input $\mathbf{x}$, the encoder produces $\boldsymbol{\mu} = [1.0, 0.5]$ and $\log \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2 = [0.0, -1.0]$ (so $\sigma_1 = 1.0$, $\sigma_2 = e^{-0.5} \approx 0.607$). Compute the KL term. The decoder's reconstruction loss is $-\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z}) = 12.3$. What is the total ELBO loss?

KL divergence: $D_\text{KL} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^2 (\mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - \log \sigma_j^2 - 1)$

Dimension 1 ($\mu=1.0$, $\sigma^2=1.0$, $\log \sigma^2 = 0.0$): $\frac{1}{2}(1.0^2 + 1.0 - 0.0 - 1) = \frac{1}{2}(1.0) = 0.5$

Dimension 2 ($\mu=0.5$, $\sigma^2 = e^{-1.0} \approx 0.368$, $\log \sigma^2 = -1.0$): $\frac{1}{2}(0.25 + 0.368 - (-1.0) - 1) = \frac{1}{2}(0.618) = 0.309$

$D_\text{KL} = 0.5 + 0.309 = 0.809$.

Total ELBO loss (to minimise, so negative ELBO): $\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{12.3}_{\text{reconstruction}} + \underbrace{0.809}_{\text{KL}} = 13.109$

Note: dimension 1 has $\mu=1.0$, pulling the posterior mean away from the prior, contributing KL of 0.5 — this is the cost of encoding meaningful information in that dimension. If $\mu=0$, $\sigma^2=1$ (exactly the prior), KL $= 0$ and that dimension carries no information about $\mathbf{x}$ — this is posterior collapse for that dimension.

Example 2 — DDPM forward process

At timestep $t=500$ with $\bar{\alpha}_{500} = 0.1$, a clean image $\mathbf{x}_0$ has pixel value 0.8 at one location. (a) What is the mean and variance of $\mathbf{x}_{500}$ at that location? (b) Compute $\mathbf{x}_{500}$ for a specific noise sample $\epsilon = 1.2$. (c) What does the model need to predict at this timestep?

(a) $q(\mathbf{x}_{500} | \mathbf{x}_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{500}}\,\mathbf{x}_0,\, (1-\bar{\alpha}_{500}))$ $= \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{0.1} \times 0.8,\, 0.9)$ $= \mathcal{N}(0.253,\, 0.9)$ Mean $= \sqrt{0.1} \times 0.8 = 0.316 \times 0.8 = 0.253$. Variance $= 1 - 0.1 = 0.9$, so $\sigma = 0.949$.

(b) $\mathbf{x}_{500} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{500}}\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{500}}\,\epsilon = 0.253 + 0.949 \times 1.2 = 0.253 + 1.138 = 1.391$

(c) The model $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{500}, 500)$ must predict $\epsilon = 1.2$ — the noise that was added. The training target is this specific $\epsilon$ drawn from $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$. At $t=500$, the signal has been heavily corrupted: the original value of 0.8 contributes only 0.253, while noise contributes 1.138. The model must learn to recover the noise pattern from this noisy observation, which is then subtracted in the reverse step to recover a slightly less noisy $\mathbf{x}_{499}$.

Example 3 — Classifier-free guidance tradeoff

A diffusion model produces two noise predictions for prompt $\mathbf{c}$ = "a red apple": conditional $\epsilon_c = [0.3, -0.5, 0.8]$ and unconditional $\epsilon_u = [0.1, -0.1, 0.2]$. Compute the guided prediction for $w = 1$ (no guidance), $w = 3$, and $w = 7$. What happens as $w$ increases?

$\tilde{\epsilon} = \epsilon_u + w(\epsilon_c - \epsilon_u)$

Difference: $\epsilon_c - \epsilon_u = [0.2, -0.4, 0.6]$.

$w=1$: $\tilde{\epsilon} = [0.1, -0.1, 0.2] + 1 \times [0.2, -0.4, 0.6] = [0.3, -0.5, 0.8]$ — same as conditional prediction, no amplification.

$w=3$: $\tilde{\epsilon} = [0.1, -0.1, 0.2] + 3 \times [0.2, -0.4, 0.6] = [0.7, -1.3, 2.0]$ — amplified toward "apple-ness".

$w=7$: $\tilde{\epsilon} = [0.1, -0.1, 0.2] + 7 \times [0.2, -0.4, 0.6] = [1.5, -2.9, 4.4]$ — strongly amplified.

As $w$ increases: the guided noise prediction increasingly diverges from what the model "naturally" predicts. The resulting image will more strongly match the prompt — brighter reds, more prototypical apple shape — but at the cost of diversity (all samples will look similar) and sometimes realism (over-saturated, hallucinated textures that maximally activate the "apple" direction in latent space). The guidance scale is effectively a temperature parameter: high $w$ = confident, low diversity; low $w$ = more varied, weaker prompt adherence.

Food for thought
  • GANs use implicit density estimation (no explicit $p(\mathbf{x})$) and can produce extremely sharp, high-fidelity images from a single forward pass. Diffusion models have explicit training objectives and stable training but require hundreds of steps for generation. What structural property of diffusion models removes GAN training instabilities? Why hasn't a GAN model competitive with modern diffusion models emerged since 2022?
    GAN instabilities stem from two structural problems: (1) Non-convergence of the minimax game. Generator and discriminator are simultaneously optimised, which is a two-player game that has no guaranteed equilibrium under gradient descent. Local gradients can spiral, oscillate, or diverge — even when both individually have well-behaved loss surfaces. (2) Mode collapse from the loss geometry. When the generator produces samples from a distribution disjoint from real data, the JS divergence gradient is 0 (the distributions don't overlap). The generator receives no useful gradient signal to fix this — it is already "winning" by fooling a confused discriminator. WGAN partially addresses this, but mode-seeking behaviour is inherent to adversarial losses. Diffusion models are immune to both: there is only one network (the denoiser), trained with a regression-style MSE loss against a fixed target (the added noise). The loss is always well-defined and non-zero. Training is stable because it reduces to a supervised regression problem at each timestep independently. Why haven't GANs caught up? Diffusion model quality is driven largely by scale (more parameters, more data, more compute). Scaling diffusion models improves sample quality predictably (scaling laws). GANs are harder to scale: training instabilities compound at scale, and mode collapse becomes harder to avoid. Consistency Models (Song et al. 2023) and flow matching (Lipman et al. 2022) have further improved diffusion sampling speed, narrowing the only remaining advantage of GANs (single-step generation).
  • Latent diffusion compresses images by a factor of $f=8$ in each spatial dimension before running diffusion. This means the diffusion model never directly models pixel values — it models latent codes from a pre-trained VAE. Does this change what the model can generate? What are the potential failure modes of this compression?
    Yes — the fundamental quality ceiling of a latent diffusion model is determined by the reconstruction quality of the VAE decoder, not by the diffusion model itself. If the VAE cannot reconstruct fine details (e.g., sharp text, complex textures, individual blades of grass), the diffusion model cannot produce them — regardless of how perfect the latent it generates. This is an explicit design choice: latent diffusion trades pixel-level fidelity for computational tractability. The assumption is that the VAE captures all perceptually relevant variation in the latent space, and that pixel-level details are "mere rendering" that the decoder can handle. Failure modes: (1) Text in images: character-level detail at small scales is extremely sensitive to VAE compression. Until very recently, all diffusion models (Stable Diffusion 1.x, 2.x) failed to generate readable text — the latent code at $f=8$ simply cannot represent individual characters. SDXL and later models use $f=8$ with much larger VAE capacity (more channels, better training) and improved text encoders to partially address this. (2) Fine-grained detail at high resolution: tiling artifacts, blurry textures in smooth regions, visible seams. (3) Color drift: the VAE mean-field posterior can systematically shift colors (known as "pink shift" in early Stable Diffusion). The choice of $f$ is a tradeoff: lower $f$ = better reconstruction quality but higher diffusion dimensionality and cost. $f=8$ with 4 latent channels ($64 \times 64 \times 4$ for 512×512) was a pragmatic choice validated empirically. FLUX, Stable Diffusion 3, and DALL-E 3 all use architectural improvements to the VAE and higher-capacity latent spaces.
  • The DDPM noise prediction objective trains the model to estimate $\epsilon$ (the noise). But the same training objective can be re-parameterised to predict $\mathbf{x}_0$ (the clean image) or the velocity $v = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{x}_0$. These are mathematically equivalent given the diffusion equations. Does it matter which parameterisation the network predicts?
    Mathematically equivalent means they produce the same distribution over generated samples in the limit of perfect optimisation. But in practice, different parameterisations differ in: (1) Loss weighting across timesteps. Predicting $\epsilon$ weights all timesteps equally by default. Predicting $\mathbf{x}_0$ gives larger gradients at high-noise timesteps (where the noisy image is mostly noise and the clean image prediction is very uncertain). Predicting $v$ (velocity parameterisation, Salimans & Ho 2022) was motivated by improved gradient signal at $t \to T$ (where $\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_T} \to 0$ and $\epsilon$-prediction gradients vanish). $v$-prediction is empirically better for high-resolution generation and is used in SDXL, SD3, and Imagen Video. (2) Numerical stability at boundaries. At $t=0$ ($\bar{\alpha}_t = 1$, no noise), $\mathbf{x}_0$-prediction is easy but $\epsilon$-prediction is undefined. At $t=T$ ($\bar{\alpha}_t \to 0$, pure noise), $\epsilon$-prediction is straightforward but $\mathbf{x}_0$-prediction faces high variance. $v$-prediction is well-behaved at both. (3) Distillation and fast sampling. Consistency models and flow matching methods use specific parameterisations ($\mathbf{x}_0$ and velocity respectively) because they have nicer properties for the consistency mapping needed to make single-step generation work. The practical takeaway: parameterisation choice is a hyperparameter that affects training dynamics, not the final distribution. For modern high-resolution, high-quality models, $v$-prediction is the preferred choice.
Connects to
The Transformer (DL6) and BERT/GPT (DL7) — DALL-E 2 and Imagen use powerful language models (CLIP, T5) as the text encoder feeding cross-attention in the diffusion U-Net. The quality of text-image alignment scales directly with the text encoder's capability. GPT-4V, Gemini, and Claude are multimodal models that integrate vision encoders trained with contrastive or generative objectives.
Training at Scale (DL8) — training Stable Diffusion required ~50,000 A100-hours on LAION-5B (5 billion image-text pairs). All the techniques of DL8 — Flash Attention, mixed precision, data parallelism, ZeRO — are used. The diffusion U-Net also uses Flash Attention in its self-attention and cross-attention layers, which is critical for batch size.
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (DL7, RLHF) — InstructPix2Pix and DreamBooth fine-tune diffusion models on human feedback, paralleling RLHF in language. RLHF-trained reward models score generated images, and the diffusion model is fine-tuned to maximise reward while staying close to the base model — the same KL-constrained optimisation used for InstructGPT.
