11 · Normalizing Flows
Change of Variables · Coupling Layers · NICE · RealNVP · GLOW · Autoregressive Flows · Flow Matching
Every generative model in DL9 — VAEs, GANs, diffusion — approximates the data density $p(\mathbf{x})$ in some indirect way: VAEs use a lower bound, GANs use an implicit discriminator, diffusion uses a Markov chain of denoising steps. Normalizing flows take a different route: transform a simple, tractable density (a standard Gaussian) via a sequence of invertible mappings until it matches the complex data distribution — and compute the resulting density exactly using the change-of-variables formula. This exact likelihood is the defining feature: you can evaluate $\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ for any $\mathbf{x}$ without approximation, and generate samples in one forward pass by transforming Gaussian noise. This lesson derives the theory, then builds up the architecture sequence that made flows practical: from planar flows → NICE coupling layers → RealNVP → GLOW → autoregressive flows → continuous flows and flow matching (the framework behind Stable Diffusion 3 and Flux).
Change of variables formula
Exact density under an invertible transformation

Let $\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z(\mathbf{z})$ (e.g., $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$) and let $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$ be a differentiable, invertible function. Define $\mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z})$. The density of $\mathbf{x}$ is given by the change of variables formula: $$p_X(\mathbf{x}) = p_Z(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) \left|\det J_{f^{-1}}(\mathbf{x})\right|$$ where $J_{f^{-1}}$ is the Jacobian matrix of $f^{-1}$. Taking logarithms: $$\log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) + \log\left|\det J_{f^{-1}}(\mathbf{x})\right|$$ Using $J_{f^{-1}} = J_f^{-1}$ and $\det(J_f^{-1}) = 1/\det(J_f)$: $$\log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(\mathbf{z}) - \log\left|\det J_f(\mathbf{z})\right|$$ where $\mathbf{z} = f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})$.

Intuition. If $f$ stretches a region of $\mathbf{z}$-space (large $|\det J_f|$), points in that region of $\mathbf{x}$-space are spread out — the density decreases. If $f$ compresses a region (small $|\det J_f|$), the density increases. The log-determinant term exactly corrects for this volume change.

Composition of flows. If $f = f_K \circ f_{K-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1$ (a sequence of $K$ invertible transforms), the log-density becomes: $$\log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(\mathbf{z}) - \sum_{k=1}^K \log\left|\det J_{f_k}(\mathbf{z}_{k-1})\right|$$ where $\mathbf{z}_0 = \mathbf{z}$, $\mathbf{z}_k = f_k(\mathbf{z}_{k-1})$, $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{z}_K$. Each $f_k$ must be invertible and have a tractable (cheap to compute) Jacobian determinant. These are the two design constraints every flow architecture must satisfy.

The log-determinant bottleneck
The general Jacobian determinant of an $d \times d$ matrix costs $O(d^3)$ to compute (Gaussian elimination). For image data with $d = 64 \times 64 \times 3 = 12{,}288$, this is completely intractable. Every practical flow architecture is designed to make $\log |\det J_f|$ either analytically simple (triangular Jacobian) or efficiently computable (e.g., a sum of diagonal terms). The key identity: if $J_f$ is triangular (upper or lower), then $\det J_f = \prod_i (J_f)_{ii}$ — the product of its diagonal entries. This is $O(d)$ to compute. Designing flows with triangular Jacobians is the central architectural challenge.
Planar and radial flows
Planar flows (Rezende & Mohamed 2015) are the simplest: $f(\mathbf{z}) = \mathbf{z} + \mathbf{u}\,h(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{z} + b)$ where $h$ is a smooth activation and $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^d$. The Jacobian determinant is $1 + h'(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{z}+b)\mathbf{u}^\top\mathbf{w}$ — scalar, $O(d)$ via the matrix determinant lemma. Each layer perturbs the distribution along a hyperplane. Stacking many layers can approximate complex distributions. But these flows are weak in practice: each layer can only bend the distribution in one direction, so many layers are needed. They remain pedagogically important as the simplest illustration of the flow framework.
Coupling layers — NICE and RealNVP
Affine coupling layer (Dinh et al. 2014/2016)

NICE (Non-linear Independent Components Estimation, Dinh et al. 2014) introduced the additive coupling layer. Partition the $d$-dimensional input $\mathbf{z}$ into two halves $\mathbf{z}_{1:m}$ and $\mathbf{z}_{m+1:d}$: $$\mathbf{x}_{1:m} = \mathbf{z}_{1:m}$$ $$\mathbf{x}_{m+1:d} = \mathbf{z}_{m+1:d} + t(\mathbf{z}_{1:m})$$ where $t: \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^{d-m}$ is an arbitrary neural network (the "translation network"). The Jacobian of this transformation is triangular: $$J_f = \begin{pmatrix} I_m & 0 \\ \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}_{m+1:d}}{\partial \mathbf{z}_{1:m}} & I_{d-m} \end{pmatrix}$$ $\det J_f = \det(I_m)\det(I_{d-m}) = 1$. The transformation is volume-preserving: $\log|\det J_f| = 0$. No volume correction needed!

Inverse: $\mathbf{z}_{1:m} = \mathbf{x}_{1:m}$ (unchanged), $\mathbf{z}_{m+1:d} = \mathbf{x}_{m+1:d} - t(\mathbf{x}_{1:m})$ (subtract instead of add). Only one forward pass of $t$ needed in either direction. $t$ never needs to be inverted — it can be any architecture (deep MLP, ResNet, etc.).

RealNVP (Real-valued Non-Volume Preserving, Dinh et al. 2016) generalises to affine coupling: $$\mathbf{x}_{m+1:d} = \mathbf{z}_{m+1:d} \odot \exp(s(\mathbf{z}_{1:m})) + t(\mathbf{z}_{1:m})$$ The Jacobian is now triangular with diagonal entries $\exp(s_i(\mathbf{z}_{1:m}))$: $$\log|\det J_f| = \sum_{i=m+1}^d s_i(\mathbf{z}_{1:m})$$ This is $O(d)$ and adds expressive power via the scale $s$. Inverse: $\mathbf{z}_{m+1:d} = (\mathbf{x}_{m+1:d} - t(\mathbf{x}_{1:m})) \odot \exp(-s(\mathbf{x}_{1:m}))$. Again, $s$ and $t$ are never inverted — only evaluated forward.

Stacking layers with alternating masks: In a single coupling layer, the first half $\mathbf{z}_{1:m}$ is unchanged. To transform all dimensions, alternate which half is the "identity" half and which is the "coupling" half in successive layers. After two layers, all dimensions have been transformed at least once. This is the key to expressive multi-layer flows.

GLOW — invertible 1×1 convolutions
GLOW (Generative Flow, Kingma & Dhariwal 2018) stacks three operations per flow step, forming a highly expressive flow for images:
  1. Actnorm (activation normalisation): per-channel scale and bias, initialised from the first minibatch so that activations are unit-variance zero-mean. Replaces batch normalisation (which is not invertible in general). Log-det: sum of log-scales, $O(d)$.
  2. Invertible 1×1 convolution: replaces the fixed channel-permutation in RealNVP. A learnable $W \in \mathbb{R}^{c \times c}$ matrix (where $c$ is the number of channels) is applied to each spatial position. This is a $1\times1$ convolution and amounts to a learned linear mixing of channels. Log-det: $h \cdot w \cdot \log|\det W|$ (height × width × log-determinant of the mixing matrix). Computing $\det W$ for small $c$ is cheap ($O(c^3)$, tiny); GLOW uses the LU decomposition $W = PLU$ (permutation × lower triangular × upper triangular) for $O(c^3)$ once and $O(c^2)$ per forward pass.
  3. Affine coupling layer (RealNVP-style, with checkerboard masking and channel-halving at different scales).
GLOW trained on CelebA-HQ produced the first photorealistic face interpolation in latent space: because the flow is invertible and exact, you can encode two real images to their latent codes $\mathbf{z}_1$, $\mathbf{z}_2$ and linearly interpolate in $\mathbf{z}$-space — the resulting decoded images smoothly blend the two faces, with the intermediate images being perceptually meaningful rather than blurry averages (a common artifact in pixel-space interpolation).
Autoregressive flows — MAF and IAF
Coupling layers only condition each half on the other — information flow is limited. Autoregressive flows condition each output on all previous outputs, achieving maximum expressiveness for a triangular Jacobian. MAF (Masked Autoregressive Flow, Papamakarios et al. 2017): $$x_i = \mu_i(\mathbf{x}_{1:i-1}) + \sigma_i(\mathbf{x}_{1:i-1}) \cdot z_i$$ Each dimension $x_i$ is an affine function of the latent $z_i$ with mean and scale conditioned on all previous dimensions. The Jacobian is triangular (each $x_i$ only depends on $z_i$ and earlier $x_j$), so $\log|\det J| = \sum_i \log \sigma_i$. The parameters $\mu_i$ and $\sigma_i$ are computed by a masked autoencoder (MADE), which evaluates all $\mu_i, \sigma_i$ in one forward pass by masking the weight matrices to enforce the autoregressive order. Generating from MAF is sequential: sample $z_1, \ldots, z_d$ i.i.d., then compute $x_1 = \mu_1 + \sigma_1 z_1$, then $x_2 = \mu_2(\mathbf{x}_1) + \sigma_2(\mathbf{x}_1) z_2$, etc. — $O(d)$ sequential steps. Evaluating $\log p(\mathbf{x})$ for a known $\mathbf{x}$ can be done in one masked forward pass: all $\mu_i(\mathbf{x}_{1:i-1})$ computed in parallel.
IAF (Inverse Autoregressive Flow, Kingma et al. 2016) is MAF with the sampling and density-evaluation roles swapped: $$z_i = \frac{x_i - \mu_i(\mathbf{z}_{1:i-1})}{\sigma_i(\mathbf{z}_{1:i-1})}$$ Sampling is one masked forward pass (fast). Density evaluation is sequential (slow). MAF and IAF are inverses of each other — literally: a MAF layer with parameters computed from $\mathbf{x}$ is the same as an IAF layer with parameters computed from $\mathbf{z}$. The duality is: fast density evaluation (MAF) vs. fast sampling (IAF). This tradeoff is fundamental and mirrors the autoregressive generation tradeoff in language models (fast evaluation via teacher-forcing; slow sampling via sequential decode).
Continuous normalizing flows
Continuous normalizing flows and the instantaneous change of variables (Chen et al. 2018)

The composition of $K$ discrete flow layers can be taken to the limit as $K \to \infty$, giving a continuous-time flow governed by an ODE: $$\frac{d\mathbf{z}(t)}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{z}(t), t)$$ where $f_\theta$ is a neural network (the "dynamics"). The flow maps the initial state $\mathbf{z}(0) \sim p_Z$ to the data $\mathbf{z}(1) = \mathbf{x}$. This is called a neural ODE (Chen et al. 2018).

Instantaneous change of variables: The log-density changes continuously along the trajectory according to the Liouville equation: $$\frac{d\log p(\mathbf{z}(t))}{dt} = -\text{tr}\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \mathbf{z}(t)}\right)$$ where $\text{tr}$ is the trace (sum of diagonal of the Jacobian). This is much cheaper than the full log-determinant: for random projection estimates, $\text{tr}(J) \approx \mathbf{\epsilon}^\top J \mathbf{\epsilon}$ for $\mathbf{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$, giving an unbiased estimator in $O(d)$ per sample. The full density is: $$\log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(\mathbf{z}(0)) - \int_0^1 \text{tr}\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \mathbf{z}(t)}\right) dt$$ Training maximises $\log p_X(\mathbf{x})$; the integral is computed by an ODE solver (e.g., Dormand-Prince RK45) with the adjoint method for gradient computation through the ODE trajectory.

Limitations: CNFs are flexible but computationally expensive: ODE solvers require many function evaluations, and the adjoint method has high memory cost. Training is much slower than discrete flows or diffusion models at scale.

Flow matching
Conditional flow matching (Lipman et al. 2022; Liu et al. 2022)

Training CNFs by maximising the log-likelihood via ODE solvers is expensive. Flow matching provides a simpler, scalable training objective that bypasses the ODE solver entirely during training.

Setup: Define a flow $\phi_t: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$ that transforms the base distribution $p_0 = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ into the data distribution $p_1 = p_\text{data}$. The velocity field $u_t(\mathbf{x})$ generates this flow via $\frac{d\phi_t(\mathbf{x})}{dt} = u_t(\phi_t(\mathbf{x}))$.

Conditional construction: For each data point $\mathbf{x}_1$, define a simple conditional flow from noise $\mathbf{x}_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ to $\mathbf{x}_1$: the simplest choice is the optimal transport (OT) path (straight line in latent space): $$\mathbf{x}_t = (1-t)\mathbf{x}_0 + t\mathbf{x}_1, \quad t \in [0, 1]$$ The conditional velocity field is then $u_t(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_1) = \mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0$. This is constant along each trajectory — just the direction from noise to data.

Conditional flow matching loss: $$\mathcal{L}_\text{CFM}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{t, \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{x}_1}\left[\|v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) - u_t(\mathbf{x}_t|\mathbf{x}_1)\|^2\right]$$ Train a neural network $v_\theta$ to predict the velocity at each $(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$. This is a pure regression objective, no ODE solver needed during training. At inference, use an ODE solver from $t=0$ (pure noise) to $t=1$ (data).

Connection to diffusion (DDIM): The DDIM sampler defines a probability flow ODE for DDPM: $$\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)[\mathbf{x} + \nabla_\mathbf{x} \log p_t(\mathbf{x})]$$ This is a CNF with a specific velocity field defined by the score function. Flow matching with straight-line OT paths (Rectified Flow / Stable Diffusion 3 / Flux) is empirically better: the straight paths are shorter (lower curvature), so fewer ODE solver steps are needed at inference — a major practical advantage. SD3, FLUX, and Stable Video Diffusion all use flow matching rather than DDPM for this reason.

Worked examples
Example 1 — Change of variables in 2D

Let $\mathbf{z} = (z_1, z_2)^\top \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$. Define a flow $f: \mathbf{z} \mapsto \mathbf{x}$: $$x_1 = 2z_1, \quad x_2 = z_1 + z_2$$ Compute the Jacobian $J_f$, its determinant, and $\log p_X(\mathbf{x})$ at $\mathbf{x} = (2, 2)^\top$.

Jacobian: $J_f = \begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial z_1} & \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial z_2} \\ \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial z_1} & \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial z_2}\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 0 \\ 1 & 1\end{pmatrix}$ (lower triangular)

Determinant: Product of diagonal = $2 \times 1 = 2$.

Inverse mapping: $z_1 = x_1/2$, $z_2 = x_2 - z_1 = x_2 - x_1/2$. At $\mathbf{x} = (2, 2)$: $z_1 = 1$, $z_2 = 2 - 1 = 1$.

Log-density: $\log p_Z(\mathbf{z}) = -\frac{1}{2}(z_1^2 + z_2^2) - \log(2\pi) = -\frac{1}{2}(1 + 1) - \log(2\pi) = -1 - \log(2\pi)$ $\log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(\mathbf{z}) - \log|\det J_f| = -1 - \log(2\pi) - \log 2$

The $-\log|\det J_f| = -\log 2$ penalty reflects that $f$ stretches volume by a factor of 2 (the $x_1$ axis is scaled by 2), so the density at $\mathbf{x}$ is lower than at the corresponding $\mathbf{z}$ by exactly this factor.

Example 2 — RealNVP coupling layer forward and inverse

A RealNVP coupling layer partitions $\mathbf{z} = (z_1, z_2, z_3, z_4)$ into $\mathbf{z}_A = (z_1, z_2)$ and $\mathbf{z}_B = (z_3, z_4)$. The scale and translation networks output: $s(\mathbf{z}_A) = (0.5, -0.5)$ and $t(\mathbf{z}_A) = (1.0, 2.0)$. Given input $\mathbf{z} = (1, 2, 3, 4)$, compute the output $\mathbf{x}$ and the log-det term. Verify the inverse.

Forward pass: $\mathbf{x}_A = \mathbf{z}_A = (1, 2)$ (identity). $x_3 = z_3 \cdot e^{s_1} + t_1 = 3 \cdot e^{0.5} + 1.0 = 3 \times 1.649 + 1.0 = 5.946$ $x_4 = z_4 \cdot e^{s_2} + t_2 = 4 \cdot e^{-0.5} + 2.0 = 4 \times 0.607 + 2.0 = 4.426$ Output: $\mathbf{x} = (1, 2, 5.946, 4.426)$

Log-det: $\log|\det J| = s_1 + s_2 = 0.5 + (-0.5) = 0$. This particular choice of scales happens to be volume-preserving. In general $\log|\det J| = \sum s_i$.

Inverse: $\mathbf{z}_A = \mathbf{x}_A = (1, 2)$ (same, since $\mathbf{x}_A$ is the identity pass). Recompute $s$ and $t$ using $\mathbf{x}_A$: $s = (0.5, -0.5)$, $t = (1.0, 2.0)$. $z_3 = (x_3 - t_1) \cdot e^{-s_1} = (5.946 - 1.0) \cdot e^{-0.5} = 4.946 \times 0.607 = 3.000$ ✓ $z_4 = (x_4 - t_2) \cdot e^{-s_2} = (4.426 - 2.0) \cdot e^{0.5} = 2.426 \times 1.649 = 4.000$ ✓

Example 3 — Flow matching OT path

A flow matching model uses straight-line OT paths. Data point $\mathbf{x}_1 = (3, 4)$, noise sample $\mathbf{x}_0 = (-1, 2)$, time $t = 0.3$. (a) Compute the interpolated point $\mathbf{x}_t$. (b) What is the conditional velocity $u_t(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_1)$? (c) What does the network $v_\theta$ need to predict at this point during training?

(a) $\mathbf{x}_t = (1-t)\mathbf{x}_0 + t\mathbf{x}_1 = 0.7 \times (-1, 2) + 0.3 \times (3, 4) = (-0.7, 1.4) + (0.9, 1.2) = (0.2, 2.6)$

(b) The conditional velocity is: $u_t(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_1) = \mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0 = (3 - (-1), 4 - 2) = (4, 2)$ This is constant along the trajectory — the velocity doesn't change over time for OT paths. The network just needs to learn the direction of each path.

(c) The network $v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) = v_\theta((0.2, 2.6), 0.3)$ must predict $(4, 2)$. At training time, this is the target in the CFM regression loss. Note that the network sees only $\mathbf{x}_t$ and $t$ — it doesn't see $\mathbf{x}_0$ or $\mathbf{x}_1$ directly. Over many training examples, the network learns the average velocity field that, when integrated from $t=0$ to $t=1$, transforms the noise distribution into the data distribution. The marginal velocity (averaged over all possible $\mathbf{x}_0$ paired with each $\mathbf{x}_1$) is the target for the learned velocity field.

Food for thought
  • Normalizing flows have exact likelihoods, fast single-step sampling, and invertible latent representations. These sound like advantages over VAEs (lower bound only) and diffusion (many sampling steps). Yet as of 2025, diffusion and flow matching models (not discrete normalizing flows) dominate image generation. Why?
    Three structural reasons: (1) Architecture constraint vs. expressiveness tradeoff. Discrete flows must have invertible, tractable-Jacobian architectures. This severely limits the design space: coupling layers fix half the dimensions at each step; autoregressive flows are sequential at generation time; invertible 1×1 convolutions can only mix channels, not spatial structure. These constraints prevent using the most powerful modern architectures (standard deep ResNets, attention mechanisms with arbitrary masking). Diffusion models use U-Nets and transformers with no invertibility constraints — they can be as expressive as any discriminative model. (2) The mode-covering vs. mode-seeking tradeoff. Training with maximum likelihood ($\log p(\mathbf{x})$) is mode-covering: the model tries to assign non-zero probability everywhere in the data distribution, including in low-probability regions. This leads to blurry samples or wasted model capacity on rare examples. Diffusion models with score matching are also mode-covering, but their iterative sampling and conditioning techniques (classifier-free guidance) allow sharpening the distribution at inference time. (3) The exact-likelihood trap. High log-likelihood does not correlate with sample quality. GLOW achieves high log-likelihood on ImageNet but generates blurry, unrealistic images. The log-likelihood metric penalises unlikely regions, but humans judge sample quality on typical samples. Models optimised purely for log-likelihood can assign high probability to a set of samples that looks nothing like natural images. Flow matching avoids the first problem (no architecture constraint) while keeping the fast sampling advantage (fewer ODE steps with straight paths). This is why SD3 and Flux use flow matching rather than discrete normalizing flows.
  • The MAF (density evaluation fast) and IAF (sampling fast) are mathematical inverses of each other. Can you use both directions simultaneously to get fast density evaluation AND fast sampling? What system would you need, and does it exist?
    This is the fundamental insight behind Parallel WaveNet (Oord et al. 2017) and the WaveGlow architecture, and more generally the training paradigm called probability density distillation. The basic idea: train a MAF (density-evaluation-fast) as the "teacher" using maximum likelihood on real data. Then train an IAF (sampling-fast) "student" to minimize the KL divergence between the student's distribution and the teacher's distribution: $D_\text{KL}(p_\text{student} \| p_\text{teacher})$. Computing this KL requires evaluating $\log p_\text{teacher}(\mathbf{x})$ for samples $\mathbf{x}$ from the student — which is fast because the teacher is a MAF. The student gets trained to match the teacher without needing sequential decoding at training time. At inference, only the fast-sampling IAF student is used. This approach achieves near-real-time speech synthesis (Parallel WaveNet ran at 500 kHz on a single GPU vs. 1500 steps per second for the sequential WaveNet teacher). The general principle — train a slow exact model, distill into a fast sampling model — also motivates consistency distillation for diffusion models: use a multi-step DDIM as the teacher, train a one-step consistency model to match it. The dual-speed tradeoff is not a fundamental limitation; it is an architectural choice resolved by distillation.
  • Flow matching with OT (straight-line) paths trains the network to predict a constant velocity $\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0$ along each trajectory. The DDPM noise-prediction objective trains the network to predict the noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ that was added. These seem quite different, yet both train a generative model by regression. What is the relationship between "predict the velocity" and "predict the noise"? Can you convert one to the other?
    They are the same model viewed through different parameterisations. In DDPM, the noisy image at time $t$ is: $\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{x}_1 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ is the added noise. In flow matching with OT paths: $\mathbf{x}_t = (1-t)\mathbf{x}_0 + t\mathbf{x}_1$ In both cases, $\mathbf{x}_t$ is a linear interpolation between noise and data, just with different scheduling functions. The velocity in OT flow matching is $\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0$. The velocity at time $t$ in the DDPM probability flow ODE is: $v = \frac{d\mathbf{x}_t}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}[\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{x}_1 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}]$ which is a function of $\dot{\bar{\alpha}}_t$, $\mathbf{x}_1$, and $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$. The $v$-parameterisation of diffusion (DL9 FFT: "predicting velocity") is exactly this conversion: $v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ is trained to predict the velocity of the DDPM probability flow ODE. Converting from noise prediction ($\epsilon_\theta$) to velocity ($v_\theta$) is just a change of variables: $v_\theta = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\epsilon_\theta - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0$ where $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0 = (\mathbf{x}_t - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\epsilon_\theta)/\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}$. The practical advantage of OT/rectified flow is that straight paths have lower curvature than DDPM's curved probability flow ODE trajectories. Lower curvature means fewer ODE steps needed at inference for the same quality. This is the primary reason SD3 and Flux moved to flow matching.
Connects to
Generative Models (DL9) — Normalizing flows complete the major generative model paradigms: VAEs (approximate ELBO), GANs (adversarial), diffusion/DDPM (iterative denoising), and flows (exact invertible transformation). Flow matching unifies flows and diffusion under the CNF framework.
State Space Models (DL10) — Both flows and SSMs are motivated by the same desire to go beyond architectures forced to approximate: flows give exact likelihoods; SSMs give exact recurrence without the quadratic attention cost. Both represent the ongoing effort to find architectures that are simultaneously expressive, tractable, and efficient.
Variational autoencoders (DL9 ELBO) — The ELBO in a VAE is tight when the approximate posterior $q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ exactly matches the true posterior. Using a normalizing flow as the approximate posterior (instead of a diagonal Gaussian) closes this gap: flow-based posteriors can match complex multi-modal posteriors exactly. This combination, "flow posterior VAE", achieves higher log-likelihoods than standard VAEs while retaining the VAE's structured latent space.
