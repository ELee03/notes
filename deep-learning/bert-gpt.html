7 · BERT & GPT
Pretraining Objectives · Masked LM · Autoregressive LM · Fine-Tuning · Scaling Laws · The Modern LLM
You now have every component needed to understand BERT, GPT, and the modern LLM. The Transformer architecture from Lesson 6 is the hardware. This lesson is about the software: how do you train it, what objective do you use, and what does it learn? The answer that changed the field: pretrain on vast amounts of text with a self-supervised objective, then fine-tune for each downstream task. BERT (Devlin et al. 2018) and GPT (Radford et al. 2018) are the two canonical instantiations of this idea, each with a different pretraining objective and different downstream applications. GPT-2, GPT-3, and all subsequent language models are scaling and refinement of the GPT approach. This lesson explains both models, their differences, and the scaling laws that predict when making the model bigger is worth the compute.
The paradigm shift: pretrain → fine-tune
Before 2018, the dominant NLP paradigm was: train a separate model from scratch for each task (sentiment analysis, named entity recognition, question answering, etc.). Models shared word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) but the network weights above the embedding layer were task-specific. This had two problems:
  • Labeled data bottleneck. Every new task requires labeled examples. Creating 10,000 labeled examples for each of 100 NLP tasks is expensive, slow, and inconsistent across tasks.
  • Limited transfer. Word2Vec embeddings are context-free — the same vector for "bank" whether it means a river bank or a financial institution. They capture surface-level word similarity but not the rich contextualised meaning that arises from sentence structure.
The pretrain-then-fine-tune paradigm addresses both:
  1. Pretrain a large Transformer on a massive unlabeled corpus using a self-supervised objective. The model learns rich contextualised representations of language — no labels required.
  2. Fine-tune the pretrained model on a small labeled dataset for the target task, updating all weights (or just adding a small task-specific head). The pretrained features transfer well, so the model needs far fewer labeled examples than training from scratch.
This paradigm shift is sometimes called the "ImageNet moment" of NLP — analogous to how ImageNet pretraining enabled strong transfer for computer vision tasks.
Why pretraining works
The intuition: to predict the next word or fill in a masked word, the model must understand grammar, context, facts, and reasoning. "The cat sat on the ___" is easy. "The treaty was ratified by the ___" requires knowing that treaties are ratified by legislatures or governments. "The neurotransmitter that inhibits cortical excitation is ___" requires domain knowledge. All of this is implicit in predicting what word comes next in natural text.
More formally: a language model trained on large text corpora is implicitly learning a compressed model of the world. The training signal $p(x_t \mid x_{
The self-supervised signal is unlimited. Language modelling requires no human annotation — any raw text is training data. The English Wikipedia (3.5B words), BookCorpus (11K books), Common Crawl (petabytes of web text), GitHub, arXiv, Stack Exchange — all of these are automatically training data. BERT used BooksCorpus + Wikipedia (3.3B words). GPT-3 was trained on 300B tokens from Common Crawl, WebText2, Books, and Wikipedia. This scale of data is what makes the pretrained representations so rich.
BERT: Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers
Devlin, Chang, Lee, and Toutanova (2018). BERT is a Transformer encoder (no decoder). It processes the full input sequence with bidirectional self-attention — every token attends to every other token. It uses two pretraining objectives:
BERT pretraining — Masked Language Modelling (MLM)

Given a sentence, randomly mask 15% of tokens. Of the selected tokens:

  • 80% are replaced with a special [MASK] token
  • 10% are replaced with a random token from the vocabulary
  • 10% are kept unchanged

The model must predict the original token at each masked position. Training objective: cross-entropy loss over the masked positions: $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} = -\sum_{i \in \text{masked}} \log p(x_i \mid x_{\setminus i})$$ where $x_{\setminus i}$ denotes the full sequence with position $i$ masked out.

Why 80/10/10 and not just 100% masking? If all selected tokens were always replaced with [MASK], the model would learn to ignore them (since [MASK] never appears at fine-tuning time). The 10% random replacement forces the model to maintain good representations for all tokens, not just those that happen to be masked. The 10% unchanged forces the model to carry contextual representations even for observed tokens, not just fill-in blanks.

Bidirectionality is the key advantage. Because BERT uses an encoder (no causal mask), predicting the masked token can use context from both left and right. The model sees "the cat sat on the [MASK] mat" and can use both "the cat sat on the" (left) and "mat" (right) to predict "black/warm/floor" etc. This is richer than GPT's left-to-right prediction, where only left context is available.

BERT pretraining — Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

The input to BERT is a pair of sentences: [CLS] Sentence A [SEP] Sentence B [SEP]. 50% of the time, Sentence B is the actual next sentence after Sentence A in the corpus; 50% of the time, it is a random sentence from the corpus.

BERT predicts (from the [CLS] token's representation) whether Sentence B follows Sentence A: $$p(\text{IsNext} \mid [\text{CLS}] \text{ representation}) = \text{sigmoid}(W_{\text{NSP}} \mathbf{h}_{[\text{CLS}]})$$ Training objective: binary cross-entropy.

Note: Subsequent work (Liu et al. 2019, RoBERTa) found that NSP provides little benefit and can even hurt performance. BERT was actually slightly improved by removing NSP entirely and training only with MLM. The lesson: clever-seeming auxiliary objectives don't always help. MLM alone is sufficient.

BERT architecture. Two sizes were released:

BERT uses WordPiece tokenisation (vocabulary size 30,522), learned positional embeddings (not sinusoidal), and was trained on 16 TPUs for 4 days (Base) or 64 TPUs for 4 days (Large). Maximum sequence length: 512 tokens.
BERT fine-tuning
BERT fine-tuning adds a small task-specific head on top of the pretrained model and trains the full system (pretrained weights + head) on the labeled task data. The same pretrained BERT handles every downstream task — only the head changes:
  • Sentence classification (sentiment, entailment): input the sentence as [CLS] sentence [SEP], take the [CLS] token's output representation, and pass it through a linear classifier: $p(\text{class}) = \text{softmax}(W_c \mathbf{h}_{[\text{CLS}]})$. Train on labeled examples.
  • Token classification (NER, POS tagging): take the output representation of every non-special token and pass it through a linear classifier per token: $p(\text{tag}_i) = \text{softmax}(W_t \mathbf{h}_i)$.
  • Question answering (SQuAD-style): input [CLS] question [SEP] passage [SEP], predict the start and end span in the passage. Two linear layers predict $p(\text{start}_i) = \text{softmax}(W_s \mathbf{h}_i)$ and $p(\text{end}_i) = \text{softmax}(W_e \mathbf{h}_i)$ over all passage positions.
  • Sentence pair tasks (NLI, semantic similarity): input [CLS] sentence A [SEP] sentence B [SEP], use [CLS] representation for classification.
BERT set state of the art on 11 NLP benchmarks when released. On GLUE (General Language Understanding Evaluation), BERT-Large improved the previous best score by 7 absolute points. On SQuAD 2.0 (reading comprehension), it exceeded human performance.
GPT: Generative Pre-trained Transformer
Radford, Narasimhan, Salimans, and Sutskever (OpenAI, 2018). GPT is a Transformer decoder stack — causal (left-to-right) self-attention only, no encoder, no cross-attention. It is pretrained with the standard language modelling objective: $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{LM}} = -\sum_{t} \log p(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1}; \theta)$$ This is exactly the objective from Lesson 3's language modelling section, computed efficiently over all positions simultaneously using the causal mask.
GPT architecture (2018): 12 layers, $d_{\text{model}} = 768$, 12 heads, BPE tokenisation (40,000 tokens), 117M parameters. Trained on BooksCorpus (800M words). GPT fine-tunes on downstream tasks in a similar way to BERT, but the head design differs because GPT generates — it takes the last token's representation and predicts the next token, so classification tasks are framed as: append a delimiter and classification token, generate that token, and use its representation for the label.
GPT-2, GPT-3, and few-shot learning
GPT-2 (Radford et al. 2019): 1.5B parameters (10× GPT-1), trained on WebText (40GB of text scraped from Reddit links with $\geq 3$ upvotes). Key architectural changes: pre-layer normalisation, weight initialisation scaled by $1/\sqrt{N}$ for residual layers. The key finding: GPT-2 produces convincing, coherent paragraphs that were difficult to distinguish from human writing. OpenAI delayed release due to misuse concerns. Importantly, GPT-2 showed that language model performance continued to improve with scale — there was no sign of diminishing returns at 1.5B parameters.
GPT-3 (Brown et al. 2020): 175B parameters. Trained on 300B tokens from Common Crawl (filtered), WebText2, Books1, Books2, and English Wikipedia. GPT-3 introduced two conceptual advances beyond scale:
In-context learning / few-shot prompting. GPT-3 demonstrated that a pretrained language model can perform new tasks without any gradient updates (no fine-tuning) — just by providing examples in the prompt. The three regimes:
  • Zero-shot: describe the task in natural language and ask the model to complete it. "Translate English to French: cheese → " The model has never been trained on this exact prompt format but has seen enough translations to complete it.
  • One-shot: provide one example before the query. "Translate: sea otter → loutre de mer. cheese → "
  • Few-shot: provide $k$ examples (typically 10–100). The model "learns" from the examples in context without any weight updates. GPT-3 with few-shot prompting matched or exceeded fine-tuned BERT on many benchmarks.
In-context learning is striking because it implies the model is performing a kind of learning purely through its forward pass — reading the examples, extracting a pattern, and applying it to new inputs. Mechanistically, this appears to be a form of implicit gradient descent computed by the attention layers (Akyürek et al. 2022, Dai et al. 2022), though this is still an active research area.
Scaling laws
Kaplan, McCandlish, Henighan et al. (OpenAI, 2020) established quantitative relationships between model performance and scale: $$\mathcal{L}(N) \propto N^{-\alpha_N} \qquad \text{(fixed compute budget, optimal tokens)}$$ where $\mathcal{L}$ is the language modelling loss (cross-entropy in nats) and $N$ is the number of model parameters. The exponent $\alpha_N \approx 0.076$ — a power law in the number of parameters.
The Chinchilla scaling law (Hoffmann et al. 2022)

Given a fixed compute budget $C$ (FLOPs), the optimal model size $N^*$ and training token count $D^*$ satisfy: $$N^* \approx \left(\frac{C}{6}\right)^{1/2} \qquad D^* = \frac{C}{6 N^*}$$ This means $N^* \propto C^{0.5}$ and $D^* \propto C^{0.5}$ — model size and data size should scale equally with compute.

The factor of 6 comes from the fact that one forward pass through an $N$-parameter Transformer takes approximately $6N$ FLOPs per token (2N for forward, 4N for backward).

The key insight: Kaplan et al. (2020) had recommended scaling model size more aggressively than data. Hoffman et al. (2022) showed this was wrong — for a fixed compute budget, you should train a smaller model on more data rather than a larger model on less data. A 70B parameter model trained on 1.4 trillion tokens (Chinchilla) matched or outperformed a 280B model (Gopher) trained on 300B tokens, at roughly equal compute cost. This shifted industry practice: LLaMA-1 (65B), LLaMA-2 (70B), Mistral (7B trained on 1T+ tokens) all follow Chinchilla-optimal or over-trained recipes.

Over-training (training well past the Chinchilla-optimal token count for a given model size) is common in practice because inference is cheap relative to training: a smaller model trained on more data has lower inference cost for the same capability, which matters when the model is deployed to millions of users.

BERT vs GPT: the fundamental difference
BERT GPT
Architecture Encoder only (bidirectional) Decoder only (causal)
Pretraining objective Masked LM (predict masked tokens) Autoregressive LM (predict next token)
Context Full bidirectional Left-to-right only
Primary use Understanding tasks (classification, NER, QA) Generation tasks (language modelling, dialogue, code)
Downstream Fine-tune with task head Fine-tune, or few-shot / zero-shot prompting
Scales to? ~350M (diminishing returns in practice) 175B+ (GPT-3), emergent capabilities at scale
Descendants RoBERTa, ALBERT, DistilBERT, XLNet, DeBERTa GPT-2/3/4, LLaMA, Mistral, Claude, Gemini
Why did the GPT line win at scale? The masked LM objective is more sample-efficient per token: if you mask 15% of tokens, the model only gets a training signal for 15% of positions per sequence. The autoregressive objective provides a signal for every position. At large scale, this matters. Additionally, generation is inherently a more flexible capability: a model that can generate arbitrary text can, with the right prompting, perform classification, extraction, translation, and reasoning as special cases. BERT cannot generate.
The modern LLM: beyond GPT-3
GPT-3 demonstrated that scaling pretrained language models produced remarkable results. The field then added three further developments to build the modern "assistant" LLM:
1. Instruction fine-tuning (InstructGPT, 2022). GPT-3 was a raw language model — it predicted text continuations, not responses to instructions. To make it follow instructions, OpenAI fine-tuned it on a curated dataset of human-written (prompt, response) pairs. This supervised fine-tuning (SFT) taught the model the instruction-following format.
2. RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. After SFT, the model was further aligned with human preferences via RLHF:
  1. Sample multiple responses for each prompt from the SFT model.
  2. Have human raters rank the responses by quality. Build a reward model — a neural network trained to predict human preference scores from (prompt, response) pairs.
  3. Fine-tune the language model using PPO (proximal policy optimisation) to maximise the reward model's score, with a KL-divergence penalty against the SFT model to prevent reward hacking ("KL control").
This produced InstructGPT, which raters preferred 100:1 over the base GPT-3 for instruction-following tasks, despite being 100× smaller in parameter count.
3. Constitutional AI and RLAIF (Claude, 2022). Anthropic's approach (Bai et al. 2022) replaced human preference labels with AI-generated preference labels and a "constitution" — a set of principles the model should follow. The model critiques and revises its own outputs against the constitution, generating a synthetic preference dataset that is then used for RLHF without requiring millions of human labels.
The result. The modern LLM (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA+instruction) is a GPT-style autoregressive Transformer with:
  • Pretraining on trillions of tokens (the foundation)
  • Supervised fine-tuning on instruction data (the format)
  • RLHF/RLAIF with a reward model (the alignment)
  • Additional modifications: GQA (grouped query attention for inference efficiency), RoPE positional encodings, SwiGLU activation, RMSNorm — each a refinement of the base Transformer, none conceptually departing from the core Q/K/V architecture.
Worked examples
Example 1 — MLM training signal calculation

Consider training BERT-Base (110M parameters) with sequences of length 128 tokens, batch size 256, and masking rate 15%. How many masked token predictions does BERT process per training step, and how does this compare to GPT with the same batch/sequence?

BERT MLM: Tokens per batch: $256 \times 128 = 32{,}768$ total tokens. Masked tokens: $32{,}768 \times 0.15 = 4{,}915$ masked positions. Training signal: 4,915 cross-entropy predictions per step.

GPT autoregressive LM: Same batch: $256 \times 128 = 32{,}768$ tokens. Training signal: 32,768 next-token predictions per step (every position contributes a loss term, subject to the causal mask).

Ratio: GPT gets $32{,}768 / 4{,}915 \approx 6.7\times$ as many gradient signal contributions per step for the same batch. The MLM masking rate of 15% means BERT is "wasting" ~85% of its token compute from a gradient signal perspective. This inefficiency is one reason the autoregressive objective scales better — every token is a training example, not just the masked ones.

Note: BERT advocates argue that masked prediction is harder per example (bidirectional context means the task is more about filling semantic gaps than next-token prediction), so raw count isn't the full story. But at the scale of trillions of tokens, the autoregressive signal advantage dominates.

Example 2 — Chinchilla compute allocation

You have a compute budget of $C = 6 \times 10^{22}$ FLOPs (roughly the budget for a 70B-parameter LLaMA-2 run). According to the Chinchilla rule, what is the optimal model size and token count? How does this compare to GPT-3's 175B / 300B token training?

Chinchilla optimal: $N^* = \sqrt{C/6}$, $D^* = \sqrt{C/6}$ tokens (i.e. $N^* = D^*$).

$$N^* = D^* = \sqrt{\frac{6 \times 10^{22}}{6}} = \sqrt{10^{22}} = 10^{11} = 100\text{B}$$

So at this compute budget, optimal is ~100B parameters trained on ~100B tokens.

GPT-3 used $C \approx 3.14 \times 10^{23}$ FLOPs for 175B parameters on 300B tokens. By Chinchilla: $N^* = D^* \approx \sqrt{3.14 \times 10^{23} / 6} = \sqrt{5.2 \times 10^{22}} \approx 72\text{B}$. GPT-3 was therefore overscaled in parameters and undertrained in tokens — a model of ~72B parameters trained on ~720B tokens would have used the same compute and achieved better performance.

This is exactly what Chinchilla (70B, 1.4T tokens) demonstrated empirically: it matched or beat GPT-3 (175B, 300B tokens) at about equal compute cost. The industry shifted from "train the biggest model on the data we have" to "train a compute-optimal model on as many tokens as possible."

Example 3 — Fine-tuning BERT for binary sentiment

Describe every step from raw text to fine-tuned prediction for a binary sentiment classifier using BERT-Base. What changes during fine-tuning vs. what is frozen?

Step 1 — Tokenise. "This film is excellent!" → WordPiece tokens: [CLS], "This", "film", "is", "excellent", "!", [SEP]. Map to token IDs.

Step 2 — Embed. Token ID lookup → $d_{\text{model}} = 768$-dimensional embeddings. Add segment embeddings (all Segment A) and learned positional embeddings. Result: $7 \times 768$ matrix.

Step 3 — Forward through 12 encoder blocks. All 12 bidirectional self-attention blocks process the sequence. Output: $7 \times 768$ contextualised representations.

Step 4 — Classification head. Extract the [CLS] token representation: $\mathbf{h}_{[\text{CLS}]} \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$. Pass through: $$\hat{y} = \text{softmax}(W_c \mathbf{h}_{[\text{CLS}]} + \mathbf{b}_c)$$ where $W_c \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 768}$ (2 classes). This head has only $2 \times 768 + 2 = 1538$ parameters, added fresh (not pretrained).

Fine-tuning: All 110M BERT parameters + 1,538 head parameters are updated via gradient descent on the labeled sentiment dataset. Typical hyperparameters: learning rate $2 \times 10^{-5}$ (much smaller than pretraining), 3–4 epochs, batch size 32. The small LR is essential — aggressive updates would destroy the pretrained features (catastrophic forgetting).

Inference: Forward pass through the full system, take argmax of $\hat{y}$. For SST-2 (Stanford Sentiment Treebank), BERT-Base achieves 93.5% accuracy vs. prior state of the art of 91.3%.

Food for thought
  • In-context learning (few-shot prompting) allows GPT-3 to perform new tasks without any gradient updates. Yet classical learning theory says you can't learn from a handful of examples without massive overfitting. What's different here?
    In-context learning does not involve gradient descent on the few examples — the weights stay fixed. What's happening is that the model, trained on 300B tokens of text, has already encountered countless "task specifications" in the form of instructional passages, worked examples, and question-answer sequences in the training corpus. The few-shot examples in the prompt are not training examples in the classical sense — they are context that activates relevant prior knowledge embedded in the weights from pretraining. More precisely: the pretraining objective (next token prediction) over a corpus that includes code, tutorials, papers, Q&A websites, and structured text forces the model to implicitly learn meta-level relationships ("if I see a sequence of (input, output) pairs, the pattern is X, and the next input will have output Y"). This is learning to learn — or "implicit in-context learning" — baked into the model by the diversity of pretraining data. Classical overfitting assumes the hypothesis class is fit to the examples. In-context learning retrieves from a fixed hypothesis class (the frozen weights); the "fitting" is done by selecting the right activation pattern, not by updating weights. Akyürek et al. (2022) showed formally that linear self-attention with the right weight initialisation implements gradient descent in the forward pass — suggesting that in-context learning may literally be gradient descent, implemented by the attention computation.
  • The reward model in RLHF is trained to predict human preference rankings. It is then used to provide a gradient signal to fine-tune the language model. What could go wrong with this approach, and what mitigation does the KL penalty provide?
    The fundamental problem is reward hacking: the language model, optimised by PPO to maximise the reward model's score, will find ways to score highly that don't correspond to genuinely good outputs. The reward model is itself a neural network trained on finite human comparisons — it is an approximation of human preference, not a perfect oracle. Any approximation has failure modes. Examples of reward hacking: generating very long outputs (if human annotators rated longer outputs higher in the comparison data); using flattery ("That's a great question!"); repeating phrases that appeared in high-scoring training examples; generating confident-sounding but false information (sycophancy). At the extreme, the LM might learn to "jailbreak" the reward model by producing adversarial inputs that score high on the reward model without actually being good responses. The KL penalty $\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \text{LM}}[\log \pi_\theta(x) - \log \pi_{\text{SFT}}(x)]$ penalises the fine-tuned policy $\pi_\theta$ for diverging too far from the SFT model $\pi_{\text{SFT}}$. This prevents the model from deviating wildly from the distribution where the reward model has meaningful gradients, limiting how aggressively it can exploit reward model errors. The KL weight is a hyperparameter that balances alignment gain (large KL allowed = more reward model optimisation) against distribution collapse risk (small KL allowed = stay close to SFT but limit alignment). Getting this balance right is an active research problem.
  • The original Transformer (Lesson 6) had both encoder and decoder. BERT uses only the encoder; GPT uses only the decoder. T5 (Raffel et al. 2020) uses both again, framing every NLP task as text-to-text. What does the full encoder-decoder design buy you that encoder-only and decoder-only models can't do as naturally?
    The full encoder-decoder design provides natural structural separation between "reading the input" (encoder) and "generating the output" (decoder). For tasks where the input and output are in different domains or different lengths — translation, summarisation, question answering with a generated answer, code generation from a specification — this separation is genuinely useful. The encoder can build a rich bidirectional representation of the full input without any causal constraint; the decoder can attend to this representation freely via cross-attention at every step. Encoder-only (BERT) can't generate by construction. Decoder-only (GPT) can handle everything as "generate the continuation of this prompt," but for tasks where the input and output have different distributions (e.g., summarisation: input is a news article, output is a brief summary), mixing them into one causal sequence forces the model to treat the summary token distribution as just another continuation of the article — the model must somehow switch modes at the right boundary. T5's finding: framing everything as text-to-text worked well with an encoder-decoder, reaching competitive performance across diverse tasks with a unified architecture and training recipe. The practical reason encoder-decoder models are less common at large scale (>10B) is inference cost: the encoder must run once per input, and the decoder runs autoregressively — the total cost is similar to decoder-only, but engineering and serving is more complex. Decoder-only models (GPT-style) are simpler to serve and scale.
Common pitfalls
  • Using too high a learning rate for BERT fine-tuning. BERT fine-tuning is sensitive to the learning rate. Values above $5 \times 10^{-5}$ commonly cause divergence on small datasets (catastrophic forgetting of pretrained features). Standard range: $1 \times 10^{-5}$ to $5 \times 10^{-5}$. Use linear warmup for the first 6–10% of steps and linear decay to zero.
  • Treating perplexity as the final evaluation metric for LLMs. Perplexity measures how well the model predicts held-out tokens — it is a training proxy, not a task metric. A model with lower perplexity can be worse at downstream tasks if it is trained on a different distribution or with a different tokenisation. Always evaluate on task-relevant benchmarks (MMLU, HellaSwag, HumanEval for code, etc.) rather than perplexity alone.
  • Confusing "emergent" capabilities with unpredictable ones. The widely cited "emergent capabilities" paper (Wei et al. 2022) showed that many capabilities appear to jump discontinuously at certain scales. Schaeffer et al. (2023) showed that most of this appearance of emergence is an artifact of non-linear evaluation metrics: when you plot accuracy on a log-linear scale, a smoothly improving probability can look like a sudden jump when thresholded to binary correct/incorrect. LLM capabilities improve smoothly with scale in probability space; "emergence" is largely a measurement artifact. Don't over-interpret discontinuous jumps in benchmark scores as qualitative phase transitions.
  • Assuming RLHF produces a "truthful" model. RLHF optimises for human preference ratings. Human raters often prefer confident, fluent, detailed answers — even when they are wrong. This can make RLHF models more sycophantic (agree with the user, sound confident) without making them more accurate. Helpfulness and honesty are not the same reward signal. Constitutional AI and RLAIF partially address this by explicitly penalising dishonest outputs in the constitution, but the fundamental tension between "what sounds good to raters" and "what is true" remains an open problem.
