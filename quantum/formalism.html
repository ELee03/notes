Formalism & Uncertainty

Hilbert Space · Dirac Notation · Hermitian Operators · Commutators · Generalised Uncertainty Principle

The first five lessons treated quantum mechanics somewhat informally — we solved specific potentials and computed specific expectation values, but always with a sense that we were applying rules without fully understanding where they came from. This lesson pulls back to the mathematical framework underlying everything. Why are observables represented by operators? Why do measurements give eigenvalues? Why is the uncertainty principle not just a practical limitation but a fundamental theorem? The answers come from the formalism: the linear algebra of Hilbert space.

Hilbert Space

Quantum states live in a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ — a vector space with an inner product that is complete (no "holes"). For a single particle in one dimension, $\mathcal{H}$ is the space of square-integrable functions $L^2(\mathbb{R})$: all functions $f(x)$ for which $\int_{-\infty}^\infty |f(x)|^2\,dx < \infty$.

The inner product of two functions is:

$$\langle f,\,g \rangle \equiv \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)^*\,g(x)\,dx$$

This satisfies the axioms of an inner product: it's linear in the second argument, conjugate-linear in the first, and $\langle f,f\rangle \geq 0$ with equality only if $f = 0$ (almost everywhere). The norm of a state is $\|f\| = \sqrt{\langle f,f\rangle}$; normalisation means $\|f\|=1$.

Two functions are orthogonal if $\langle f,g\rangle = 0$. An orthonormal set satisfies $\langle f_m, f_n\rangle = \delta_{mn}$ — this is exactly the condition we verified for the energy eigenstates of the infinite square well. The eigenstates of any Hermitian operator form an orthonormal basis for $\mathcal{H}$.

Dirac Notation

Dirac's bra-ket notation is a cleaner way to write the same linear algebra. A state is a ket $|\psi\rangle$ — think of it as an abstract vector, independent of any particular basis (just like a vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ exists independently of coordinates). The corresponding bra $\langle\psi|$ is the dual vector (the row vector if you think in matrices). The inner product is written:

$$\langle \phi\,|\,\psi\rangle \equiv \int \phi(x)^*\,\psi(x)\,dx$$

In the position basis, $\langle x|\psi\rangle = \psi(x)$ — the wavefunction is the component of $|\psi\rangle$ along the "direction" $|x\rangle$. In momentum space, $\langle p|\psi\rangle = \phi(p)/\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}$ — it's the same state vector, just expressed in a different basis.

Operators as matrices. An operator $\hat{Q}$ maps kets to kets: $\hat{Q}|\psi\rangle = |\chi\rangle$. In a basis $\{|e_n\rangle\}$, the matrix elements are $Q_{mn} = \langle e_m|\hat{Q}|e_n\rangle$. The expectation value is: $$\langle Q\rangle = \langle\psi|\hat{Q}|\psi\rangle = \int \psi^*\,\hat{Q}\psi\,dx$$ This is just the inner product of $\psi$ with $\hat{Q}\psi$ — exactly what we've been computing all along.
Hermitian Operators

An operator $\hat{Q}$ is Hermitian (or self-adjoint) if:

$$\langle f\,|\,\hat{Q}g\rangle = \langle\hat{Q}f\,|\,g\rangle \quad \text{for all } f,g \in \mathcal{H}$$

In matrix terms, $\hat{Q}^\dagger = \hat{Q}$ (the conjugate transpose equals itself). All observables in quantum mechanics are represented by Hermitian operators. Two crucial consequences:

Theorem 1: Eigenvalues of Hermitian operators are real.
Proof: if $\hat{Q}f = qf$, then $q\langle f,f\rangle = \langle f,\hat{Q}f\rangle = \langle\hat{Q}f,f\rangle = q^*\langle f,f\rangle$. Since $\langle f,f\rangle > 0$, we have $q = q^*$, so $q\in\mathbb{R}$. ✓

This is the reason Hermitian operators represent observables: a measurement must return a real number.

Theorem 2: Eigenfunctions belonging to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
Proof: suppose $\hat{Q}f = q_1 f$ and $\hat{Q}g = q_2 g$ with $q_1\neq q_2$. Then $q_1\langle f,g\rangle = \langle\hat{Q}f,g\rangle = \langle f,\hat{Q}g\rangle = q_2\langle f,g\rangle$. Since $q_1 \neq q_2$, we need $\langle f,g\rangle = 0$. ✓

The Spectral Theorem and Measurement

The central theorem of quantum mechanics:

Spectral theorem (informal). The (normalised) eigenfunctions of a Hermitian operator form a complete orthonormal set. Any state in $\mathcal{H}$ can be expanded as: $$|\psi\rangle = \sum_n c_n |f_n\rangle \quad\text{or}\quad |\psi\rangle = \int c(z)|f_z\rangle\,dz$$ (discrete or continuous spectrum), where $c_n = \langle f_n|\psi\rangle$.

This is completeness — the set $\{|f_n\rangle\}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{H}$. It generalises the familiar notion that any vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a sum of components along basis vectors.

Measurement postulate (generalised). If you measure observable $\hat{Q}$ on a state $|\psi\rangle = \sum_n c_n|f_n\rangle$:

  • The only possible results are the eigenvalues $q_n$.
  • The probability of getting $q_n$ is $|c_n|^2 = |\langle f_n|\psi\rangle|^2$.
  • Immediately after measuring $q_n$, the state collapses to $|f_n\rangle$.

The normalisation condition $\sum_n|c_n|^2 = 1$ (or $\int|c(z)|^2\,dz = 1$) is automatic from $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1$ and the orthonormality of eigenfunctions.

Example 1 — Expanding a state and computing measurement probabilities

A particle in an infinite square well (width $a$) is in the state:

$$\psi(x,0) = A\left[3\psi_1(x) - 4i\psi_2(x)\right]$$

(a) Find $A$ from normalisation.

Since the $\psi_n$ are orthonormal: $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = |A|^2(|3|^2 + |-4i|^2) = |A|^2(9+16) = 25|A|^2 = 1$, so $A = 1/5$.

(b) What are the possible energies and their probabilities?

$c_1 = 3/5$, $c_2 = -4i/5$, all other $c_n = 0$. So:

$$P(E_1) = |3/5|^2 = 9/25, \qquad P(E_2) = |-4i/5|^2 = 16/25$$

(These sum to 1.) If we measure and get $E_1$, the state collapses to $\psi_1(x)e^{-iE_1t/\hbar}$ thereafter.

(c) Compute $\langle H\rangle$.

$$\langle H\rangle = \frac{9}{25}E_1 + \frac{16}{25}E_2 = \frac{9}{25}\cdot\frac{\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2} + \frac{16}{25}\cdot\frac{4\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2} = \frac{\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2}\cdot\frac{9+64}{25} = \frac{73\pi^2\hbar^2}{50ma^2}$$
Commutators and Compatible Observables

The commutator of two operators is:

$$[\hat{A},\hat{B}] \equiv \hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A}$$

If $[\hat{A},\hat{B}] = 0$, the operators commute. The fundamental commutation relation in quantum mechanics is:

$$[\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar$$

Proof: Apply to an arbitrary test function $f(x)$: $[\hat{x},\hat{p}]f = x(-i\hbar f') - (-i\hbar)(xf)' = x(-i\hbar f') + i\hbar(f + xf') = i\hbar f$. ✓

Why does the commutator matter? Because it controls whether two observables can be simultaneously definite. More precisely:

Compatible observables theorem. Two Hermitian operators $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{B}$ have a complete set of simultaneous eigenfunctions — states with definite values for both — if and only if $[\hat{A},\hat{B}] = 0$.

If you can simultaneously diagonalize $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{B}$, measuring one doesn't disturb the other. If $[\hat{A},\hat{B}] \neq 0$, there is no state with both $\sigma_A = 0$ and $\sigma_B = 0$ simultaneously — this is the deep reason for the uncertainty principle.

Some useful commutator identities (provable directly from the definition):

$$[\hat{A},\hat{B}] = -[\hat{B},\hat{A}], \quad [\hat{A},\hat{B}+\hat{C}] = [\hat{A},\hat{B}]+[\hat{A},\hat{C}]$$ $$[\hat{A},\hat{B}\hat{C}] = [\hat{A},\hat{B}]\hat{C} + \hat{B}[\hat{A},\hat{C}]$$

From these you can derive, for example, $[\hat{x},\hat{p}^n] = i\hbar n\hat{p}^{n-1}$ and $[\hat{x}^n,\hat{p}] = i\hbar n\hat{x}^{n-1}$, and in general $[\hat{x},f(\hat{p})] = i\hbar f'(\hat{p})$ and $[\hat{p}, g(\hat{x})] = -i\hbar g'(\hat{x})$.

The Generalised Uncertainty Principle

We now prove a general bound on the product of standard deviations of any two observables. Define the deviation operators $\hat{\alpha} \equiv \hat{A} - \langle A\rangle$ and $\hat{\beta} \equiv \hat{B} - \langle B\rangle$. Then $\sigma_A^2 = \langle\alpha^2\rangle = \|\hat\alpha|\psi\rangle\|^2$ and similarly for $B$.

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, $\langle\hat\alpha f|\hat\alpha f\rangle\langle\hat\beta f|\hat\beta f\rangle \geq |\langle\hat\alpha f|\hat\beta f\rangle|^2$. For any complex number $z$, $|z|^2 \geq (\text{Im}\,z)^2 = \left(\frac{z - z^*}{2i}\right)^2$. Writing $z = \langle\hat\alpha f|\hat\beta f\rangle$:

$$\sigma_A^2\,\sigma_B^2 \geq \left(\frac{\langle[\hat{A},\hat{B}]\rangle}{2i}\right)^2$$

Taking the positive square root:

$$\boxed{\sigma_A\,\sigma_B \geq \frac{1}{2}\left|\langle[\hat{A},\hat{B}]\rangle\right|}$$

This is the generalised uncertainty principle. Applying it to $\hat{A} = \hat{x}$, $\hat{B} = \hat{p}$ with $[\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar$:

$$\sigma_x\,\sigma_p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

This is not a statement about measurement disturbance — it's a statement about any state. Even if you never touch the particle, any state must have $\sigma_x\sigma_p \geq \hbar/2$. The inequality is an equality (minimum uncertainty) for Gaussian states — exactly what we found for the harmonic oscillator ground state and Gaussian wave packets.

Example 2 — Uncertainty principle for position and energy

Compute $[\hat{x},\hat{H}]$ for $\hat{H} = \hat{p}^2/2m + V(\hat{x})$.

Since $[\hat{x},V(\hat{x})] = 0$ (they're both functions of $\hat{x}$):

$$[\hat{x},\hat{H}] = \left[\hat{x},\frac{\hat{p}^2}{2m}\right] = \frac{1}{2m}[\hat{x},\hat{p}^2]$$

Using the product rule: $[\hat{x},\hat{p}^2] = [\hat{x},\hat{p}]\hat{p} + \hat{p}[\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar\hat{p} + i\hbar\hat{p} = 2i\hbar\hat{p}$. So:

$$[\hat{x},\hat{H}] = \frac{i\hbar\hat{p}}{m}$$

The generalised uncertainty principle then gives:

$$\sigma_x\,\sigma_H \geq \frac{1}{2}\left|\frac{i\hbar\langle\hat{p}\rangle}{m}\right| = \frac{\hbar|\langle p\rangle|}{2m}$$

This has a nice interpretation: if the particle has mean velocity $|\langle p\rangle|/m \neq 0$, there's a constraint between position uncertainty and energy uncertainty. For a stationary state (which has $\langle p\rangle = 0$, since the probability current $j \propto \langle p\rangle$ must be zero for a real wavefunction), the bound is trivial — and indeed stationary states can have any $\sigma_x$.

The Energy–Time Uncertainty Principle

There's a famous relation $\Delta E\,\Delta t \geq \hbar/2$ that looks like the position-momentum uncertainty principle. But there's a crucial difference: time is not an operator in quantum mechanics. There is no Hermitian operator $\hat{t}$ acting on the Hilbert space — time is a parameter, not an observable.

What does $\Delta t$ mean then? It's the characteristic time for the state to change appreciably. Define $\Delta t$ as the time for the expectation value of some observable $\hat{Q}$ to change by one standard deviation $\sigma_Q$:

$$\Delta t \equiv \frac{\sigma_Q}{|d\langle Q\rangle/dt|}$$

Using $d\langle Q\rangle/dt = (i/\hbar)\langle[\hat{H},\hat{Q}]\rangle$ (for time-independent $\hat{Q}$) and the generalised uncertainty principle on $\hat{H}$ and $\hat{Q}$:

$$\sigma_H\,\sigma_Q \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle[\hat{H},\hat{Q}]\rangle| = \frac{\hbar}{2}\left|\frac{d\langle Q\rangle}{dt}\right|$$

Dividing both sides by $|d\langle Q\rangle/dt|$:

$$\sigma_H\,\Delta t \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

where $\sigma_H = \Delta E$. This is the energy-time uncertainty principle, and $\Delta t$ is the "lifetime" of the state — how long it takes to change. A state that changes quickly (small $\Delta t$) must have a large energy spread. A stationary state has $\Delta t = \infty$ (it never changes) and $\Delta E = 0$ (definite energy) — perfectly consistent.

Applications of $\Delta E\,\Delta t \geq \hbar/2$.
  • Spectral linewidth. An atomic excited state with lifetime $\tau \sim 10^{-8}$ s has an energy uncertainty $\Delta E \sim \hbar/2\tau \sim 3\times10^{-8}\,\text{eV}$. This sets the natural linewidth of spectral lines — you can't resolve a transition more precisely than the intrinsic width of the levels.
  • Virtual particles. In QFT, a particle can "borrow" energy $\Delta E$ from the vacuum for a time $\Delta t \sim \hbar/(2\Delta E)$ — this is the heuristic basis for Feynman diagram intermediate states. (The rigorous statement involves propagators, not this simple picture.)
  • Unstable particles. A particle that decays in time $\tau$ has a rest-mass uncertainty (width) $\Gamma = \hbar/\tau$. The $Z$ boson has $\Gamma \approx 2.5\,\text{GeV}$, implying a lifetime $\tau \approx 3\times10^{-25}$ s.

Discrete and Continuous Spectra

So far we've mostly dealt with discrete spectra — bound states with a countable set of energy eigenvalues. But operators can also have continuous spectra. The position operator $\hat{x}$ has eigenvalues $x' \in \mathbb{R}$ with "eigenfunctions" $\delta(x-x')$ — unnormalisable. Similarly, the free-particle momentum operator has eigenfunctions $e^{ikx}/\sqrt{2\pi}$ — also unnormalisable.

For continuous spectra, the expansion becomes an integral:

$$|\psi\rangle = \int c(z)|f_z\rangle\,dz, \qquad c(z) = \langle f_z|\psi\rangle, \qquad \int |c(z)|^2\,dz = 1$$

The probability of measuring $z$ in the interval $(z, z+dz)$ is $|c(z)|^2\,dz$. The orthonormality relation for continuous eigenfunctions uses a Dirac delta: $\langle f_z|f_{z'}\rangle = \delta(z-z')$ — "Dirac orthonormality".

In the position basis, $c(x') = \langle x'|\psi\rangle = \psi(x')$ — the wavefunction is the set of expansion coefficients in the position eigenstate basis. In the momentum basis, $c(k) = \langle k|\psi\rangle = \phi(k)/\sqrt{2\pi}$ — the momentum-space wavefunction is the coefficients in the momentum eigenstate basis. Fourier's theorem says these two representations carry identical information.

Worked Examples
Example 3 — Commutators and the angular momentum algebra preview

Compute $[\hat{x},\hat{p}^2]$ using the product rule, and use it to verify Ehrenfest's theorem.

Step 1: $[\hat{x},\hat{p}^2] = [\hat{x},\hat{p}]\hat{p} + \hat{p}[\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar\hat{p} + i\hbar\hat{p} = 2i\hbar\hat{p}$.

Step 2: For $\hat{H} = \hat{p}^2/2m + V(\hat{x})$:

$$[\hat{x},\hat{H}] = \frac{1}{2m}[\hat{x},\hat{p}^2] + [\hat{x},V] = \frac{i\hbar\hat{p}}{m} + 0 = \frac{i\hbar\hat{p}}{m}$$

Step 3: The Heisenberg equation of motion for $\hat{x}$ (Lesson 2 result, derived from the TDSE):

$$\frac{d\langle\hat{x}\rangle}{dt} = \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle[\hat{H},\hat{x}]\rangle = \frac{i}{\hbar}\left\langle-\frac{i\hbar\hat{p}}{m}\right\rangle = \frac{\langle\hat{p}\rangle}{m}$$

This is Ehrenfest's theorem — the expectation value of position moves at the classical velocity $\langle p\rangle/m$. Same calculation gives $d\langle\hat{p}\rangle/dt = -\langle\partial V/\partial x\rangle$. Both results follow purely from the canonical commutation relation $[\hat{x},\hat{p}]=i\hbar$.

Example 4 — Minimum uncertainty states

The generalised uncertainty principle is an equality when the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is tight and the imaginary part of $\langle\hat\alpha f|\hat\beta f\rangle$ dominates. The condition for equality is:

$$\hat\beta|f\rangle = ic\,\hat\alpha|f\rangle \quad\text{for some real } c$$

For position-momentum, $\hat\alpha = \hat{x} - \langle x\rangle$ and $\hat\beta = \hat{p} - \langle p\rangle$, so the condition becomes:

$$(\hat{p} - \langle p\rangle)\psi = ic(\hat{x} - \langle x\rangle)\psi$$
$$-i\hbar\frac{d\psi}{dx} = (ic\,x + \langle p\rangle - ic\langle x\rangle)\psi$$

Separating and integrating: $\ln\psi = -(c/2\hbar)x^2 + (\langle p\rangle/i\hbar)x + \text{const}$. Exponentiating (with $c > 0$ for normalisability):

$$\psi(x) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{c}{2\hbar}x^2 + \frac{i\langle p\rangle}{\hbar}x\right)$$

This is a Gaussian centred at $\langle x\rangle = 0$ (by choice of origin) with $\sigma_x = \sqrt{\hbar/(2c)}$, carrying mean momentum $\langle p\rangle$. Every minimum-uncertainty state is a Gaussian. This confirms that the harmonic oscillator ground state (a Gaussian) and Gaussian wave packets are the only states that saturate $\sigma_x\sigma_p = \hbar/2$.

Food for Thought
  • The position operator $\hat{x}$ has a continuous spectrum and its "eigenfunctions" are Dirac delta functions, which aren't in $L^2(\mathbb{R})$. Yet we freely use position states and eigenfunctions in calculations. How do we reconcile this with the Hilbert space framework?
    Rigorously, you need the theory of "rigged Hilbert spaces" (Gelfand triples): $\Phi \subset \mathcal{H} \subset \Phi'$, where $\Phi$ is a space of smooth, rapidly decreasing functions (Schwartz space) and $\Phi'$ is its dual (which includes distributions like $\delta(x-x')$). Physical states live in $\mathcal{H}$; "generalised eigenstates" like $|x\rangle$ live in $\Phi'$. Measurements are then described by projections onto this extended space. In practice, every real physical state has a finite-width probability distribution — pure position eigenstates are idealisations, just like plane waves are idealisations. The framework holds together rigorously, but you need the extra mathematical machinery.
  • If two operators commute, they share a complete set of simultaneous eigenstates. But $[\hat{H},\hat{p}] = 0$ for a free particle, and indeed both are diagonalised by plane waves. What breaks down for the infinite square well — why doesn't $[\hat{H},\hat{p}] = 0$ hold there even though $V = 0$ inside?
    For the infinite square well, the Hamiltonian is defined on the restricted domain of functions that vanish at the walls — the boundary conditions are part of the operator definition, not just the potential. The momentum operator $\hat{p} = -i\hbar\partial/\partial x$ on this domain is not Hermitian (integration by parts gives boundary terms), and it doesn't have a complete set of real eigenvalues on this domain. So $\hat{p}$ is not a valid observable for the infinite square well, and asking whether $[\hat{H},\hat{p}]=0$ doesn't quite make sense. The well's energy eigenstates are standing waves (superpositions of $e^{ikx}$ and $e^{-ikx}$), not momentum eigenstates.
  • The uncertainty principle $\sigma_x\sigma_p \geq \hbar/2$ is derived from the commutator $[\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar$, which is a property of the operators. But sigma here depends on the state. So the uncertainty principle is really a statement that different states can't make both uncertainties small simultaneously. Is there a single state for which both $\sigma_x = 0$ and $\sigma_p = 0$ individually (not just their product)?
    No — you cannot have both $\sigma_x = 0$ and $\sigma_p = 0$ for any state in the Hilbert space. $\sigma_x = 0$ would mean the wavefunction is a delta function (perfect position localisation), which requires all momenta equally (infinite momentum spread). $\sigma_p = 0$ means a plane wave (infinite position spread). Neither state is normalisable. The uncertainty principle tells us that trying to localise in position inevitably broadens the momentum distribution, and vice versa — this is not about noise or measurement error, but about what it means to be a quantum state in the first place.
Common Pitfalls
  • The uncertainty principle is not about measurement disturbance. The common story — "measuring position disturbs momentum" — is Heisenberg's original (wrong) heuristic. The rigorous statement is about the statistical spread of outcomes in an ensemble of identically prepared systems. Even if you could measure without any disturbance, the uncertainty principle would still hold.
  • $\langle[\hat{A},\hat{B}]\rangle$ is state-dependent — the bound can be zero for specific states. The generalised uncertainty principle says $\sigma_A\sigma_B \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle[\hat{A},\hat{B}]\rangle|$. For $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{p}$, $[\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar$ is a c-number (not operator-valued), so the bound $\hbar/2$ is state-independent. But for angular momentum, $[\hat{L}_x,\hat{L}_y] = i\hbar\hat{L}_z$, and the bound $\frac{\hbar}{2}|\langle\hat{L}_z\rangle|$ depends on the state — it's zero for states with $\langle L_z\rangle = 0$.
  • Hermitian $\neq$ self-adjoint (in infinite dimensions). In finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces these coincide. In infinite dimensions, an operator must also have the same domain as its adjoint to be truly self-adjoint. The difference matters for operators like the momentum operator on an interval. In most introductory physics, "Hermitian" is used loosely to mean "self-adjoint," and the distinction rarely matters for first-year quantum mechanics.
  • Collapse is to a single eigenfunction only for non-degenerate eigenvalues. If $q_n$ is degenerate (multiple eigenstates with the same eigenvalue), measuring $q_n$ collapses the state to the projection onto the degenerate subspace — not to a single eigenfunction. You need a second compatible measurement to determine the state fully.
