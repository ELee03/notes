The Harmonic Oscillator

Ladder Operators · Algebraic Solution · Energy Levels · Zero-Point Energy · Matrix Elements

The quantum harmonic oscillator is the most important exactly-solvable system in all of physics. Any potential that has a stable equilibrium can be approximated as a harmonic oscillator near that minimum — so the results here apply to molecular vibrations, phonons in solids, photons in a cavity, and the zero-point fluctuations of quantum fields. Unlike the square well, the harmonic oscillator also has the right structure to generalise: the algebraic method introduced here — with raising and lowering operators — underlies angular momentum, spin, and quantum field theory.

The potential is $V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2$, giving the TISE:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\psi = E\psi$$
The Algebraic Method: Ladder Operators

The Hamiltonian can be written $\hat{H} = \hat{p}^2/2m + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2\hat{x}^2$. We factor it by introducing the raising ($\hat{a}_+$) and lowering ($\hat{a}_-$) operators:

$$\hat{a}_\pm \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar m\omega}}\left(\mp i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x}\right)$$

Their product is:

$$\hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ = \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{H} + \frac{1}{2}, \qquad \hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- = \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{H} - \frac{1}{2}$$

so the Hamiltonian is:

$$\hat{H} = \hbar\omega\!\left(\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- + \frac{1}{2}\right) = \hbar\omega\!\left(\hat{N} + \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

where $\hat{N} \equiv \hat{a}_+\hat{a}_-$ is the number operator. The key commutator is $[\hat{a}_-, \hat{a}_+] = 1$, which follows from $[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$.

Now the crucial result: if $\psi$ is an eigenstate of $\hat{H}$ with energy $E$, then $\hat{a}_+\psi$ is an eigenstate with energy $E + \hbar\omega$, and $\hat{a}_-\psi$ is an eigenstate with energy $E - \hbar\omega$. The ladder operators step up and down the energy ladder in increments of $\hbar\omega$.

Proof: $\hat{H}(\hat{a}_\pm\psi) = (E \pm \hbar\omega)(\hat{a}_\pm\psi)$. This follows from $[\hat{H}, \hat{a}_\pm] = \pm\hbar\omega\,\hat{a}_\pm$, which is a consequence of $[\hat{a}_-, \hat{a}_+] = 1$.

The Ground State and Quantised Energies

The ladder cannot extend downward forever — energy must be positive (since $V \geq 0$ and $T \geq 0$). There must be a lowest rung: a ground state $\psi_0$ for which $\hat{a}_-\psi_0 = 0$. Explicitly:

$$\hat{a}_-\psi_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar m\omega}}\!\left(i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x}\right)\psi_0 = 0$$ $$\frac{d\psi_0}{dx} = -\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}x\,\psi_0 \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \psi_0(x) = A_0\,e^{-m\omega x^2/2\hbar}$$

Normalising: $A_0 = (m\omega/\pi\hbar)^{1/4}$. The ground state is a Gaussian. Its energy is $E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega$, confirmed by $\hat{H}\psi_0 = \hbar\omega(\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- + \frac{1}{2})\psi_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\,\psi_0$.

Applying $\hat{a}_+$ repeatedly generates all higher eigenstates:

$$\psi_n(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n!}}(\hat{a}_+)^n \psi_0 \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad E_n = \hbar\omega\!\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

The energy spectrum is equally spaced with spacing $\hbar\omega$, and the ground state has energy $E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega > 0$ — the zero-point energy. Note the contrast with the square well: here $E_n \propto n$ (linear), vs $E_n \propto n^2$ there.

The Wavefunctions

Applying $\hat{a}_+$ to $\psi_0$ generates wavefunctions involving Hermite polynomials $H_n$:

$$\psi_n(x) = \left(\frac{m\omega}{\pi\hbar}\right)^{1/4}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n n!}}\,H_n(\xi)\,e^{-\xi^2/2}, \quad \xi \equiv \sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}}\,x$$

The first few Hermite polynomials are $H_0 = 1$, $H_1 = 2\xi$, $H_2 = 4\xi^2 - 2$, $H_3 = 8\xi^3 - 12\xi$. Each $\psi_n$ is a polynomial of degree $n$ times the Gaussian envelope $e^{-\xi^2/2}$. Key properties:

  • $\psi_n$ has exactly $n$ nodes.
  • For even $n$, $\psi_n$ is an even function; for odd $n$, an odd function.
  • The wavefunction extends into the classically forbidden region ($|x| > \sqrt{2E/m\omega^2}$) — tunneling into the potential walls.
  • For large $n$, $|\psi_n|^2$ is highest near the classical turning points, matching the classical probability distribution (correspondence principle).
Harmonic Oscillator — Energy Levels and Wavefunctions
Matrix Elements of $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{p}$

The ladder operators give elegant expressions for $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{p}$:

$$\hat{x} = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}(\hat{a}_+ + \hat{a}_-), \qquad \hat{p} = i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}(\hat{a}_+ - \hat{a}_-)$$

The action of the ladder operators on energy eigenstates is:

$$\hat{a}_+\psi_n = \sqrt{n+1}\,\psi_{n+1}, \qquad \hat{a}_-\psi_n = \sqrt{n}\,\psi_{n-1}$$

This gives the non-zero matrix elements immediately. For position:

$$\langle m | \hat{x} | n \rangle = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}\left(\sqrt{n+1}\,\delta_{m,n+1} + \sqrt{n}\,\delta_{m,n-1}\right)$$

So $\hat{x}$ only connects states that differ by exactly one quantum number. This is the origin of the quantum harmonic oscillator selection rule $\Delta n = \pm 1$, which governs vibrational absorption and emission in molecules. Similarly:

$$\langle n|\hat{x}|n\rangle = 0, \quad \langle n|\hat{p}|n\rangle = 0$$

For stationary states of the harmonic oscillator, the mean position and mean momentum are both zero — the particle oscillates symmetrically.

Zero-Point Energy and the Uncertainty Principle

The ground-state energy $E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega$ is a direct consequence of the uncertainty principle. In the ground state:

$$\langle x \rangle = 0, \quad \langle p \rangle = 0, \quad \langle x^2 \rangle = \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}, \quad \langle p^2 \rangle = \frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}$$ $$\sigma_x\sigma_p = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}\cdot\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}} = \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

The ground state of the harmonic oscillator is the minimum-uncertainty state: it saturates the uncertainty principle $\sigma_x\sigma_p = \hbar/2$ exactly. No quantum state can have a smaller product of position and momentum uncertainties. This is why the Gaussian wavefunction appears so often in quantum mechanics — it is the most classical-like quantum state.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Commutator $[\hat{a}_-, \hat{a}_+] = 1$

Verify $[\hat{a}_-, \hat{a}_+] = 1$ using $[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$.

$[\hat{a}_-, \hat{a}_+] = \hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ - \hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- = \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{H} + \frac{1}{2} - \left(\frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{H} - \frac{1}{2}\right) = 1$.

More explicitly, from the definitions: $$\hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ = \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}(i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x})(-i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x}) = \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\!\left(\hat{p}^2 + m^2\omega^2\hat{x}^2 + im\omega[\hat{x},\hat{p}]\right)$$ Since $[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$: $\hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ = \hat{H}/\hbar\omega + \frac{1}{2}$. Similarly $\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- = \hat{H}/\hbar\omega - \frac{1}{2}$. Subtracting: $[\hat{a}_-, \hat{a}_+] = 1$. $\square$

Example 2 — Expectation value $\langle x^2 \rangle$ in the $n$-th state

Find $\langle x^2 \rangle$ for the $n$-th energy eigenstate of the harmonic oscillator.

Using $\hat{x} = \sqrt{\hbar/2m\omega}(\hat{a}_+ + \hat{a}_-)$: $$\hat{x}^2 = \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}(\hat{a}_+ + \hat{a}_-)^2 = \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}\!\left(\hat{a}_+^2 + \hat{a}_-^2 + \hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- + \hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+\right)$$ Taking the expectation value in state $|n\rangle$: the $\hat{a}_+^2$ and $\hat{a}_-^2$ terms give zero (they connect $n$ to $n \pm 2$, which are orthogonal to $n$). The remaining terms: $$\langle n|\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_-|n\rangle = n, \qquad \langle n|\hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+|n\rangle = n+1$$ Therefore: $$\langle x^2 \rangle = \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}(n + n + 1) = \frac{\hbar}{m\omega}\!\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{E_n}{m\omega^2/2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}$$ This makes sense: by the virial theorem, $\langle V \rangle = \langle T \rangle = E_n/2$, and $\langle V \rangle = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2\langle x^2\rangle = E_n/2$ gives $\langle x^2\rangle = E_n/m\omega^2 \cdot 1 = \hbar(n+\frac{1}{2})/m\omega$. ✓

Example 3 — Diatomic molecule vibrational spectrum

HCl has a vibrational frequency $\nu = 8.66 \times 10^{13}$ Hz (in the infrared). Model the vibration as a quantum harmonic oscillator. (a) What is the ground-state vibrational energy? (b) What wavelength photon is emitted in the $n=1 \to n=0$ transition?

(a) $E_0 = \frac{1}{2}h\nu = \frac{1}{2}(6.626\times10^{-34})(8.66\times10^{13}) = 2.87\times10^{-20}$ J $= 0.179$ eV. Even at absolute zero, the molecule vibrates with this zero-point energy.

(b) The energy of the emitted photon equals the level spacing $\Delta E = h\nu = 2E_0 = 5.74\times10^{-20}$ J. Wavelength: $\lambda = c/\nu = (3\times10^8)/(8.66\times10^{13}) = 3.46\,\mu$m — mid-infrared, matching the observed HCl absorption band.

The selection rule $\Delta n = \pm 1$ (from the matrix element structure above) means only adjacent levels are connected by electric dipole transitions, giving a single absorption/emission line — the fundamental vibration band.

Fast-Fire Thinking
  • The harmonic oscillator energy levels are equally spaced by $\hbar\omega$. The infinite square well has levels spaced by $E_n - E_{n-1} = (2n-1)E_1$, which grows. What physical difference causes this?

    The potential shape. In the infinite square well, the walls are vertical — confining the particle more and more effectively as it gains energy, since the particle always bounces between the same walls. The only energy scale is $\hbar^2/2ma^2$, and higher modes simply fit more half-wavelengths into the same box, so $k_n \propto n$ and $E_n \propto k_n^2 \propto n^2$. In the harmonic oscillator, the walls are soft — they get steeper as the particle climbs. A particle at higher energy has a wider classical turning range $x_\text{max} = \sqrt{2E/m\omega^2} \propto \sqrt{n}$, so the effective box width grows as $\sqrt{n}$, partially cancelling the effect of more nodes, and resulting in equal spacing. Mathematically, this reflects the equally-spaced action variable $J = E/\omega$ of the classical oscillator.

  • The raising operator $\hat{a}_+$ generates excited states from $\psi_0$. Does applying $\hat{a}_+$ an infinite number of times generate infinitely many valid states?

    Yes, and they are all normalisable. Each application generates $\psi_n = (\hat{a}_+)^n\psi_0/\sqrt{n!}$, which is normalisable because the Gaussian $e^{-m\omega x^2/2\hbar}$ decays faster than any polynomial grows. The spectrum is genuinely infinite (unbounded above) — the harmonic oscillator has infinitely many bound states. This contrasts with, say, the hydrogen atom, where there are infinitely many bound states converging to a finite ionisation energy. The harmonic oscillator has no continuum — every eigenstate is bound.

  • For the ground state, $\sigma_x \sigma_p = \hbar/2$ exactly. What does it mean for a state to "saturate" the uncertainty principle?

    The uncertainty principle $\sigma_x\sigma_p \geq \hbar/2$ is a lower bound. Most states have a much larger product — for example the square well ground state has $\sigma_x\sigma_p > \hbar/2$. Saturation occurs only for states whose wavefunction is a Gaussian (in position space, which corresponds to a Gaussian in momentum space as well). The harmonic oscillator ground state $\psi_0 \propto e^{-m\omega x^2/2\hbar}$ is a Gaussian, so it saturates the bound. These Gaussian minimum-uncertainty states are called coherent states — they are the quantum states that most closely mimic classical motion. Coherent states of the electromagnetic field describe laser light.

Pitfalls
  • The ground state is $n = 0$, not $n = 1$. Unlike the square well (where $n=0$ is excluded), the harmonic oscillator ground state is $\psi_0$ with energy $E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega$. The quantum numbers run $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$
  • $\hat{a}_\pm$ are not Hermitian. They are each other's adjoint: $(\hat{a}_+)^\dagger = \hat{a}_-$. The number operator $\hat{N} = \hat{a}_+\hat{a}_-$ is Hermitian (it is the one that corresponds to a physical observable — the occupation number). Never use $\hat{a}_\pm$ as observables.
  • The classical turning point is not a hard wall. The wavefunction does not vanish at $x = \pm\sqrt{2E/m\omega^2}$ — it decays exponentially beyond that point, but is nonzero. This is quantum tunneling into the classically forbidden region, and it has measurable consequences (zero-point fluctuations of crystal lattices, for example).
  • Equal spacing is specific to the perfect harmonic oscillator. Real molecular potentials (Morse potential, etc.) are anharmonic — the energy levels are not exactly equally spaced, and higher transitions differ in frequency. Quantum chemistry corrections to the harmonic approximation are the anharmonic corrections.
