The WKB Approximation

Semiclassical Methods · Turning Points · Bohr-Sommerfeld Quantization · Alpha Decay

Between the exactly solvable potentials (square well, harmonic oscillator, hydrogen) and the perturbative regime lies a broad landscape of problems where the potential changes slowly on the scale of the de Broglie wavelength. In this semiclassical regime, the particle behaves almost classically — it has a well-defined local momentum — with quantum corrections that show up as a slowly-varying phase. The WKB approximation (Wentzel–Kramers–Brillouin, 1926) makes this precise and delivers two major results: a quantization condition for bound states that reproduces Bohr's old quantum theory (and goes further), and a formula for tunneling rates through smooth barriers that is exact to exponential order. These results are used throughout nuclear physics (alpha decay lifetimes), atomic physics (ionization by laser fields), and condensed matter (band theory, Zener tunneling).

The Classical Limit and the de Broglie Wavelength

The key parameter controlling the WKB approximation is how quickly the potential (and hence the local de Broglie wavelength) changes. Define the local momentum:

$$p(x) \equiv \sqrt{2m(E - V(x))}$$

and the local de Broglie wavelength $\lambda(x) = h/p(x) = 2\pi\hbar/p(x)$. In classically allowed regions ($E > V$), $p(x)$ is real and the wavefunction oscillates. In classically forbidden regions ($E < V$), $p(x) = i\kappa(x)$ is imaginary and the wavefunction decays (or grows) exponentially.

The WKB approximation is valid when the potential varies slowly on the scale of $\lambda$:

$$\left|\frac{d\lambda}{dx}\right| \ll 1 \implies \left|\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{p(x)}\right)\right| \ll \frac{1}{\hbar} \implies \left|\frac{dp/dx}{p^2}\right| \ll \frac{1}{\hbar}$$

Physically: if the potential changes so slowly that the wavelength barely shifts over one wavelength, the particle "doesn't know" it's in a varying potential — it just sees a locally uniform medium. This is the semiclassical regime.

The approximation fails near turning points, where $p(x) \to 0$ and $\lambda \to \infty$. At $E = V(x_0)$, the classical particle momentarily stops and reverses; quantum mechanically, the wavefunction transitions from oscillatory to exponential, and $\lambda$ diverges — the "slow variation" condition breaks down catastrophically. Handling turning points carefully (via the connection formulae) is the main technical challenge of WKB.

The WKB Wavefunctions

Start from the time-independent Schrödinger equation:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = (E - V(x))\psi \implies \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = -\frac{p(x)^2}{\hbar^2}\psi$$

Write $\psi(x) = e^{i\phi(x)/\hbar}$ (allowing $\phi$ to be complex to handle both oscillatory and decaying regions). Substituting:

$$\frac{i}{\hbar}\frac{d^2\phi}{dx^2} - \frac{1}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{d\phi}{dx}\right)^2 = -\frac{p(x)^2}{\hbar^2}$$

Expand $\phi = \phi_0 + \hbar\phi_1 + \hbar^2\phi_2 + \cdots$ (WKB is an expansion in $\hbar$, the semiclassical parameter).

Leading order ($\hbar^0$): $(\phi_0')^2 = p(x)^2 \implies \phi_0 = \pm\int p(x)\,dx$.

Next order ($\hbar^1$): $i\phi_0'' - 2\phi_0'\phi_1' = 0 \implies \phi_1' = \frac{i\phi_0''}{2\phi_0'} = \frac{i p'}{2p} \implies \phi_1 = \frac{i}{2}\ln p$.

Putting it together, $\psi \approx e^{i(\phi_0 + \hbar\phi_1)/\hbar} = e^{\pm\frac{i}{\hbar}\int p\,dx}\cdot e^{-\frac{1}{2}\ln p} = \frac{C}{\sqrt{p(x)}}\,e^{\pm\frac{i}{\hbar}\int p\,dx}$.

The general WKB solutions are:

Classically allowed ($E > V$, real $p$):

$$\psi(x) \approx \frac{A}{\sqrt{p(x)}}\exp\!\left(\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_{x_0}^x p(x')\,dx'\right) + \frac{B}{\sqrt{p(x)}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_{x_0}^x p(x')\,dx'\right)$$

Classically forbidden ($E < V$, real $\kappa = \sqrt{2m(V-E)}/\hbar$):

$$\psi(x) \approx \frac{C}{\sqrt{\kappa(x)}}\exp\!\left(\int_{x_0}^x \kappa(x')\,dx'\right) + \frac{D}{\sqrt{\kappa(x)}}\exp\!\left(-\int_{x_0}^x \kappa(x')\,dx'\right)$$

The $1/\sqrt{p}$ prefactor has a simple physical interpretation: the probability density is $|\psi|^2 \propto 1/p(x)$. A classical particle moving faster spends less time at each point, so the classical probability density is also $\propto 1/p(x)$ (the quantum and classical distributions agree, as they should in the semiclassical limit — Bohr's correspondence principle).

Connection Formulae at Turning Points

Near a classical turning point $x_0$ (where $V(x_0) = E$), the WKB approximation breaks down. The standard approach: solve the Schrödinger equation exactly in the vicinity of $x_0$ by linearizing the potential, $V(x) \approx V(x_0) + V'(x_0)(x - x_0)$. The exact solutions to the linear potential are Airy functions $\text{Ai}(z)$ and $\text{Bi}(z)$.

By matching the Airy function solutions to the WKB forms on each side of the turning point, we derive the connection formulae. For a turning point $x = a$ on the left (classically forbidden for $x < a$, allowed for $x > a$):

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{\kappa}}\,e^{-\int_x^a \kappa\,dx'} \longleftrightarrow \frac{2}{\sqrt{p}}\cos\!\left(\int_a^x p\,dx' - \frac{\pi}{4}\right)$$
$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{\kappa}}\,e^{+\int_x^a \kappa\,dx'} \longleftrightarrow -\frac{1}{\sqrt{p}}\sin\!\left(\int_a^x p\,dx' - \frac{\pi}{4}\right)$$

The $\pi/4$ phase shift — called the Maslov index correction — comes from the Airy function matching. Its presence is the key difference between naive WKB and the correct result.

The connection formulae are not just a technical detail — the $\pi/4$ phase shift is physically real and experimentally observable. In the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition, it shifts the energy levels by exactly half a quantum number's worth. For the harmonic oscillator it gives the correct zero-point energy; without it you'd miss $\hbar\omega/2$.

The WKB Quantization Condition

For a bound state with two turning points $x = a$ (left) and $x = b$ (right), apply the connection formula at each turning point. The wavefunction must be consistent — a single-valued, decaying solution — which imposes a quantization condition.

Applying the connection formula at the left turning point gives a cosine in the classically allowed region. Applying it at the right turning point gives another cosine. For these to be the same function (self-consistency), the phases must match modulo $\pi$. Working through the algebra gives the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition:

$$\boxed{\int_a^b p(x)\,dx = \left(n - \frac{1}{2}\right)\pi\hbar, \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots}$$

The left side is half the action integral around one classical orbit (the particle goes from $a$ to $b$ and back, so the full orbit gives $2\int_a^b p\,dx$). The right side gives quantized values. The $-\frac{1}{2}$ in $(n - \frac{1}{2})$ comes from the two Maslov index corrections — each turning point contributes $-\pi/4$, so both together give $-\pi/2$, which converts to $-1/2$ quantum units.

Equivalently, writing the full classical action over one complete orbit $\oint p\,dq$:

$$\oint p\,dq = \left(n - \frac{1}{2}\right)h \qquad \text{(Bohr-Sommerfeld + Maslov)}$$

The old Bohr-Sommerfeld condition (1916) used $\oint p\,dq = nh$ — it missed the Maslov correction and got the zero-point energy wrong. The correct WKB condition is $\oint p\,dq = (n-\frac{1}{2})h$.

Application to the Harmonic Oscillator

For $V = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2$, the turning points at energy $E$ are $x = \pm a$ where $a = \sqrt{2E/m\omega^2}$. The action integral:

$$\int_{-a}^{a} \sqrt{2m(E - \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2)}\,dx = \frac{\pi E}{\omega}$$

(This is the area of an ellipse in phase space.) The quantization condition gives:

$$\frac{\pi E}{\omega} = \left(n - \frac{1}{2}\right)\pi\hbar \implies E_n = \left(n - \frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega$$

With $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$: this gives $E_0 = \hbar\omega/2$, $E_1 = 3\hbar\omega/2$, etc. Exact. WKB is exact for the harmonic oscillator — the potential is parabolic and the approximation captures the exact answer.

Application to the Infinite Square Well

The infinite square well has hard walls (infinite potential), which are not soft turning points — they're "hard wall" turning points that contribute $\pi/2$ phase each instead of $\pi/4$. The quantization condition becomes:

$$\int_0^a p\,dx = n\pi\hbar \implies E_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2}$$

Again exact — the hard wall correction shifts the phase compared to the standard WKB formula, and both the infinite square well and harmonic oscillator happen to be exactly reproduced by WKB.

Tunneling Rates and Alpha Decay

When a particle encounters a barrier ($E < V$ over some range), the WKB wavefunction decays exponentially through the barrier. The transmission probability is dominated by the exponential factor:

$$T \approx e^{-2\gamma}, \qquad \gamma = \int_a^b \kappa(x)\,dx = \frac{1}{\hbar}\int_a^b\sqrt{2m(V(x)-E)}\,dx$$

This is the WKB tunneling formula. The pre-exponential factor (from the connection formulae) is approximately 1 for thick barriers. The key message: the tunneling probability is exponentially suppressed by the classical action integral through the barrier, $\gamma$. Wider or taller barriers give exponentially smaller transmission.

Gamow's Theory of Alpha Decay

Alpha decay — the emission of an $\alpha$ particle (helium-4 nucleus) from a heavy nucleus — was one of the great mysteries of early nuclear physics. An $\alpha$ particle inside uranium has an energy of roughly 5 MeV, but the nuclear potential well is $\sim 30\ \text{MeV}$ deep. Classically, the $\alpha$ can never escape. Quantum mechanically, it tunnels.

The potential seen by the $\alpha$ particle is:

  • Inside the nucleus ($r < R$): attractive nuclear potential, net $V \approx -V_0$ (the alpha is bound)
  • Outside ($r > R$): purely Coulombic repulsion $V(r) = \frac{Z_d Z_\alpha e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} = \frac{2(Z-2)e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}$ (where $Z_d$ is the daughter nucleus charge)

The $\alpha$ has total energy $E > 0$ (it escapes), but $E < V(R)$ — the Coulomb barrier at the nuclear radius is much higher than the $\alpha$'s kinetic energy. The outer turning point is $b = 2(Z-2)e^2/4\pi\epsilon_0 E$, and the tunneling exponent is:

$$\gamma = \frac{\sqrt{2m_\alpha}}{\hbar}\int_R^b\sqrt{\frac{2(Z-2)e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} - E}\,dr$$

Evaluating this integral (it's an arccos/arcsin substitution):

$$\gamma = \frac{\pi Z_d e^2}{2\epsilon_0 h v_\alpha} - \frac{2\sqrt{2m_\alpha R}}{\hbar}\sqrt{V(R) - E}$$

where $v_\alpha = \sqrt{2E/m_\alpha}$ is the $\alpha$ velocity. The decay rate is:

$$\Gamma = \nu_0\,e^{-2\gamma}$$

where $\nu_0 \sim v_\alpha/2R$ is the frequency with which the $\alpha$ "bounces" inside the nucleus and attempts to escape.

The Geiger-Nuttall law: Taking the log, $\log\Gamma \approx C_1 + C_2/\sqrt{E}$ — the decay rate should depend exponentially on $1/\sqrt{E}$. This is Gamow's prediction, and it fits the data for alpha emitters across 24 orders of magnitude in decay rate with the same simple formula. Uranium-238 has a half-life of $4.5\times10^9$ years; polonium-212 has a half-life of $3\times10^{-7}$ seconds. The difference is entirely in the tunneling exponent $e^{-2\gamma}$ — a factor of $\sim10^{40}$. This is one of the most striking confirmations of quantum mechanics.

WKB Tunneling: Transmission Through a Smooth Barrier
Worked Examples
Example 1 — WKB Quantization for the Linear Potential
Find the approximate energy levels of the linear potential $V(x) = mgx$ for $x > 0$ with an infinite wall at $x = 0$ (a quantum bouncing ball).

The turning points are $x = 0$ (hard wall) and $x = b = E/mg$ (soft turning point). The hard wall contributes a phase of $\pi/2$ (not $\pi/4$), so the quantization condition is:

$$\int_0^b \sqrt{2m(E - mgx)}\,dx = \left(n - \frac{1}{4}\right)\pi\hbar, \quad n = 1,2,3,\ldots$$

(One hard wall at $x=0$ gives $\pi/2$ phase = $1/2$ Maslov units; one soft wall at $x=b$ gives $\pi/4$ phase = $1/4$ units; total $(n - 3/4)$ would be wrong — the standard result for one hard wall + one soft wall is $(n-3/4)$... let me be more careful.)

Hard wall at $x = 0$ contributes Maslov index $\mu = 2$ (full reflection), soft turning point at $x = b$ contributes $\mu = 1$. Total Maslov correction: $\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}\cdot(2+1)$...

More carefully: the standard formula for two turning points each contributing $\pi/4$ is $\int p\,dx = (n-\frac{1}{2})\pi\hbar$. A hard wall contributes $\pi/2$ instead of $\pi/4$, so:

$$\int_0^b p\,dx = \left(n - \frac{3}{4}\right)\pi\hbar$$

The left side: let $u = b - x$, $p = \sqrt{2m\cdot mg(b-x)} = \sqrt{2m^2 g\, u}$.

$$\int_0^b \sqrt{2m\cdot mg(b-x)}\,dx = \sqrt{2m^2g}\int_0^b \sqrt{u}\,du = \sqrt{2m^2g}\cdot\frac{2}{3}b^{3/2} = \frac{2}{3}\sqrt{2m^2g}\left(\frac{E}{mg}\right)^{3/2}$$

Setting equal to $(n-3/4)\pi\hbar$:

$$E_n = \left[\frac{3\pi\hbar mg}{2\sqrt{2m}}\left(n - \frac{3}{4}\right)\right]^{2/3} = \left[\frac{3\pi(n-\frac{3}{4})}{2}\right]^{2/3}\left(\frac{\hbar^2 m g^2}{2}\right)^{1/3}$$

For $n = 1$: $E_1 \approx (0.566)^{2/3}(\hbar^2 mg^2/2)^{1/3} \approx 1.856(\hbar^2 mg^2/2)^{1/3}$. The exact numerical value (Airy function zero) gives the same characteristic scale $(\hbar^2 mg^2/2)^{1/3}$ with coefficient $\approx 1.856$ for $n=1$ — the WKB result is accurate even for the lowest level.

Example 2 — Alpha Decay Lifetime of Polonium-210
Estimate the alpha decay half-life of $^{210}$Po using the Gamow formula. Given: $Q = 5.3$ MeV, $Z = 84$ (Z of Po), nuclear radius $R \approx 1.07\,A^{1/3}\ \text{fm} \approx 6.4\ \text{fm}$.

The daughter nucleus has $Z_d = 82$ (Pb), $Z_\alpha = 2$, so the Coulomb barrier height at the nuclear surface:

$$V(R) = \frac{Z_d Z_\alpha e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R} = \frac{(82)(2)(1.44\,\text{MeV·fm})}{6.4\,\text{fm}} = 36.9\,\text{MeV}$$

The $\alpha$ energy $E = Q = 5.3$ MeV $\ll V(R)$, so the barrier is substantial. The outer turning point:

$$b = \frac{Z_d Z_\alpha e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 E} = \frac{(82)(2)(1.44)}{5.3}\,\text{fm} = 44.6\,\text{fm}$$

The tunneling exponent (using the exact integral result):

$$\gamma = \frac{Z_d Z_\alpha e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar v_\alpha}\left[\arccos\sqrt{\frac{R}{b}} - \sqrt{\frac{R}{b}\left(1-\frac{R}{b}\right)}\right]$$

With $R/b = 6.4/44.6 = 0.144$: $\arccos\sqrt{0.144} \approx 1.19$, $\sqrt{0.144\cdot 0.856} \approx 0.351$, so the bracket $\approx 0.84$.

The velocity: $v_\alpha = \sqrt{2Q/m_\alpha} = \sqrt{2\times5.3\times10^6\times1.6\times10^{-19}/(4\times1.67\times10^{-27})} \approx 1.6\times10^7\ \text{m/s}$.

$\frac{Z_d Z_\alpha e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar v_\alpha} = \frac{(82)(2)(1.44\ \text{MeV·fm})}{197.3\ \text{MeV·fm}\cdot(v_\alpha/c)} = \frac{236}{197\times0.053} \approx 22.6$

So $\gamma \approx 22.6\times0.84 \approx 19$. Decay rate: $\Gamma \approx \nu_0 e^{-38}$ where $\nu_0 = v_\alpha/2R \approx 1.6\times10^7/(2\times6.4\times10^{-15}) \approx 1.25\times10^{21}\ \text{s}^{-1}$.

$\Gamma \approx 1.25\times10^{21} \times e^{-38} \approx 1.25\times10^{21}\times3.1\times10^{-17} \approx 4\times10^4\ \text{s}^{-1}$

Half-life: $t_{1/2} = \ln 2/\Gamma \approx 1.7\times10^{-5}\ \text{s}$. The measured value is $1.4\times10^{-5}$ s. Excellent agreement for such a simple calculation — the WKB tunneling formula nails the right order of magnitude across a problem spanning 24 orders of magnitude in decay rate across different nuclei.

Example 3 — Hydrogen Atom via WKB
Apply the WKB quantization condition to the radial equation of hydrogen and derive the energy levels.

The radial Schrödinger equation in hydrogen (with the substitution $u = rR$) is:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2u}{dr^2} + V_{\text{eff}}(r)\,u = E\,u, \qquad V_{\text{eff}} = -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} + \frac{\hbar^2 l(l+1)}{2mr^2}$$

The effective potential has two turning points $r_1 < r_2$. The WKB quantization condition (both turning points are soft):

$$\int_{r_1}^{r_2}\sqrt{2m(E - V_{\text{eff}})}\,dr = \left(n_r + \frac{1}{2}\right)\pi\hbar, \quad n_r = 0,1,2,\ldots$$

This integral can be evaluated via contour integration (treating it as a complex integral with poles). For the Coulomb potential:

$$\int_{r_1}^{r_2}\sqrt{-2mE + \frac{me^2}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r} - \frac{\hbar^2(l+\frac{1}{2})^2}{r^2}}\,dr = \left[\frac{me^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0\sqrt{-2mE}} - \hbar\left(l+\frac{1}{2}\right)\right]\pi$$

Setting this equal to $(n_r + \frac{1}{2})\pi\hbar$ and solving for $E$:

$$E = -\frac{m e^4}{32\pi^2\epsilon_0^2\hbar^2}\cdot\frac{1}{(n_r + l + 1)^2} = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = n_r + l + 1$$

The WKB reproduces the exact hydrogen energy levels — a remarkable coincidence related to the special $1/r^2$ symmetry of the Coulomb problem. Note: the WKB uses $l(l+1) \to (l+\frac{1}{2})^2$ (Langer correction) for better accuracy near $r = 0$.

  • The WKB approximation is called "semiclassical." In what precise sense is it classical? What is the small parameter, and what does the limit $\hbar \to 0$ actually mean physically?

    WKB is an expansion in powers of $\hbar$ — specifically, in the dimensionless combination $\hbar/pL$ where $L$ is a length scale over which $p$ changes appreciably. The condition $|d\lambda/dx| \ll 1$ (slow variation of de Broglie wavelength) is equivalent to $\hbar/(pL) \ll 1$. In the limit $\hbar \to 0$ with $p$ and $L$ held fixed, this ratio goes to zero and the approximation becomes exact. Physically, $\hbar \to 0$ means the quantum of action is negligible compared to classical actions — classical mechanics is recovered. But "classical limit" doesn't mean classical mechanics is right; it means quantum corrections are negligible. In alpha decay, the tunneling exponent $\gamma \propto 1/\hbar$ blows up as $\hbar \to 0$ — the transmission becomes $e^{-\infty} = 0$. Classically, no tunneling. WKB captures the leading quantum correction to this classical result.

  • Gamow's formula explains why alpha decay lifetimes span 24 orders of magnitude for different nuclei. The energies vary by only a factor of 3 (from ~4 to ~9 MeV). How can a factor of 3 in energy produce a factor of $10^{24}$ in decay rate?

    The tunneling exponent $\gamma \propto 1/\sqrt{E}$ appears in the exponential: $T \sim e^{-2\gamma} \sim e^{-C/\sqrt{E}}$. Even a modest change in $E$ produces a dramatic change in $\gamma$, and that change is then exponentiated. For $^{238}$U ($E = 4.27$ MeV) vs $^{212}$Po ($E = 8.78$ MeV), the ratio $1/\sqrt{E_{U}}/1/\sqrt{E_{Po}} \approx \sqrt{8.78/4.27} \approx 1.43$. If $\gamma_{Po} \approx 23$ (a typical value), then $\gamma_U \approx 23\times1.43 \approx 33$. The ratio of transmission coefficients: $e^{-2\times23}/e^{-2\times33} = e^{20} \approx 5\times10^8$. Combining with the different nuclear attempt frequencies and repeating for more extreme cases, you accumulate $10^{24}$ across the periodic table. The exponential amplifies small differences enormously — this is why nuclear physics is so sensitive to the exact energy, and why WKB (which is exact to exponential order) works so well here.

  • The Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition $\oint p\,dq = nh$ predates quantum mechanics and was the basis of the "old quantum theory." Where does it succeed and where does it fail compared to the correct WKB condition with the Maslov correction?

    The old Bohr-Sommerfeld condition $\oint p\,dq = nh$ gets the energy spacings right but misses the zero-point energy. For the harmonic oscillator: $nh\omega$ gives $E_n = n\hbar\omega$, missing the $\hbar\omega/2$ ground state energy. For hydrogen: it gives $E_n = -13.6/n^2$ exactly (accidentally, due to the Coulomb degeneracy), but gets the $l = 0$ states wrong because the centrifugal term near $r = 0$ needs the Langer correction $l(l+1) \to (l+1/2)^2$. The Maslov-corrected WKB condition $\oint p\,dq = (n-1/2)h$ fixes the zero-point energy issue. But both old QT and WKB fail for: (1) spherically symmetric problems without the Langer correction, (2) problems near separatrix orbits (where the topology of phase space changes), (3) degenerate or nearly-degenerate systems, and (4) any case where the WKB validity condition $|d\lambda/dx|\ll 1$ is violated. The old QT also completely failed for the helium atom — it gave the wrong answer for any system with more than one electron, which was a significant crisis before modern QM.

Common Pitfalls
  • Using WKB near turning points. The approximation breaks down exactly where $p(x) \to 0$. Never use the WKB wavefunction to evaluate anything at or close to the turning point. The connection formulae are derived precisely to handle this region, using Airy functions as the bridge.
  • Missing the Maslov correction ($\pi/4$ per turning point). The naive WKB condition $\int p\,dx = n\pi\hbar$ gives wrong energies. The correct condition includes $-\pi/4$ per soft turning point. Two soft turning points give the Bohr-Sommerfeld condition $\int p\,dx = (n - 1/2)\pi\hbar$. A hard wall (infinite potential) contributes $\pi/2$ instead of $\pi/4$.
  • Applying the tunneling formula without checking that the barrier is thick. The formula $T \approx e^{-2\gamma}$ is valid for $\gamma \gg 1$ (thick barriers). For thin barriers or near the top of the barrier, the pre-exponential factor matters and the exact transmission formula (from solving the Schrödinger equation exactly, as in Lesson 5) should be used.
  • Forgetting the $1/\sqrt{p}$ prefactor. The WKB wavefunction isn't just $e^{\pm i\int p\,dx/\hbar}$ — it has the $1/\sqrt{p(x)}$ amplitude factor. This factor is essential for probability current conservation, and its divergence at turning points signals the WKB breakdown. Omitting it gives wrong normalization and wrong matching.
Connects to
  • Quantum Mechanics — Free Particle & Tunneling (Lesson 5): Lesson 5 computed the exact tunneling transmission for a rectangular barrier. WKB extends this to arbitrary smooth barriers with a simple formula — the rectangular barrier is a special case where WKB is exact at the exponential level.
  • Classical Mechanics & Optics: WKB is the quantum analogue of geometric (ray) optics in electromagnetism. The eikonal approximation in optics — $E \propto e^{ikS(r)}$ where $S$ is the optical path length — is identical in structure to WKB. The breakdown at caustics in optics corresponds to turning points in WKB, and Airy functions appear in both settings. The correspondence principle runs deep: WKB is literally the bridge between quantum and classical mechanics.
  • Nuclear and Atomic Physics: Alpha decay, proton radioactivity, fission barrier penetration, field ionization of atoms in strong laser fields, and Zener tunneling between bands in semiconductors all rely on the WKB tunneling formula. The Gamow factor is a standard ingredient in nuclear reaction rate calculations used in stellar astrophysics (nuclear burning in stars).
