Spin

Stern-Gerlach · Spinors · Pauli Matrices · Spin-Orbit Coupling · Angular Momentum Addition

Every particle in nature carries an intrinsic angular momentum called spin. It's not rotation in space — electrons don't spin like tops — but it obeys exactly the same algebra as orbital angular momentum and contributes to magnetic moments just as orbital motion does. Spin-½ (the electron's case) is the simplest non-trivial quantum system: two states, a 2×2 matrix algebra, and a rich phenomenology that includes the Pauli exclusion principle, the periodic table, magnetic resonance imaging, and quantum computing.

The Stern-Gerlach Experiment

In 1922, Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach sent silver atoms through a strongly inhomogeneous magnetic field. Silver has one unpaired outer electron; classically, this electron's magnetic moment $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ should be randomly oriented, giving a continuous smear on the detector screen. Instead, they saw exactly two spots — the beam split into two discrete components.

This is space quantization made visible. The magnetic moment is proportional to the angular momentum: $\mu_z \propto S_z$. Since only two spots appear, $S_z$ can take only two values — the angular momentum along the $z$-axis is quantised in exactly two steps. For $m_s = \pm\frac{1}{2}$, the quantum number $s = \frac{1}{2}$: this is spin-half.

Crucially: if you take one of the two output beams (say, spin-up) and send it through a second Stern-Gerlach apparatus tilted at 90°, you again get two spots — each with 50% probability. If you then recombine them and pass through a third apparatus aligned with the original $z$-axis, you get 50/50 again, not the original pure spin-up. The intermediate measurement irreversibly disturbed the state. This sequence of Stern-Gerlach experiments is the clearest possible demonstration of quantum measurement and non-commuting observables.

Spin-½ Formalism

Spin is angular momentum: it satisfies the same algebra as $\hat{L}$:

$$[\hat{S}_x,\hat{S}_y] = i\hbar\hat{S}_z, \quad [\hat{S}_y,\hat{S}_z] = i\hbar\hat{S}_x, \quad [\hat{S}_z,\hat{S}_x] = i\hbar\hat{S}_y$$
$$\hat{S}^2|s,m_s\rangle = \hbar^2 s(s+1)|s,m_s\rangle, \qquad \hat{S}_z|s,m_s\rangle = \hbar m_s|s,m_s\rangle$$

For spin-½, $s = \frac{1}{2}$ and $m_s = \pm\frac{1}{2}$. The two basis states are $|\uparrow\rangle \equiv |\frac{1}{2},+\frac{1}{2}\rangle$ (spin-up) and $|\downarrow\rangle \equiv |\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2}\rangle$ (spin-down). A general spin-½ state is a spinor:

$$|\chi\rangle = a|\uparrow\rangle + b|\downarrow\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}a\\b\end{pmatrix}, \qquad |a|^2+|b|^2=1$$

The complete state of an electron includes both spatial and spin parts: $|\Psi\rangle = \psi(\mathbf{r})\otimes|\chi\rangle$ — a function of position times a two-component spinor.

The Pauli Matrices

In the $\{|\uparrow\rangle, |\downarrow\rangle\}$ basis, the spin operators are represented as $2\times2$ matrices. $\hat{S}_i = (\hbar/2)\hat{\sigma}_i$ where the Pauli matrices are:

$$\sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$$

Key properties (provable directly from matrix multiplication):

  • $\sigma_i^2 = I$ (each squares to the identity)
  • $\sigma_x\sigma_y = i\sigma_z$ (and cyclic) — encodes the commutation relations
  • $\{\sigma_i,\sigma_j\} = 2\delta_{ij}I$ (anticommutation: Pauli matrices anticommute for $i\neq j$)
  • $\text{Tr}(\sigma_i) = 0$, $\det(\sigma_i) = -1$
  • Together with $I$: $\{I, \sigma_x, \sigma_y, \sigma_z\}$ forms a basis for all $2\times2$ Hermitian matrices

The eigenstates of $\hat{S}_x$ and $\hat{S}_y$ are:

$$|\uparrow_x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}, \quad |\downarrow_x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}; \qquad |\uparrow_y\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\i\end{pmatrix}, \quad |\downarrow_y\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-i\end{pmatrix}$$

A spin state along an arbitrary unit vector $\hat{n} = (\sin\theta\cos\phi,\sin\theta\sin\phi,\cos\theta)$ is:

$$|\uparrow_{\hat{n}}\rangle = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}|\uparrow\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}|\downarrow\rangle$$

This parametrises the unit sphere — the Bloch sphere. Every pure spin-½ state corresponds to a point on the Bloch sphere; the two poles are $|\uparrow\rangle$ and $|\downarrow\rangle$; the equator contains the $|\uparrow_x\rangle$, $|\downarrow_x\rangle$, $|\uparrow_y\rangle$, $|\downarrow_y\rangle$ states.

Spin in a Magnetic Field

A spinning charged particle has a magnetic moment. For an electron:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_s = -g_e\frac{e}{2m_e}\hat{\mathbf{S}}, \qquad g_e \approx 2.00232\ldots$$

The factor $g_e \approx 2$ (the $g$-factor) differs from the orbital case ($g_L = 1$). It arises from quantum field theory and is one of the most precisely measured quantities in physics ($g_e/2 = 1.00115965218...$, matching QED calculations to 12 decimal places).

In a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B\hat{z}$, the Hamiltonian for the spin degree of freedom is:

$$\hat{H} = -\boldsymbol{\mu}_s\cdot\mathbf{B} = g_e\frac{e}{2m_e}B\hat{S}_z = \gamma B\hat{S}_z, \qquad \gamma = g_e e/(2m_e)$$

The energy eigenstates are $|\uparrow\rangle$ and $|\downarrow\rangle$ with energies $E_\pm = \pm\frac{1}{2}\hbar\gamma B$. This is the Zeeman splitting of spin states. The energy gap is:

$$\Delta E = \hbar\gamma B = \hbar\omega_L, \qquad \omega_L = g_e\frac{eB}{2m_e}$$

where $\omega_L$ is the Larmor frequency — the frequency at which the spin precesses around $\mathbf{B}$. For a general initial spin state $|\chi(0)\rangle = a|\uparrow\rangle + b|\downarrow\rangle$:

$$|\chi(t)\rangle = ae^{-i\omega_L t/2}|\uparrow\rangle + be^{+i\omega_L t/2}|\downarrow\rangle$$

The expectation values $\langle\hat{S}_x\rangle$ and $\langle\hat{S}_y\rangle$ precess in the $xy$-plane at frequency $\omega_L$, while $\langle\hat{S}_z\rangle$ is constant — exactly like a classical magnetic dipole precessing around a field. This Larmor precession is the physical basis of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): nuclear spins (protons) precess at a frequency $\omega_L = \gamma_p B$ where $\gamma_p$ is the proton gyromagnetic ratio. Applying a radiofrequency pulse at $\omega_L$ resonantly flips spins; detecting the subsequent precession (the NMR signal) gives spatial information about water density in tissue.

Spin-Orbit Coupling and Fine Structure

An electron in a hydrogen atom has both orbital angular momentum $\hat{\mathbf{L}}$ and spin $\hat{\mathbf{S}}$. In the electron's rest frame, it sees the proton orbiting around it — a current loop that creates a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}_\text{eff} \propto \mathbf{L}$. The electron's spin magnetic moment interacts with this field:

$$\hat{H}'_\text{SO} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{m_e^2 c^2 r^3}\hat{\mathbf{L}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{S}}$$

(The factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ is Thomas precession — a relativistic correction to the electron's rest frame.) This $\hat{\mathbf{L}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{S}}$ term couples orbital and spin angular momenta.

Since $\hat{\mathbf{L}}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{S}}$ are no longer separately conserved (they precess around the fixed total angular momentum $\hat{\mathbf{J}} = \hat{\mathbf{L}} + \hat{\mathbf{S}}$), the good quantum numbers become $n$, $l$, $j$, and $m_j$ (the $z$-component of $\hat{\mathbf{J}}$), where:

$$j = l + \frac{1}{2} \quad\text{or}\quad j = l - \frac{1}{2} \qquad (l > 0); \qquad j = \frac{1}{2} \quad (l=0)$$

Evaluating $\hat{\mathbf{L}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{S}} = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{J}^2 - \hat{L}^2 - \hat{S}^2)$ in the $|n,l,j,m_j\rangle$ basis:

$$\langle\hat{\mathbf{L}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{S}}\rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}\left[j(j+1) - l(l+1) - \frac{3}{4}\right]$$

The spin-orbit correction splits each $l > 0$ level of hydrogen into a doublet ($j = l+\frac{1}{2}$ and $j = l-\frac{1}{2}$). Combined with the relativistic kinetic energy correction (from expanding $\sqrt{p^2c^2 + m_e^2c^4} - m_ec^2$ to order $p^4$), the total fine structure energy correction is:

$$E_\text{fs}^{(1)} = -\frac{E_n^2}{2m_e c^2}\left(\frac{4n}{j+\frac{1}{2}} - 3\right)$$

Fine structure is of order $\alpha^2 \approx (1/137)^2 \sim 5\times10^{-5}$ relative to the gross structure energies — tiny but measured to extraordinary precision in atomic spectroscopy.

Adding Angular Momenta

Given two angular momenta $\hat{\mathbf{J}}_1$ (with $j_1$) and $\hat{\mathbf{J}}_2$ (with $j_2$), the total $\hat{\mathbf{J}} = \hat{\mathbf{J}}_1 + \hat{\mathbf{J}}_2$ has quantum numbers:

$$j = |j_1 - j_2|,\;\; |j_1-j_2|+1,\;\; \ldots,\;\; j_1+j_2$$

Each $j$ value contributes $2j+1$ states, and the total count is $(2j_1+1)(2j_2+1)$. The transformation between the product basis $|j_1,m_1\rangle|j_2,m_2\rangle$ and the total basis $|j,m\rangle$ uses Clebsch-Gordan coefficients:

$$|j,m\rangle = \sum_{m_1+m_2=m} \langle j_1 m_1; j_2 m_2 | j m\rangle\; |j_1,m_1\rangle|j_2,m_2\rangle$$

For combining spin-½ with orbital $l$: $j = l \pm \frac{1}{2}$. The highest-weight state for $j = l + \frac{1}{2}$ is simply $|m_l = l\rangle|\uparrow\rangle$ (both at maximum). Applying $\hat{J}_-$ generates all others. The resulting Clebsch-Gordan coefficients for spin-½ are:

$$\left|l+\tfrac{1}{2},\, m\right\rangle = \sqrt{\frac{l+m+\frac{1}{2}}{2l+1}}\left|m-\tfrac{1}{2}\right\rangle|\uparrow\rangle + \sqrt{\frac{l-m+\frac{1}{2}}{2l+1}}\left|m+\tfrac{1}{2}\right\rangle|\downarrow\rangle$$

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Eigenstates and eigenvalues of $\hat{S}_x$

Find the eigenvalues and normalised eigenstates of $\hat{S}_x = (\hbar/2)\sigma_x$.

Eigenvalues: $\det(\hat{S}_x - \lambda I) = 0$.

$$\det\!\begin{pmatrix}-\lambda & \hbar/2\\ \hbar/2 & -\lambda\end{pmatrix} = \lambda^2 - (\hbar/2)^2 = 0 \implies \lambda = \pm\hbar/2$$

Eigenvectors for $\lambda = +\hbar/2$:

$$\frac{\hbar}{2}\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}a\\b\end{pmatrix} = \frac{\hbar}{2}\begin{pmatrix}a\\b\end{pmatrix} \implies b = a$$

Normalising: $|\uparrow_x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Eigenvectors for $\lambda = -\hbar/2$: $b = -a$, so $|\downarrow_x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

Measurement probability: If the electron starts in $|\uparrow\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and we measure $S_x$:

$$P(+\hbar/2) = |\langle\uparrow_x|\uparrow\rangle|^2 = \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1\cdot 1 + 1\cdot 0)\right|^2 = \frac{1}{2}$$

Equal probabilities for both outcomes — consistent with the Stern-Gerlach sequential experiment.

Example 2 — Larmor precession: time evolution in a magnetic field

An electron starts in $|\chi(0)\rangle = |\uparrow_x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$. A field $\mathbf{B} = B\hat{z}$ is applied. Find $\langle S_x\rangle(t)$.

Time evolution: The Hamiltonian $\hat{H} = \gamma B\hat{S}_z$ is diagonal in the $\{|\uparrow\rangle,|\downarrow\rangle\}$ basis.

$$|\chi(t)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}e^{-i\omega_L t/2}\\e^{+i\omega_L t/2}\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \omega_L = \gamma B$$

Expectation value of $S_x$:

$$\langle S_x\rangle = \langle\chi(t)|\hat{S}_x|\chi(t)\rangle = \frac{\hbar}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}e^{i\omega_L t/2} & e^{-i\omega_L t/2}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}e^{-i\omega_L t/2}\\e^{i\omega_L t/2}\end{pmatrix}$$
$$= \frac{\hbar}{4}\left(e^{i\omega_L t/2}\cdot e^{i\omega_L t/2} + e^{-i\omega_L t/2}\cdot e^{-i\omega_L t/2}\right) = \frac{\hbar}{4}(e^{i\omega_L t} + e^{-i\omega_L t}) = \frac{\hbar}{2}\cos(\omega_L t)$$

Similarly $\langle S_y\rangle = -\frac{\hbar}{2}\sin(\omega_L t)$ and $\langle S_z\rangle = 0$. The spin precesses in the $xy$-plane at the Larmor frequency $\omega_L = \gamma B = g_e eB/2m_e$. For a proton in a 1 T MRI field: $\omega_L^p \approx 2\pi \times 42.6\,\text{MHz}$ (the $^1$H NMR frequency).

Example 3 — Spin-orbit coupling: the $2p$ fine structure of hydrogen

The 2p states of hydrogen have $n=2$, $l=1$, $s=\frac{1}{2}$. Determine the $j$ values and the energy splitting from spin-orbit coupling.

$j$ values: $j = l + s = \frac{3}{2}$ and $j = l - s = \frac{1}{2}$. The degeneracy count: $j=\frac{3}{2}$ has $2(3/2)+1=4$ states; $j=\frac{1}{2}$ has $2(1/2)+1=2$ states. Total: 6 = $(2l+1)(2s+1) = 3\times2$ ✓.

$\hat{\mathbf{L}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{S}}$ eigenvalues:

$$\langle\hat{\mathbf{L}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{S}}\rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}[j(j+1)-l(l+1)-s(s+1)] = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}\left[j(j+1) - 2 - \frac{3}{4}\right]$$

For $j=\frac{3}{2}$: $\frac{\hbar^2}{2}[\frac{15}{4}-\frac{11}{4}] = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}\cdot 1 = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}$.

For $j=\frac{1}{2}$: $\frac{\hbar^2}{2}[\frac{3}{4}-\frac{11}{4}] = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}(-2) = -\hbar^2$.

The $j=\frac{3}{2}$ states (4 states: $2p_{3/2}$) lie higher in energy; $j=\frac{1}{2}$ (2 states: $2p_{1/2}$) lie lower. This splitting is about $4.5\times10^{-5}\,\text{eV}$ — the 2p fine structure doublet of hydrogen, visible in high-resolution spectroscopy of the Balmer-α line at 656 nm.

Food for Thought
  • In the sequential Stern-Gerlach experiment, passing $|\uparrow_z\rangle$ through an $x$-oriented magnet and then a $z$-oriented magnet gives a 50/50 mixture — as if the first spin-up preparation was erased. What would happen if you could recombine the $|\uparrow_x\rangle$ and $|\downarrow_x\rangle$ beams without measuring which path the atom took?
    If you recombine coherently (no which-path information), the interference terms survive and the output is back to $|\uparrow_z\rangle$ — 100% probability of spin-up when measured along $z$ again. The $x$-magnet effectively had no effect. This is the spin version of quantum erasure: the middle "measurement" doesn't collapse the state if you recombine before reading out. Modern atom interferometers do exactly this, and Stern-Gerlach devices are the prototypical qubit gate in many theoretical frameworks. The key: quantum erasure requires that no information about the intermediate measurement ever reaches the environment in an irreversible way.
  • Electrons have $g_e \approx 2$, not 1 as you'd expect from the orbital analogy. Where does this factor of 2 come from, and why isn't it exactly 2?
    The $g_e = 2$ (to lowest order) is a prediction of the Dirac equation — the relativistic wave equation for spin-½ particles. When you extend non-relativistic quantum mechanics to be Lorentz-invariant, spin emerges automatically and the magnetic moment coupling comes out with $g=2$. The deviation from exactly 2 — the anomalous magnetic moment $a_e = (g_e-2)/2 \approx 1.16\times10^{-3}$ — arises from quantum field theory (quantum electrodynamics). Virtual photon loops and virtual electron-positron pairs slightly modify the interaction. The QED prediction for $a_e$ has been calculated to 10th order in the fine structure constant $\alpha$ and matches the experimental measurement to 12 significant figures — one of the most precise agreements between theory and experiment in all of science.
  • Spin is not "spinning" — what is it then? Is there a physical picture of what spin-½ actually is?
    There is no satisfying classical picture. Spin-½ is fundamentally a quantum property with no classical analogue. Some physical facts: electrons do have magnetic moments that cause real, measurable deflections (Stern-Gerlach); spin contributes to angular momentum conservation (in a molecule that absorbs a circularly polarised photon, the photon's $\hbar$ of spin angular momentum transfers to the electron — this is verified experimentally). But trying to imagine a tiny spinning ball fails immediately: you can't fit the electron's known size, charge, and magnetic moment into a consistent classical spinning sphere model. The deepest answer is that spin is a consequence of combining quantum mechanics with special relativity (Dirac equation), and it doesn't have a non-relativistic spatial description. It's a new degree of freedom that has angular momentum algebra but no "axis of rotation."
Common Pitfalls
  • Spin is not orbital angular momentum — $s=\frac{1}{2}$ is genuinely half-integer. The same algebra gives rise to spin, but orbital angular momentum must have integer $l$ (single-valuedness of the position-space wavefunction). Spin-½ states aren't single-valued under $2\pi$ rotations — they pick up a factor of $-1$ (a global phase, which doesn't affect physical predictions). The full group that leaves spin-½ invariant is $SU(2)$, not $SO(3)$.
  • $|\uparrow\rangle$ and $|\downarrow\rangle$ are eigenstates of $\hat{S}_z$, not $\hat{S}_x$ or $\hat{S}_y$. When you measure $S_x$ on a spin-up state, you get $\pm\hbar/2$ with equal probability — the state is not an eigenstate of $S_x$. Confusion about which axis you're measuring leads to systematic errors.
  • The Zeeman effect splits $m_j$ states, not $m_l$ states alone. In the presence of spin-orbit coupling, the good quantum number is $m_j = m_l + m_s$, not $m_l$ separately. The full Zeeman structure requires knowing both the orbital and spin contributions.
  • A spin "measurement" in the $z$-direction doesn't tell you the $x$ or $y$ components. After measuring $S_z = +\hbar/2$, the state is projected to $|\uparrow\rangle$, which has $\langle S_x\rangle = \langle S_y\rangle = 0$ but $\sigma_{S_x} = \sigma_{S_y} = \hbar/2$ — both components are maximally uncertain.
