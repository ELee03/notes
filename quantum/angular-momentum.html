Angular Momentum

Commutation Relations · Ladder Operators · Spherical Harmonics · Central Potentials

Angular momentum sits at the heart of atomic structure. The shapes of atomic orbitals, the periodic table's shell structure, the selection rules for optical transitions — all of it flows from the quantum mechanics of angular momentum. This lesson builds the theory in two ways: first algebraically, deriving everything from the commutation relations alone; then in position space, arriving at the spherical harmonics. The algebra is particularly powerful because it works for spin too (Lesson 9) — a kind of angular momentum with no classical analogue that the algebraic method captures automatically.

The Angular Momentum Operators

Classical angular momentum is $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}$. Promote to operators by replacing $p_i \to -i\hbar\partial/\partial x_i$:

$$\hat{L}_x = \hat{y}\hat{p}_z - \hat{z}\hat{p}_y, \qquad \hat{L}_y = \hat{z}\hat{p}_x - \hat{x}\hat{p}_z, \qquad \hat{L}_z = \hat{x}\hat{p}_y - \hat{y}\hat{p}_x$$

and the total squared magnitude:

$$\hat{L}^2 = \hat{L}_x^2 + \hat{L}_y^2 + \hat{L}_z^2$$

Since each component involves products of different coordinates and momenta (e.g. $\hat{y}$ and $\hat{p}_z$ commute because they act on different variables), we can freely reorder these. The critical question is whether the three components commute with each other and with $\hat{L}^2$.

The Lie Algebra of Angular Momentum

Using $[r_i, p_j] = i\hbar\delta_{ij}$ and $[r_i, r_j] = [p_i, p_j] = 0$:

$$[\hat{L}_x,\hat{L}_y] = i\hbar\hat{L}_z, \qquad [\hat{L}_y,\hat{L}_z] = i\hbar\hat{L}_x, \qquad [\hat{L}_z,\hat{L}_x] = i\hbar\hat{L}_y$$

These three relations (cyclic permutations of one another) are the angular momentum algebra. In contrast:

$$[\hat{L}^2,\hat{L}_x] = [\hat{L}^2,\hat{L}_y] = [\hat{L}^2,\hat{L}_z] = 0$$

$\hat{L}^2$ commutes with all three components. By the compatible observables theorem from Lesson 6, $\hat{L}^2$ and any single component (conventionally $\hat{L}_z$) are simultaneously diagonalisable. But $\hat{L}_x$, $\hat{L}_y$, $\hat{L}_z$ are mutually incompatible — you can't simultaneously know two components of angular momentum, only its magnitude and one component.

Proof of $[\hat{L}^2, \hat{L}_z] = 0$ (sketch). $[\hat{L}^2,\hat{L}_z] = [\hat{L}_x^2,\hat{L}_z] + [\hat{L}_y^2,\hat{L}_z] + [\hat{L}_z^2,\hat{L}_z]$. The last term vanishes. For the first: $[\hat{L}_x^2,\hat{L}_z] = \hat{L}_x[\hat{L}_x,\hat{L}_z] + [\hat{L}_x,\hat{L}_z]\hat{L}_x = -i\hbar\hat{L}_x\hat{L}_y - i\hbar\hat{L}_y\hat{L}_x$. For the second: $[\hat{L}_y^2,\hat{L}_z] = i\hbar\hat{L}_y\hat{L}_x + i\hbar\hat{L}_x\hat{L}_y$. Adding: $[\hat{L}^2,\hat{L}_z] = 0$. ✓
Eigenvalues from the Algebra Alone

Define the ladder operators:

$$\hat{L}_\pm \equiv \hat{L}_x \pm i\hat{L}_y$$

Their key commutation relations (following directly from the algebra):

$$[\hat{L}_z,\hat{L}_\pm] = \pm\hbar\hat{L}_\pm, \qquad [\hat{L}^2,\hat{L}_\pm] = 0, \qquad [\hat{L}_+,\hat{L}_-] = 2\hbar\hat{L}_z$$

Let $|l,m\rangle$ denote a simultaneous eigenstate: $\hat{L}^2|l,m\rangle = \lambda\hbar^2|l,m\rangle$ and $\hat{L}_z|l,m\rangle = \mu\hbar|l,m\rangle$. We want to find the allowed values of $\lambda$ and $\mu$.

$\hat{L}_\pm$ raises/lowers $m$ by 1. From $[\hat{L}_z,\hat{L}_+] = \hbar\hat{L}_+$: $\hat{L}_z(\hat{L}_+|l,m\rangle) = (\mu+1)\hbar(\hat{L}_+|l,m\rangle)$. So $\hat{L}_+|l,m\rangle \propto |l,m+1\rangle$ — it raises the $z$-component by $\hbar$. Similarly $\hat{L}_-$ lowers it by $\hbar$. The $\hat{L}^2$ eigenvalue is unchanged.

The ladder must terminate. Since $\langle\hat{L}_x^2\rangle, \langle\hat{L}_y^2\rangle \geq 0$, we have $\lambda\hbar^2 = \langle\hat{L}^2\rangle \geq \langle\hat{L}_z^2\rangle = \mu^2\hbar^2$, so $|\mu| \leq \sqrt{\lambda}$. There must be a maximum value $m_\text{top}$ with $\hat{L}_+|l,m_\text{top}\rangle = 0$ and a minimum $m_\text{bot}$ with $\hat{L}_-|l,m_\text{bot}\rangle = 0$.

Using $\hat{L}^2 = \hat{L}_-\hat{L}_+ + \hat{L}_z^2 + \hbar\hat{L}_z$: $\lambda = m_\text{top}(m_\text{top}+1)$. Similarly from the bottom rung: $\lambda = m_\text{bot}(m_\text{bot}-1)$. These are consistent if $m_\text{bot} = -m_\text{top}$. Since each step changes $m$ by 1, the difference $m_\text{top} - m_\text{bot} = 2m_\text{top}$ must be a non-negative integer — so $m_\text{top} = l$ where $l = 0, \frac{1}{2}, 1, \frac{3}{2}, \ldots$

Angular momentum quantum numbers. $$\hat{L}^2|l,m\rangle = \hbar^2 l(l+1)|l,m\rangle, \qquad \hat{L}_z|l,m\rangle = \hbar m|l,m\rangle$$ with $l = 0,\frac{1}{2},1,\frac{3}{2},2,\ldots$ and $m = -l,\,-l+1,\,\ldots,\,l-1,\,l$. For orbital angular momentum (position-space eigenfunctions), only integer $l$ occurs. Half-integer values correspond to spin (Lesson 9).

The ladder operator matrix elements (from the norm of $\hat{L}_\pm|l,m\rangle$):

$$\hat{L}_\pm|l,m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{l(l+1)-m(m\pm 1)}\;|l,m\pm 1\rangle$$
Spherical Harmonics

In spherical coordinates $(r,\theta,\phi)$, the angular momentum operators become differential operators in $\theta$ and $\phi$ only (no $r$). In particular:

$$\hat{L}_z = -i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}$$
$$\hat{L}^2 = -\hbar^2\left[\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\right) + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\phi^2}\right]$$

The simultaneous eigenfunctions $Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$ of $\hat{L}^2$ and $\hat{L}_z$ are the spherical harmonics:

$$Y_l^m(\theta,\phi) = \epsilon\sqrt{\frac{(2l+1)}{4\pi}\frac{(l-|m|)!}{(l+|m|)!}}\,P_l^m(\cos\theta)\,e^{im\phi}$$

where $P_l^m$ are the associated Legendre polynomials and $\epsilon = (-1)^m$ for $m > 0$, $\epsilon = 1$ for $m \leq 0$ (Condon-Shortley convention). They are normalised and orthogonal over the unit sphere:

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi} Y_l^{m*}(\theta,\phi)\,Y_{l'}^{m'}(\theta,\phi)\,\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi = \delta_{ll'}\delta_{mm'}$$

The first few spherical harmonics:

$$Y_0^0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi}}, \qquad Y_1^0 = \sqrt{\frac{3}{4\pi}}\cos\theta, \qquad Y_1^{\pm 1} = \mp\sqrt{\frac{3}{8\pi}}\sin\theta\,e^{\pm i\phi}$$
$$Y_2^0 = \sqrt{\frac{5}{16\pi}}(3\cos^2\theta-1), \qquad Y_2^{\pm 1} = \mp\sqrt{\frac{15}{8\pi}}\sin\theta\cos\theta\,e^{\pm i\phi}$$
$$Y_2^{\pm 2} = \sqrt{\frac{15}{32\pi}}\sin^2\theta\,e^{\pm 2i\phi}$$

The probability density $|Y_l^m|^2$ is the angular distribution of finding the particle at angle $(\theta,\phi)$. For $m \neq 0$ states, $|e^{im\phi}|^2 = 1$ so the density is independent of $\phi$ — azimuthally symmetric despite the complex phase. The lobes you've seen in chemistry diagrams are surfaces of constant $|Y_l^m|^2$.

Example 1 — Verify $Y_1^0$ is an eigenfunction of $\hat{L}^2$ and $\hat{L}_z$

$Y_1^0 = \sqrt{3/4\pi}\cos\theta$.

$\hat{L}_z Y_1^0$: $\hat{L}_z = -i\hbar\partial/\partial\phi$. Since $Y_1^0$ has no $\phi$-dependence, $\hat{L}_z Y_1^0 = 0 = 0\cdot Y_1^0$. So $m=0$ ✓.

$\hat{L}^2 Y_1^0$:

$$\hat{L}^2 Y_1^0 = -\hbar^2\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\right)\sqrt{\frac{3}{4\pi}}\cos\theta$$

$\partial(\cos\theta)/\partial\theta = -\sin\theta$, so $\sin\theta\cdot(-\sin\theta) = -\sin^2\theta$.

$$\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial(-\sin^2\theta)}{\partial\theta} = \frac{-2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} = -2\cos\theta$$

Therefore: $\hat{L}^2 Y_1^0 = -\hbar^2\cdot(-2)\cdot\sqrt{3/4\pi}\cos\theta = 2\hbar^2 Y_1^0$.

This equals $\hbar^2 l(l+1) Y_1^0 = \hbar^2\cdot 1\cdot 2\cdot Y_1^0$ ✓.

Central Potentials and Separation of Variables

For a potential $V = V(r)$ that depends only on the distance from the origin, the Hamiltonian commutes with all three angular momentum components:

$$[\hat{H},\hat{L}^2] = 0, \qquad [\hat{H},\hat{L}_z] = 0$$

This means energy eigenstates can simultaneously have definite $L^2$ and $L_z$ — they're labelled by quantum numbers $(n, l, m)$. The 3D Schrödinger equation separates in spherical coordinates:

$$\psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\phi) = R_{nl}(r)\,Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$$

Plugging into the TISE and separating, the radial part $R_{nl}(r)$ satisfies the radial equation:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2 u}{dr^2} + \left[V(r) + \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{l(l+1)}{r^2}\right]u = Eu, \qquad u(r) \equiv rR(r)$$

This looks like a 1D Schrödinger equation with an effective potential:

$$V_\text{eff}(r) = V(r) + \underbrace{\frac{\hbar^2 l(l+1)}{2mr^2}}_{\text{centrifugal barrier}}$$

The centrifugal barrier $\hbar^2 l(l+1)/2mr^2$ acts like a repulsive potential that pushes the particle away from the origin when $l > 0$ — exactly the classical effect of angular momentum in a central force. The radial quantum number $n$ determines how many nodes $R_{nl}$ has; the specific allowed energies depend on the form of $V(r)$ (Coulomb for hydrogen, harmonic for the 3D oscillator, etc.).

Degeneracy from $l$ and $m$. For a given $l$, there are $2l+1$ values of $m$ (from $-l$ to $l$). For a Hamiltonian that depends only on $n$ and $l$ (like hydrogen), all $2l+1$ states have the same energy — giving a $(2l+1)$-fold degeneracy in each $l$ level. Additional degeneracy can arise when the energy doesn't depend on $l$ at all (as it happens for hydrogen, due to the special $1/r$ symmetry).

Worked Examples
Example 2 — Angular momentum uncertainty relation

For a particle in the state $|l=1, m=0\rangle$, compute $\sigma_{L_x}$.

Step 1: $\langle L_x\rangle$. Since $[\hat{L}_x,\hat{L}_z] = -i\hbar\hat{L}_y$, the generalised uncertainty principle gives $\sigma_{L_x}\sigma_{L_z} \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}|\langle L_y\rangle|$. But first find $\langle L_x\rangle$.

Write $\hat{L}_x = (\hat{L}_+ + \hat{L}_-)/2$. In the $|1,0\rangle$ state: $\hat{L}_+|1,0\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{1(2)-0(1)}|1,1\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{2}|1,1\rangle$ and $\hat{L}_-|1,0\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{2}|1,-1\rangle$. Since $\langle 1,0|1,\pm 1\rangle = 0$: $\langle L_x\rangle = 0$. By symmetry $\langle L_y\rangle = 0$.

Step 2: $\langle L_x^2\rangle$. $\hat{L}_x^2 = (\hat{L}_+ + \hat{L}_-)^2/4 = (\hat{L}_+^2 + \hat{L}_+\hat{L}_- + \hat{L}_-\hat{L}_+ + \hat{L}_-^2)/4$.

Since $\hat{L}_+^2|1,0\rangle \propto |1,2\rangle$ (doesn't exist for $l=1$), those terms are zero when sandwiched with $\langle 1,0|$. We need:

$$\langle L_x^2\rangle = \frac{1}{4}\langle 1,0|(\hat{L}_+\hat{L}_- + \hat{L}_-\hat{L}_+)|1,0\rangle$$

Use $\hat{L}^2 = \hat{L}_+\hat{L}_- + \hat{L}_z^2 - \hbar\hat{L}_z$, so $\hat{L}_+\hat{L}_- = \hat{L}^2 - \hat{L}_z^2 + \hbar\hat{L}_z$. Similarly $\hat{L}_-\hat{L}_+ = \hat{L}^2 - \hat{L}_z^2 - \hbar\hat{L}_z$. Adding: $\hat{L}_+\hat{L}_- + \hat{L}_-\hat{L}_+ = 2(\hat{L}^2 - \hat{L}_z^2)$.

$$\langle L_x^2\rangle = \frac{1}{4}\cdot 2(l(l+1)\hbar^2 - m^2\hbar^2) = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}(l(l+1)-m^2)$$

For $l=1, m=0$: $\langle L_x^2\rangle = \hbar^2/2\cdot 2 = \hbar^2$. So $\sigma_{L_x} = \hbar$.

Verification: $\sigma_{L_x}^2 + \sigma_{L_y}^2 + \langle L_z\rangle^2 = \hbar^2 + \hbar^2 + 0 = 2\hbar^2$. But $\langle L^2\rangle = \hbar^2\cdot 1\cdot 2 = 2\hbar^2$ ✓.

Example 3 — Adding angular momenta (vector model)

Two particles each with $l = 1$. What are the possible total angular momentum values $L$?

When we add two angular momenta $l_1$ and $l_2$, the total $L$ ranges from $|l_1 - l_2|$ to $l_1 + l_2$ in integer steps: $$L = |l_1 - l_2|,\; |l_1-l_2|+1,\; \ldots,\; l_1+l_2$$ For $l_1 = l_2 = 1$: $L = 0, 1, 2$.

Counting states: the original $(2l_1+1)(2l_2+1) = 3\times 3 = 9$ product states reorganise into: $L=0$: 1 state; $L=1$: 3 states; $L=2$: 5 states. Total: $1+3+5=9$ ✓.

The $L=0$ state (symmetric in the magnitudes, but antisymmetric in orientation) has $M_L = 0$ and no preferred direction — a rotationally invariant "singlet." The $L=2$ states include the $M_L = \pm 2$ states, which require both angular momenta to be aligned with the $z$-axis.

Physical relevance: In helium, the two electrons each have orbital $l$; this is how we get $S$, $P$, $D$ states with $L = 0,1,2$. The actual energy depends on $L$ through exchange interactions, even though $V$ is a central potential — the geometry of the two-electron wavefunction matters.

Food for Thought
  • The classical angular momentum vector $\mathbf{L}$ has a definite direction. In quantum mechanics, $L^2$ and only one component ($L_z$) are simultaneously definite. So where is the angular momentum "pointing"? Is there a coherent picture?
    The vector model: think of $\mathbf{L}$ as a vector of length $\hbar\sqrt{l(l+1)}$ that is precessing around the $z$-axis at an angle $\cos^{-1}(m/\sqrt{l(l+1)})$. Since $L_x$ and $L_y$ are uncertain, the vector traces a cone — definite magnitude and $z$-component, but $x$ and $y$ components are uncertain and average to zero. This picture is semiclassical (not exact), but it's a useful intuitive image. The cone of precession is a standard diagram in atomic physics textbooks. For large $l$, it becomes classical: the vector points in a definite direction to good approximation.
  • The algebra of angular momentum allows half-integer values $l = 1/2, 3/2, \ldots$, but we said orbital angular momentum (from $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}$) requires integer $l$. Why? What breaks down for half-integer $l$ in position space?
    The spherical harmonics $Y_l^m \propto e^{im\phi}$ must be single-valued: $Y_l^m(\theta, \phi+2\pi) = Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$. For half-integer $m$, $e^{im\cdot 2\pi} = e^{i\pi} = -1$, so the function changes sign under a full rotation — it's double-valued, which is not allowed for a function on the sphere. Integer $l$ is required by this single-valuedness condition. Spin-1/2 avoids this because it doesn't live in position space — spin wavefunctions are two-component spinors, not functions of $(\theta,\phi)$, and single-valuedness doesn't apply to them in the same way.
  • For a central potential, the energy doesn't depend on $m$ — all $2l+1$ values of $m$ are degenerate. This seems to say the physics is rotationally symmetric, which makes sense. But if you add a small magnetic field (the Zeeman effect), the degeneracy is lifted and each $m$ gets a different energy. What breaks the symmetry?
    The magnetic field defines a preferred direction (the $z$-axis), breaking the rotational symmetry of the Hamiltonian. The term added is $\hat{H}' = -\boldsymbol{\mu}\cdot\mathbf{B} = (e/2m)\hat{L}_z B$. Since $\hat{L}_z|l,m\rangle = m\hbar|l,m\rangle$, each $m$ state shifts by an energy $\Delta E_m = m\hbar eB/2m_e = m\mu_B B$ where $\mu_B = e\hbar/2m_e$ is the Bohr magneton. The $2l+1$ degenerate states split into $2l+1$ equally spaced levels — this is the (normal) Zeeman effect. The original degeneracy was a consequence of the symmetry; break the symmetry, and the degeneracy breaks with it.
Common Pitfalls
  • $\hat{L}^2$ eigenvalue is $\hbar^2 l(l+1)$, not $\hbar^2 l^2$. The magnitude of angular momentum is $|\mathbf{L}| = \hbar\sqrt{l(l+1)}$, which is always larger than $\hbar l$. In particular, even for $m = l$ (maximum $z$-component), the total angular momentum vector does not point exactly along $z$ — there's always residual $x$-$y$ uncertainty. This is a quantum effect with no classical analogue.
  • $|Y_l^m|^2$ is azimuthally symmetric for all $m$ (not just $m=0$). The complex phase $e^{im\phi}$ has $|e^{im\phi}|^2 = 1$, so the probability density is independent of $\phi$. The "dumbbell" and "donut" shapes drawn in chemistry are always axially symmetric around $z$, regardless of $m$. The real-valued spherical harmonics ($p_x$, $p_y$, $d_{xy}$, etc.) used in chemistry are linear combinations of $Y_l^m$ and $Y_l^{-m}$ — useful for visualising bonding geometry but not eigenstates of $\hat{L}_z$.
  • The quantum numbers $(n, l, m)$ don't uniquely specify the state in a general central potential. The radial quantum number $n$ counts nodes in $R(r)$ and depends on the specific $V(r)$. For hydrogen, by convention $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ labels energy levels; but in other central potentials the counting differs. The quantum numbers $l$ and $m$ always have their angular momentum meaning.
  • The $z$-axis is conventional, not physical. We chose to diagonalise $\hat{L}_z$ by convention. Any direction is equally valid. Physical predictions (measurement probabilities) don't depend on which axis you call $z$, but individual matrix elements do. Be careful when comparing results from different textbooks that may use different conventions.
Connects to
  • The Hydrogen Atom (Lesson 8) — Hydrogen uses the full $(n,l,m)$ structure derived here. The Coulomb potential $V = -e^2/4\pi\epsilon_0 r$ goes into the radial equation; the angular part is exactly the spherical harmonics $Y_l^m$.
  • Spin (Lesson 9) — Spin is angular momentum with $s = 1/2$ — the same algebra $[\hat{S}_x,\hat{S}_y] = i\hbar\hat{S}_z$, the same ladder operators, the same eigenvalues $\hbar^2 s(s+1)$ and $\hbar m_s$. The only difference is that spin is intrinsic (not from $\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}$) and half-integer.
  • Classical Mechanics (planned) — Angular momentum is conserved in a central potential because rotating the system is a symmetry. Noether's theorem formalises this: each continuous symmetry of the Lagrangian gives a conserved quantity. Quantum mechanics inherits this connection — $[\hat{H},\hat{L}^2]=0$ is the quantum version of rotational invariance of $V(r)$.
