Quantum Mechanics

Wave Functions · Operators · Exactly Solvable Systems · Angular Momentum · Perturbation Theory

Quantum mechanics is the framework that describes nature at atomic and subatomic scales. It replaces the deterministic trajectories of classical mechanics with probability amplitudes, replaces physical quantities with operators, and replaces certainty with the irreducible randomness of measurement. Every device based on semiconductor physics, every laser, every MRI machine — all of it depends on quantum mechanics being exactly right.

The theory is mathematically precise and experimentally perfect. It has never been wrong. What makes it hard is not the mathematics — much of it is undergraduate linear algebra and differential equations — but the concepts. Particles that are waves. Measurement that disturbs what is measured. Cats that are alive and dead. The goal of this cluster is to make those concepts genuinely clear, not merely familiar, before turning them into calculations.

Prerequisites: Single-variable calculus, basic differential equations, complex numbers, and a little linear algebra (vectors, matrices, eigenvalues). Physics intuition from classical mechanics helps but is not assumed — we will explicitly point out where classical intuition fails and why.
