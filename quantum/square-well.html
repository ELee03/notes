Infinite Square Well

Particle in a Box · Energy Quantisation · Wavefunctions · Orthonormality · Superposition · Time Evolution

The infinite square well — a particle confined between impenetrable walls — is the simplest non-trivial quantum system. It has an exact solution, exhibits all the key features of quantum mechanics (energy quantisation, zero-point energy, discrete spectra, superposition), and serves as the prototype for understanding more complex systems. It is to quantum mechanics what the simple harmonic oscillator is to classical mechanics — simple enough to solve completely, rich enough to reveal deep principles.

The Potential and Boundary Conditions

The infinite square well potential places a particle in a box of width $a$:

$$V(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \leq x \leq a \\ \infty & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Outside the box, the potential is infinite, so the wavefunction must vanish: $\psi(x) = 0$ for $x \leq 0$ and $x \geq a$. Inside the box, $V = 0$, so the TISE becomes:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = E\psi \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = -k^2\psi, \quad k \equiv \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar}$$

For $E > 0$ this has the general solution $\psi(x) = A\sin(kx) + B\cos(kx)$. Applying the boundary conditions:

  • $\psi(0) = 0$: forces $B = 0$, so $\psi(x) = A\sin(kx)$.
  • $\psi(a) = 0$: forces $\sin(ka) = 0$, which means $ka = n\pi$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

The condition $n = 0$ is excluded because it gives $\psi = 0$ everywhere (non-normalisable). Negative integers just repeat the same physical states with opposite sign.

Quantised Energies

The boundary condition $ka = n\pi$ fixes the allowed values of $k$: $k_n = n\pi/a$, which gives the quantised energies:

$$\boxed{E_n = \frac{\hbar^2 k_n^2}{2m} = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2ma^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots}$$

This is the central result. The allowed energies are discrete — not a continuum. The quantum number $n$ labels the states. Several features:

  • $E_n \propto n^2$. Energies grow quadratically with $n$. The gap between adjacent levels grows: $E_2 - E_1 = 3E_1$, $E_3 - E_2 = 5E_1$, etc.
  • Zero-point energy. The lowest possible energy is $E_1 = \pi^2\hbar^2/2ma^2 > 0$. The particle can never be at rest — a direct consequence of the uncertainty principle. Confining the particle to width $a$ means $\Delta x \lesssim a$, so $\Delta p \gtrsim \hbar/a$, giving kinetic energy $\sim \hbar^2/2ma^2$. This matches $E_1$ up to a numerical factor.
  • Smaller box, higher energies. $E_n \propto 1/a^2$. Squeezing the box raises all energy levels — the quantum pressure that gives atomic electrons their finite ground-state energy and prevents atomic collapse.
Normalised Wavefunctions

The normalised stationary-state wavefunctions are:

$$\psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right), \quad 0 \leq x \leq a$$

To verify: $\int_0^a |\psi_n|^2\,dx = \frac{2}{a}\int_0^a \sin^2(n\pi x/a)\,dx = \frac{2}{a}\cdot\frac{a}{2} = 1$. ✓

Key features of $\psi_n$:

  • $\psi_n$ has $n-1$ nodes (zeros) in the interior of the box. More nodes means more oscillation, hence higher curvature, hence higher kinetic energy.
  • $\psi_1$ (the ground state) is always positive inside the box. Each successive state alternates sign one more time.
  • $\psi_n$ for odd $n$ is symmetric about the centre of the box; for even $n$, antisymmetric. This is a consequence of the potential's symmetry about $x = a/2$.
Infinite Square Well — Wavefunctions and Energy Levels
Orthonormality and Completeness

The energy eigenfunctions satisfy:

$$\int_0^a \psi_m^*(x)\,\psi_n(x)\,dx = \delta_{mn}$$

This is orthonormality: $\delta_{mn} = 1$ if $m = n$, zero otherwise. Orthogonality is not accidental — it follows from the Hermiticity of $\hat{H}$ (eigenfunctions of a Hermitian operator with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal). It also follows directly from the integral: $\int_0^a \sin(m\pi x/a)\sin(n\pi x/a)\,dx = (a/2)\delta_{mn}$.

The eigenfunctions are also complete: any normalisable function on $[0, a]$ can be expanded as:

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n\,\psi_n(x), \qquad c_n = \int_0^a \psi_n^*(x)\,f(x)\,dx$$

This is nothing but a Fourier sine series on $[0,a]$. The coefficients $c_n$ give the probability $|c_n|^2$ of measuring energy $E_n$ if the particle is in state $f(x)$.

Time Evolution of a Superposition

The general state at time $t$ is:

$$\Psi(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n\,\psi_n(x)\,e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}$$

When $\Psi$ is a superposition of two or more eigenstates, $|\Psi(x,t)|^2$ oscillates in time at the beat frequencies $(E_m - E_n)/\hbar$. The probability density "sloshes" back and forth inside the well, exactly as shown in the chart in Lesson 2. This is the quantum analogue of a classical particle bouncing back and forth — but the motion is in the probability density, not in a definite position.

For the equal superposition $\Psi = (1/\sqrt{2})(\psi_1 + \psi_2)e^{0}$ at $t=0$:

$$|\Psi(x,t)|^2 = \frac{1}{2}|\psi_1|^2 + \frac{1}{2}|\psi_2|^2 + \psi_1\psi_2\cos\!\left(\frac{(E_2-E_1)t}{\hbar}\right)$$

The cross term oscillates at angular frequency $\omega_{21} = (E_2-E_1)/\hbar$. After a full period $T = 2\pi\hbar/(E_2-E_1)$, the probability density returns to its initial form. This is the quantum revival — a purely quantum-mechanical phenomenon with no classical analogue.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Finding the coefficients $c_n$

A particle in an infinite square well of width $a$ is prepared in the state $\Psi(x,0) = Ax(a-x)$ for $0 \leq x \leq a$. Find the normalisation constant $A$ and the expansion coefficients $c_n$.

Normalisation: $\int_0^a |A|^2 x^2(a-x)^2\,dx = |A|^2\left[\frac{a^5}{3} - \frac{2a^5}{4} + \frac{a^5}{5}\right] = |A|^2\frac{a^5}{30} = 1$, giving $A = \sqrt{30/a^5}$.

Coefficients: $c_n = \int_0^a \psi_n^* \Psi(x,0)\,dx = \sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\sqrt{\frac{30}{a^5}}\int_0^a x(a-x)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\!dx$. The integral evaluates using integration by parts twice: $$c_n = \frac{4\sqrt{15}}{(n\pi)^3}\left[1 - (-1)^n\right]$$ So $c_n = 0$ for even $n$, and $c_n = 8\sqrt{15}/(n\pi)^3$ for odd $n$. The state has no even-$n$ components because $x(a-x)$ is symmetric about $x=a/2$ — it only overlaps with the odd (symmetric) eigenfunctions.

The probability of measuring $E_1$ is $|c_1|^2 = (8\sqrt{15}/\pi^3)^2 \approx 0.9986$. Nearly all the weight is in the ground state — the parabola $x(a-x)$ closely resembles $\psi_1$.

Example 2 — Expectation values in a stationary state

Find $\langle x \rangle$, $\langle x^2 \rangle$, $\langle p \rangle$, $\langle p^2 \rangle$ for the $n$-th stationary state of the infinite square well, and verify the uncertainty principle.

$\langle x \rangle = \frac{2}{a}\int_0^a x\sin^2(n\pi x/a)\,dx = \frac{a}{2}$ (centre of well, by symmetry for all $n$)

$\langle x^2 \rangle = \frac{a^2}{3} - \frac{a^2}{2n^2\pi^2}$

$\langle p \rangle = -i\hbar\frac{2}{a}\int_0^a \sin(n\pi x/a)\frac{d}{dx}\sin(n\pi x/a)\,dx = 0$ (by symmetry: $\psi_n$ is real, real functions have zero mean momentum)

$\langle p^2 \rangle = \frac{2}{a}\int_0^a \sin(n\pi x/a)\left(-\hbar^2\frac{d^2}{dx^2}\right)\sin(n\pi x/a)\,dx = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{a^2}$

Note: $\langle p^2\rangle/2m = n^2\pi^2\hbar^2/2ma^2 = E_n$ ✓ — all energy is kinetic inside the well.

$\sigma_x = \sqrt{\langle x^2\rangle - \langle x\rangle^2} = a\sqrt{1/12 - 1/2n^2\pi^2}$, $\quad\sigma_p = n\pi\hbar/a$

$\sigma_x\sigma_p = n\pi\hbar\sqrt{1/12 - 1/2n^2\pi^2} \geq \hbar/2$ ✓ (the minimum is $\hbar/2$ for the harmonic oscillator ground state; the square well has a larger product).

Fast-Fire Thinking
  • The ground state of the infinite square well has $E_1 > 0$. But $V = 0$ inside the well. Where does this energy come from?

    It is purely kinetic energy, arising from the uncertainty principle. Confining the particle to a region of width $a$ requires $\Delta x \lesssim a$, which implies $\Delta p \gtrsim \hbar/a$, and hence kinetic energy $\langle p^2\rangle/2m \gtrsim \hbar^2/2ma^2$. This is the zero-point energy. It does not "come from" anywhere in the sense of energy being transferred — it is the minimum kinetic energy consistent with the spatial constraint. You cannot make the ground-state energy zero by removing the potential, because without confinement there is no potential well, hence no bound state. The energy is an intrinsic consequence of quantum confinement.

  • For large quantum number $n$, the probability density $|\psi_n|^2$ oscillates rapidly. Is this consistent with the classical limit?

    Yes — this is the correspondence principle in action. A classical particle bouncing inside a box spends equal time at all positions (uniform probability density). For $|\psi_n|^2 = (2/a)\sin^2(n\pi x/a)$, as $n \to \infty$ the function oscillates so rapidly that no detector with finite resolution can resolve the individual peaks. The average value of $\sin^2$ is $1/2$, so coarse-grained $|\psi_n|^2 \to 1/a$ — the uniform classical distribution. Quantum mechanics reproduces classical results when the de Broglie wavelength $\lambda = 2a/n$ is much smaller than the box size. This is the general principle: quantum discreteness becomes irrelevant when $\lambda \ll$ (size of system).

  • Why is $E_n \propto 1/a^2$ rather than $1/a$ or $1/a^3$?

    Dimensionally: the only length in the problem is $a$, and the only energy scale from $\hbar$ and $m$ is $\hbar^2/ma^2$. So $E_n \propto \hbar^2/ma^2$ on dimensional grounds alone. The $1/a^2$ dependence is forced by the combination of $\hbar$ (units of energy·time = kg·m²/s), $m$ (kg), and $a$ (m): the unique combination with units of energy is $[\hbar^2/ma^2]$ = kg·m²·s⁻² = J. Physically: the momentum scale in the box is $p \sim \hbar/a$ (de Broglie), so kinetic energy $\sim p^2/2m \sim \hbar^2/ma^2$.

Pitfalls
  • Do not include $n = 0$. $\psi_0 = 0$ everywhere — not a physical state. The quantum numbers run $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ The ground state is $n = 1$.
  • The derivative of $\psi$ is discontinuous at the walls. Normally we require $d\psi/dx$ to be continuous. The exception is an infinite potential jump: the derivative can be discontinuous at $x=0$ and $x=a$ because $V = \infty$ there. Finite potentials always give continuous derivatives; only the idealized infinite wall is the exception.
  • Orthogonality means $\int\psi_m^*\psi_n = \delta_{mn}$, not that $\psi_m\psi_n = 0$ pointwise. Two wavefunctions can be nonzero at the same location and still be orthogonal — orthogonality is a statement about the inner product (integral), not pointwise vanishing.
  • The expansion coefficients depend on the initial state, not the box. $c_n = \int \psi_n^* \Psi(x,0)\,dx$ changes with $\Psi(x,0)$. The energy eigenstates $\psi_n$ are properties of the Hamiltonian (the box); how much of each eigenstate is present is a property of the particular quantum state you prepared.
