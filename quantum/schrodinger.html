The Schrödinger Equation

TDSE · Stationary States · Born Rule · Expectation Values · Operators · Ehrenfest's Theorem

In classical mechanics, Newton's second law $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ is the equation of motion: given initial position and velocity, it tells you where the particle will be at every future time. In quantum mechanics, the wavefunction $\Psi(x,t)$ replaces position and velocity as the complete description of a particle's state, and the equation that governs its evolution is the Schrödinger equation, written down by Erwin Schrödinger in 1926.

The Schrödinger equation cannot be derived from more fundamental principles — it is a postulate, justified by the fact that its predictions match experiment exactly. What we can do is motivate it and understand its structure.

The Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation

For a particle of mass $m$ moving in a potential $V(x,t)$, the Schrödinger equation is:

$$i\hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial x^2} + V\Psi \equiv \hat{H}\Psi$$

The right-hand side is the Hamiltonian operator $\hat{H}$ acting on $\Psi$. This is the quantum version of the classical Hamiltonian $H = p^2/2m + V$ — kinetic plus potential energy — but with momentum replaced by the operator $\hat{p} = -i\hbar\, \partial/\partial x$.

Several features are immediately notable:

  • First-order in time. Unlike the wave equation (second order in $t$), the Schrödinger equation is first order in time. Given $\Psi(x,0)$, the future is completely determined — the evolution is deterministic. Randomness only enters at measurement.
  • Complex. The factor $i$ on the left forces $\Psi$ to be complex-valued. This is not a trick or an approximation. The imaginary unit is fundamental to quantum mechanics.
  • Linear. If $\Psi_1$ and $\Psi_2$ are solutions, so is any linear combination $c_1\Psi_1 + c_2\Psi_2$. This is the superposition principle.
Separation of Variables: Stationary States

For time-independent potentials $V = V(x)$, we can factor the wavefunction: $\Psi(x, t) = \psi(x)\,\varphi(t)$. Substituting into the Schrödinger equation and dividing both sides by $\psi\varphi$:

$$i\hbar \frac{1}{\varphi}\frac{d\varphi}{dt} = \frac{1}{\psi}\left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V\psi\right]$$

The left side depends only on $t$, the right only on $x$. Both must equal the same constant, which we call $E$ (for reasons that will become clear). This splits into two equations:

$$\frac{d\varphi}{dt} = -\frac{iE}{\hbar}\varphi \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \varphi(t) = e^{-iEt/\hbar}$$ $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V\psi = E\psi \qquad \text{(time-independent Schrödinger equation, TISE)}$$

The full solution is:

$$\Psi(x,t) = \psi(x)\,e^{-iEt/\hbar}$$

These are called stationary states. The probability density is:

$$|\Psi(x,t)|^2 = |\psi(x)|^2 \left|e^{-iEt/\hbar}\right|^2 = |\psi(x)|^2$$

which is time-independent — the probability distribution does not change in time, hence "stationary." The time dependence is entirely in the phase factor $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$, which cancels when you take the absolute square. This justifies calling $E$ energy: $E$ is the definite energy of the state, and the TISE $\hat{H}\psi = E\psi$ is an eigenvalue equation for the Hamiltonian.

Normalisation and Probability

Because $|\Psi(x,t)|^2 dx$ is a probability, the wavefunction must be normalised:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\Psi(x,t)|^2\, dx = 1$$

For a stationary state this becomes $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\psi(x)|^2\, dx = 1$. If a solution $\psi$ satisfies the TISE but is not normalised, we multiply by a constant $A$ such that $|A|^2 \int |\psi|^2 dx = 1$. Not all solutions of the TISE are normalisable (free-particle plane waves, for example, extend over all space). Only normalisable solutions correspond to physically realisable quantum states.

A key result: if $\Psi$ is normalised at $t = 0$, it stays normalised for all time. This follows from the Schrödinger equation itself — we can show $d/dt \int |\Psi|^2 dx = 0$. This consistency is not trivial: it is what makes the probabilistic interpretation self-consistent.

Expectation Values and Operators

Since measurement outcomes are probabilistic, we characterise quantum states by expectation values — the average value of a measurement over many identically prepared systems:

$$\langle x \rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\, |\Psi(x,t)|^2\, dx$$

For momentum, the story is more subtle. A particle with definite momentum $p$ has wavefunction $e^{ipx/\hbar}$ (a plane wave). To extract momentum information from a general $\Psi$, we use the momentum operator:

$$\hat{p} = -i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial x}$$

Expectation values are computed by sandwiching the operator between $\Psi^*$ and $\Psi$:

$$\langle p \rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi^*\left(-i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\right)\Psi\, dx$$

This generalises. Any observable $Q(x, p)$ is represented by a quantum operator $\hat{Q}(x, \hat{p}) = \hat{Q}(x, -i\hbar\,\partial/\partial x)$, and its expectation value is:

$$\langle Q \rangle = \int \Psi^*\,\hat{Q}\,\Psi\, dx$$

The kinetic energy operator is $\hat{T} = \hat{p}^2/2m = -(\hbar^2/2m)\,\partial^2/\partial x^2$, and the total energy (Hamiltonian) is $\hat{H} = \hat{T} + V$. For a stationary state $\psi(x)e^{-iEt/\hbar}$, the expectation value of $\hat{H}$ is just $E$ — the definite energy of that state.

Ehrenfest's Theorem

Quantum mechanics must reduce to classical mechanics in the appropriate limit. Ehrenfest's theorem makes this precise. Starting from the Schrödinger equation and computing $d\langle x\rangle/dt$ and $d\langle p \rangle/dt$:

$$\frac{d\langle x \rangle}{dt} = \frac{\langle p \rangle}{m}$$ $$\frac{d\langle p \rangle}{dt} = -\left\langle \frac{\partial V}{\partial x} \right\rangle$$

These are the quantum analogues of Hamilton's equations. If the wavefunction is a narrow wave packet — well-localised in space — then $\langle \partial V/\partial x \rangle \approx \partial V/\partial x \big|_{\langle x \rangle}$, and we recover Newton's second law exactly: $m\,d^2\langle x\rangle/dt^2 = F(\langle x\rangle)$. Classical mechanics governs the motion of expectation values in the limit of narrow wave packets. This is why macroscopic objects follow classical trajectories despite being quantum mechanical.

The Continuity Equation

Since $|\Psi|^2$ is a probability density, it must satisfy a conservation law: probability cannot appear or disappear, only flow. Define the probability current:

$$J(x,t) = \frac{\hbar}{2mi}\left(\Psi^*\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial x} - \Psi\frac{\partial\Psi^*}{\partial x}\right)$$

Then the Schrödinger equation implies the continuity equation:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\Psi|^2 + \frac{\partial J}{\partial x} = 0$$

This says that any local decrease in probability density is accompanied by a net outward flow of probability current — exactly like conservation of electric charge or fluid mass. The probability current $J$ is real-valued and tells us the net flow of probability per unit time across a given point. This will be essential when we analyse quantum scattering and tunneling in Lesson 5.

Superposition and the General Solution

The TISE is an eigenvalue equation, and for a given potential it typically has a discrete or continuous set of solutions $\{\psi_n\}$ with corresponding energies $\{E_n\}$. The general solution of the full (time-dependent) Schrödinger equation is a superposition of stationary states:

$$\Psi(x,t) = \sum_n c_n\,\psi_n(x)\,e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}$$

The coefficients $c_n$ are determined by the initial condition $\Psi(x,0) = \sum_n c_n \psi_n(x)$. They encode the probability: the probability of measuring energy $E_n$ is $|c_n|^2$. Normalisation requires $\sum_n |c_n|^2 = 1$.

When the potential has a continuous spectrum (as for the free particle), the sum becomes an integral. In either case, the strategy is the same: find the stationary states, expand the initial condition in terms of them, and attach the appropriate phase factors $e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}$ to each component.

Important: a superposition state does not have a definite energy. A measurement of energy on the state $\Psi = c_1\psi_1 + c_2\psi_2$ will return $E_1$ with probability $|c_1|^2$ or $E_2$ with probability $|c_2|^2$. After measurement, the wavefunction "collapses" to whichever eigenstate was observed. This is wavefunction collapse — the most conceptually contentious part of quantum mechanics.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Showing normalisation is preserved

Show that if $\int|\Psi(x,0)|^2 dx = 1$, then $\int|\Psi(x,t)|^2 dx = 1$ for all $t$.

Differentiate with respect to time: $$\frac{d}{dt}\int_{-\infty}^\infty |\Psi|^2\,dx = \int \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\Psi|^2\,dx = -\int \frac{\partial J}{\partial x}\,dx = -[J]_{-\infty}^{\infty}$$ Since normalisable wavefunctions vanish at $\pm\infty$, $J \to 0$ at both limits, so $d/dt \int |\Psi|^2 dx = 0$. The integral is constant in time. $\square$

Example 2 — Expectation value of energy

A particle is in the state $\Psi = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\psi_1 + \psi_2)e^{0}$ at $t = 0$, where $\psi_1$, $\psi_2$ are energy eigenstates with energies $E_1$, $E_2$. What is $\langle H \rangle$?

The state at time $t$ is $\Psi = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\psi_1 e^{-iE_1 t/\hbar} + \psi_2 e^{-iE_2 t/\hbar})$. Using $\langle H \rangle = \int \Psi^* \hat{H} \Psi\, dx$ and the fact that $\hat{H}\psi_n = E_n\psi_n$ with $\int \psi_n^* \psi_m dx = \delta_{nm}$: $$\langle H \rangle = \frac{1}{2}(E_1 + E_2)$$ This is time-independent (energy conservation). Each individual measurement gives $E_1$ or $E_2$ with equal probability $\frac{1}{2}$; the average is $(E_1+E_2)/2$. Note that $\langle H \rangle \neq E_1$ or $E_2$ — the particle is in a superposition and has no definite energy.

Example 3 — Momentum operator on a plane wave

Show that $\psi(x) = Ae^{ikx}$ is an eigenstate of $\hat{p}$ and find the eigenvalue.

Apply the momentum operator: $$\hat{p}\,\psi = -i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}(Ae^{ikx}) = -i\hbar(ik)(Ae^{ikx}) = \hbar k \cdot Ae^{ikx} = \hbar k\,\psi$$ So $e^{ikx}$ is a momentum eigenstate with eigenvalue $p = \hbar k$. This matches the de Broglie relation: $p = h/\lambda = h/(2\pi/k) = \hbar k$. The plane wave has a perfectly definite momentum but is not normalisable — it extends over all space, consistent with $\Delta x = \infty$ demanded by $\Delta x \Delta p \geq \hbar/2$ when $\Delta p = 0$.

Fast-Fire Thinking
  • A stationary state has $|\Psi|^2$ that is independent of time. Does this mean nothing is happening?

    No. The wavefunction itself is still oscillating — the phase factor $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$ rotates in the complex plane at frequency $\omega = E/\hbar$. What is time-independent is $|\Psi|^2$ — the probability density — because the phase factor cancels when you take the modulus squared. All expectation values of time-independent operators ($\hat{x}$, $\hat{p}$, $\hat{H}$) are constant. But the particle is not "sitting still" — it still has kinetic energy, and the probability current $J$ may be nonzero in complex systems. "Stationary" means stationary in the statistical sense, not in the mechanical sense.

  • Why must quantum observables be represented by Hermitian operators?

    A Hermitian operator $\hat{Q}$ satisfies $\langle f|\hat{Q}|g\rangle = \langle\hat{Q}f|g\rangle$ for all $f,g$. Two key consequences: (1) The eigenvalues of a Hermitian operator are real. Since eigenvalues correspond to measurement outcomes — energies, momenta, positions — they must be real numbers. (2) Eigenfunctions with different eigenvalues are orthogonal, which is required for the measurement probabilities $|c_n|^2$ to be well-defined and sum to 1. Any non-Hermitian operator would give complex measurement outcomes or probabilities that don't add up to 1 — both physically meaningless.

  • Ehrenfest's theorem says $d\langle p\rangle/dt = -\langle \partial V/\partial x\rangle$. Is this the same as Newton's second law?

    Almost, but not exactly. Newton's law (in the classical limit) is $dp/dt = -\partial V/\partial x\big|_x$, evaluated at the particle's position. Ehrenfest gives $d\langle p\rangle/dt = -\langle \partial V/\partial x\rangle$, where the average is over the wavefunction. These are the same only if $\partial V/\partial x$ is constant (uniform force) or if the wavefunction is so sharply peaked that $\langle f(x) \rangle \approx f(\langle x\rangle)$. For a spread-out wavefunction in a non-linear potential, the two differ — this is quantum spreading and has no classical analogue. A quantum wave packet in an anharmonic potential will spread out and eventually not follow the classical trajectory at all.

Pitfalls
  • The Schrödinger equation is not relativistic. The TDSE as written applies to non-relativistic particles (speeds much less than $c$). The relativistic generalisations are the Klein-Gordon equation (spin-0 particles) and the Dirac equation (spin-½ particles). For electrons in atoms, non-relativistic QM is an excellent approximation — relativistic corrections are the "fine structure" of order $(v/c)^2 \sim \alpha^2 \approx (1/137)^2$.
  • Don't confuse $\Psi$ with $|\Psi|^2$. The wavefunction $\Psi$ is complex — it has phase information that matters for interference. $|\Psi|^2$ is real and is the probability density. You cannot recover $\Psi$ from $|\Psi|^2$ alone — the phase is lost. This matters when combining wavefunctions (superpositions): you add $\Psi$'s, not $|\Psi|^2$'s.
  • Stationary states are not the only allowed states. Any normalisable function can be a quantum state — it just won't have a definite energy. Stationary states are special because they have definite energy and simple time evolution, making them useful as basis states. The general solution is always a superposition of them.
  • The separation constant $E$ is real. This follows from the requirement that $\Psi$ remain normalisable for all time. If $E$ had a positive imaginary part, the time factor $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$ would grow exponentially, violating normalisation. Hermiticity of $\hat{H}$ guarantees real eigenvalues.
