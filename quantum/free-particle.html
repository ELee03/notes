Free Particle & Tunneling

Wave Packets · Dispersion · Delta Potential · Quantum Tunneling · WKB

A particle with no forces acting on it — the free particle — turns out to be much stranger than it first appears. Its energy eigenstates aren't normalizable: a particle with a perfectly sharp momentum must be spread over all of space. Real particles are wave packets, superpositions of plane waves that localize in space but spread over time. Then things get weirder: put a barrier in the way, and the particle has a nonzero probability of appearing on the other side even when classical mechanics forbids it. That's quantum tunneling — and it powers everything from nuclear fusion to the scanning tunneling microscope.

The Free Particle

Set $V(x) = 0$ everywhere. The time-independent Schrödinger equation becomes:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = E\psi \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = -k^2\psi, \qquad k \equiv \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar}$$

The general solution is $\psi(x) = Ae^{ikx} + Be^{-ikx}$, representing a rightward traveling wave plus a leftward traveling wave. Including the time factor $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$:

$$\Psi_k(x,t) = Ae^{i(kx - \omega t)}, \qquad \omega \equiv \frac{\hbar k^2}{2m}$$

where $k > 0$ means rightward propagation and $k < 0$ means leftward (we can allow both signs if we define $k = \pm\sqrt{2mE}/\hbar$). These are plane waves — sinusoids that fill all of space.

The normalisation problem. Plane waves can't be normalised: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |Ae^{ikx}|^2\,dx = |A|^2 \cdot \infty$. A free particle with a definite momentum $p = \hbar k$ is not a physically realisable state — it would require the particle to be found with equal probability at every point in the universe. Real free particles are wave packets.

There's a deeper point here. The problem isn't just normalisability — it's that a free particle can't strictly be in a stationary state at all. Stationary states require a potential to confine the particle; without confinement, any sharp-energy state is delocalised over all space. This is the uncertainty principle in action: a perfectly definite momentum ($\Delta p = 0$) demands infinite position uncertainty ($\Delta x = \infty$).

Wave Packets and Dispersion

A physical free particle is a wave packet: a superposition of plane waves with a spread of wave numbers centred around some $k_0$. By the inverse Fourier theorem:

$$\Psi(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi(k)\, e^{i(kx - \omega(k)\,t)}\,dk$$

where $\phi(k)$ is the momentum-space wavefunction. At $t=0$, this reduces to the Fourier transform of $\Psi(x,0)$:

$$\phi(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi(x,0)\,e^{-ikx}\,dx$$

So given any initial wavepacket shape $\Psi(x,0)$, you Fourier-transform to get $\phi(k)$, stick in the time factors $e^{-i\omega(k)t}$, then inverse-Fourier-transform to propagate forward in time. This is the complete solution for a free particle.

Now a critical question: how fast does the packet move? There are two velocities:

Phase velocity vs group velocity.
The phase velocity of a single plane wave component is how fast the wavecrest moves: $$v_\text{phase} = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{\hbar k}{2m} = \frac{p}{2m}$$ The group velocity is how fast the envelope (the packet) moves: $$v_\text{group} = \frac{d\omega}{dk} = \frac{\hbar k}{m} = \frac{p}{m}$$ The group velocity equals the classical particle velocity $p/m$. The phase velocity is exactly half of that. The packet moves at the right speed; individual wave crests inside it move at half that speed and drift backward relative to the envelope.

This happens because the free-particle dispersion relation is dispersive: $\omega \propto k^2$, not $\omega \propto k$. Different frequency components travel at different speeds, so the packet spreads out over time. A Gaussian wave packet that starts with width $\sigma$ at $t=0$ has width

$$\sigma(t) = \sigma\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\hbar t}{2m\sigma^2}\right)^2}$$

This spreading is irreversible — you can't un-spread a free wavepacket. It's a direct consequence of Heisenberg: a packet with finite position uncertainty $\Delta x = \sigma$ has momentum uncertainty $\Delta p \geq \hbar/(2\sigma)$, and those different momentum components propagate at different speeds, pulling the packet apart.

Example 1 — Gaussian wave packet: momentum spread and spreading rate

Start with a Gaussian wave packet centred at $x=0$ with initial width $\sigma$:

$$\Psi(x,0) = A\,e^{-x^2/(4\sigma^2)}\,e^{ik_0 x}$$

where $k_0 = mv_0/\hbar$ gives the packet a mean momentum $p_0 = \hbar k_0$.

Step 1: Find $A$ from normalisation. $\int_{-\infty}^\infty |Ae^{-x^2/(4\sigma^2)}|^2\,dx = |A|^2\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma = 1$, so $A = (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-1/4}$.

Step 2: Compute $\phi(k)$.

$$\phi(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^\infty (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-1/4}\,e^{-x^2/(4\sigma^2)}\,e^{i(k_0-k)x}\,dx = \left(\frac{2\sigma^2}{\pi}\right)^{1/4} e^{-\sigma^2(k-k_0)^2}$$

This is also Gaussian — a Gaussian in position gives a Gaussian in momentum. The widths multiply to give $\Delta x \,\Delta k = 1/2$, or $\Delta x\,\Delta p = \hbar/2$: the Gaussian saturates the uncertainty bound.

Step 3: Spreading rate. For a 10 g marble with $\sigma = 1\,\text{mm}$: $$\tau = \frac{2m\sigma^2}{\hbar} = \frac{2(0.01)(10^{-3})^2}{1.055\times10^{-34}} \approx 2\times10^{26}\,\text{s}$$ The age of the universe is $\sim 4\times10^{17}$ s. Macroscopic objects don't spread appreciably — the quantum effect is completely negligible. For an electron with $\sigma = 1\,\text{nm}$: $$\tau_e = \frac{2(9.1\times10^{-31})(10^{-9})^2}{1.055\times10^{-34}} \approx 1.7\times10^{-14}\,\text{s}$$ Electron wave packets spread on femtosecond timescales.

The Delta Function Potential

The simplest potential that has both bound and scattering states is:

$$V(x) = -\alpha\,\delta(x), \qquad \alpha > 0$$

This is an infinitely deep, infinitely narrow well at the origin — the limit of a rectangular well as you make it taller and thinner while keeping the area $\alpha$ constant. It's a useful toy model for a short-range attractive interaction (e.g. a nucleus), and it's exactly solvable.

The TISE is $-(\hbar^2/2m)\psi'' - \alpha\delta(x)\psi = E\psi$. Away from the origin ($x \neq 0$) this is just the free-particle equation $\psi'' = -k^2\psi$ (if $E > 0$) or $\psi'' = \kappa^2\psi$ (if $E < 0$, with $\kappa \equiv \sqrt{-2mE}/\hbar > 0$).

Matching conditions at $x=0$. Integrating the TISE from $-\epsilon$ to $+\epsilon$ and taking $\epsilon \to 0$:

$$\psi'(0^+) - \psi'(0^-) = -\frac{2m\alpha}{\hbar^2}\psi(0)$$

So the wavefunction is continuous at $x=0$, but its derivative has a discontinuous jump proportional to $\psi(0)$. This is the key matching condition for any delta-function potential.

Bound state ($E < 0$). We need $\psi \to 0$ as $|x| \to \infty$. Writing $\kappa = \sqrt{-2mE}/\hbar$:

$$\psi(x) = \begin{cases} Be^{\kappa x} & x < 0 \\ Be^{-\kappa x} & x > 0 \end{cases}$$

(Continuity at 0 forces both coefficients equal.) Applying the derivative jump condition: $-B\kappa - B\kappa = -(2m\alpha/\hbar^2)B$, giving $\kappa = m\alpha/\hbar^2$. There is exactly one bound state, with energy and wavefunction:

$$E_\text{bound} = -\frac{m\alpha^2}{2\hbar^2}, \qquad \psi(x) = \frac{\sqrt{m\alpha}}{\hbar}\,e^{-m\alpha|x|/\hbar^2}$$

Scattering states ($E > 0$). Now $k = \sqrt{2mE}/\hbar > 0$. Send a wave in from the left:

$$\psi(x) = \begin{cases} Ae^{ikx} + Be^{-ikx} & x < 0 \quad\text{(incident + reflected)} \\ Fe^{ikx} & x > 0 \quad\text{(transmitted)} \end{cases}$$

Continuity at 0 gives $A + B = F$. The derivative jump gives $ik(F - A + B) = -(2m\alpha/\hbar^2)F$. Defining $\beta \equiv m\alpha/(\hbar^2 k)$, the reflection and transmission coefficients are:

$$R = \left|\frac{B}{A}\right|^2 = \frac{\beta^2}{1+\beta^2}, \qquad T = \left|\frac{F}{A}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{1+\beta^2}$$

Note $R + T = 1$ as required. And crucially: $T < 1$ for all finite energies — even classically allowed scattering off an attractive potential reflects some of the wave. This is a quantum effect with no classical analogue.

The Finite Square Well

The finite square well — $V = -V_0$ for $|x| < a$ and $V = 0$ outside — is the delta function's big brother. It has a finite number of bound states and admits scattering states at all positive energies.

Bound states ($-V_0 < E < 0$). Inside the well define $l \equiv \sqrt{2m(E+V_0)}/\hbar$ (real, since $E > -V_0$). Outside define $\kappa \equiv \sqrt{-2mE}/\hbar$. Matching the even-parity solution ($\psi \propto \cos(lx)$ inside) at the boundary $x=a$ gives the transcendental equation:

$$l\tan(la) = \kappa$$

with odd-parity solutions satisfying $-l\cot(la) = \kappa$. These must be solved numerically. The number of bound states is $\lceil \sqrt{2mV_0}\,a/(\pi\hbar/2) \rceil$ — it grows with the well depth and width. In the limit $V_0 \to \infty$, the energies approach those of the infinite square well.

Scattering states and resonant transmission. At $E > 0$, waves can scatter off the well. The transmission coefficient for a rectangular well of width $2a$ and depth $V_0$ is:

$$T = \left[1 + \frac{V_0^2 \sin^2(2la)}{4E(E+V_0)}\right]^{-1}, \qquad l = \frac{\sqrt{2m(E+V_0)}}{\hbar}$$

When $\sin(2la) = 0$, i.e. $2la = n\pi$, we get $T = 1$: perfect transmission at specific energies. This is the Ramsauer–Townsend effect — electrons pass through noble gas atoms with anomalously high transmission at certain energies, confirming the wave nature of electrons. The resonance condition $2a = n\lambda/2$ means the well contains an integer number of half-wavelengths — standing waves fit perfectly.

Quantum Tunneling

Now flip the potential: consider a barrier $V = +V_0$ for $0 < x < d$, zero elsewhere. Classically, a particle with $E < V_0$ is reflected at the barrier. In quantum mechanics, it tunnels through.

Inside the barrier, the TISE gives $\psi'' = \kappa^2\psi$ with $\kappa = \sqrt{2m(V_0-E)}/\hbar > 0$. The solutions are real exponentials $e^{\pm\kappa x}$ — the wavefunction decays across the barrier rather than oscillating. The amplitude at the far side is suppressed, but nonzero.

Working through the matching conditions at both walls of the barrier gives the transmission coefficient:

$$T = \left[1 + \frac{\sinh^2(\kappa d)}{4\frac{E}{V_0}\left(1-\frac{E}{V_0}\right)}\right]^{-1}$$

For a wide or high barrier ($\kappa d \gg 1$), $\sinh(\kappa d) \approx \frac{1}{2}e^{\kappa d}$, and:

$$T \approx 16\frac{E}{V_0}\left(1-\frac{E}{V_0}\right)\,e^{-2\kappa d}$$

The dominant factor is the exponential in barrier width and height. Doubling the barrier width squares the suppression factor; increasing the height increases $\kappa$ and suppresses exponentially.

WKB approximation (for non-uniform barriers). When the barrier height $V(x)$ varies slowly, the tunneling exponent generalises to an integral: $$T \approx \exp\!\left(-\frac{2}{\hbar}\int_{x_1}^{x_2} \sqrt{2m(V(x)-E)}\,dx\right)$$ where $x_1, x_2$ are the classical turning points where $V(x) = E$. This is the WKB (Wentzel–Kramers–Brillouin) approximation. It lets you estimate tunneling rates for realistic potentials — alpha decay, field emission, and quantum chemistry all use this formula.

Applications of quantum tunneling:

  • Alpha decay. An alpha particle inside a heavy nucleus tunnels through the Coulomb barrier. The Geiger–Nuttall law — the empirical correlation between half-life and alpha energy — follows directly from the WKB formula. A factor of 2 change in decay energy changes the half-life by many orders of magnitude.
  • Scanning tunnelling microscope (STM). A metallic tip held nanometres above a surface: electrons tunnel across the vacuum gap. Because $T \propto e^{-2\kappa d}$, a 1 Å change in gap changes the current by roughly an order of magnitude — giving atomic-scale height resolution.
  • Josephson junctions. Cooper pairs in a superconductor tunnel through a thin insulating layer; the resulting supercurrent underpins SQUIDs (quantum magnetometers) and qubit devices.
  • Nuclear fusion in stars. Protons in the solar core have energies ~1 keV, far below the ~1 MeV Coulomb barrier. Fusion happens only because of tunneling — without it, the Sun wouldn't shine.
  • Tunnel diodes and transistors. Modern transistor gates are thin enough that gate-leakage tunneling is a real engineering constraint; tunnel diodes exploit resonant tunneling deliberately.

Worked Examples
Example 2 — Delta function potential: bound state and scattering

Consider $V(x) = -\alpha\delta(x)$ with $\alpha = 3.81\,\text{eV·Å}$ (chosen to give a round energy for an electron).

(a) Find the bound state energy.

$$E = -\frac{m_e\alpha^2}{2\hbar^2} = -\frac{(9.109\times10^{-31})(3.81\times10^{-10}\times 1.602\times10^{-19})^2}{2(1.055\times10^{-34})^2}$$

Converting $\alpha$: $3.81\,\text{eV·Å} = 3.81 \times 1.602\times10^{-19}\,\text{J} \times 10^{-10}\,\text{m} = 6.104\times10^{-29}\,\text{J·m}$.

$$E = -\frac{(9.109\times10^{-31})(6.104\times10^{-29})^2}{2(1.055\times10^{-34})^2} \approx -1.51\,\text{eV}$$

This is the energy of the $n=2$ level of hydrogen — not a coincidence; a 1D delta well models the hydrogen atom in a rough sense.

(b) Find T and R for an electron with kinetic energy $E = 4E_0 = 6.04\,\text{eV}$ incident on the barrier.

Here $\beta = m\alpha/(\hbar^2 k)$. We have $k = \sqrt{2m_e E}/\hbar$. Define $E_0 = |E_\text{bound}| = m_e\alpha^2/(2\hbar^2)$. Then:

$$\beta = \frac{m_e\alpha}{\hbar^2 k} = \frac{m_e\alpha}{\hbar\sqrt{2m_e E}} = \sqrt{\frac{E_0}{E}} = \sqrt{\frac{1.51}{6.04}} = \frac{1}{2}$$
$$T = \frac{1}{1+\beta^2} = \frac{1}{1+1/4} = \frac{4}{5} = 0.80, \qquad R = \frac{1}{5} = 0.20$$

Even at $4\times$ the binding energy, only 80% of the incident wave is transmitted. There is no classical analogue to this reflection off an attractive well.

Example 3 — Tunneling through a nuclear Coulomb barrier (alpha decay model)

Model the Coulomb barrier around a nucleus as a rectangular barrier for a rough estimate. Consider an alpha particle ($m = 4 \times 1.67\times10^{-27}\,\text{kg} = 6.68\times10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) facing a barrier of height $V_0 = 30\,\text{MeV}$ and width $d = 10\,\text{fm} = 10^{-14}\,\text{m}$, with kinetic energy $E = 5\,\text{MeV}$.

Step 1: Compute $\kappa$.

$$\kappa = \frac{\sqrt{2m(V_0-E)}}{\hbar} = \frac{\sqrt{2(6.68\times10^{-27})(25\times10^6)(1.602\times10^{-19})}}{1.055\times10^{-34}}$$
$$= \frac{\sqrt{5.356\times10^{-19}}}{1.055\times10^{-34}} = \frac{7.319\times10^{-10}}{1.055\times10^{-34}} \approx 6.94\times10^{24}\,\text{m}^{-1}$$

Step 2: Compute $\kappa d$.

$$\kappa d = (6.94\times10^{24})(10^{-14}) \approx 69$$

Step 3: Approximate $T$ for large $\kappa d$.

$$T \approx 16\frac{E}{V_0}\left(1-\frac{E}{V_0}\right)e^{-2\kappa d} = 16\cdot\frac{5}{30}\cdot\frac{25}{30}\cdot e^{-138} \approx 2.2\, e^{-138}$$
$$T \approx 10^{-59}$$

This is extraordinarily small. But an alpha particle inside a nucleus bounces back and forth $\sim 10^{21}$ times per second. The decay rate is proportional to $T$ times this collision frequency, giving half-lives of order $10^{38}\,\text{s}$ for this rough model — far too long. Real alpha emitters have much lower effective barriers (the Coulomb potential decreases as $1/r$, not as a rectangle), and the WKB integral gives the right answer. The key insight: tiny changes in $\kappa d$ produce enormous changes in $T$ — the Geiger–Nuttall law's dramatic range of half-lives (from microseconds to billions of years) follows directly.

Example 4 — Group velocity and uncertainty principle for a Gaussian packet

A Gaussian wave packet has $\phi(k) \propto e^{-(k-k_0)^2/(4\Delta k^2)}$, centred at $k_0$ with spread $\Delta k$.

(a) Show the position uncertainty is $\Delta x = 1/(2\Delta k)$ at $t=0$.

The Fourier transform of $e^{-(k-k_0)^2/(4\Delta k^2)}$ is $\propto e^{-(\Delta k)^2 x^2}$, so $|\Psi|^2 \propto e^{-2(\Delta k)^2 x^2}$. The standard deviation (half-width at $e^{-1/2}$) is $\Delta x = 1/(2\Delta k)$, giving $\Delta x\,\Delta k = 1/2$, i.e. $\Delta x\,\Delta p = \hbar/2$. The Gaussian saturates the uncertainty bound.

(b) Show the packet spreads and find $\Delta x(t)$.

Each $k$-component picks up phase $e^{-i\omega(k)t} = e^{-i\hbar k^2 t/(2m)}$. Completing the square in the exponent of the Fourier integral:

$$\phi(k)e^{-i\hbar k^2 t/(2m)} \propto e^{-(k-k_0)^2/(4\Delta k^2) - i\hbar k^2 t/(2m)}$$

This is still Gaussian in $k$ but now with a complex variance. The Fourier transform back gives a Gaussian in $x$ with complex width — the magnitude gives:

$$\Delta x(t) = \frac{1}{2\Delta k}\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{2\hbar(\Delta k)^2 t}{m}\right)^2}$$

At late times, $\Delta x(t) \approx \hbar\,\Delta k\,t/m = (\Delta p/m)t$: the spread grows at rate $\Delta v = \Delta p/m$, exactly as expected — the range of velocities in the packet pulls it apart at rate $\Delta v$.

Food for Thought
  • A free particle can't be in a stationary state — so what is it actually in? If every physical state is a superposition of plane waves (energy eigenstates), doesn't that mean energy is uncertain? How does that square with energy conservation?
    Yes — a physical free particle always has an uncertain energy. But energy is still conserved in the sense that the energy expectation value $\langle E\rangle = \int|\phi(k)|^2\hbar\omega(k)\,dk/2\pi$ is time-independent (no external forces, no potential). The energy isn't "changing" — it was always uncertain. Measuring the energy would collapse the state to a plane wave, giving a definite result, but that result is distributed according to $|\phi(k)|^2$. Conservation means the distribution doesn't change; it doesn't require the distribution to be sharp.
  • Tunneling probability falls exponentially with barrier width. Does this mean that if you made a barrier infinitely wide, the tunneling probability would be exactly zero — meaning the particle is perfectly reflected? Or is there always a finite chance of tunneling no matter how wide the barrier?
    For a finite barrier, $T \propto e^{-2\kappa d} \to 0$ as $d \to \infty$ but is never exactly zero for any finite $d$. In the strict mathematical limit $d = \infty$ (semi-infinite barrier), $T$ is exactly zero — the exponentially decaying solution must vanish to keep normalisability, and the amplitude at $x \to \infty$ is zero. So there's a discontinuity: for any finite thickness, some tunneling occurs; for a truly infinite barrier, none does. In practice, barriers of 10–20 nm already reduce tunneling to completely negligible levels for electrons in normal conditions.
  • The transmission coefficient formula has $T = 1$ at the Ramsauer–Townsend resonances for a well. But for a barrier (flipped sign), there are no such resonances below the barrier. Why? What's special about the attractive case?
    Inside an attractive well, the kinetic energy is enhanced (KE = E + V₀ > 0), so the wavefunction oscillates with a shorter wavelength than outside. At resonance, exactly half an integer number of wavelengths fit inside — the reflected waves from the two walls are in perfect destructive interference and cancel, leaving 100% transmission. Inside a repulsive barrier where E < V₀, there's no oscillation — the solutions are real exponentials that can't produce the same standing-wave resonance. Above the barrier (E > V₀), the same physics applies and you do get resonances there too.
Common Pitfalls
  • Plane waves are not physical states. $\Psi = Ae^{i(kx-\omega t)}$ cannot be normalised — it represents equal probability density everywhere. Using it as though it were a real particle state leads to nonsense. Real particles are always wave packets.
  • The group velocity, not the phase velocity, is the particle velocity. For a quantum particle, $v_\text{group} = \hbar k/m = p/m$ matches the classical result. The phase velocity $\hbar k/2m$ is half that — it's the speed of individual wave crests, which have no direct physical meaning for probability density.
  • Tunneling doesn't mean the particle travels through the barrier. There is no trajectory inside the barrier — asking "how long does it spend inside?" or "what is its kinetic energy inside?" has no well-defined answer. The particle doesn't lose energy crossing the barrier; it arrives on the other side with exactly the same kinetic energy it had approaching.
  • The wavefunction inside a classically forbidden region is real, not imaginary. In the region $E < V(x)$, the TISE gives $\psi'' = \kappa^2\psi$ with real, positive $\kappa^2$. The solutions are real exponentials $e^{\pm\kappa x}$, not complex oscillations. The wavefunction is real there, it just decays rather than oscillates.
  • $R + T = 1$ always — even for attractive potentials. It's tempting to think an attractive potential can "create" particles (T > 1). It can't — the particle is never absorbed or emitted in elastic scattering. The reflection from an attractive well just comes as a surprise because classically there would be no reflection at all.
