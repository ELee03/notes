2 · Multi-Armed Bandits
Exploration · Exploitation · Action Values · ε-greedy · UCB · Gradient Bandit
Notation used in this lesson
$q_*(a)$ True (unknown) expected reward of arm $a$
$Q_t(a)$ Agent's estimate of $q_*(a)$ after $t$ steps
$N_t(a)$ Number of times arm $a$ has been pulled before step $t$
$\mathbb{E}[\cdot]$ Expected value — the long-run average of a random quantity
$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ Expected value of $X$ given that $Y$ has occurred (conditional expectation)
$\pi_t(a)$ Probability of selecting arm $a$ at step $t$ (in gradient bandit)
$\bar{R}_t$ Running average of all rewards received up to step $t$ (baseline)
Before tackling RL with state transitions and long-horizon credit assignment, it pays to study a simpler problem that isolates the most fundamental challenge: the exploration-exploitation dilemma. The multi-armed bandit is that problem. It has no state, no delayed consequences, no sequences of decisions — just the pure question of how to act when you are learning from your own actions.
The k-armed bandit problem
Imagine $k$ slot machines (the "arms"). Each arm $a \in \{1, \ldots, k\}$ has an unknown probability distribution over rewards. When you pull arm $a$, you receive a reward sampled from that distribution. You have a fixed budget of $T$ pulls. How do you allocate them to maximise total reward?
The difficulty is that you do not know the reward distributions in advance. You can only learn them by pulling arms and observing what comes back. But every pull spent learning (exploring) is a pull not spent on the best arm you currently know about (exploiting). This is the dilemma in its purest form.
The agent's estimates $Q_t(a)$ can diverge from the true values $q_*(a)$. A greedy agent picks arm 5 (highest estimate), but arm 3 is actually optimal. This is why exploration is necessary.
Action values
The true value of an action is the expected reward when that action is taken:
$$q_*(a) = \mathbb{E}[R_t \mid A_t = a]$$
If we knew $q_*(a)$ for all arms, the problem would be trivial — always pull the arm with the highest value. We do not know these values. We estimate them. The simplest estimate is the sample average: the mean of all rewards received from arm $a$ so far.
$$Q_t(a) = \frac{\text{sum of rewards when } a \text{ was taken}}{N_t(a)}$$
where $N_t(a)$ is the number of times arm $a$ has been pulled before time $t$. By the law of large numbers, $Q_t(a) \to q_*(a)$ as $N_t(a) \to \infty$. The problem is getting enough pulls of each arm to make the estimates reliable — which conflicts with exploiting the best arm you currently know about.
Incremental estimation and the update rule
Computing a running average by summing all past rewards and dividing is wasteful — it requires storing all past rewards. Instead, use the incremental update rule. After receiving the $n$-th reward $R_n$ from arm $a$:
$$Q_{n+1} = Q_n + \frac{1}{n}\bigl[R_n - Q_n\bigr]$$
This form deserves attention. The term $R_n - Q_n$ is the error: the difference between what we just observed and what we expected. We move our estimate toward the observation by a fraction $\frac{1}{n}$ of that error. The same structure — estimate += step_size × error — appears in nearly every RL update rule in this cluster.
$$\text{NewEstimate} \leftarrow \text{OldEstimate} + \alpha \bigl[\underbrace{R_n}_{\text{target}} - \underbrace{Q_n}_{\text{estimate}}\bigr]$$
This update rule is the template for RL. The target is the thing we observed (or a better estimate we will derive later). The estimate is our current belief. The step size $\alpha$ controls how fast we move toward the target. In the bandit case $\alpha = 1/n$; in TD learning (Lesson 7) $\alpha$ becomes a constant, and the target changes to incorporate future value. But the form is always the same.
ε-greedy action selection
The simplest approach to balancing exploration and exploitation is ε-greedy: most of the time, select the action with the highest current estimate (the greedy action); with probability $\varepsilon$, select a random action uniformly from all arms.
$$A_t = \begin{cases} \arg\max_a Q_t(a) & \text{with probability } 1 - \varepsilon \\ \text{random arm} & \text{with probability } \varepsilon \end{cases}$$
Over time, as $N_t(a)$ grows for all arms, the estimates improve, and the greedy choice increasingly aligns with the true optimal. ε-greedy guarantees that every arm is pulled infinitely often, so $Q_t(a) \to q_*(a)$ for all $a$.
The parameter $\varepsilon$ controls the tradeoff. Small $\varepsilon$ (e.g. 0.01) exploits heavily and learns slowly; large $\varepsilon$ (e.g. 0.1) explores more aggressively but wastes pulls on suboptimal arms even after the best has been found. A common refinement is to decrease $\varepsilon$ over time as confidence in the estimates grows.
Optimistic initial values
A simple trick that encourages early exploration: initialise all estimates $Q_1(a)$ to a value higher than any reward you expect to observe. For example, if all rewards lie in $[0, 1]$, initialise $Q_1(a) = 5$.
The effect: every arm looks excellent initially. The agent picks greedily, is disappointed by the actual reward (which is less than 5), and its estimate for that arm falls. It then turns to another arm that still looks good (because it hasn't been tried yet). This forces the agent to try every arm early in the experiment before settling. No $\varepsilon$ parameter needed — the optimism does the work.
The limitation: the initial optimism fades over time. Once all arms have been tried enough times, estimates converge to their true values and the optimism effect disappears. For nonstationary environments where $q_*(a)$ drifts over time, reinitialising periodically is needed — but constant $\varepsilon$ is usually more robust.
Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) action selection
ε-greedy treats all non-greedy arms equally — it explores uniformly at random. But not all uncertainty is equal. An arm that has never been tried is far more uncertain than one pulled 1000 times with a consistent reward. It makes sense to direct exploration toward arms where our uncertainty is high.
The Upper-Confidence-Bound method does exactly this. Select the arm with the highest upper confidence bound: the estimate plus a term that grows with uncertainty.
$$A_t = \arg\max_a \left[ Q_t(a) + c\,\sqrt{\frac{\ln t}{N_t(a)}} \right]$$
The second term is the confidence interval half-width. It is large when $N_t(a)$ is small (the arm has been pulled rarely — high uncertainty) and shrinks as the arm is pulled more. The $\ln t$ in the numerator grows slowly, meaning that even well-tried arms keep getting a small exploration bonus as time goes on — ensuring no arm is ignored forever.
The parameter $c > 0$ controls the width of the confidence interval. This formula comes from concentration inequalities (Hoeffding's inequality) and is mathematically principled — not just a heuristic. UCB algorithms can be shown to achieve near-optimal regret in the bandit setting.
UCB intuition. Imagine bidding on a search engine ad slot where click-through rates are unknown. Arms with few impressions have wide confidence intervals: the true rate could be much higher than our estimate. UCB says: give those arms a chance proportional to how uncertain we are about them — not just because we hope they're good, but because we genuinely don't know.
Gradient bandit algorithms
The methods so far estimate action values and derive a policy from them. A different approach learns a numerical preference $H_t(a)$ directly for each arm and selects actions according to a softmax distribution:
$$\pi_t(a) = \frac{e^{H_t(a)}}{\sum_{b} e^{H_t(b)}}$$
Preferences are updated after each reward using stochastic gradient ascent on expected reward. For the selected arm $A_t$ with reward $R_t$ and baseline $\bar{R}_t$ (the running average of all rewards):
$$H_{t+1}(A_t) = H_t(A_t) + \alpha(R_t - \bar{R}_t)(1 - \pi_t(A_t))$$ $$H_{t+1}(a) = H_t(a) - \alpha(R_t - \bar{R}_t)\pi_t(a) \quad \text{for all } a \neq A_t$$
The baseline $\bar{R}_t$ is critical: it makes the update relative to the agent's recent average performance. If $R_t > \bar{R}_t$ (better than usual), the selected arm's preference increases; if $R_t < \bar{R}_t$, it decreases. This directly anticipates the advantage concept in policy gradient methods (Lesson 9).
Nonstationary problems
All the methods above implicitly assume that $q_*(a)$ is fixed. Many real problems are nonstationary: reward distributions drift over time. The best search ad today may not be the best next month; the best network route changes with load. Sample averages fail here — they give equal weight to a reward from 10,000 steps ago as to one from last step.
The fix is to use a constant step size $\alpha \in (0, 1]$ instead of $1/n$:
$$Q_{n+1} = Q_n + \alpha(R_n - Q_n)$$
Expanding this recursion reveals that it is an exponential recency-weighted average — past rewards are weighted by $(1-\alpha)^k$ where $k$ is their age. Recent rewards dominate; old ones fade exponentially. With $\alpha = 0.1$, a reward from 10 steps ago has weight $(0.9)^{10} \approx 0.35$; from 50 steps ago, $(0.9)^{50} \approx 0.005$. The agent effectively forgets its distant past and stays responsive to recent changes.
Worked example: tracing ε-greedy by hand
Trace the first five steps of ε-greedy ($\varepsilon = 0.1$) on a 3-armed bandit. True values: $q_*(1) = 1.0$, $q_*(2) = 1.5$, $q_*(3) = 0.5$. Start with $Q_1(a) = 0$ for all arms. Rewards are deterministic for simplicity (equal to the true value each pull).

1 All tied at 0 → greedy picks any; say a=1 11.0 Q(1) = 0 + (1/1)[1.0−0] = 1.0 (1.0, 0, 0)
2 Greedy: a=1 (Q=1.0 highest) 11.0 Q(1) = 1.0 + (1/2)[1.0−1.0] = 1.0 (1.0, 0, 0)
3 ε-explore (10% chance) → random → a=2 21.5 Q(2) = 0 + (1/1)[1.5−0] = 1.5 (1.0, 1.5, 0)
4 Greedy: a=2 (Q=1.5 highest) 21.5 Q(2) = 1.5 + (1/2)[1.5−1.5] = 1.5 (1.0, 1.5, 0)
5 Greedy: a=2 (Q=1.5 highest) 21.5 Q(2) = 1.5 + (1/3)[1.5−1.5] = 1.5 (1.0, 1.5, 0)
After step 2 the agent is stuck exploiting arm 1. The single ε-exploration at step 3 accidentally discovers arm 2 (the true best). From step 4 onward it exploits arm 2 correctly. Key observation: arm 3 ($q_*(3)=0.5$, worst arm) was never pulled — ε-greedy provides no guarantee of trying every arm within a fixed horizon. It will get there eventually, but the rate is uniform random, not uncertainty-driven. This is UCB's advantage.

Food for thought
Reflection questions — no answers given
  1. UCB achieves near-optimal regret — the total reward lost by not always pulling the true best arm. If you ran ε-greedy ($\varepsilon = 0.1$) and UCB-1 side by side on the same 5-armed bandit for 1000 steps, which would you expect to accumulate more total reward? Why — and does your answer depend on the spread of the true values?
  2. The gradient bandit increases the selected arm's preference when $R_t > \bar{R}_t$ and decreases it when $R_t < \bar{R}_t$. The update is doing stochastic gradient ascent on expected reward — but the algorithm never explicitly computes a gradient. How is this possible? What is the algorithm actually differentiating with respect to?
  3. Suppose the true best arm has very high variance: rewards range from $-10$ to $+10$ with a mean of 1.5, while all other arms give exactly 1.0. How would high variance affect UCB compared to ε-greedy? Which converges to the correct answer faster, and why?
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Using 1/n step size for nonstationary problems. The sample-average step size $\alpha = 1/n$ converges to zero, which means the agent stops updating once it has seen enough data. For a nonstationary bandit where $q_*(a)$ drifts over time, this is disastrous — use a constant $\alpha$ so the agent stays responsive to recent rewards.
  • Treating $Q_t(a)$ as the true value. $Q_t(a)$ is the agent's estimate of $q_*(a)$, not the truth. The gap between them is exactly what makes exploration necessary. Never act as if your current estimate is guaranteed correct — that is the greedy trap.
  • Thinking small ε means the agent is nearly optimal. Even $\varepsilon = 0.01$ means 1% of pulls are random forever. Over a long horizon, that is thousands of pulls wasted on arms the agent already knows are suboptimal. For finite-horizon problems, the exploration cost accumulates and ε-greedy has no mechanism to reduce it over time (unless you anneal ε explicitly).
  • Removing the baseline from gradient bandits. The update rule still converges in expectation without the baseline, but with much higher variance. The baseline $\bar{R}_t$ acts as a control variate: by making the update relative to recent performance, it cancels out noise that isn't informative about which arms are genuinely better. Removing it is like turning off variance reduction in a Monte Carlo estimator.
Connects to
  • Probability Theory — the convergence $Q_t(a) \to q_*(a)$ as $N_t(a) \to \infty$ follows from the law of large numbers; the UCB confidence bound is derived from Hoeffding's inequality.
  • Optimization · Stochastic Gradient Ascent — the gradient bandit update is stochastic gradient ascent on expected reward; this is the direct precursor to policy gradient methods (Lesson 9), which extend the same idea to full MDPs.
  • RL · Policy Gradient Methods (Lesson 9) — the baseline subtraction in gradient bandits is the bandit analog of advantage functions in actor-critic algorithms; the softmax policy is the bandit analog of a stochastic policy over states.
