9 · Policy Gradient Methods
Policy Parameterization · Policy Gradient Theorem · REINFORCE · Baseline Subtraction · Actor-Critic · Advantage Functions
Every method in Lessons 4–8 learned a value function and derived a policy from it — either by looking up the best action, or by acting greedily w.r.t. $Q(s,a)$. Policy gradient methods take a different route: skip the value function and directly optimize a parameterized policy $\pi(a \mid s, \boldsymbol{\theta})$. This turns RL into a gradient ascent problem on a scalar objective — the expected return. The result is a family of algorithms that work naturally with continuous action spaces, can learn stochastic policies, and form the foundation of nearly every modern deep RL system.
Notation used in this lesson
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ Policy parameter vector; $\pi(a|s,\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the parameterized policy
$J(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ Performance objective: expected return under $\pi_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$
$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln \pi(a|s,\boldsymbol{\theta})$ Score function or log-gradient: direction in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$-space that increases the probability of action $a$ in state $s$
$b(s)$ Baseline function — subtracted from the return to reduce variance without biasing the gradient estimate
$A_t$ Advantage estimate at time $t$: how much better action $A_t$ was than average from state $S_t$
$h(s,a,\boldsymbol{\theta})$ Action "preference" (unnormalized logit) used to define a softmax policy
Why policy gradients?
Value-based methods work by finding the optimal $Q^*(s,a)$ and then acting greedily: $\pi(s) = \arg\max_a Q(s,a)$. This is effective for many problems, but fails in several important cases:
  • Continuous action spaces. If actions are real-valued (robot joint torques, steering angles), $\arg\max_a Q(s,a)$ requires solving an optimization problem at every decision — expensive or intractable for high-dimensional actions. Policy gradient methods parameterize the action distribution directly (e.g. as a Gaussian), and sample from it.
  • Stochastic optimal policies. In partially observable environments or adversarial games, the optimal policy is stochastic. Q-learning always produces a deterministic policy. Policy gradients can represent and learn genuinely stochastic policies.
  • Policy may be simpler than the value function. For some problems, a good policy has a simple structure (e.g. "rotate left when the obstacle is close") even if the value function is complex. Parameterizing the policy directly exploits this.
  • Convergence. Policy gradient methods have smoother optimization landscapes in some settings, and their convergence proofs (for appropriate step sizes) are more straightforward than those of Q-learning with function approximation.
Policy parameterization
A parameterized policy $\pi(a \mid s, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ maps states to distributions over actions, indexed by a parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Any differentiable function family can work; two common choices:
Softmax for discrete actions: Define an action preference $h(s, a, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ — a scalar score for each $(s,a)$ pair (could be a linear combination of features or a neural network output). The policy is: $$\pi(a \mid s, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{\exp(h(s,a,\boldsymbol{\theta}))}{\sum_{a'} \exp(h(s,a',\boldsymbol{\theta}))}$$ Actions with higher preference get higher probability. The policy is always valid (probabilities sum to 1, all positive). As $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ changes, probability mass shifts smoothly between actions — unlike the argmax policy which can flip discontinuously.
Gaussian for continuous actions: Parameterize the mean and (optionally) variance of a Gaussian: $$\pi(a \mid s, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(s),\, \sigma^2_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(s))$$ Action $a$ is sampled from this distribution at each step. The mean $\mu$ is what the policy "wants" to do; $\sigma$ controls exploration — larger $\sigma$ means more varied behavior. Both can be outputs of a neural network.
Softmax policies never go fully greedy. No matter how extreme the preferences $h(s,a,\boldsymbol{\theta})$ become, every action retains some nonzero probability (unlike ε-greedy, where with $\varepsilon \to 0$ the non-greedy actions can be completely ignored). This means the policy always explores slightly — a built-in exploration mechanism that often works better than separate ε schedules.
The objective $J(\boldsymbol{\theta})$
We want to find $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ that maximizes the expected return under $\pi_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$. For episodic tasks, the natural objective is: $$J(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = v_{\pi_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}}(s_0) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}}\left[G_0\right]$$ This is the expected discounted return starting from some fixed starting state $s_0$. We want to maximize $J(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ via gradient ascent: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t + \alpha\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} J(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$ The challenge: how to compute $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} J(\boldsymbol{\theta})$? Changing $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ changes the policy, which changes the distribution over trajectories — including which states are visited and how frequently. It's not obvious how to differentiate through a distribution over trajectories.
The policy gradient theorem
The policy gradient theorem gives a tractable expression for $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} J$:
$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} J(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \propto \sum_s d_{\pi}(s) \sum_a q_\pi(s,a)\, \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \pi(a \mid s, \boldsymbol{\theta})$$
where $d_\pi(s)$ is the on-policy state distribution under $\pi_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$. Crucially, this expression does not require differentiating through $d_\pi(s)$ — the derivative of the state distribution with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ does not appear. This is what makes the theorem powerful: we only need to differentiate the policy $\pi(a|s,\boldsymbol{\theta})$ itself.
Derivation — Policy Gradient Theorem

We derive the episodic case (starting-state objective $J(\theta) = v_\pi(s_0)$). By the Bellman equation for $v_\pi$: $$\nabla_\theta v_\pi(s) = \nabla_\theta \sum_a \pi(a|s,\theta)\,q_\pi(s,a)$$ Applying the product rule (note $q_\pi$ depends on $\theta$ through $\pi$ at future states): $$= \sum_a \bigl[\nabla_\theta \pi(a|s,\theta)\,q_\pi(s,a) + \pi(a|s,\theta)\,\nabla_\theta q_\pi(s,a)\bigr]$$

Expand $\nabla_\theta q_\pi(s,a) = \nabla_\theta \sum_{s',r} p(s'|s,a)[r + \gamma v_\pi(s')] = \gamma\sum_{s'} p(s'|s,a)\,\nabla_\theta v_\pi(s')$ (the reward is independent of $\theta$). Substituting: $$\nabla_\theta v_\pi(s) = \sum_a \nabla_\theta\pi(a|s,\theta)\,q_\pi(s,a) + \gamma\sum_a\pi(a|s,\theta)\sum_{s'} p(s'|s,a)\,\nabla_\theta v_\pi(s')$$

This is a recursion. Unrolling it (applying the same expansion to $\nabla_\theta v_\pi(s')$, etc.) gives a sum over all reachable future states: $$\nabla_\theta v_\pi(s) = \sum_{s'} \sum_{k=0}^\infty \Pr(s \to s', k \text{ steps}, \pi)\sum_a \nabla_\theta\pi(a|s',\theta)\,q_\pi(s',a)$$ where $\Pr(s\to s', k, \pi)$ is the probability of reaching $s'$ from $s$ in exactly $k$ steps under $\pi$.

Define the on-policy state distribution $d_\pi(s') = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k \Pr(s_0 \to s', k, \pi)$ (discounted visitation). Then: $$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \nabla_\theta v_\pi(s_0) = \sum_s d_\pi(s) \sum_a \nabla_\theta\pi(a|s,\theta)\,q_\pi(s,a)$$ Applying the log-derivative trick $\nabla_\theta\pi = \pi\cdot\nabla_\theta\ln\pi$: $$= \sum_s d_\pi(s) \sum_a \pi(a|s,\theta)\,\nabla_\theta\ln\pi(a|s,\theta)\,q_\pi(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\bigl[\nabla_\theta\ln\pi(A_t|S_t,\theta)\,q_\pi(S_t,A_t)\bigr] \quad \blacksquare$$

The key insight: $d_\pi(s)$ appears in the derivation (it comes from unrolling the recursion), but it cancels out of the gradient of $J$ — we never need to differentiate through how $\theta$ changes the state distribution.

Using the log-derivative trick ($\nabla \pi = \pi \cdot \nabla \ln \pi$): $$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} J(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \propto \mathbb{E}_\pi\bigl[q_\pi(S_t, A_t)\, \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln \pi(A_t \mid S_t, \boldsymbol{\theta})\bigr]$$ This is an expectation we can estimate by sampling: run episodes under $\pi$, collect $(S_t, A_t)$ pairs, compute $q_\pi(S_t, A_t) \approx G_t$, and use $G_t \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln \pi(A_t|S_t,\boldsymbol{\theta})$ as an unbiased estimate of the gradient.
⟶ Worth understanding: the log-derivative trick. The identity $\nabla_\theta \pi(a|s,\theta) = \pi(a|s,\theta) \cdot \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a|s,\theta)$ converts the gradient of a probability into an expectation. Write it as: $$\sum_a \nabla\pi(a|s,\theta) q(s,a) = \sum_a \pi(a|s,\theta) \nabla\ln\pi(a|s,\theta) \cdot q(s,a)$$ The right side is $\mathbb{E}_{A \sim \pi}[\nabla\ln\pi(A|s,\theta) \cdot q(s,A)]$. This is now an expectation under $\pi$ — something we can estimate by sampling. Work through the one-line derivation: it is the core algebraic step of all policy gradient methods.
REINFORCE
REINFORCE is the Monte Carlo policy gradient algorithm. It substitutes the return $G_t$ for the unknown $q_\pi(S_t, A_t)$:
$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t + \alpha G_t\, \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln \pi(A_t \mid S_t, \boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$
The algorithm:
  1. Initialize $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ arbitrarily.
  2. Generate an episode: $S_0, A_0, R_1, S_1, A_1, R_2, \ldots, S_{T-1}, A_{T-1}, R_T$.
  3. For each step $t = 0, 1, \ldots, T-1$: compute $G_t = \sum_{k=t}^{T-1}\gamma^{k-t}R_{k+1}$.
  4. Update: $\boldsymbol{\theta} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta} + \alpha G_t \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln \pi(A_t|S_t,\boldsymbol{\theta})$.
  5. Repeat.
The update has a beautiful intuition. $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln \pi(A_t|S_t,\boldsymbol{\theta})$ points in the direction that increases the probability of action $A_t$ in state $S_t$. Multiplying by $G_t$:
  • If $G_t > 0$ (the episode went well from here): move $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ to make actions like $A_t$ more likely in states like $S_t$.
  • If $G_t < 0$ (the episode went badly): move $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ to make those actions less likely.
This is "reinforce what works, suppress what doesn't" — exactly the intuition behind reinforcement learning. REINFORCE is unbiased (it uses true Monte Carlo returns) but has high variance. The variance comes from the stochasticity of $G_t$: the same action from the same state can have very different returns depending on what happens later in the episode.
Baseline subtraction
The variance of the REINFORCE estimate can be dramatically reduced by subtracting a baseline $b(s)$ from the return: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t + \alpha (G_t - b(S_t))\, \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln \pi(A_t \mid S_t, \boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$
As long as $b(s)$ does not depend on the action $A_t$, subtracting it does not change the expected value of the gradient estimate — it is unbiased. To see why: $$\mathbb{E}\bigl[b(S_t)\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln\pi(A_t|S_t)\bigr] = \sum_a \pi(a|s)\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln\pi(a|s) \cdot b(s) = b(s)\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\underbrace{\sum_a \pi(a|s)}_{=1} = 0$$ The expected contribution of the baseline to the gradient is zero — it cancels out. But it reduces variance by making the "how good was this?" signal relative rather than absolute.
The most common baseline is the current value estimate $b(S_t) = \hat{v}(S_t, \mathbf{w})$. The difference $G_t - \hat{v}(S_t, \mathbf{w})$ is an estimate of the advantage: how much better was this particular trajectory than the expected value from $S_t$?
With a baseline, the REINFORCE algorithm requires a separate value function $\hat{v}(s,\mathbf{w})$ that is trained alongside the policy. The value function parameters $\mathbf{w}$ are updated by minimizing $(G_t - \hat{v}(S_t, \mathbf{w}))^2$ — this is standard supervised regression on MC returns.
Actor-critic methods
REINFORCE with baseline is still a Monte Carlo method — it waits for the full episode before updating. Actor-critic methods apply the same idea but use a TD estimate instead of $G_t$, enabling online (per-step) updates.
The architecture has two components running in parallel:
  • Actor ($\boldsymbol{\theta}$): the policy $\pi(a|s,\boldsymbol{\theta})$. Takes actions in the environment. Updated to increase the probability of actions that lead to good outcomes.
  • Critic ($\mathbf{w}$): the value function $\hat{v}(s, \mathbf{w})$. Evaluates how good the current state is. Updated via semi-gradient TD to improve its accuracy as a state-value estimator.
The TD error from the critic, $\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma\hat{v}(S_{t+1},\mathbf{w}) - \hat{v}(S_t,\mathbf{w})$, serves as the advantage estimate for the actor update:
$$\boldsymbol{\theta} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta} + \alpha_\theta\, \delta_t\, \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln\pi(A_t \mid S_t, \boldsymbol{\theta})$$ $$\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} + \alpha_w\, \delta_t\, \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \hat{v}(S_t, \mathbf{w})$$
Both updates happen at every time step (no need to wait for episode completion). The TD error $\delta_t$ simultaneously: (a) tells the actor whether this action was better or worse than the critic's prediction, and (b) is the error signal used to improve the critic. This feedback loop is the core of actor-critic algorithms.
Advantage functions
The advantage function is defined as: $$A_\pi(s, a) = q_\pi(s, a) - v_\pi(s)$$ It measures how much better (or worse) taking action $a$ in state $s$ is compared to the average action under $\pi$. If $A_\pi(s,a) > 0$, action $a$ is above average from $s$; if $A_\pi(s,a) < 0$, it is below average.
Using the advantage as the weight in the policy gradient update gives an estimator with zero expected return for any constant baseline, and with the minimum possible variance for the state-dependent baseline $b(s) = v_\pi(s)$: $$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} J(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\bigl[A_\pi(S_t, A_t)\, \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln\pi(A_t|S_t,\boldsymbol{\theta})\bigr]$$ The advantage tells the actor exactly the right signal: increase the probability of actions that beat the expected value, decrease those that fall below it.
In actor-critic, the TD error $\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma V(S_{t+1}) - V(S_t)$ is a one-step estimate of the advantage. If the critic is accurate, $\delta_t \approx A_\pi(S_t, A_t)$. More accurate advantage estimates can be computed using $n$-step returns or Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE), which averages over all $n$-step TD errors.
Modern extensions
The algorithms described above are the foundation. Modern deep RL builds on them with practical improvements:
A2C / A3C (Advantage Actor-Critic). Runs multiple actor threads in parallel, each collecting experience and computing gradients. A central learner aggregates the gradients. This reduces correlation between updates and improves sample efficiency. A2C is the synchronous version (wait for all workers); A3C is asynchronous (workers update as soon as ready). Both use the advantage (TD error with $n$-step returns) as the policy gradient signal.
PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization). A common practical algorithm that constrains each gradient update to not change the policy too much. Without this constraint, a single large gradient step can move the policy to a bad region of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$-space, after which recovery is slow. PPO clips the gradient objective so that the policy ratio $\pi_{\text{new}}(a|s) / \pi_{\text{old}}(a|s)$ stays within $[1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon]$. This is simple to implement and works reliably across a wide range of tasks — it is arguably the most widely used RL algorithm today.
SAC (Soft Actor-Critic). For continuous control tasks (robotics), SAC adds an entropy bonus to the reward, encouraging the policy to remain as random as possible while still performing well. This maximum-entropy objective improves exploration and robustness. SAC is currently state-of-the-art on many continuous control benchmarks.
Worked examples
Example 1 — REINFORCE update on a softmax policy
Two-action problem at state $M$. Softmax preferences: $\theta_1 = h(M,\text{right}) = 0.5$, $\theta_2 = h(M,\text{left}) = 0.0$. An episode starts at $M$, takes action right, return $G_0 = +2$. Use $\alpha = 0.1$. Compute the update to $\theta_1$ and the new $\pi(\text{right}|M)$.
Current policy: $\pi(\text{right}|M) = e^{0.5}/(e^{0.5}+e^0) = 1.649/2.649 \approx 0.622$.
Log-gradient: $\nabla_{\theta_1}\ln\pi(\text{right}|M) = 1 - \pi(\text{right}|M) = 0.378$. (Softmax log-gradient w.r.t. the logit of the chosen action equals $1 - \pi(a|s)$.)
Update: $\theta_1 \leftarrow 0.5 + 0.1 \times 2 \times 0.378 = 0.5 + 0.076 = \mathbf{0.576}$.
New policy: $\pi(\text{right}|M) = e^{0.576}/(e^{0.576}+e^0) \approx \mathbf{0.640}$. The probability of "right" increased by ~2% — the policy was nudged toward the action that produced a positive return.
Example 2 — Actor-critic update
$\hat{v}(S_t=3)=2.0$, $\hat{v}(S_{t+1}=7)=3.5$. Observed $R_{t+1}=0$, $\gamma=0.9$, $\alpha_\theta=0.05$, $\alpha_w=0.1$. Compute the TD error and write out the critic and actor update directions.
TD error: $\delta_t = 0 + 0.9 \times 3.5 - 2.0 = 3.15 - 2.0 = \mathbf{1.15}$
Critic update: $\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} + 0.1 \times 1.15 \times \nabla_\mathbf{w}\hat{v}(3,\mathbf{w})$. For linear FA, $\nabla\hat{v}(3,\mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{x}(3)$ (feature vector of state 3).
Actor update: $\boldsymbol{\theta} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta} + 0.05 \times 1.15 \times \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\ln\pi(A_t|3,\boldsymbol{\theta})$. The positive TD error ($\delta_t = 1.15 > 0$) means the next state was better than the critic predicted — the actor is nudged toward action $A_t$ because it led to a pleasant surprise.
Example 3 — Why the baseline doesn't introduce bias
Show that $\mathbb{E}_{A\sim\pi}[b(s)\,\nabla_\theta\ln\pi(A|s)] = 0$ for any $b(s)$ that does not depend on $A$. This is the key identity that makes baselines bias-free.
$$\sum_a \pi(a|s)\,b(s)\,\nabla_\theta\ln\pi(a|s) = b(s)\sum_a \pi(a|s)\frac{\nabla_\theta\pi(a|s)}{\pi(a|s)} = b(s)\sum_a\nabla_\theta\pi(a|s) = b(s)\,\nabla_\theta\underbrace{\sum_a\pi(a|s)}_{=1} = b(s)\,\nabla_\theta(1) = 0$$
The probabilities always sum to 1, so their gradient (w.r.t. $\theta$) is zero — the baseline contributes nothing to the expected gradient. It affects only the variance of the estimate, which is why the optimal baseline is the one that minimises that variance.

Food for thought
Reflection questions
  1. The policy gradient theorem says we can estimate $\nabla J(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ without differentiating through the state distribution $d_\pi(s)$. But changing $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ clearly changes which states are visited. Intuitively, why doesn't this matter? And in what sense does $d_\pi(s)$ "show up" anyway — look at how we sample $S_t \sim d_\pi$.
    The policy gradient theorem proves mathematically that the gradient of $J$ can be written as $\mathbb{E}_{S\sim d_\pi, A\sim\pi}[G_t\,\nabla_\theta\ln\pi(A|S)]$ — a term involving $d_\pi$ appears in the derivation but then cancels with its own reciprocal, leaving only the derivative of $\ln\pi$. Intuitively: the fact that $d_\pi$ changes with $\theta$ produces a second-order effect; the dominant (first-order) effect comes from changing how likely each action is, not from changing which states are visited.

    $d_\pi$ "shows up" as the sampling distribution: when we run the agent and collect trajectories, the states we visit are automatically distributed according to $d_\pi$. So we're estimating the expectation over $d_\pi$ by Monte Carlo, just not differentiating through it.
  2. PPO clips the policy ratio $\pi_\text{new}/\pi_\text{old}$ to $[1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon]$. Suppose a particular transition had a very large estimated advantage $A_t = 10$. Without clipping, the update would be large and might overshoot. With clipping ($\varepsilon = 0.2$), the ratio is capped at 1.2. Trace through what this means for the magnitude of the gradient step — and why repeated smaller steps can still get to the same place without the instability of a single large step.
    Without clipping: objective contribution from this transition is $r_t(\theta) A_t$. Gradient w.r.t. $\theta$ is large — potentially catastrophically so when $A_t = 10$.

    With clipping: when $r_t > 1.2$ (policy already moved in the right direction), the gradient of the clipped objective is zero — further increasing the probability of $A_t$ brings no additional gradient signal. This prevents the single step from overshooting to near-deterministic policies.

    Repeated steps: PPO runs multiple gradient steps on the same batch of data. Each step is limited to a ratio in $[0.8, 1.2]$. After a step, the ratio is recalculated from the new $\theta$ — so the constraint applies fresh each time. Accumulating multiple small moves can achieve the same total policy change as one large step, but each individual step avoids the instability of a large gradient magnitude.
  3. Actor-critic uses the TD error $\delta_t$ as the advantage estimate. But the TD error is an estimate of the advantage only when the critic is accurate. Early in training, the critic is poor, so $\delta_t$ is a noisy and biased signal for the actor. How might you handle this? What would happen if you trained the critic for several epochs before each actor update?
    Training the critic for multiple epochs before each actor update sounds appealing but has a catch: the critic is fitting Bellman targets that depend on the current policy. As long as the actor doesn't move, those targets are stationary and more epochs improve the critic. But if the actor then makes a large update, the critic's learned values are immediately stale — no longer accurate for the new policy.

    PPO's approach: collect data, run multiple (3–10) gradient steps on both critic and actor from the same batch, but constrain the actor to stay close to the data collection policy (via clipping). The constraint prevents the "stale critic" problem by limiting how far the actor moves per batch.

    More generally: the critic bootstrapping bias problem is fundamental to AC methods. One practical mitigation is to use a larger $n$-step return for the advantage estimate — more observed reward steps reduce the reliance on the (inaccurate) critic's value estimate.
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Using reward directly instead of return. The REINFORCE update uses $G_t$ (the discounted return from $t$ onward), not just $R_{t+1}$. Using only the immediate reward as the gradient weight produces a biased estimate — it ignores the long-term consequences of the action at time $t$. Compute returns backward from the end of the episode.
  • Normalizing returns incorrectly. A common trick is to normalize returns within a batch: subtract the mean and divide by the standard deviation. This is a form of adaptive baseline and helps with training stability. However, normalizing across different episodes (rather than within a batch) can introduce bias — be careful about exactly what is being normalized.
  • Expecting policy gradients to work without a baseline in deep RL. The return $G_t$ for a neural network policy on a real task might be in the range $[0, 1000]$, all positive. Every gradient step would increase the probability of whatever actions happened to be taken, regardless of whether they were above or below average. This leads to slow learning and instability. A baseline (or normalized returns) is essentially mandatory for practical policy gradient training.
  • Confusing on-policy and off-policy in policy gradient context. REINFORCE and actor-critic (in their basic forms) are on-policy: data must be collected under the current policy $\pi_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$. Old data cannot be reused without importance sampling corrections. Experience replay (as used in DQN) does not straightforwardly apply to policy gradient methods — this is why REINFORCE can be much less sample-efficient than Q-learning. PPO and off-policy actor-critic variants address this limitation.
Connects to
  • Probability Theory · Expectation & log-derivative — The core algebraic trick in the policy gradient theorem — the identity $\nabla\pi = \pi\cdot\nabla\ln\pi$ — is an instance of the REINFORCE/score function estimator from statistics. It appears in variational inference, likelihood-ratio methods, and the theory of stochastic gradient estimators. Understanding it from the probability side gives a unified view of why this trick works so broadly.
  • Optimization · First-order methods — Policy gradient methods are gradient ascent in a high-dimensional, non-convex landscape. PPO's clipping is related to trust-region methods (TRPO); natural policy gradients use the Fisher information matrix as a preconditioner, turning gradient ascent into natural gradient ascent on the policy manifold. These connections make RL optimization a rich specialization of general optimization theory.
  • RL · Bandits (Lesson 2) — The gradient bandit algorithm covered in Lesson 2 is exactly REINFORCE applied to the single-state case. Every concept here — softmax policy, preference update, baseline subtraction — was already present in miniature in that lesson. Coming back to bandits after this lesson reveals the connection in full generality.
