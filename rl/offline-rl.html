11 · Offline and Batch Reinforcement Learning
Fixed Datasets · Distributional Shift · Conservative Q-Learning · Importance Sampling
Every RL algorithm so far has assumed the agent can interact with the environment during training — collecting new data, exploring, and refining its policy in real time. This assumption fails in many high-stakes real-world settings. A medical treatment policy cannot be improved by randomizing patient care. An autonomous vehicle policy cannot be debugged by crashing real cars. A recommendation system cannot be tuned by deliberately showing users bad content. Offline RL (also called batch RL) breaks the interaction assumption entirely: the agent must learn from a fixed dataset of historical transitions collected by some other policy — and then produce a policy that can be deployed in the real world.
Notation recap
$\mathcal{D}$Offline dataset: $\{(s_i, a_i, r_i, s'_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, fixed at training time
$\mu(a|s)$Behavior policy — the policy that collected $\mathcal{D}$
$\pi(a|s)$Learned policy — what we want to deploy
$\rho_\pi(s,a)$State-action distribution under $\pi$
$w(s,a)$Importance weight $= \pi(a|s)/\mu(a|s)$
The offline RL problem
Formally, offline RL receives a dataset $\mathcal{D}$ and must return a policy $\pi$ maximising the expected return $J(\pi)$ — without any further environment interaction. The dataset $\mathcal{D}$ was generated by a behavior policy $\mu$ (which might be a human expert, a heuristic, a mixture of historical controllers, or even a random policy).
The challenge is that $\mathcal{D}$ only covers the state-action pairs that $\mu$ visited. Any $(s, a)$ that $\mu$ never tried is absent from $\mathcal{D}$. Yet the learned policy $\pi$ might want to take those actions — and there is no way to find out what happens if it does. This is the distributional shift problem: $\pi$ might generalize beyond $\mathcal{D}$, visiting states and taking actions for which the Q-function estimate is unreliable.
Why standard off-policy RL fails offline. Q-learning is off-policy — it can learn from data collected by any behavior policy. So naively, just run Q-learning on $\mathcal{D}$. The problem: Q-learning uses a greedy target $\max_{a'}Q(s',a')$. If $Q$ overestimates the value of some out-of-distribution action $a'$ (one that $\mu$ never took, so $\mathcal{D}$ has no evidence about it), the greedy policy will select that action. Then Bellman backups propagate this overestimation to neighboring states, creating a positive feedback loop of compounding errors — Q-value overestimation explosion. The policy eventually collapses to actions that look good in the Q-function but are catastrophic in reality.
Distributional shift in detail
The root cause of offline RL's difficulty is that the state-action distribution under the learned policy $\rho_\pi(s,a)$ differs from the distribution in the dataset $\rho_\mu(s,a)$. The Q-function is trained on $\rho_\mu$ but evaluated by a policy that acts according to $\rho_\pi$. Wherever $\rho_\pi(s,a) > 0$ but $\rho_\mu(s,a) \approx 0$ — i.e., wherever $\pi$ wants to visit but $\mu$ never did — the Q-function is extrapolating beyond its training distribution.
This is an instance of a general problem in supervised learning called covariate shift: the test distribution differs from the training distribution. In standard supervised learning, covariate shift degrades performance but doesn't usually cause catastrophic feedback loops. In RL it does, because Q-values are targets for themselves — overestimates in one region propagate to adjacent regions through Bellman backups.
The severity depends on the coverage of $\mathcal{D}$:
  • Full coverage — $\mu$ is a random or highly exploratory policy that visits all $(s,a)$. Offline RL works fine; Q-learning on $\mathcal{D}$ converges correctly.
  • Partial coverage — $\mu$ is an expert policy that only visits the narrow corridor of states near the optimal trajectory. The dataset is high quality but low coverage. The learned policy can exploit the well-covered states well but has no information about alternatives.
  • Mismatched coverage — $\mathcal{D}$ contains data from multiple policies at different quality levels (e.g. a mix of human demonstrations and random exploration). This is the most realistic but hardest setting.
Conservative Q-Learning (CQL)
The most widely-used offline RL algorithm is Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) (Kumar et al. 2020). The core idea: add a regularization term to the Q-learning loss that explicitly penalizes high Q-values for out-of-distribution actions, while rewarding high Q-values for actions in the dataset.
The CQL objective augments the standard Bellman loss with: $$\mathcal{L}_\text{CQL}(Q) = \mathcal{L}_\text{Bellman}(Q) + \alpha \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{s\sim\mathcal{D}}\left[\log\sum_a e^{Q(s,a)} - \mathbb{E}_{a\sim\mu}[Q(s,a)]\right]}_{\text{conservative penalty}}$$ The first term pushes down all Q-values (via the log-sum-exp soft-max over all actions), while the second term pushes up Q-values for actions in the dataset. The net effect: Q-values for actions not in the dataset are conservatively pessimistic.
Intuition. The policy derived from $Q$ will prefer high-Q actions. If out-of-distribution actions have artificially deflated Q-values, the derived policy will stay within the support of $\mathcal{D}$ — it will prefer actions that the behavior policy also took. This prevents exploitation of Q-function errors in unexplored regions.
Theoretical guarantee — CQL lower-bounds the true Q-function

CQL can be shown to produce a $Q$ that is a lower bound on the true $q_\pi$ under the learned policy, up to a concentrability term. Formally (simplified): with appropriate $\alpha$, the CQL solution $\hat{Q}$ satisfies $\hat{Q}(s,a) \leq q_\pi(s,a)$ for all $(s,a)$ in $\mathcal{D}$.

Why is a lower bound useful? Because the policy $\pi = \arg\max_a \hat{Q}(s,a)$ maximises a lower bound — it finds actions that are conservatively guaranteed to be at least as good as the estimate. The policy cannot "exploit" overestimates because there are none by construction. This gives a safety guarantee: the policy selected by CQL cannot be worse than what the lower bound predicts.

The penalty coefficient $\alpha$ controls the conservatism-performance tradeoff: large $\alpha$ gives tighter lower bounds (safer) but may be overly pessimistic about actions near the dataset boundary; small $\alpha$ gives weaker guarantees but potentially better performance when the dataset has good coverage.

Importance sampling corrections
An alternative approach to the distributional shift problem is importance sampling (IS) — a classical statistical technique for estimating expectations under one distribution using samples from another.
The return under policy $\pi$ can be written as an expectation under $\mu$: $$J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_\mu\left[\frac{\rho_\pi(s,a)}{\rho_\mu(s,a)} r\right] = \mathbb{E}_\mu\left[\prod_{t=0}^T \frac{\pi(a_t|s_t)}{\mu(a_t|s_t)} \cdot G_0\right]$$ where the product of ratios $\prod_t \frac{\pi(a_t|s_t)}{\mu(a_t|s_t)}$ is the importance weight that reweights each trajectory from $\mu$ as if it had been collected under $\pi$.
In principle this gives an unbiased estimate of $J(\pi)$ from offline data. In practice, importance weights have severe problems:
  • Variance explosion. The product of per-step ratios $\prod_{t=0}^T w_t$ has variance that grows exponentially with $T$. For a 100-step episode, even a modest per-step ratio of $w_t = 1.5$ gives an overall weight of $1.5^{100} \approx 4 \times 10^{17}$ for the lucky trajectory — dominated by a single sample.
  • Zero-density problem. If $\mu(a|s) = 0$ for some $(a,s)$ that $\pi$ wants to take, the weight is undefined ($0/0$). This is exactly the out-of-distribution action problem.
  • Per-decision IS. The variance explosion can be partially mitigated by using per-decision IS, which takes the ratio at each individual time step rather than the product over the whole trajectory. This reduces variance while maintaining unbiasedness.
Worked examples
Example 1 — Q-value overestimation in offline setting
State $s$, two actions $\{a_0, a_1\}$. The dataset $\mathcal{D}$ contains only $(s, a_0, r=0, s')$. True values: $q_*(s,a_0)=0$, $q_*(s,a_1)=5$. Suppose function approximation gives $Q(s,a_1) = 12$ due to poor generalisation. What policy does Q-learning select, and why is it dangerous?
Q-learning selects $\pi(s) = \arg\max_a Q(s,a) = a_1$ since $Q(s,a_1)=12 > Q(s,a_0)=0$. But $a_1$ was never in $\mathcal{D}$ — there is no actual data about what happens when you take $a_1$ in state $s$. The estimate $Q(s,a_1)=12$ is pure extrapolation from the function approximator. In reality, $q_*(s,a_1)=5$, but if the agent is deployed and takes $a_1$, it gets return 5, not 12. Worse: Bellman backups will propagate $Q(s,a_1)=12$ to predecessors of $s$, inflating their Q-values too. The overestimation spreads backward through the value function. CQL would penalise $Q(s,a_1)$ specifically because $a_1 \notin \text{support}(\mathcal{D})$, pushing it below $Q(s,a_0)$ and causing the policy to stick with the known action.
Example 2 — Importance weight calculation
Single-step ($T=1$). Behavior policy: $\mu(a_0|s)=0.9$, $\mu(a_1|s)=0.1$. Target policy: $\pi(a_0|s)=0.3$, $\pi(a_1|s)=0.7$. What are the importance weights for $(s, a_0)$ and $(s, a_1)$?
$w(s,a_0) = \pi(a_0|s)/\mu(a_0|s) = 0.3/0.9 = 0.33$ $w(s,a_1) = \pi(a_1|s)/\mu(a_1|s) = 0.7/0.1 = 7.0$ The trajectory where $a_1$ was taken gets upweighted by $7\times$, because $\pi$ prefers $a_1$ much more than $\mu$ did. But $\mu$ sampled $a_1$ only 10% of the time — so most trajectories in $\mathcal{D}$ took $a_0$ and get downweighted to 0.33. Very few trajectories took $a_1$, but each one counts for 7. In a multi-step episode, these weights multiply: after 10 steps, a trajectory consistently taking $a_1$ would have weight $7^{10} \approx 282$ million — essentially one trajectory dominates the entire estimate.
Food for thought
Reflection questions
  1. CQL is pessimistic: it lower-bounds Q-values for out-of-distribution actions. But what if the dataset was collected by a suboptimal behavior policy? Pessimism would then keep the learned policy close to the suboptimal behavior policy. How can offline RL ever learn a better-than-behavior-policy solution?
    Offline RL can outperform the behavior policy by stitching together different parts of different trajectories in the dataset. Even if no single trajectory is optimal, the Q-function learns the value of state-action pairs across trajectories — allowing the learned policy to take the best action seen from any source at each state. The constraint is that the learned policy can only improve within the support of $\mathcal{D}$; it cannot discover actions or states never visited by $\mu$.
  2. Offline RL is closely related to imitation learning (behavioral cloning). Behavioral cloning simply clones the behavior policy $\mu$ via supervised learning. When would CQL significantly outperform behavioral cloning, and when would they produce similar results?
    CQL outperforms when: (1) the dataset contains suboptimal trajectories that BC would faithfully replicate, but CQL can identify the better actions; (2) the dataset covers a mix of policies and CQL can stitch together the best parts. BC and CQL converge when the dataset is entirely composed of near-optimal expert demonstrations — in this case, cloning the expert and maximising return produce the same result.
Common pitfalls
Mistakes worth knowing about in advance
  • Running standard Q-learning on offline data. This almost always fails due to Q-value overestimation. Always use a conservatism mechanism (CQL, BC regularisation, model uncertainty penalties) when the dataset is fixed.
  • Evaluating offline RL by training set performance. The learned policy must be evaluated by actually deploying it (or using a held-out dataset for off-policy evaluation). A policy that achieves high Q-values on $\mathcal{D}$ might exploit Q-function errors and perform poorly in deployment.
  • Thinking more data always helps. A large dataset collected by a random policy has full coverage but mostly useless transitions. A small dataset of expert demonstrations has excellent quality but may miss important transitions. The quality and diversity of $\mathcal{D}$ matter as much as its size.
